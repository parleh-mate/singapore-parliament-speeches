NATIONAL LANGUAGE IN GOVERNMENT AND AIDED SCHOOLS


    8. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Minister for Education whether and when, the Government proposes to make the National Language a compulsory subject in all Government and Aided schools.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Before the Government can make the National Language a compulsory subject in schools, it is the Government's duty to ensure that it can provide the necessary staff for teaching the National Language.

    The Member for Kampong Kembangan .must be well aware of the acute shortage of Malay teachers both in Malay schools and in non-Malay schools, and also the comparatively small number of English and Chinese medium teachers who are sufficiently proficient in the Malay language to be able to teach it.

    May I now give him the figures? For an enrolment of 19,000 Malay children in Malay schools, we have only 303 trained teachers, the balance of 297 teachers being only trainee teachers.

    For the teaching of Malay in English and Chinese medium schools, the position was pathetic when this Government took office. Statistics show that there were only 34 Malay teachers. As the present total school population, excluding those in the Malay schools, is about 320,000, it is not practical to talk of making the National Language a compulsory subject in our font-Malay schools at the present time. But the new Government wasted no time in getting on with the task of providing the teachers. Language classes were started in the Teachers' Training College, and the Ministry devised examinations to ensure that accepted standards were established. To date, two examinations have been held, and a total of 2,718 teachers, of whom 2,304 are from the English medium, 409 from the Chinese medium, and 5 from the Tamil medium, have sat for the Std. I examination, while 41 teachers, of whom 31 are from the English medium and 10 from the Chinese medium, have sat for their Std. II

I examination, while 1,113 teachers, of whom 845 will be from the English medium, 264 from the Chinese medium and 4 from the Tamil medium, will sit for the Std. II examination.

SINGAPORE SAILORS' INSTITUTE INCORPORATION
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Minister for Labour and Law (Mr K. M. Byrne): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Sailors' Institute, commonly known as Connell House, was established in 1924, under the provisions of the Singapore Sailors' Institute Incorporation Ordinance (Chapter 321 of the Revised Edition), to provide accommodation and recreational facilities for members of the mercantile marine using this port. Being the only statutory institution providing such facilities at that time, it was necessary to have some system of priorities in dealing with a large number of visiting seafarers seeking accommodation there. Hence it was provided in section 4 of the Singapore Sailors' Institute Incorporation Ordinance that preference was to be given in all cases to British subjects. But as a result of the general improvement and expansion in the

Column: 750

accommodation facilities at the Sailors' Institute, and after the establishment of the Singapore Asian Seamen's Club, it has been found that it is no longer necessary for any such system of priorities to be adhered to, and accordingly this preferential treatment has, in practice, been discarded over the last few years. Therefore, the Government seeks, in clause 2 of the Bill presently before the House, to regularise the position.

    Further, in the past, it was common for all matters connected with shipping and seamen, regardless of whether they were concerned with technical matters of port administration or shipping, or merely recreational or welfare activities, to be left under the administrative control of the Master Attendant. Under such circumstances, the Master Attendant, a nautical officer who should only be concerned with technical matters, was, under the provisions of the Singapore Sailors' institute incorporation Ordinance, charged with responsibility for management of the Sailors' Institute in his ex-officio capacity of Chairman of the Committee of Management appointed under section 7 thereof. With all seamen's welfare matters recently transferred to my Ministry, the Government seeks, through clause 3 of the Bill presently before the House, powers to enable, me, as Minister in charge, to appoint a member of the Committee other than the Master Attendant to be chairman of the Sailors' Institute Committee. This would leave the Master Attendant free to devote his full attention to those technical duties for which purpose he has been appointed.
