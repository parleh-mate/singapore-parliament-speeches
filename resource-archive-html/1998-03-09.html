RETRENCHED WORKERS

(Retraining for alternative jobs)


    The following Question stood in the name of Mr Chiam See Tong -

    1. To ask the Minister for Labour whether, in the light of the economic down turn and the possibility of more workers being retrenched, the Government has taken any steps to retrain workers, who are retrenched, for alternative jobs.

    Mr Bernard Chen (West Coast): Question No. 1.

    The Minister for Labour (Dr Lee Boon Yang): There are presently several Government-supported schemes to assist retrenched workers in enhancing their employability.

    Under the Skills Redevelopment Programme (SRP) funded by Government and managed by the National Trades Union Congress, employers who recruit retrenched workers could apply for training grant to upgrade the skills of the workers so that they could meet the needs of the new jobs. The SRP is targetted at less educated workers in their 30s and 40s, who may face greater difficulty seeking re-employment. Government's support is up to 80% of course fees to be paid by the Productivity and Standards Board (PSB) from the Skills Development Fund (SDF) and up to 70% of absentee payroll subject to a maximum of $4.20 per trainee-hour, paid by EDB.

    Sir, I have been informed that to date, 1,230 workers have gone through or are undergoing certifiable training under the Skills Redevelopment Programme. Of these workers, more than 50% are above the age of 40 and approximately 70% have below Secondary 2 education.

    Assistance is also granted to the retrenched workers under the tripartite "Back to Work" Programme launched in September 1996 by my Ministry together with the Productivity and Standards Board, the National Trades Union Congress and the Singapore National Employers Federation. The assistance

includes helping the retrenched workers find suitable jobs and encouraging employers to employ retrenched workers through a training grant which enables the workers to undergo Core Skills and Job Skills Training.

    Core Skills Training includes confidence building, personal effectiveness and basic computer literacy. The training is fully supported by the SDF which provides a flat grant of $350 per trainee including an allowance of $100 to each trainee to help defray his meals and transport expenses. Job Skills Training is for the purpose of helping the workers to acquire specific skills required for the jobs. For such training, SDF will provide enhanced funding up to 80% of the course fees for external training programme or $4 per trainee hour for on-the-job training.

The Panel will also encourage employers to take advantage of available training incentives to improve the skills of their workers during the lull period. MEASURES TO SAFEGUARD AGAINST MASSIVE BLACKOUT


    2. Mr S Iswaran asked the Minister for Trade and Industry what measures already exist, and what additional measures are needed, to safeguard against the occurrence of a massive blackout like that which recently affected Auckland, New Zealand.

Column: 589



    The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Tang Guan Seng) (for the Minister for Trade and Industry): The cause of the recent cable failures in Auckland, New Zealand, has yet to be established. According to some reports, they were probably caused by the abnormal drying up of the soil surrounding the cables due to the prolonged hot and dry summer. Consequently, heat could not be conducted away from the heavily loaded cables fast enough. The cable temperature could have risen well above its design specifications, leading to the cascading cable failures.

    Such form of cable failure is unlikely in Singapore as PowerGrid Ltd, the electricity network operator in Singapore, has instituted the following measures:

    (a) Firstly, PowerGrid has built a strong transmission network. For example, the CBD network is supplied by multiple 230 kV sources and is interconnected to provide contingencies to take care of unforeseen cable failures or damages.

    (b) Secondly, PowerGrid's 230 kV transmission cables are installed with backfill designed to ensure efficient heat dissipation and avoid thermal overloading.

    (c) Thirdly, with advances in technology, Distributed Temperature Sensing (DTS) cables are now available which enable real-time monitoring of the actual cable temperature. PowerGrid has installed such DTS cable on a 400 kV cable and will be installing it on two additional 400 kV cables and future 230 kV cables.

    PowerGrid has a rolling 10-year development plan to expand and renew the network to meet Singapore's electricity demand in a reliable, secure and timely manner. For example, PowerGrid is currently developing a new 230 kV substation at Peng Nguan Street to reinforce the network in anticipation of the CBD's rising demand in the years to come.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Mah Bow Tan): Mr Speaker, Sir, road pricing is a major pillar in our long-term strategy to keep our roads free-flowing. Our roads are our economic arteries. If they get clogged, transport costs will go up, businesses will lose time and money, and the productivity of our workers will suffer.

    Road pricing has been implemented in Singapore since 1975 with the Area Licensing Scheme, and recently with the Road Pricing Scheme on our expressways. It has been successful in managing the traffic on our roads. But more needs to be done because on one hand our vehicle population is growing, yet on the other hand our land area remains constant.

    The Electronic Road Pricing (ERP) will replace both the ALS and the RPS schemes. It is more flexible, more efficient and in the long run, more cost effective. It marks a shift in emphasis, from charging for vehicle ownership to charging for usage. It will encourage more judicious usage and keep our roads congestion free.

    There is no reason to delay the start of ERP on 1st April because of the current economic situation. RESPONSIBILITY FOR FOREIGN WORKERS


    4. Dr Teo Ho Pin asked the Minister for Labour whether his Ministry will consider making sub-contractors solely responsible for the foreign workers whom they employ instead of the current practice of making main contractors jointly responsible for these foreign workers who are beyond the control of the main contractors.

    Dr Lee Boon Yang:

    Work Permits are issued to subcontractors on the support of main contractors for their projects. The main contractors are thus partially responsible for the foreign workers brought in by their subcontractors to work on their projects. However, in the past, some main contractors had not exercised vigilance in the support of their subcontractors' work permit applications. Their careless endorsement of the subcontractors' work permit applications allowed some unscrupulous subcontractors to either inflate their foreign worker needs, or submit applications using false contracts. As a result, they operate as labour suppliers and bring in foreign workers, receive commission, default on levy payments and even abandon the workers, resulting in a host of social problems.

    To stem this abuse, the Ministry had decided in December 1997 to refer subcontractors' work permit applications to main contractors for verification and hold main contractors jointly responsible for any workers abandoned by their subcontractors. This encouraged main contractors to scrutinise their subcontractors' work permit applications. It also emphasised the important role of main contractors to ensure the proper employment of foreign construction workers by their subcontractors. The measure has met with some success. Since its implementation, approximately 25% of the subcontractors' applications had not been endorsed by main contractors. The main contractors' involvement has helped to reduce the incidence of abuse to some extent.

    Once foreign workers are brought in by the subcontractors on the support of main contractors, the subcontractors and main contractors would be accountable for the accommodation and placements of foreign workers should they be subsequently displaced. Main contractors have the resources to house workers abandoned by their subcontractors, or transfer their employment to other subcontractors within the same project or in other projects.

    It is thus not true that main contractors have no control over the foreign workers employed by the subcontractors. These foreign workers were brought in to work on their projects in the first instance. Therefore, requiring main contractors to share the responsibility with their subcontractors is necessary to avoid abuses and illegal practices.
