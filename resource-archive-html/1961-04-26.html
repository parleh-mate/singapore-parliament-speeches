ECONOMIC ASSISTANCE TO MALAY CITIZENS OF SINGAPORE


    2. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Minister for Finance whether the Government has any plans for giving economic assistance to the Malay citizens of Singapore.

    Dr Goh Keng Swee: Mr Speaker, Sir, before I go into the details of what the Government is now doing and what it intends to do to assist Singapore Malays, I wish to outline some of the principal features of the economic position of the Malays in Singapore.

    Any plan for economic assistance must be based on a correct appraisal of the present situation and present needs. The 1957 census gives us some indications. Unfortunately, the census figures do not distinguish between Malays and Indonesians in the statistics on occupation. But this is only a minor disadvantage as the broad picture is clear enough.

    In 1957 there were 57,300 Malays and Indonesians working. There were 3,800 stated to be unemployed. Of the 57,300 persons working, a great majority worked as employees. The number of employees amounted to 51,400. These are the people who work for a wage or a salary. There were also, in addition, some 5,900 who were self-employed, and most of these are fishermen, hawkers and such like.

    The first feature about the economic status of the Malays in Singapore is that a great majority of them are paid employees. In this regard, the Malays in Singapore differ fundamentally from the Malays in the Federation where a great

majority are not employees but are farmers and fishermen - people who work on their own account.

    Further, of the 51,400 employees, no less than 26,900 are employed by the Government, statutory corporations, the City Council and the British Defence Departments. Some 11,300 work for the Government and the City Council, and some 11,800 work for the British Defence Departments. From this fact, it therefore follows that employment opportunities in the public service are matters of great importance to the Malay citizens in Singapore. More detailed and up-to-date figures in respect of Central Government monthly-rated employees are available. On 31st December, 1959, of 22,966 employees in the Central Government, no less than 6,908 are Malays.

    As regards the general standard of living of the Malay population, not much by way of a scientific study has been made, although this does not prevent people from hazarding all kinds of loose generalisations. When I was a civil servant, I carried out a study of incomes in Singapore among the urban working-class population. In 1953/54, I conducted an extensive social survey in the City of Singapore to find out the conditions of those families depending on earners whose incomes do not exceed $400 per month each. Some 83 per cent of Singapore families living in the city fall within this income bracket. Within the survey group which, by and large, represents what we may loosely term the working class, the average income of a Malay family compares well with the others. Malay families earn an average of $160 per month as against $148 of the general average. Within this selected group, that is to say, among working-class families, it can be said that the notion that Malays are worse off than others does not hold true.
