SOURCES OF WATER SUPPLY TO SINGAPORE
(Estimated maximum and safe capacity)


    1. Mr A. P. Rajah asked the Prime Minister what is the estimated maximum and safe capacity, in millions of gallons per day, of the following sources of water supply to Singapore:-

    (i) MacRitchie Reservoir;

    (ii) Peirce and Seletar Reservoirs;

Column: 1242



    (iii) Gunong Pulai and Pontian; and

    (iv) Tebrau River.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Prime Minister (Mr Chan Sun Wing) (for the Prime Minister): Mr Speaker, for impounding reservoirs the maximum and safe daily capacity is the average quantity of water that can be drawn off daily. One must allow for the available storage capacity of the reservoirs, dry weather conditions, and the capacity of our treatment plants to supply good quality water. The maximum safe capacity of a reservoir is therefore its safe yield which takes into account these considerations.

    The estimated safe yields of our reservoirs are as follows: 

           MacRitchie                 8 million gallons daily

           Peirce and Seletar        12 million gallons daily

           Gunong Pulai and Pontian  18 million gallons daily

           Tebrau River              30 million gallons daily

                                        during wet weather

                                              or

                                     15 million gallons daily

                                        during dry weather


    We therefore draw an estimated total of 38 million gallons daily from our reservoirs. But our estimated average daily consumption is 62.5 million gallons. The remaining 30 million gallons during good climatic conditions is drawn daily from the Tebrau River which goes direct to the consumer after treatment.

WATER SUPPLY FROM TEBRAU RIVER
(Capacity to supply additional quantity required for 1960)


    2. Mr A. P. Rajah asked the Prime Minister whether the dry-weather flow in the Tebrau River is capable of supplying the additional quantity of water required for 1960.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Prime Minister (Mr Chan Sun Wing) (for the Prime Minister): Mr Speaker, the Tebrau River, because of the absence of storage facilities, can give a safe yield of 15 million gallons a day during dry

Column: 1243

weather. It is therefore not capable of supplying the additional quantity of water estimated to be required for next year. But our water supply does not depend on Tebrau during dry weather. The general water supply scheme is to draw off all the available water from Tebrau during the wet months and to conserve as much water as possible in the impounding reservoirs against dry spells.

    The storage capacity of our reservoirs allows for a maximum water bank of a total of 5,900 million gallons. The break-down storage figures are as follows:- 

          MacRitchie                    1,038 million gallons

          Peirce and Seletar            1,073 million gallons

          Gunong Pulai and Pontian      3,789 million gallons


    As mentioned earlier, we draw a total of 38 million gallons daily from these reservoirs which are replenished by rain-falls. The margin of safety will be further increased when the Tebrau water-works output is stepped up and the Johore River scheme is completed.

    Mr Rajah: Can the Parliamentary Secretary to the Prime Minister give any indication as to when the Johore River scheme will be completed?

    Mr Chan Sun Wing: I require notice of that question.

ALIENS BECOMING SINGAPORE CITIZENS AND SINGAPORE
CITIZENS RENOUNCING CITIZENSHIP (Numbers)


    7. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Minister for Home Affairs to state how many aliens became citizens of Singapore between 1st January and 15th December, 1959, and how many citizens of Singapore voluntarily renounced their Singapore citizenship during the same period.

    Mr Ong Pang Boon: Mr Speaker, Sir, it is assumed that by "aliens", the hon. Member is referring to non-Commonwealth citizens. 9,608 persons were registered or naturalized as Singapore citizens under sections 8, 9 and 15 of the Singapore Citizenship Ordinance during the period in question. It is regretted, however, that the information asked for in the first part of the question is not readily available as the registers of citizens do not distinguish between aliens and the other categories of persons eligible for registration.

    With regard to the second part of the question, 335 persons voluntarily renounced their Singapore citizenship during the period in question.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Low Por Tuck): Mr Speaker, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Mr Speaker, this Bill is intended to give legislative sanction for the expenditure of additional sums of money voted on by the Assembly during the course of this year.

    The Estimates of Revenue and Expenditure for 1959 were approved by the last Assembly in 1958. It is obvious that some unforeseen or new expenditure like that on the National Loyalty Week or the expenditure on the creation of the two new Ministries of Culture and National Development would require to be incurred in addition to the estimates since it is not possible to budget for all eventualities. Therefore, during the course of the year, the Government had occasion to submit supplementary estimates for the approval of the Assembly to meet unforeseen expenditure. In addition, it was also necessary for supplementary estimates to provide for certain financial requirements laid down in the Constitution. For instance, in Cmd. (New Series) No. 14 of 1959, $4 million was provided to establish a Contingencies Fund. Then in Cmd. (New Series) No. 34 of 1959, $42.5 million was voted to create Advance Accounts. In all, therefore, a total of $58,039,072 was voted by the Assembly as Supplementary Estimates in the Ordinary Budget and $3,808,590 as Supplementary Estimates in the Development Budget.

    Now, under section 97 (2) of the Singapore (Constitution) Order in Council, 1958, a Supplementary Appropriation Bill containing under appropriate heads of expenditure, the sums of money which have been approved by the Assembly in the Supplementary Estimates for 1959 shall be introduced into the Assembly. The Bill lays out all the

    Mr Speaker, as Members of the House would have already been aware of, the Standing Orders Committee have made two Reports and amendments which have already been accepted by the Assembly during the past seven months. The Report that is presented today is the third and final Report of the Standing Orders Committee.

    In the space of seven months, a complete revision has been made of the Standing Orders according to the light of our experience and here at this point, Mr Speaker, I must pay tribute to you yourself and your staff for the intensive perusal of the Standing Orders. The Standing Orders Committee certainly must acknowledge the considerable assistance and guidance you have given to the Committee in making these revisions.

    Standing Orders in any Parliament or Assembly are essential. In Singapore, for that matter in Malaya, the concept of parliamentary democracy is a new one and we are at the beginnings of nation building where we try to introduce the idea that where you have disagreements or disputes let them be solved by negotiation and talk by the representatives of the people in the Legislature. It is important for us therefore to nurture not only the concept of parliamentary democracy but also to see to it that the forms of parliamentary democracy are observed, otherwise the concept will become sour and the idea of parliamentary democracy will be nothing but a mockery.

    Standing Orders are a guide for Members of the House. They are only a guide. Much more important, there must be also a desire and a willingness among Members to observe Standing Orders, otherwise they become pieces of paper and are ineffective.
