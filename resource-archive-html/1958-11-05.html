FLOOD PREVENTION
(Statement by the Minister for Communications and Works)


10.29 a.m.

    Mr Francis Thomas: Mr Speaker, Sir, some hon. Members will probably have received, communications from their constituents as a result of the flooding caused by heavy rain on Monday last, the 3rd of November, and I think it may be useful if I make a statement on what has been achieved so far by the Government's flood control works; on what remains to be done; and on the broad picture, in short, as it is today and as far as we can forsee it for the next few years.

    I had better start by enumerating some of the basic simple points to which I have referred in the past in the Assembly, but which may not be clear in hon. Members' recollection.

    The first point is that we have to get the water right off the land and into the sea, or else we have to retain it in a properly planned and constructed reservoir. It would be useless to approach this flooding problem on a short-term vote-catching basis. It would be fairly easy sometimes to shift the flood water off the land of political supporters and on to the land of political opponents. But I would not wish to do that and I am sure that my honourable political opponents on the Opposition Benches would not wish me to do it to them. The only way to deal with flood problems is by proper overall schemes after a full study of the facts.

    My second point is that flooding cannot be dealt with as simply as some people suppose. It is not just a matter of deepening or widening the channels of the drains. If deepening brings the bottom of the drain down to sea level, then the sea will flow up the drain at high tide, and this is not a desirable result. The only way of stopping this

Column: 910

is to have a fairly expensive tidal gate at the mouth of the drain, such as we have put in at the mouth of the Geylang River.

    Moreover, even where the land is above sea level, deepening of a drain may have serious results. It lowers the water table and dries out the subsoil. For example, in the Queenstown area, the land has been reclaimed by putting earth-fill on top of swamp mud. A deepening of the drain would lower the water table and dry out the foundations and thus affect the stability of the houses.

    Even where no buildings would suffer from deepening the drains, lowering the water table will draw away the water from the roots of the farmers' crops and then they cannot grow their vegetables for the market.

    The third point is that flood difficulties are very much increased as development proceeds. This operates in two ways. Firstly, every new piece of development increases both the size and the speed of the run-off of water. Along Bukit Timah Road, each new estate that is developed sends more water down more quickly to the lower parts of the roads. In the same way along Thomson Road on Sunday last I was asked by residents to look at a local flooding problem. Although it was some hours after the rain had stopped, I found a house where six inches of watery mud was still standing in and around the house and this was being accumulated by being trapped between the old and the new Thomson Roads. Residents nearby on the other side of the new Thomson Road telephoned me on Monday to say that their houses which had never been flooded before, were being flooded now. I have not yet got a final report on this particular area. I quote it because it illustrates the danger of new development sending flood waters down to people at the bottom of the hills.

    Development also causes flooding difficulties when it puts valuable capital investment on to land which would otherwise have been used for drainage. For example, the flooding below the Peirce Reservoir to which I was just

Column: 911

referring could have been dealt with by a drain out towards Ponggol but before the drain could be surveyed or constructed or even planned the appropriate line for it had been covered by development at Serangoon Garden Estate and that way out is now closed.

    The fourth point I should make is the problem of achieving co-ordination between different authorities. The City Council in the City area has to deal with water which is flowing down to them from the rural area and in the rural area the decisions which have been or will be made about the three big City Council reservoirs in the centre of the island affect the planning of flood control works from Mandai right down to Potong Pasir. To co-ordinate plans between separate authorities is never easy.

    My fifth general point is that adequate flood control schemes are very expensive. They not only take a lot of construction work but they also involve big requirements for land; and in the overcrowded state of Singapore, land requirement means also heavy costs for land clearance and resettlement.
