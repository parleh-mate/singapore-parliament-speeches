ACT
(Amending legislation for enhanced compensation)


    2. Inche Abdul Aziz Karim asked the Minster for Labour if he will introduce legislation to amend the Workmen's Compensation Act (Chapter 130)

Column: 465

(i) to ensure that a workman who suffers permanently disabling injury is compensated for loss of earnings, medical costs, rehabilitation, pain, suffering, shock, loss of standing in the community, humiliation, mortification and all other forms of tangible and intangible ill-effects; and (ii) to entitle a permanently disabled workman to monetary compensation exceeding several-fold the rates and sums laid down under the existing law.

    The Minister of State for Labour (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Labour): (i) Mr Speaker, Sir, the Workmen's Compensation Act provides that the workman who suffers as a result of an accident arising out of and in the course of his employment shall be entitled to compensation. The compensation is related to the loss of earning capacity and is intended to be a substitute income contributed by the employer. In addition, the employer is required to bear medical costs arising out of a workman's hospitalisation and treatment, including artificial and surgical appliances.

    According to the Act, the injured worker is entitled to compensation, regardless of whether or not he is responsible for the accident. Members will no doubt agree that this is a practical and realistic approach to the question of compensation. However, the worker has the option of proceeding to claim damages in the Civil Courts if he feels that the liability for the accident can be attributed to the employer or a third party.

    The Member for Kallang will no doubt agree that whatever the quantum of compensation, it can never fully compensate the worker who sustains an injury resulting in permanent disablement. Moreover, compensation on the lines suggested by the Member will present numerous difficulties in practice, such as quantifying the compensation payable for such intangible ill-effects like pain, shock, mortification, humiliation and loss of standing in the

community. However, may I assure the Member that practical suggestions from him would always be given due consideration by the Ministry.

    (ii) Members will recall that the Workmen's Compensation (Amendment) Act, 1971, passed by Parliament on the 19th October, 1971, had increased considerably, in fact, threefold in most cases the quantum of workmen's compensation payable to workmen and their dependants. Under the amended provisions, a workman above the age of 18 years would be entitled to a sum of $28,800, or a lump sum equivalent to 60 months' earnings, whichever is the less, for injuries resulting in total permanent incapacity. The Act also specifies separate rates of compensation payable to workmen below the age of 18 years and those below the age of 16 years. In addition to the compensation for total permanent incapacity, the Act provides that the workman would also receive an additional one-quarter of the computed sum where he is unable to perform the essential actions of life without the constant attention of another person.

RENEWAL OF CLASS IV DRIVING LICENCES
(Imposition of limit in unladen weight)


    4. Inche Abdul Aziz Karim asked the Minister for Communications (a) if he knows to the best of his knowledge that persons who had been granted class 4 licences under the Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules, 1948, which qualified them to drive heavy motor cars with no maximum limit in unladen weight, have on renewal of their licences been granted a similar class licence under the Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules, 1969, which

Column: 469

automatically disqualified and debarred them from driving vehicles exceeding 7 tons in unladen weight; (b) if he is aware that it is common occurrence that holders of class 4 heavy motor car licences are still driving vehicles exceeding 7 tons in unladen weight with the full knowledge, but with no valid authority; of the Registrar of Vehicles; and (c) in view of the fact that such persons might be committing road traffic offences and might face serious consequences, if he will take urgent steps to restore to such persons, who held class 4 heavy motor car licences granted under the Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules, 1948, their status and qualification in all fairness to them and in the interest of the general public in the other respect.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, when the Registry of Vehicles driving licence records were being computerised on 1st February, 1969, the 1948 Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules were replaced by the 1969 Rules. In order to streamline the driving licence records and to facilitate issuance of driving licences, the classification for motor vehicles was at the same time reduced from nine to five classes.

    It is true that persons issued with Class IV licences under the old rules were allowed to drive heavy motor-cars with no maximum limit in the unladen weight, whereas persons issued with Class IV licences under the new rules are not licensed to drive heavy motorcars above seven tons, for which a Class V licence is required. This, however, does not mean that under the Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules, 1969, those who had acquired Class IV driving licences before 1st February, 1969 are being automatically disqualified and debarred from driving heavy motor-cars above seven tons. The validity of licences issued under the 1948 Rules is not affected, as the 1969 Rules were not introduced with retrospective effect. In addition, there is a saving clause under Rule 17 (3) of the Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences)

Column: 470

Rules, 1969, which provides that the new rules do not alter the classes of motor vehicles which a holder of a licence has been licensed to drive before the date when the new rules come into operation. Those issued with Class 1V licences before 1st February, 1969 are, therefore, legally entitled to drive heavy motor-cars above seven tons. The question of restoring to these people their status and qualifications to drive vehicles above seven tons, therefore, does not arise.

    In reducing the classes of vehicles from nine to five, an unfortunate anomaly was created when those persons issued with Class IV licences prior to 1st February, 1969 renewed the old licences for new ones, as the new driving licences contained a pre-printed description of all the five classes of vehicles as defined under the new rules. In this description, Class IV vehicles are defined as heavy motor-cars above 2j tons up to seven tons, although these people are legally entitled to drive heavy motor-cars above seven tons as they had X been granted licences prior to 1st February, 1969.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, I wish to express my gratitude and thanks to the Member for Kallang for being so alert and drawing the attention of my Department to this anomaly. The necessary amending Rules re-classifying the classes of motor vehicles under the 1969 Rules to make driving licences issued under the 1948 and 1969 Motor Vehicles (Driving Licences) Rules equitable, have been published in the Government Gazette on 25th February, 1972. Action will also be taken by the Registrar of Vehicles to change the pre-printed description of the five classes of vehicles as contained in the new driving licences.

Survey of International Economic Events

    That year will probably go down in the annals of economic and financial history as a turning point for the world monetary system. For the first time since it established its complete dominance after the Second World War, the strength of the U.S. Dollar was called seriously into question. At first, it was suggested that the U.S, should favour a policy of "benign neglect" of the dollar's weakness. This casual attitude soon disappeared. In May, the first indications of potential trouble came with the free floating upwards of the German Mark, and the revaluation of the Swiss Franc and the Austrian Schilling. This was followed by a period of intense speculation in the Mark, the Yen, and finally against the U.S. Dollar, which resulted in a rapid deterioration of the dollar's position. Finally, the international monetary system devised at Bretton-Woods, of internationally agreed par values and strictly maintained exchange margins of not more than 2 per cent, collapsed with the "Nixon shock" in August, announcing

Many countries were obliged to float their currencies or to restrict dealings in their currency exchanges, and the resultant uncertainties on prices had a depressive effect upon world trade. (2) Earned income Relief up to $1,000 under the Income Tax Act
