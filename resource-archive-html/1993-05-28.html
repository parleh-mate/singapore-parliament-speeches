ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

HDB RENTAL FLATS
(Demand)


    1. Dr S. Vasoo asked the Minister for National Development whether applications for rental flats increased significantly in 1991 and 1992, and whether there are adequate rental flats to cater to the increase in demand.

     The Minister of State for National Development (Mr Lim Hng Kiang) (for the Minister for National Development): Mr Speaker, Sir, the total number of applications for HDB rental flats increased from 2,400 in 1989 to about 5,900 in 1991 and 1992.

    As I have explained to this House last year, the increase in demand for rental flats is largely due to the lowering of the minimum age limit for rental applicants. In 1989, the age limit for rental applicants who apply with a proper family nucleus was lowered from 29 to 21 years of age. In 1991, the age limit for rental of HDB flats by single persons, under the Joint Singles Scheme, was lowered from 40 years for males, and 35 years for female applicants, to 35 years for both male and female applicants.

    I am happy to say that, despite the rise in demand for rental flats, HDB has succeeded in maintaining the number of applicants on its waiting list at a steady level of around 3,000 by releasing more flats for rental. At present, there are about 3,100 applicants on the waiting list for rental flats. About 1,400 flats are currently available for rental to citizen families. To meet the rise in demand for rental flats, HDB has increased the supply of rental flats. An additional 700 flats will be released for rental in the coming months. HDB will endeavour to keep the waiting time for rental flats as short as possible.

    Let me first address the first category. New HDB flats are priced to reflect the construction and land costs, premium factors such as good locality, storey height, and so on, as well as design improvements.

    In the last nine months, from July 1992 to April 1993, construction costs for HDB flats have been stable. As a result, price increases for new HDB flats have averaged only 1.8%. HDB expects the price adjustments for 1993 to be minimal. If construction and land costs continue to remain stable, purchasers of new HDB flats need not worry about higher prices.

    Let me now turn to HDB resale flats. Although there are restrictions on the sale and purchase of HDB resale flats, their prices are largely determined by market forces. HDB's records show that resale prices have increased by about 5-10% since HDB announced on 23rd March this year that it would increase the amount of mortgage financing it provided to buyers. One possible reason for this increase is that the larger HDB loans have made buyers more willing to pay higher prices for a flat of their choice. HDB expects the increase in resale prices due to this reason to be a one-off phenomenon.
