    Mr Lim Hng Kiang: Mr Speaker, Sir, the recent closure of trading of Malaysian shares on Clob, after Malaysia imposed capital controls and new rules on the clearing and settlement of Malaysian shares, has highlighted the risks of investing in shares. There is a price risk inherent in any share. But in addition, shares listed on overseas exchanges are subject to regulatory risk arising from changes in rules by the overseas exchange.

    The Government cannot protect investors from such risks. Investors have to keep their eyes open and judge for themselves whether the returns are adequate to justify the risks.

    The Malaysian government's opposition to trading of Malaysian shares on Clob is long standing, and has always been reported in the press. When the SES established Clob in January 1990, after Malaysia unilaterally delisted all Malaysian shares from the SES, the KLSE objected strongly. It declared publicly that

This exchange was widely reported in the press, and put investors on notice of Malaysia's intentions.

    The SES has issued a statement explaining why it did not stop trading of Malaysian shares on Clob earlier, for example, immediately after the KLSE announced its new rules on 31st August 1998, or even before that.     Mr Lee Yock Suan: Given the close interdependence of the Singapore and Malaysian economies, it is inevitable that Singapore will be affected by Malaysia's capital and exchange control measures. The closure of CLOB is an immediate consequence.

    Activity in Singapore's foreign exchange market has been reduced by the cessation of Ringgit trades. However, trading in

Ringgit had already declined over the past months and was only a small proportion of total turnover just prior to the exchange controls.     Sir, the Bill seeks to amend the Housing and Development Act in three main areas: first, to empower the HDB to raise loans inside and outside Singapore through the issue of bonds and other financial instruments; second, to remove insolvency protection attached to HDB flats in cases where the flats are wholly owned by Singapore permanent residents; and, third, to confer upon HDB limited power to acquire individual strata units within HDB buildings for the specific purpose of upgrading the buildings.

    First, the issue of bonds - clause 8. The Bill seeks to enhance HDB's flexibility to raise loans from a wider range of sources in any way it deems advantageous. At present, HDB's development programmes and operational requirements are financed mainly by Housing Development Loans provided by the Government. With the passing of the Bill, HDB will launch its first bond issue in the later part of this financial year to fund part of its housing development programme.

    By tapping the bond market, HDB can reap the benefits of a more efficient financing structure that closely matches its development expenditure needs. The issuing of bonds by statutory boards will also help broaden and deepen the Singapore bond market, adding to its diversity.

Column: 1062



    Let me now turn to the protection of HDB flats owned by Singapore permanent residents - clauses 6 and 10. The present Act protects all HDB flats whether owned by Singapore citizens or Singapore permanent residents (PRs) from being vested in the Official Assignee following the bankruptcy of their owners. It also provides that no HDB flat shall be sold by court order to satisfy a judgment debt. The Bill proposes to remove such protection in cases where HDB flats are wholly owned by Singapore PRs. We want to restrict the scope of the Act in so far as the issue of immunity from insolvency law is concerned to make a distinction between Singapore citizens and Singapore PRs. The amendment will cover all Singapore PR flat owners regardless of when they might have purchased their HDB flats. However, it will apply only to persons who are declared bankrupt after the commencement of the amendment Act.

    The third change is the acquisition of HDB flats for the purpose of the Main Upgrading Programme - clause 7. The Bill also seeks to incorporate a new provision in the Act to confer upon HDB the limited power to acquire individual strata units within HDB buildings for the specific purpose of implementing the Main Upgrading Programme (MUP). This will be necessary in order to build additional features in some MUP projects. For example, in blocks with no void decks, the ground floor units may need to be acquired and cleared in order to set up communal facilities not provided for in the past, such as a kindergarten or an old folks' corner. Let me assure Members that compulsory acquisition will be carried out only after careful evaluation of the various possible options. Furthermore, the final decision lies with the residents of the block in question who will vote for the MUP package that they prefer. The flats will be acquired only if 75% or more of eligible voters are in favour of a particular upgrading package which involves such acquisition. HDB will give the affected lessees a fair compensation package based on market value similar to that given under the Selective En-Bloc Redevelopment

LAND LEASE PAYMENTS


    2. Mr Gerard Ee asked the Minister for Finance whether he will allow land lease payments to be made tax deductible in the same manner as for rentals, as such payments represent a substantial business cost and are in substance similar to rental payments.

    Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau:

    Under our existing tax laws, whether a payment is tax deductible will depend on whether it is a revenue or capital expenditure in nature. If it is the latter, it would not be allowed a deduction as conversely, we do not tax capital gains. Lease payments, if made for the purpose of acquiring an asset of enduring benefit, would be a capital expenditure and thus not allowed a tax deduction.

    Mr Ee may be aware that to assist companies to weather the tough economic conditions, JTC has introduced a number of changes with regard to its land lease policy. One of the measures is to offer industrial land tenants the option to pay their land rents for a period of up to 30 years in an upfront lump-sum payment, with effect from 1st January 1998. This option will help to reduce the cost of land for industrial users.

    To complement this measure, I have also decided to allow tax deduction for amortisation of such upfront payments made by JTC and HDB lessees for industrial land leases with a duration of less than 30 years. This is despite the fact that under our normal tax laws, such payments are generally capital expenditure and not allowed a tax deduction. The deduction is applicable to all qualifying new leases entered into on or after 1st January 98.

HEALTHCARE FOR THE ELDERLY


    5. Mdm Claire Chiang See Ngoh asked the Minister for Health, concerning healthcare for the elderly, (a) what kind of medical subsidy is given to the aged sick who have no Central Provident Fund savings or medical security funds; (b) what schemes his Ministry can offer to lighten the burden of the "sandwiched generation" of young working adults who are stressed by having to use their own Medisave to pay for the increasing medical expenses of their aged parents; and (c) if his Ministry will consider offering more substantial medical subsidies for the aged as a recognition of their contribution to nation building.

    Mr Yeo Cheow Tong:

    The elderly receive heavily subsidised primary health care at Government polyclinics, at 75% of the treatment cost. For hospital care, all patients have a choice of wards. For Class C beds, patients pay only 20% of the cost; the Government pays the remaining 80%. For Class B2 beds, the patients pay 35% of cost, Class B1 80%. There is no subsidy for Class A wards. Financial counselling is provided at the point of admission to enable patients to choose their ward class according to their financial means. To ensure that everyone has equal access to the heavily subsidised wards, Class B2 and C beds are kept at about 70% of the number of public acute hospital beds.

    We would like to assure the member that the public sector hospitals will not turn away patients who are unable to pay. The necessary treatment is always given first. In instances of financial hardship, Medifund acts as a safety net to ensure that no Singaporeans will be denied essential medical care just because of the inability to pay. Therefore, the existing systems are able to help the aged sick with no CPF savings or other sources of medical funds.

    As the aged can opt for treatment at Government polyclinics and acute hospitals which are already heavily subsidised, my Ministry does not consider it necessary to revise the subsidy level to them further. PROGRAMMES FOR THE RETRAINING OF WORKERS


    6. Mdm Claire Chiang See Ngoh asked the Minister for Manpower how his Ministry intends to measure the effectiveness of the various programmes set up by the National Trades Union Congress and other bodies to retrain workers, in terms of the impact of the programmes on workers' earnings, job placements and career mobility.

    Dr Lee Boon Yang:

    Government support for worker retraining is primarily through a $50 million grant to the National Trades Union Congress (NTUC) for the Skills Redevelopment Programme (SRP). This grant is matched by another $50 million from the Skills Development Fund. Another $20 million was set aside by the Government as an off-budget measure in July this year to expand training facilities and other infrastructure in support of the SRP.

    The SRP was initiated to address the issue of long-term employability of lower-skilled workers, especially the older ones. For this reason, it is a requirement of training under the SRP that it should lead to skill certification. At present, this means certification conferred by the Institute of Technical Education and the Polytechnics. Courses leading to skill certification by these institutions have been formulated with the inputs from industry. Workers who are skills certified as a result of training under the SRP are in better position to benefit from the recognition in the market for such skills. For example, the gross median monthly salary for an NTC-2 graduate is $1,250, while for an ITC graduate it is $1,500. As 70% of SRP trainees have an educational level below `N' levels, the ability to obtain such skill certification is in itself an achievement.

    In the present difficult economic climate, when workers face retrenchment, the Ministry is more concerned with job preservation as an indicator of effectiveness. In this respect, the SRP has so far given encouraging results. As at end-September, over 2,800 workers have been sent by their companies for training under the SRP. Another 1,300 have been sent for training by companies, which had adopted a shorter workweek; in other words, they were sent for training as an alternative to being retrenched. To date, none of these 4,100 workers have been retrenched. They are either still undergoing their training or have completed training and continue to be gainfully employed by their current employers.

    A further 400 workers were sent for training by companies prior to being retrenched. These were mainly companies that were winding down operations and had no choice but to let their staff go. They agreed to send these workers for training under the SRP in order to enhance their chances of getting another job. All the workers have since been successfully placed with new employers.

    The NTUC and the Singapore Hotel Association have just launched the sector-driven SRP for the hotel industry. To date, 55 hotels have signed up to retrain a total of 2,000 workers. Because of the economic downturn, these workers would have been vulnerable to retrenchment. Instead, the SRP has provided an opportunity for the hotel industry to upgrade its manpower at affordable cost during a lull period.

    The Ministry will continue to monitor the success of SRP training in enabling workers to retain their jobs.

    As the SRP is employer-based, the focus has been on persuading companies to send their existing workers for training. However, as the numbers of unemployed have risen, we have also embarked on an initiative to match unemployed workers with employers who are willing to take them on and support their training under the SRP. The Ministry will monitor the number of such unemployed workers who are successfully placed as a result of this initiative.

    In the longer term, the Ministry intends to study the overall effectiveness of the SRP in terms of the employability and increase in the earnings capacity of trainees. However, we should be mindful that such analyses would be more meaningful when the economic situation has stabilised.

SCHOOL DROP-OUTS



    13. Mr Gerard Ee asked the Minister for Education (a) how many students dropped out of the education chain in the years 1995 to 1997; (b) whether there has been any study to determine the causes; and (c) what measures have been taken to reduce such incidences.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    From 1995 to 1997, about 1,880 students dropped out of the education system yearly. This represents about 0.4% of the enrolment each year. This figure does not include those who left for overseas studies.

    A study to determine the causes for pupils dropping out of the education system was completed in 1995 by NIE with the involvement of MOE officers. The study revealed that pupils' negative attitude towards school and problems at home were the main factors that caused them to drop out of the school system.

    In addition, feedback from schools has enabled MOE to build a profile of the premature school leaver. This has also enabled schools to identify potential premature school leavers for preventive action.

    Schools actively identify and counsel potential dropouts. They work closely with parents of pupils concerned to keep them in schools. Where necessary, cases are also referred to the various Self-Help Groups for assistance. Measures to reduce dropout rate have shown some success. Compared to 1990, the number of pupils who left school prematurely in 1997 has decreased by 521, representing a drop from 0.6% in 1990 to the current 0.4%.

    In the event that these premature school leavers wish to return to school, schools are generally ready to re-admit them, provided they are not over aged.
