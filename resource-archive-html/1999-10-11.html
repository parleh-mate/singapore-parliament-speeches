PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS

Debate on the Address
(First Allotted Day)

12.44 pm

    Mr Davinder Singh (Bishan-Toa Payoh): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move,

    That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the Opening of the Second Session of this Parliament."

    The President's Speech neatly captures the vulnerability of our economy. Just two years ago, when the Ninth Parliament opened, Singapore was prospering. We are now barely emerging from an economic crisis, which has left the region badly scarred.

    We have been able to overcome our difficulties because of the strength and integrity of our political and social institutions. More importantly, because of the rational way with which we deal with our problems. This, coupled with the unwavering support from employers and workers, has helped us score top marks with investors. Singapore has earned nothing but praise for its resilience.

    In the good times, some Singaporeans had the benefit of sharing in the prosperity while being critical of the very Government whose policies made for that prosperity. They felt that growth was in the natural order of things and that this Government was undeservedly immodest about what it had achieved for the country. But the regional crisis has forced

Column: 66

them to reassess their cynicism. It is painfully apparent that different countries suffered differently in the crisis and there is a good reason. The greatest pain was suffered by those with the weakest systems.

    Now very few people harbour doubts about the wisdom of the Government's policies. Very few people believe that there will always be good times, whether or not we have a good Government. Singaporeans are experiencing first hand the positive effects of the close relationship between Government, employers and workers.

    Perhaps, the most significant result of this crisis is that while other Asian countries are channelling their resources to restructuring their economies, we have moved on. We are already talking about developing our people, about strengthening our "heartware". That is a luxury which today other Asian countries can only dream about. They have too many political, social and economic problems on their plate.

    The President's Speech is aptly entitled "Building Our Future Together". It is about the responsibility that we have to share to shape the future of this country. The centrepiece of the Government's programme is the people. It signals a gradual shift in emphasis from infrastructure development to people development. The hardware which makes Singapore work is already in place. It is now time to make Singapore special.

    The President's Speech is an invitation to all Singaporeans to consider how we can achieve that goal. This is an opportunity for us to construct a new model. By its nature, the model must be flexible so that it can evolve in changing circumstances. It must also be realistic. There must be serious debate.

    There is no place in the debate of such an issue for cliches and platitudes. In this respect, I refer to the NCMP's proposed amendment to the motion which, in my view, is a perfect example of a cliche. He laments the absence of measures in the

Column: 67

President's Speech. But he knows that the President's Address is, by convention, a mission statement. Details are left to the Addenda and to this debate. Unless he is constructive, he will contribute very little or nothing to the debate.

    What are the Government's aims? The Government has said that it wants to promote active citizenship, independent creative thought and constructive disagreement. They are noble aims. But first, we have to settle some basic questions.

    There are, in my view, three threshold issues. First, what are the basic terms on which empowerment can take place? Second, do Singaporeans know what they want? Third, is the Government prepared for the changes which liberalisation will bring in its train?

The terms of empowerment

    The first issue turns on the meaning of active citizenship. A close reading of the President's Speech suggests that, true to form, the Government has no intention of giving handouts. Active citizenship is not about the transfer of rights and power to the people. Active citizenship encapsulates a bargain. It is premised on the simple principle that there cannot be greater rights without greater responsibility. Put simply, Singaporeans cannot expect to sit back and ask for more "space" or the right to engage in "constructive disagreement" without contributing to the system which can accommodate those rights.

    The Government has always said that contribution to the community is an integral part of active citizenship. This idea is reinforced in the President's Speech which balances the development of the individual with the abiding need to maintain social cohesion.

    What then are the ingredients of our cohesiveness? Singapore's success is due to certain basic strengths: teamwork, religious and racial harmony and a strong sense of family and community. But these

Column: 68

have come at a price. Singaporeans have had to sacrifice some of their individual rights for the larger interests of the community. For example, we do not make disparaging remarks about race or religion, language or culture. The result is stability. We do not defame without basis. The result is that reputations which are painstakingly built up over years are protected.

    The model that we seek to construct cannot ignore these basic values. There is no point in demanding a larger voice if we are not prepared to maintain the fundamentals that have made this place work. The sooner we recognise this, the better.

    But as in all bargains, the Government too has an obligation. It has a duty to spell out its vision as clearly as it can. While I accept that by its nature, the issue of empowerment is difficult to define. It is nonetheless incumbent on the Government, as part of the quid pro quo, to be as clear as it can about what it has in mind.

    That is not only fair, it is wise. Fair because people are entitled to know what they can legitimately expect if they take up the challenge of active citizenship. Wise because it is a dangerous business to hold out the promise of greater participation in public life without a clear explanation of what that involves. If people do not know what the Government has in mind, they will have all sorts of expectations. They will feel let down if their expectations are not met. That, from the Government's standpoint, will defeat the entire purpose of this exercise.

    This is very different from a programme to upgrade the infrastructure or grow the economy. Those tangibles can be measured. But an undertaking to give people a greater role in the affairs of the nation is an intangible one. People must believe that it is not an empty promise. Singaporeans must, in due course, be told in clear terms what active citizenship entails in terms of rights and responsibilities.

    Singaporeans must have a road map with clear pointers and markers. An

Column: 69

ambiguous policy can be counter productive. Perceived retreats from the pledges in the President's Speech will be magnified many times over, as will the disillusionment with the Government.

What do Singaporeans want?

    The second issue which we need to address is what do Singaporeans want. There are a number of dimensions to this question.

    First, it is not always easy to gauge public opinion. It is particularly difficult when the concept of "political space" is being discussed. There is a significant section of loyal Singaporeans which will welcome a relaxation of the norms. But there is a large and equally committed group of conservatives in this country which is very comfortable with the status quo. Their interests and aspirations cannot be ignored.

    These conservatives cross racial and religious boundaries. They come from different backgrounds and strata of society. They have grown up in and are accustomed to a system where in return for their contribution to the community they receive the benefits of a safe environment, steady jobs, good health care, good education. They may not want change.

    Too often the debate between those who do not want change and those who do is reduced to whether the East or the West has the better model. But that is unhelpful. It distracts us from the issue of what is right for us. We should design our own formula. In doing that, we must take account of the views of all Singaporeans, not only those who are vocal. It is bad governance to cater only to those who want more without regard for the anxieties and the comfort levels of those who do not.

    The second dimension to this question is this. There has been very little debate about what we want, even among those who want change. Perhaps this is due to the unfortunate turn which the argument takes sometimes. Attempts to push at the

Column: 70

limits have been met by vigorous and robust reactions. This has resulted in different signals being sent to different people. But at the end of the day, we are no wiser about what the Government and the people should aim to construct. Unless we identify what we want and what we are prepared for, we cannot sensibly advance these discussions.

    Third, we have to ask ourselves whether we are prepared and ready for an increased transfer of responsibility. Today, we rely on the Government for almost everything. For example, the Government decides what we can and cannot watch or read in Singapore. Many people find it convenient to abdicate these difficult decisions to the State.

    But empowerment is not only about having more rights. It is also about greater responsibility. Are Singaporeans prepared to take more responsibility for their lives and the lives of their children? The Internet is a good example. How many parents are prepared to tell the Government to stop censorship of the Internet because they will decide what their children can and cannot access at home, I suspect that you will find that many Singaporeans are happy with the status quo. It is neither easy nor popular to be a watchdog.

    Fourth, and perhaps the most important question is, can we take the heat? Friction will be generated by "constructive disagreement". Debate means give and take. If we want to criticise, we must accept that the Government has a corresponding right to rebut. We know this Government. It is not known for the subtlety of its replies. The moment we say we want more participation but not the embarrassment of having our arguments torn apart, then the debate about this debate ends.

Is the Government prepared for the change?

    The third issue we have to consider is whether the Government is prepared for the changes that will come with its policies. The recent debate about whether

Column: 71

we are ready to have the equivalent of a Hyde Park has sent mixed signals. That is interesting because it tells us that at the very top level, we are still grappling for some form of consensus about the desirable pace of change. It is encouraging because it reflects an active discussion and a deep interest in the issue.

    But other forces are at work which will soon test the Government's resolve to embrace change. The President's Speech reiterates the need to encourage individual creative work. That is an admirable objective. It will have the support of all Singaporeans. But it will take time. We need to change the mindset of a whole generation of parents, teachers and children. But in time, we will see a large group of highly creative people in our country, and that will be positive for our economy.

    But it will also mean a new generation of Singaporeans with expectations and aspirations very different from ours. One cannot encourage creativity and then compartmentalise it. Creativity will dramatically affect an individual's worldview. A person who is trained to think for himself and to think out of the box may well have little tolerance for rigid structures, whether political or social. He may have no sympathy for a government which tries to think for him. He is likely to reject the concept of OB markers.

    This new generation will want a say in everything that concerns or interests them. The Government must be prepared for their quirks and aspirations. Our institutions must be sufficiently tolerant and flexible to accommodate a wide range of different and sometimes, extraordinary, views. A knee-jerk rejection of these views would be disastrous. All the good work will come to nought. It will trigger the very migration of talent that our policies are designed to prevent.

    I will not be surprised if, over the next 10 to 15 years, young adults begin to insist on having their own OB markers; "space"

Column: 72

where the Government cannot intrude. Is the Government prepared for this?

    These are some of the issues which we must confront squarely and honestly if we are to have a meaningful debate. I have not touched on the other aspects of the President's Speech. I am sure Members will do so. I look forward to an interesting debate.
