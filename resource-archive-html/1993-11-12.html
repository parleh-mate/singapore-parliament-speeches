    Sir, the White Paper on Affordable Health Care is a well written document with some good and innovative concepts but is weighted towards the subject of affordability as the title suggests. The focus on health has to be addressed not only on affordability but also on equity and quality.

    Let us first look at national health expenditure and see the trends for the past 10 years so that we can predict the future direction of health care costs to the nation.

    From 1982 to 1984, national health expenditure accounted for 2.5% of the GDP each year. In 1985, it was 2.9% and then 3.4% in 1986, after which it came

down to 3% in 1989 and it was 3.1% in 1992. Thus, for the last 10 years, the figure has stayed static at around 3%. Hence, there is nothing in the pattern of our past and present history to suggest that we are going to have an explosion of health care expenditure.

    Between 1980 and 1990, the average yearly increase in health expenditure for both public and private sectors was 11.2%. However, part of this increase in health spending occurred by virtue of the larger population size. If there is an adjustment made for this population increase, then the average yearly increase comes down to 10%.

    When inflation is factored into the analysis, the average yearly increase is reduced much more. Thus, the average rise in yearly increase in health spending is also modest and will remain so as our health system is not based mainly on taxation or insurance.

    On the other hand, Singapore's economic growth, as captured by the per capita GDP or PPP, is something that we can be proud of and even boast about. The per capita GDP for Singapore is US$17,000 while for Japan it is US$18,000. Japan spends about 6% of its GDP on health and this figure has remained relatively constant in spite of social insurance and universal coverage since 1961.

    However, in the US, the figure has increased alarmingly. It was 11% in 1989 and 14% in 1992. Drastic surgery is needed to mend America's health care system because it is the biggest public policy mess of the past two decades. There is no valid comparison at all with Singapore's generally well conceived health care system and we should stop this practice of comparing two entirely different entities.

    With health expenditure of only 3% of GDP, Singapore is getting first world standards of health care at less than half the cost that is incurred in some first world countries. Thus, there is no great burden on the nation as a whole where national health expenditure is concerned.

    Now, let us look at the burden of health expenditure on the Government. In 1964, Government health spending was high and accounted for 20% of its main expenditure. It then began declining steadily to 7% in 1970 and in the 90s, the amount spent on health is only about 5-6% of the total Government expenditure. The comparable figure for education is 17% and a whopping 24% for defence.

    Thus, the Government's budget for health is a very prudent figure of 5-6% of the total expenditure. Not only has this figure come down but the Government subsidy of national health expenditure has also been coming down.

    According to the main report of the Review Committee on National Health Policies, and I quote from it:

    "As a share of Government health expenditure, the subsidy has been falling steadily from 88% in 1965 to 75% in 1985, after which the percentages of subsidies dropped to 57% in 1990."

    As a percentage of total private consumption expenditure, private health expenditure was only 5.4% of the total compared with 18.6% of the total for education. Thus, we cannot refute the fact that both public and private expenditure on health in Singapore has remained steady and constitutes a very reasonable and manageable proportion of the total national expenditure. Of course, the absolute amount spent on health has increased but so has the absolute amount spent on education, housing, defence, etc. What is important is that we do not spend an increasing percentage of the GDP each year on health so that the other sectors of national life are affected adversely and, so far, there is no evidence to suggest this.

    Thus, I feel that the Ministerial Committee's fears that health costs will escalate much beyond 3% of GDP and we will get into the predicament of the US where the country's competitiveness has become blunted because of the high health component cost are not justified on present evidence.
