ORAL ANSWER TO QUESTION
TELEVISION IN SINGAPORE
(Government Policy on Introduction)


    Mr Lee Choon Eng asked the Chief Secretary whether the Government is considering the introduction of television in Singapore next year, and if he

Column: 1199

will make a statement on the policy of the Government towards the introduction of television in Singapore.

    Mr E. B. David: Sir, the Committee which was appointed to examine this matter recommended in its report, which was tabled as Command Paper No. 20 of 1957, that television should be run in conjunction with our broadcasting. The Government agreed with this recommendation and, therefore, deferred consideration until the future of Radio Malaya had been settled through the negotiations which have been taking place with the Federation Government this year.

    Although the exact terms of the financial settlement consequent on the operation of two separate Broadcasting Departments from the beginning of next year have not yet been finally settled with the Federation Government, it is clear that there will be no reconsideration of the decision to break up the Pan-Malayan department, and the Singapore Government is therefore free to make its own plans for the future. As already announced, plans are being put in hand for a commercial service.

    Television, however, as indicated in the report of the Committee, would entail a very considerable initial outlay of Government funds, whether it is operated by the Department of Broadcasting or, as recommended in the report, by a public corporation. In the present financial stringency, the Government does not consider that the provision of these funds would be justified nor would it be proper to commit the incoming Government to such expenditure.

    The Financial Secretary (Mr T. M. Hart): Sir, I have His Excellency's authority to move the Second Reading of the Bill.

    The Bill is a very simple one. Its object is to increase the rate of tax on the chargeable incomes of companies from 30 per cent to 40 per cent, to enable companies to deduct tax at the new rate from dividends paid to shareholders, and to enable the new rate to be levied on the chargeable income of non-resident individuals, trustees (other than the trustees of incapacitated persons) and executors, and on the interest on debentures, mortgages and loans paid to non-residents. To this end, sections 43, 44 and 45 of the Income Tax Ordinance are amended in the manner indicated in the Bill.

    The decision to increase the rate of tax on the chargeable incomes of companies has not been taken lightly or without careful consideration of all the implications.

    The Government has been of the opinion for some time that the rate of tax could be increased substantially. It has been supported in this opinion by its Economic Advisers who have both held the view that the rate could be substantially increased without doing harm either to the general economy of Singapore or to the companies affected. It has been supported by an examination of the results achieved by companies operating locally over a period of years, which, by and large, have been extremely good. It has been supported by a comparison of the company rate in Malaya with the rates applied to companies operating in other countries. It has been supported by the fact that leaders of the business community have from time to time privately expressed the view that company tax could stand some increase without causing very much harm.

    But the Government has so far been unwilling to take action to raise the rate of tax because other sources of revenue

Column: 1278

still remain to be tapped; because the pressing need for more revenue which would justify the imposition could not be clearly demonstrated; because, in this more than in any other sphere of direct taxation, common action with the Federation was essential; because we wished to do everything possible to attract investment capital and because we feared that an increase in the rate of company tax, in the absence of any counterbalancing legislation such as Pioneer Industry legislation, might inhibit investment.

    Now, Sir, the matter has been precipitated by the need for more revenue to meet the cost of essential services like police, defence, education, medical, health, and communications, without which companies could not exist, far less make profits.

    The need for increased revenue has been clearly demonstrated and the point need not be elaborated. Similarly, I think it has also been clearly demonstrated that Government expenditure has been cut to the bone and that provision has only been made for what is generally agreed to be essential. I mention this because, in the past, any increase in direct, or indeed in any, taxation has met with a storm of protest against the waste and extravagance of Government expenditure, the argument being that no increased taxation would be necessary if Government would only limit its expenditure to what was essential. The argument is no longer valid - if indeed it ever were valid. All other sources of revenue within the customary pattern of taxation have been exhausted. Direct taxation under the Income Tax Ordinance is the main source remaining. Of the sources in the field of direct taxation, company tax is the most productive and likely to give the greatest yields over the longest period. The Government has therefore no option but to avail itself of it.

    At the same time, Sir, the Government regrets that it has to introduce this measure at a time when the economy is in process of throwing off the effects of a recession. It would naturally have preferred to delay introduction until the economy was back in full stride as it

    The increase is from 30 per cent to 40 per cent and it is generally accepted that 30 per cent is a very low figure. Neither the increase itself nor the new rate can be called crippling, as some critics have termed it, and I confidently believe that companies operating in Singapore will be able to absorb the increase without undue difficulty.

In the United Kingdom, the standard rate is 42 per cent plus a profits tax of 10 per cent.

    (i) reversion to the original method of calculation of duty by weight;

    (ii) the introduction of a "step" in the tariff for imported cigarettes;

    (iii) an increase in the duty on imported cigarettes above the "step", and on all brands of imported pipe tobacco, to Federation levels.

The reasons why these changes have been introduced are briefly as follows.

Column: 1294



    Firstly, the change in the method of calculation of duty on imported cigarettes and pipe tobacco which was introduced in October did not work particularly well in practice. It led to certain anomalies which were undesirable and contrary to the long-term fiscal objectives of Government. For example, it led to certain popular brands of cigarettes in tins costing more than the same cigarettes in packets with a consequential switch in consumer preference and a corresponding effect in yield of duty. Again, it led to certain brands of cigarettes costing more in Singapore than in the Federation, which was contrary to the policy of Government in the matter. Yet again, the yield from the measures introduced in October promised to be disappointingly small. In all the circumstances, after very careful thought, it was decided to abandon the scheme introduced in October and to revert to the method of calculation on weight alone which had been used for very many years both here and in the Federation.

    Secondly, the Government has been considering for some time the possibility of differentiating, in terms of duty and consequently of retail price, between the two main groups of imported cigarettes, namely, the popular brands of cigarettes like Capstan, Players, Gold Flake, Churchman, and so on, and the cheaper brands like Rough Rider, Double Ace and Torchlight. The object is to extract the maximum amount of revenue from

the tobacco tariff and at the same time to continue to provide a satisfactory type of imported cigarette for those who cannot afford the increased cost of the more popular brands. It has therefore been decided to make a "step" in the tobacco tariff between imported cigarettes weighing less than 2.32 lbs, per mille and cigarettes weighing more than 2.32 lbs, per mille. The former category will consist of the Double Ace, Rough Rider and Torchlight brands and the duty will remain at the existing level of $8.60 a pound full and $8.10 a pound preferential. The latter category will consist of all other imported cigarettes and the rate of duty will be raised to Federation levels, namely $10.50 a pound full and $10 a pound preferential. And since we do not at this juncture intend to increase the duty on cut tobacco imported for the local manufacture of cigarettes by mechanical means, there will also be yet a third category of cigarettes which will retail at prices substantially below the Rough Rider category. In short, by introducing a "step" in the imported cigarette duty tariff, we are providing three broad categories of good quality cigarettes and meeting the needs of all purses of all in-come categories.
