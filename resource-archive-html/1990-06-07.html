PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS



    The President (accompanied by two A.D.C.s) entered the Chamber, accompanied by Mr Speaker, who was preceded by the Serjeant at Arms (without the Mace), the First Assistant Clerk, the Principal Assistant Clerk and the Clerk.

    On the Clerk announcing "The President", the President delivered his Opening Speech.

     The President (Mr Wee Kim Wee): Honourable Members, at the opening of the Seventh Parliament in January 1989, I outlined my Government's programme to create a better life for all Singaporeans. Since then, a substantial part of this programme has been completed. Several policies address longer term issues: these cannot be solved overnight. But the steps we are taking will in time yield results. A few remaining items will be taken up within the next few months. This will mark the end of a major phase in our nation building.

Education

    My Government will continue to emphasise investments in education, both to develop the talent of Singaporeans to the fullest, and as the best way to raise the standard of living of Singaporeans over

Column: 8

the long term. In the last two years, we have systematically reviewed and improved the entire education system.

    Nanyang Technological Institute will become a full-fledged university - Nanyang Technological University - in 1991. We are expanding university intakes, without lowering admission standards, to train more students in skills which will be in demand. We are building a third polytechnic to complement the present two. Singaporeans can now use their CPF savings to pay for their children's local tertiary education fees.

    Our priorities in the coming years will be to strengthen primary education, restructure and further improve vocational education, and offer parents and students more flexibility and choice in our educational system.

Housing

    The Government's housing programme seeks not only to provide high standards of accommodation for the population, but also to achieve important social objectives.

    Town Councils have already taken over the management of most HDB estates from the HDB. Through them, residents will have more say on how they want to

Column: 9

run their neighbourhood communities. Increasingly, individual Town Councils will launch their own projects to improve their estates and to meet their different needs. Eventually, each neighbourhood will develop a character and identity of its own.

    We have set limits on the racial composition of individual HDB blocks and neighbourhoods, to ensure that each is racially balanced. This has checked a disquieting trend towards greater segregation between the different races.

    HDB will launch demonstration projects to upgrade some blocks of flats in the older HDB estates. If these projects prove successful and popular, we will refurbish other older HDB neighbourhoods in stages, to standards approximating the newer estates. This will improve the quality of housing Singaporeans enjoy, without uprooting them from familiar surroundings, thus keeping the communities intact. It should increase the value of HDB properties, and enable Singaporeans to own a greater share of the wealth of the country which all have helped to create.

Health Care

    In health care, we are restructuring existing Government hospitals, and building new ones, to provide better medical care to Singaporeans, and to offer everyone a wider choice. As Medisave balances build up, Singaporeans will be able to afford more sophisticated health care. The Government will always subsidise health services for those in need of treatment who cannot afford to pay for it. No Singaporean, whatever his income, will ever be deprived of the basic medical treatment he needs.

Lower Income Groups

    With economic growth and full employment, incomes have risen across the board. This has been the best way to improve living standards of poorer Singaporeans, but the Government will do more: it will help to increase their assets, and help lighten the burden of bringing up their children, particularly in meeting their school expenses.

    We must also look after the disadvantaged and handicapped among us.

Column: 10

The Government will help, preferably through community and charitable organisations. The 25th Anniversary Charity Fund shows one effective way to combine the resources of the state with the idealism and commitment of voluntary organisations.

Consultative Government and Consensus Building

    The Government has consciously sought input and feedback from the public on national issues. This process of consensus building is part of our system of participatory democracy. It has helped the Government to improve policies and generate fresh ideas, and enabled the public to understand issues and policies better.

    We have debated thoroughly matters of wide public interest, like land transport, Nominated MPs, and the maintenance of religious harmony, at public hearings of Select Committees. On more technical issues, decisions which government departments once took upon themselves are now regularly discussed publicly. For example, the Ministry of National Development and the Urban Redevelopment Authority discuss Development Guide Plans for urban planning and redevelopment at public forums with private architects and city planners. In fact, two such plans are currently being prepared by private sector professionals.

    Through the Government Parliamentary Committees, the Government has consulted Members of Parliament and the public on new policies, often before they have been finalised.

    The Government introduced the scheme of Nominated MPs (NMPs) to give independent, non-partisan views a voice in Parliament. Now that the legislation is in place, the Government intends to ask Parliament to nominate the first few NMPs. Parliament will benefit from their different experience and perspectives.

Religious Harmony

    The White Paper on the Maintenance of Religious Harmony laid out ground rules which both political and religious groups

Column: 11

should observe. They should not mix religion with politics, nor cause disharmony between different faiths. The Bill which followed sought to translate these principles into practical, enforceable provisions, with adequate safeguards to prevent the powers from being abused.

    The public discussion of these delicate issues has helped to alert Singaporeans to the potential problem, and generated a consensus on the need to do something about it. When Parliament was prorogued, the Bill was still in Select Committee, and thus lapsed. The Government will reintroduce the Bill, as it originally stood, in the new Parliament, and again refer it to a Select Committee.

Progress of the Malays

    A continuing issue is the varying rates of progress of the different races in Singapore. The Malay community has taken an important step towards uplifting itself by setting up Yayasan MENDAKI, to tackle comprehensively the social, educational and economic problems which they face.

    The Government supports MENDAKI. It will give MENDAKI whatever assistance it needs, so long as the Malay community backs its programmes, and so long as MENDAKI shows that it wants such help and can put it to good use.

    One key issue before MENDAKI is the proposal to charge Malay students from better-off families tertiary education fees. This will make available more money for projects which will benefit the children of the less successful, who can do with more assistance. More importantly, it will send a strong signal to all Singaporeans, both Malay and non-Malay, to show the way Malay Singaporeans want to go - to stand tall alongside the other communities, and not to be treated indefinitely as a community in need of support.

    MENDAKI has decided to meet the problem squarely. As a start, up to half the Malay students in tertiary institutions will pay fees. Some may be awarded partial subsidy, while those who can afford it will pay the full fees. The rest, who are less

Column: 12

well off, will continue to enjoy full subsidy. The scheme will be implemented in 1991. This decision shows clearly the aspiration of the Malays to take their rightful and respected place in Singapore society. It augurs well for the progress of the Malays, and the integration of the different races in Singapore.

Shared Values

    The purpose of a National Ideology is to crystallise a set of shared values, values which will help bind Singaporeans together, despite differences in race and religion, and in the face of many external influences, which are often not germane to their circumstances in Singapore. The Government intends to publish a White Paper on Shared Values, setting out those values which all the communities hold in common. Such a formal statement will help us gradually to evolve a Singaporean identity.

Elected President

    The Government proposed creating an Elected President with veto powers, in order to safeguard the national reserves and the integrity of key appointments. It has taken some time to deliberate over this legislation, which is a major modification to our constitution. The Bill to provide for an Elected President is almost ready. The legislation will be tabled in Parliament this year, and will be referred to a Select Committee.

The Changing World

    Outside Singapore, momentous changes in international affairs have reshaped the geopolitical landscape beyond recognition. It will be some time before their full implications become clear. Amid the uncertainties, there are many opportunities. Our foreign policy must adapt without compromising fundamental national interests.

    We will strengthen existing ASEAN ties, and take greater advantage of the economic dynamism of the Asia-Pacific. As we open a new chapter in relations with the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, we will strengthen our ties with the US, and encourage the US to maintain its presence

Column: 13

in the region. Singapore must remain nimble and agile in adjusting to this rapidly changing world.

A New Phase

    With the Bills on Maintenance of Religious Harmony and on the Elected President, and the White Paper on Shared Values, all the items in my previous address to Parliament will have been dealt with.

    The leadership transition is also almost complete. Mr Goh Chok Tong will take over as Prime Minister by the end of the year.

    This will be a watershed in our political development. We have prepared for it for a long time. The circumstances are as auspicious as we could have hoped for. The economy is robust and broad-based. We have provided adequately for our security and defence. Relations with our neighbours are good. Our sense of nationhood,

Column: 14

while still not fully mature, is stronger than ever before.

    We have reasons to be confident about our future. In the next phase, we must find new challenges which will inspire our people to continue to strive for a better life. So long as we remain conscious of our vulnerabilities and of the potential dangers, so long as we maintain the same spirit, cohesion, and will to achieve which we have demonstrated, we shall not cease to forge ahead.

    At the conclusion of the President's Address, Mr Speaker, preceded by the Serjeant at Arms, the Clerk, the Principal Assistant Clerk and the First Assistant Clerk, escorted the President (accompanied by two A.D.C.s) out of the Chamber and the House adjourned without question put, pursuant to the Standing Order.

MINISTRY OF COMMUNITY DEVELOPMENT
MR WONG KAN SENG
Minister for Community Development


    Singaporeans now look forward to a better quality of life. MCD will, with community participation, promote programmes to help the family and foster a cohesive, culturally vibrant and robust society.

Helping the Family

    We will encourage community organisations to set up Family Service Centres in housing estates, in order to help families to cope with their responsibilities and be socially independent.

    We will support voluntary welfare organisations with basic infrastructure and financial assistance, and help them to provide welfare services to the needy.

    To help the aged remain physically, socially and mentally active, we will promote community-based services, and assist families to look after the aged in their own homes. We will upgrade senior citizens' clubs into activity centres to create more social and recreational opportunities for senior citizens.

    We are encouraging child care centres to be set up in housing estates and workplaces. We will train more personnel to meet the expanding demands for child care.

Building Community Cohesion

    Community cohesion is important for social defence. MCD will create more opportunities for individuals, families, grassroots and community organisations to play active roles in building a cohesive society. We will strengthen the network of grassroots organisations, to improve rapport between the people and the government. These organisations will organise more family centred activites, and also reach out to residents of private estates and condominiums.

Column: 16



    Public education programmes and community activities will promote family and community values, inter-ethnic understanding and cross-cultural appreciation.

    The Feedback Unit has developed into an effective and credible channel for two-way communication between the people and the Government. The general public and the grassroots organisations are writing to it in larger numbers. The Unit publishes "Perspective", a regular newsletter to help Singaporeans understand major Government policies and proposals. We will continue to use the Feedback Unit as an instrument for consensus building.

Improving Quality of Life

    We will set up a National Arts Council to spearhead the development and provision of cultural programmes and facilities, raise the standards of our artistes and provide a fuller arts calendar.

    We will build an Arts Complex, and possibly develop new museum galleries, to provide better facilities for the arts. Over the next five years, we will erect a new National Library building and three more branch libraries in Yishun, Tampines and Hougang. We will build five new sports stadia, four swimming complexes and four sports halls for new housing estates as part of our continuing "Sports for All" policy.

    The People's Association will redevelop the Outward Bound School into a modern adventure centre for youths.

Malay/Muslim Matters

    The Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura (MUIS) will help in community efforts to eradicate drug addiction among Muslim youths. More Religious Counsellors will be recruited to counsel Muslim drug addicts.

    The Government will continue to work with Malay/Muslim organisations, especially Yayasan MENDAKI, to improve the educational, economic and socio-cultural status of the Malay/Muslim community.

MINISTRY OF COMMUNICATIONS & INFORMATION
DR YEO NING HONG
Minister for Communications and Information


Air Transport

    Changi Airport has earned the reputation of being the best airport in the world. When the second passenger terminal is operational in the last quarter of 1990, the first terminal will be upgraded. Together they can handle up to 24 million passengers a year.

Sea Transport

    The new container terminal at Brani will be operational in 1992. We will strive to maintain our premier position, and to raise productivity through wider application of information technology.

    We also aim to develop Singapore into a regional cargo distribution centre and cruise centre, and an international maritime centre offering a comprehensive range of maritime services at competitive prices. We will continue efforts to enlarge Singapore's ship-owning and ship-management base.

Telecommunications

    In 1990, Teleview, the world's first public photo-video text system, will begin full scale operations. This will bring information technology right into living rooms, and enable Singaporeans to improve their productivity and quality of life.

    In 1989 we became the first country in the world to have a nation-wide Integrated Services Digital Network. The service is currently linked to major cities in Japan. In the coming year, it will be extended to cities in the US, Australia and the UK where such services are available.

    We will also upgrade the national and international telephone networks with a fully digitised national telephone network by 1995. To further enhance the quality of mail services, we will build a new mail and parcel sorting centre by 1995.

Column: 18



Land Transport

    We have implemented the quota system for vehicle registration. We are studying a scheme of weekend cars, to enable more Singaporeans to own cars without causing more congestion on the roads, especially during peak hours. Preparations for introducing the Electronic Road Pricing (ERP) system are going ahead.

    Construction of the MRT is nearly complete. The last station, at Boon Lay, will be open in July 1990. We are actively studying extending the MRT system to serve Woodlands and the North East sector.

    We will continue to improve public transport, by integrating bus and MRT services and introducing through-ticketing. Public transport operators will be encouraged to improve their quality of service.

Information

    We will continue to manage and promote domestic and overseas publicity of Singapore and its national policies, and develop among Singaporeans a commitment to the nation by fostering a sense of identification with Singapore. Programmes, campaigns and publications will inform the Singapore public, instil pride and confidence in Singapore, and promote national values and ideals shared by all Singaporeans.

    We will extend our external information programme to countries outside the region, to strengthen our friendly relations with them and to promote investor confidence in Singapore.

Broadcasting

    The 1990s will see the Singapore Broadcasting Corporation (SBC) investing more in human resources and technology, to improve the quality and variety of its programmes to better serve our people. SBC intends to meet rising audience expectations through audience feedback, recruitment and development of creative talent, and the use of new technology.

    With the installation of a second satellite reception dish, SBC will offer viewers more up-to-date and varied programmes.

Column: 19

It will also introduce digitally based stereo sound on television. In addition, SBC has also just extended transmission hours of telecast on Saturday mornings.

MINISTRY OF DEFENCE
MR GOH CHOK TONG
First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence


    Singapore's national security depends upon a peaceful and prosperous Southeast Asia. A durable balance of power is necessary to assure regional stability. We remain committed to the Five-Power Defence Arrangements, and will continue to foster good relations with our ASEAN neighbours. At the same time, we will maintain the SAF as an effective fighting force, to deter armed threats, and to secure peace and security for Singaporeans.

Renewing the SAF

    The SAF will have to upgrade and modernise, to meet the demands of the battlefield of the future. Where it is cost-effective, we will upgrade older weapons and equipment. Where this is not worth doing, we will replace old items by new equipment.

Optimising Manpower Resources

    The SAF is already feeling the effects of low birth rates. The SAF structure will have to evolve. Units will have to become leaner. Over the long term, we will have to make maximum use of reservists. More reserve units will be formed. SAF divisions will have to incorporate both active and reserve formations.

Motivation of SAF Personnel

    To gain maximum value from the weapons and equipment in the SAF, the people who maintain and operate them must be well motivated and effectively led. MINDEF will develop programmes to strengthen the motivation of all personnel, both active and reservist, and to enhance the command and leadership ability of officers and NCOs.

Column: 20

MINISTRY OF EDUCATION
DR TONY TAN KENG YAM
Minister for Education


    Over the past few years, we have taken several initiatives to improve the quality of secondary and post-secondary education. In the next few years, the Ministry will accord a higher priority to strengthening primary education, restructuring and further improving vocational education, and giving parents and students more flexibility and choice in our education system.

Secondary Education

    Secondary school pupils will enjoy a more conducive learning environment as we phase in single-session schools. The new civics and moral education programme will be introduced in the upper-secondary levels in 1992. It will help pupils to imbibe a sense of civic and social responsibility and sound moral values. The academic, personal and social development of students will also be enhanced through more effective and innovative curricular and co-curricular programmes. All these will better develop pupils into caring and thinking adults and prepare them for a fast-changing technological era.

    As the independent schools consolidate, they will initiate new programmes, paving the way for more innovations in all schools.

Post-Secondary and Vocational Education

    To meet the demand for trained manpower in the 1990s, we will build a third polytechnic, complementing the two existing polytechnics. It will have a full enrolment of 9,000 students, and will provide more training opportunities for 'O' level school leavers. Its permanent campus will be ready in 1993.

    The Vocational and Industrial Training Board (VITB) will continue to improve the quality of its training programmes. VITB will also assist and support voluntary welfare organisations in vocational training for the disabled and in running their training centres.

Column: 21



Tertiary Education

    We will be upgrading the Nanyang Technological Institute into a comprehensive university in 1991, giving us two strong competing universities, the Nanyang Technological University (NTU) and the National University of Singapore (NUS). Together, these two institutions will offer a wide variety of courses, including courses in the performing and visual arts.

    NUS and NTU will also step up their post-graduate studies and research. In the next four years, together they will establish five institutes. NUS will have an Institute of Micro-electronics, a Clinical Research Centre at the Faculty of Medicine and a Centre for Advanced Legal Studies. NTU will have the National Institute of Education and the Institute of Manufacturing Technology.

Primary Education

    To give our children a good start in education in the primary school years, we will improve the professional training of primary school teachers. In 1991, the Institute of Education will merge with the College of Physical Education to form the National Institute of Education (NIE), which will become an institute of the NTU. We will upgrade the present Certificate in Education programme to a Diploma in Education programme, and remodel the existing Diploma in Education course for graduates to a postgraduate diploma programme preparing teachers for both primary and secondary schools. We will also introduce a new four-year undergraduate programme to train primary school teachers.

    To provide a more conducive learning environment, we will build better-designed primary schools, improve facilities in existing schools and work towards the introduction of single-session primary schools.

    Language acquisition is central to learning. We will review the language curriculum with a view to providing pupils with a strong foundation for learning English and the mother tongue. Young Singaporeans must learn their mother tongues if they are to acquire a strong cultural foundation,

Column: 22

and retain their Asian identities and values. We have started with the preparatory year programme in ten primary SAP schools. With the experience gained from this programme, the Ministry will consider how the benefits can be extended to the other primary schools.

Column: 22

MINISTRY OF THE ENVIRONMENT
DR AHMAD MATTAR
Minister for the Environment


    The Ministry will continue to strive to make Singapore a model environmental city, with a high standard of environmental cleanliness and with citizens who are mindful of their social responsibilities.
