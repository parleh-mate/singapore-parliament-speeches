Rail Clips Dislodged from MRT Tracks

1 Mr Alex Yam asked the Minister for Transport in light of the recent incidents of rail clips dislodged from MRT tracks (a) how often are the rail tracks inspected; (b) whether the integrity of the tracks has been compromised; and (c) as some early rail tracks were built in 1987, whether the Ministry has a rail track replacement or refurbishment plan to ensure that all tracks are in the best operational condition.

Page: 2258

The Minister of State for Transport (Mrs Josephine Teo) (for the Minister for Transport) : Mr Speaker, Sir, I thank the Member for his question. The operator inspects sections of the track each night to check if the tracks and its components are in good condition. This includes the rail clips that secure the running rails to the timber sleepers. Any clip that is observed to have broken off is replaced immediately upon detection.

The clips do break from time to time. However, as the clips are connected at intervals along the entire running track, a single broken clip ought not to affect the safety of rail operations significantly, as the other clips continue to secure the track. Following the dislodgements, SMRT carried out a check of the entire system and assessed that the safety of the system had not been compromised and that normal train operations could continue. To ensure that safety of passers-by, temporary safety nets have also been put up between Dover and Jurong East Stations to minimise the recurrence of the rail clips dropping from the viaduct.

Nonetheless, we have taken precautions as a result of the dislodgements. LTA has asked the operator to send the broken rail components from the dislodgements to a laboratory to analyse how the damage may have occurred as an additional check. The operator will also be required to give LTA a full report on the incidents in due course.

LTA works closely with the operator on those aspects of the rail infrastructure which require Government funding for replacement. This includes major track replacement or refurbishment plans. On its part, the operator is responsible for checking and maintaining the rail system regularly, including the replacement of individual parts or components that may show signs of wear and tear.

Based on input from SMRT, both LTA and SMRT had already assessed that certain stretches of the MRT system along the North-South Line would need to have the timber sleepers replaced. The implementation details are currently being worked out. Let me assure the House that LTA will continue to work with the operators to ensure that the safety of the system is not compromised.

Mr Alex Yam (Chua Chu Kang) : I thank the Minister of State for her comprehensive reply. With regard to the dislodgement, other than clips, there has been some feedback as well from areas where the tracks are curved that some of the lining of the undercarriage of tracks have also occasionally dislodged. Are these also under the purview of the SMRT or the operators, or are these under LTA?

Mrs Josephine Teo : I thank the Member for his supplementary question. The SMRT conducts daily checks along the tracks and the LTA independently also conducts checks of the viaducts and the tunnels every two months. If the LTA in this independent check discovered that there are other dislodgements or defects, they would inform the SMRT, the operator, to replace it immediately. That is the working arrangement between the two.

Recent Fraudulent Withdrawals from DBS Accounts

2 Mrs Lina Chiam asked the Prime Minister in light of the recent fraudulent withdrawals from the ATMs of DBS Bank (a) what have MAS and the Association of Banks in Singapore done to prevent such incidents from recurring at DBS Bank and other banks; and (b) whether MAS is assured that DBS Bank has definitively identified the problem behind the ATM security lapses.

The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Finance (Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam) (for the Prime Minister) : Mr Speaker, Sir, the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) expects all financial institutions in Singapore to have in place risk management processes and security controls to safeguard their systems and operating capability. Banks are required to take prompt action to rectify any vulnerabilities or weaknesses in their systems.

MAS has been working with the financial institutions since 2010 on a comprehensive programme to strengthen the security of payment cards. The programme includes measures to reduce the risks associated with the use of magnetic stripe ATM cards. Following the recent incident, the Association of Banks in Singapore (ABS) announced, in January 2012, that the industry will adopt the EMV standard in ATM cards to address the risk of card skimming. EMV stands for Europay, MasterCard and Visa. It is a global standard for payment cards based on chip card technology. The industry has said that it intends to adopt this standard to address the risk of card skimming by 2014. Its implementation will require infrastructural changes to the ATM networks, and will take two years for completion. In the interim, banks are putting in place additional layers of security to enhance the protection of their customers' bank accounts. These include disabling overseas withdrawals unless requested by the customer, SMS alerts for cash withdrawals above a certain threshold and replacing ATM cards that are assessed to be at risk. Banks are also strengthening their internal surveillance. MAS has urged banks to implement the new measures as soon as they can, even ahead of the industry's target timeline, where possible.

DBS has assured MAS that it is treating the issue of fraudulent withdrawals with utmost priority. MAS has required that the bank reviews all control processes to minimise the risk of ATM fraud, and will assess the adequacy of the measures being taken. MAS will continue to work with the industry to enhance security measures to safeguard the integrity of consumers' bank accounts.

Mrs Lina Chiam (Non-Constituency Member) : Sir, I would like to thank the Minister. I would just like to ask one more question. Will the Government make it compulsory for all banks to put in this magnetic computer chip? Will it be a compulsory exercise?

Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam : Thank you. So there is a distinction between the magnetic stripe technology which has been the prevailing method and the EMV chip-based cards which they are moving towards. They intend to move by 2014. This roadmap to increase the security of the system has been worked out, together with MAS, over the last few years. We expect all banks coordinated by the Association of Banks (ABS) to comply with this new standard by 2014 and preferably earlier, if possible.

Crime Committed in Singapore by Foreigners

3 Ms Tin Pei Ling asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs (a) what will be the estimated date of completion of the police inquiry into the case involving three foreigners assaulting Singaporeans outside Suntec City Convention Centre in April 2010; (b) what is the standard operating procedure when a foreigner has committed or is suspected to have committed a crime; (c) how are such foreigners assessed for flight risk; (d) what measures are there to prevent such foreigners from fleeing jurisdiction before the trial; and (e) what criteria are applied in the assessment of whether extradition proceedings against such foreigners who have fled jurisdiction will be commenced.

The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Teo Chee Hean) : Mr Speaker, Sir, the Police are conducting an internal inquiry to establish the full facts of how the Suntec case was handled. If there were any lapses or negligence, Police will take appropriate disciplinary action against the officers involved. We expect the internal inquiry to be completed next month, that is, April 2012.

Warrants of arrest have been issued against Robert Stephen Dahlberg and Robert James Springall, the two accused persons who absconded while on bail. Police will work closely with INTERPOL and its overseas counterparts to locate the two men and bring them back to face the charges that have been laid against them.

On the broader question of how foreigners who have been suspected of or accused of violating the law are dealt with, under our laws, all accused persons are treated in the same way, regardless of whether they are Singaporeans or foreigners. As set out in the Criminal Procedure Code (CPC), there is a standard set of procedures for the handling of accused persons, whatever their nationality, from arrest, to investigation, to charging them in court, and to police bail.

During investigations, the CPC gives Police the powers to ask an accused person to surrender his passport as a condition of bail or personal bond. For accused persons who are assessed to be a flight risk, Police will set the bail or bond, commonly referred to as "police bail", at a higher amount. This is during an investigation. Flight risk is assessed holistically, taking into account multiple factors such as whether the accused person has roots in Singapore and the extent of these roots, the gravity of the offence, the behaviour of the accused person in the course of investigations and whether the person had attempted to abscond previously.

Once a person is charged in Court, discretion on whether to release the accused person on bail, as well as the conditions of bail, is with the Courts. This is commonly known as "court bail". In situations where the accused person is a likely flight risk, Police would flag this out and request the Courts to set bail at a higher amount. Police may also request that the passport of the accused person be impounded as a condition of bail.

The Courts would consider each case on its own merits, weighing the arguments by the defence and the prosecution and assessing the risk factors in determining the flight risk of the accused person. The Courts will make the decision on bail quantum and conditions after considering representations by all parties.

If a wanted person who has fled abroad has been located, whether extradition proceedings will be initiated against him depends on several factors, including whether we have an extradition treaty or extradition arrangement with the country that the person has fled to, and whether the offence committed by the person is an extraditable offence. Police will take all steps to obtain the return of those who have absconded, to the fullest extent legally possible.

Ultimately, the discretion rests with the Court to decide whether an accused person should be allowed to leave our jurisdiction taking into account submissions by both the prosecution and the defence. Casino Self-exclusion Programme and Profile of Singapore Gamblers

4 Er Dr Lee Bee Wah asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) how effective has the casino self-exclusion programme been in keeping gambling addicts away from the casinos; (b) how can the Ministry encourage more foreign workers and their employers to apply for self-exclusion for their workers; and (c) how can the Ministry improve on the current system to prevent those who are under the casino self-exclusion or third-party exclusion programmes from slipping into the casinos using false identification documents.

5 Er Dr Lee Bee Wah asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what is the profile of a typical Singaporean gambler who goes to Marina Bay Sands versus those who gamble at Resorts World Sentosa; (b) what can casinos do to limit play time and spending amounts; and (c) how can the Ministry ensure that casinos enforce the rules on all local visitors.

The Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (Mr Chan Chun Sing) : Mr Speaker, may I have your permission to take Question Nos 4 and 5 together?

Mr Speaker : Yes, please.

Mr Chan Chun Sing : Sir, the casino self-exclusion programme was launched in November 2009. As of 31 January 2012, there were 42,700 self-exclusions in force. About 35,200 were foreigners, while 7,500 were citizens and PRs. Self-exclusion, for both locals and foreigners, is voluntary under the Casino Control Act. We recognise that foreign workers, particularly Work Permit Holders, may need assistance with the self-exclusion process. The National Council on Problem Gambling (NCPG) has worked with the Ministry of Manpower (MOM) to allow employers to print self-exclusion application forms via the Work Permit Online System and for the employers to help workers submit the signed form to NCPG. The Migrant Workers' Centre also assists foreign workers who come to their drop-in centre to submit self-exclusion applications.

Page: 2262

NCPG has so far directly reached out to 30,000 foreign workers through talks and roadshows at companies and workers' dormitories, and in partnership with the Migrant Workers' Centre. It also carries out efforts to raise awareness, such as regular reminders to employers through the Work Permit Online system.

For those with a gambling problem, casino exclusion will help them in their recovery. Unfortunately, some patrons may not recognise or acknowledge that they are addicted to gambling. For this reason, we have also provided family exclusion orders to allow families to exclude them. So far, about 900 such orders from families have been issued.

Under the Casino Control Act (CCA), the onus is on the casino operators to keep excluded persons and minors out of the casino premises, and their failure to do so would constitute a breach liable for disciplinary action. The Casino Regulatory Authority (CRA) has and will continue to take the operators to task when they fail to comply. Last month, action was taken against both operators for failing to prevent excluded persons and minors from entering and/or remaining in the casino premises. The Government also takes a serious view on impersonation as well as the use of false IDs for the purposes of entry into the casinos. It is an offence under the Penal Code which carries a fine, an imprisonment of up to five years, or both.

Information on casino patrons, including the profiles of Singaporean patrons is owned by the casino operators. We are studying targeted measures to protect the financially vulnerable and introducing "circuit breakers" for local frequent casino gamblers. We will look into extending third party exclusion to more groups under ComCare. These would be over and above the current 24,600 bankrupts and 3,200 Public Assistance and Special Grant beneficiaries.

As for "circuit breakers", we are looking at relevant practices of other casinos and jurisdictions. For example, Holland Casino has a responsible gambling policy that curbs frequent visits by patrons following host interviews while Casino Austria excludes or sets visits limits for patrons suspected to have gambling problems or addiction. Crown Casino runs a help centre for those who may be problem gamblers and also has a Play Safe Limits that sets limits on the time played and betting amounts.

My Ministry, NCPG and CRA will work closely with the casino operators. As Minister Iswaran has mentioned previously, MHA will be, holistically, reviewing the Casino Control Act in the second half of this year. MCYS will work closely with MHA to ensure that any targeted measures we introduce can and will be implemented effectively by the casinos. My ministry has started discussions with both casinos in this regard. Both casinos have indicated their support for targeted measures against problem and financially vulnerable gamblers.

Profile of Singaporean Students Studying in Private Schools

1 Mr Zainal Sapari asked the Minister for Education (a) what is the number of Singapore citizens aged 7 to 17 years old who are studying in private schools (excluding those in special schools); (b) what is the breakdown of these numbers in terms of the students at primary and secondary levels; and (c) what is the number of Singapore citizens who are exempted from the Compulsory Education Act and what are the reasons for these exemptions.

Mr Heng Swee Keat : As at February 2012, the number of Singaporeans who are exempted from Compulsory Education is 5,229. This is around 2% of all primary-level Singaporean children. Visitor Numbers and Tax Revenue Generated by Casinos

2 Mr Gan Thiam Poh asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) for 2010 and 2011 respectively, what is the total tax revenue generated by the two casinos; (b) what is the total number of visitors who have visited the two casinos so far; and (c) of these, how many are Singaporeans and how many are repeat visits by Singaporeans.

Mr Chan Chun Sing : Based on MOF estimates, the overall net increase in tax revenue from the Integrated Resort (IR) casinos was about $900 million for FY10. For the first 10 months of FY11 (between April 2011 and January 2012), the figure was also about $900 million. Both amounts have taken into account the loss of tax revenues from the resultant decline of other gaming activities such as lotteries, horse and sports betting and fruit machines operated by clubs.

Data on the total number of visitors to the two casinos reside with the IR operators. The two operators recently released data which showed that 136,434 and 137,259 unique local patrons visited Resorts World Sentosa (RWS) and Marina Bay Sands (MBS) casinos respectively in 2011. Taking into account an estimated 30% overlap in local visitors between both casinos, the number of locals who have visited the IR casinos in 2011 is thus around 200,000. The respective operators have previously released information on the proportion of local casino visitorship, and these were reported to be in the range of 20%-30% of total casino visitorship.

Most locals, even if they patronise the casinos, do not appear to visit frequently. There is however a small group of frequent gamblers which we are concerned about. MCYS is looking into introducing "circuit-breakers" and expanding third-party casino exclusion for a small group of problem gamblers and financially vulnerable local gamblers who visit the casinos frequently. MCYS will also be studying responsible gambling practices in other casino jurisdictions, such as Holland, Austria and Australia, and will consult the IR operators and public on these measures.
