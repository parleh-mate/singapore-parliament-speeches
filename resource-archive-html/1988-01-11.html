TOXIC ASH FROM GARBAGE DUMPS


    4. Dr Aline K. Wong asked the Minister for the Environment what steps his Ministry is taking to ensure that the ash from incinerated garbage which is dumped in landfills does not contain toxic substances.

     The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for the Environment (Mr Eugene Yap Giau Cheng) (for the Minister for the Environment): Sir, I am answering on behalf of my Minister.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, incinerator ash contains toxic substances especially heavy metals such as cadmium, chromium and nickel. These heavy metals are present in trace quantities in domestic as well as industrial refuse, and are left behind in the ash after incineration. Although we do not allow certain types of industrial waste, such as sludge and chemical wastes to be incinerated as they are known to contain a high proportion of heavy metals, it is not possible to segregate domestic waste as even waste such as wood and paper contain traces of heavy metals. Hence, like other developed countries that employ incineration, our ash also contains heavy metals.

    These heavy metals are toxic if ingested through the food we eat or the water we

    In Singapore, the ash from our incineration plants is dumped in controlled landfills located outside our water catchments. There is therefore no possibility that our water supplies could be contaminated with incinerator ash. Tests of our water quality confirm this to be true.

Bill to establish GRCs

    This Bill seeks to prevent such an unhealthy situation from arising. It is the culmination of five years of discussion within the PAP leadership, including the Malay MPs. It seeks to ensure that Parliament will always be multi-racial by requiring some constituencies to be contested on a group basis. These will be larger constituencies, each having about three times as many voters as single-member constituencies. They will be represented by three MPs each. Details of the Bill

    Now, I shall deal with the details of the Bill.

    Clause 3 enables the President to declare a certain number of constituencies where Parliamentary elections will be held on a group basis. Such constituencies will be known as Group Representation Constituencies (GRCs). Each group will have three candidates and this clause allows the President to stipulate that one of the three candidates in every group shall be a person belonging to the Malay community, or a person belonging to any other minority community.

    This clause also limits the number of MPs to be returned by all GRCs to half the total number of MPs to be returned at a general election. In the next election, if the total number of MPs remains at 79, the number of GRCs will not exceed 13, or 39 MPs. There will be at least 40 seats where opposition parties which cannot field a team of multi-racial candidates can still contest. Such opposition parties are not shut out by this Bill.

    Clause 3 stipulates that no by-election will be held to fill any vacancy unless all the three members of that GRC have vacated their seats in Parliament. This is to ensure that no Member who has been elected under a GRC will be forced to vacate his seat through the departure of his team-mates, for whatever reason.

under one common logo. This requirement is to ensure that each group of three candidates contesting in a GRC share generally the same political values and can work as a team to serve the constituents. It is to discourage marriages of convenience for the purpose of contesting in a GRC.

    Clause 6 also requires any person who desires to contest as a candidate from the Malay or other minority community to apply to the appropriate committee for a certificate stating that he belongs to that particular community.
