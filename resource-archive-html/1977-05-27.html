ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

"SANATRONIC CLINICS"



    1. Mr Ivan Baptist asked the Minister for Health whether the treatment using electrostatic charges available in Singapore to sufferers of ailments like rheumatism, migraine, insomnia, mental fatigue, asthma, indigestion and skin trouble is efficacious and safe.

     The Minister for Health (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, ion generators are used to purify or sterilize the atmosphere. Microscopic particles such as bacteria, fungal spores, dust and smoke particles can acquire an electrostatic charge and remain suspended in the air. Particles which are

Column: 8

positively charged are neutralized by negative ions and negatively charged particles by positive ions. When fine particles are neutralized they aggregate or collect together to form larger particles that are too heavy to remain suspended in the atmosphere and are deposited or settle down on the ground by gravity.

    The claim that positive or negative ions are effective in treating rheumatism, migraine, insomnia, mental fatigue, asthma, indigestion, skin diseases, constipation as well as diarrhoea and impotence is not substantiated in scientific or medical literature. If it were true that a wonderful instrument exists that can cure or treat such a variety of physical ailments, then hospitals all over the world would have installed such ion generators today.

    The danger of untruthful advertising is that gullible patients who should really be receiving treatment from their medical doctors are led astray and, by avoiding medical treatment, they are exposing themselves to unnecessary risks and complications arising from their ill health.

    However, I ought to add that the Ministry of Health is not involved in the licensing of clinics operating ion generators. I understand that there are six such establishments in Singapore. What is surprising is that they were allowed to open for business without the knowledge of Building Control Division and the PUB. Since we are concerned about the sale of unsafe electrical domestic appliances, I ought to inform the House that preliminary inquiries reveal that persons who patronize these so-called sanatronic clinics sit in an electrical field of several thousand volts in order to attract the negative ions that are generated to their bodies. They accumulate an electrostatic charge so that when they touch a person or object outside the treatment area which is earthed they experience a slight electric shock accompanied by a spark discharge. In layman's language the patient is acting like a lightning conductor except that the current passing through him is too small to electrocute him.

Column: 9



    On the other hand a real danger exists if by any mischance the insulation system between patient and earth breaks down. Under these circumstances the patient will be sitting in an electric chair.

    It is in the public interest that the operators of these sanatronic clinics should submit the technical specifications of their installation to the PUB for scrutiny.

    As far as the Ministry of Health is concerned, the whole business is nothing more than a fraud being perpetrated on a section of the public that is always gullible to fantastic claims.



     Mr Ivan Baptist (Potong Pasir): Mr Speaker, Sir, what steps is the Minister for Health going to take, liaising with the relevant Government departments, to ensure that this fraudulent practice is stopped immediately for the safety of our community?



     Dr Toh Chin Chye: Mr Speaker, Sir, I have mentioned that the licensing of these sanatronic clinics should have gone through the Building Control Division, and I am told that the Building Control Division will look into the matter. The Public Utilities Board likewise has asked for specifications of the installations in these clinics and I am sure that the problem will be solved quite early in the next week or so.

POLICY ON RETIREMENT AGE



    3. Mr Phey Yew Kok asked the Minister for Labour, in view of the policy of the Government to reduce the need for Singapore to engage guest workers and in view of the effectiveness of the Government's medical health service in lengthening the working life-span of Singaporeans of both sexes, whether he will consider introducing legislation to raise the age of retirement to 60 years for all Singaporeans capable of working for several years beyond the age of 55.



     The Minister of State for Labour (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Labour): Mr Speaker, Sir, the present retirement ages in the Civil Service, with the exception of Police personnel, are 55 years compulsory and 50 years optional for those who joined the Service on or before 1st July, 1956, and 60 years compulsory and 55 years optional for those who joined the Service after 1st July, 1956. Notwithstanding that, officers in the former category have to retire at 55 years. Selected ones may opt to extend their retirement age to 60 years or to accept re- employment for a period of five years after their retirement.

    For those working in the private sector, their retirement age is not stipulated under any law.

    Although there is a provision in the Central Provident Fund enabling those who have reached the age of 55 years to withdraw their CPF contributions, it does not, however, preclude them from continuing to work after the age of 55 years. In practice, as reflected in most collective agreements, the retirement age in the private sector is 55 years and in some instances 60 years. However, in those firms where the retirement age in the

Column: 11

collective agreement is fixed at 55 years, there are also provisions to allow employees to work until 60 years, subject to mutual agreement and medical fitness.

    I agree with the Member for Boon Teck that raising the retirement age to 60 years would be a natural development, as people nowadays generally tend to live longer because of better living conditions, improved diet and medical services. It will also be a measure to increase the national supply of manpower. By prolonging the working life- span of individuals, it will enable them to increase their CPF contributions, thus enabling them to retire in more comfortable circumstances. However, I think a non- legislative approach will serve the needs of our industries better than legislating the retirement age at 60 years. Legislation will be counter-productive in that it might force the employer to retain dead-wood employees. On the other hand, the voluntary acceptance of the retirement age at 60 years by employers will allow the worker to retire before the age of 60 years if his productivity has decreased for whatever reasons and will also enable the worker who is still physically and mentally fit and capable of high productivity to carry on even after 60 years. In time, market and social forces will condition the employers to accept the age of 60 years as the retirement age.

Column: 12

electricity and water are only applicable to premises which are used exclusively as private dwellings. (Effect on farmers' livelihood)


    6. Mr Ng Kah Ting: To ask the Minister for National Development and Communications whether the local farm produce will be affected under the bilateral agreement to set up the Thai-Singapore Enterprise whereby Singapore will buy farm products and other commodities from Thailand in exchange for Singapore products, and, if so, whether and what steps will be taken to ensure that our farmers' livelihood will not be affected.

     Mr Speaker: Mr Ng Kah Ting.

     Mr Yeo Toon Chia (Ang Mo Kio): Question No. 6, Sir.

     Dr Tan Eng Liang (for the Minister for National Development and Communications): Mr Speaker, Sir, as Singapore has limited land available for agriculture, we are dependent on foreign imports for a wide range of agricultural produce. Column: 14

vegetables and over 90% of fruits consumed. Raw ingredients used for the manufacture of pig and poultry feed are also imported.

     Dr Tan Eng Liang (for the Minister for National Development and Communications): Mr Speaker, Sir, there were 2,926 farms within the Kranji Catchment area keeping a total of 217,000 pigs. 640 of these farms rear more than 100 pigs each, giving a total number of 178,045 pigs or 82% of the total pig population in Kranji. Farms with more than 100 pigs each were given the option to be resettled in Punggol while the smaller farms were required to phase out pig-rearing and to convert into other non-pollutive forms of farming.

    Since July, 1975, 114 of the larger farms have been resettled in Punggol. A recent count showed that the pig population in Kranji has been reduced by almost 50% to 111,000. Another 15 farms will be resettled in Punggol during the next few months while the remaining farms have been given six months' grace period from April 1977 to dispose of all their pigs in their farms and cease pig-rearing altogether.

WRITTEN ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

PUNGGOL ESTATE (FLATTED FACTORIES)


    1. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for National Development and Communications to ascertain from the Housing and Development Board whether flatted factories will be built in the vicinity of Punggol Estate, and if so, when plans will be implemented and what are the types of industries proposed.

     Mr Lim Kim San: Currently, there are no plans for industrial development in the vicinity of the Punggol Estate within the Punggol Constituency. However, in the nearby Teck Hock Estate, the development of an industrial estate is being planned. The types of industries which will be accommodated in this industrial estate include sauce factory, workshops for repairing tractors and heavy machinery and manufacture of wooden boxes.

TAMPINES WAY (OVERHEAD CROSSING)



    2. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for National Development and Communications whether and when an overhead pedestrian crossing will be constructed across Tampines Way which is a dual carriage-way between Blocks 1 and 19.

     Mr Lim Kim San: Tampines Way is a newly completed 3-lane dual carriageway linking Tampines Road and Upper Serangoon Road. Investigations have shown that traffic volume along this road is very light and the construction of an overhead pedestrian bridge is at present not justified. However, my Ministry will consider puffing up a zebra crossing at an appropriate site.

WOODSVILLE (TRAFFIC CONGESTION)



    3. Mr Ivan Baptist asked the Minister for National Development and Communications what action is being planned to ease traffic congestion at Woodsville where six major roads meet, viz. Upper Serangoon Road, Jalan Kolam Ayer, Serangoon Road, MacPherson Road, Jalan Toa Payoh and Bendemeer Road, causing unnecessary hardship and danger for the people of Potong Pasir, especially those living in Sennett Estate, Jalan Potong Pasir, Lorong Ahadiat and Meyappa Chettiar Road.

     Mr Lim Kim San: There are plans to upgrade Paya Lebar Way, Jalan Kolam Ayer and Jalan Toa Payoh to expressway standards to form part of the Pan-Island Expressway. For the major road intersection at Woodsville, a flyover is planned to link Jalan Kolam Ayer with Jalan Toa Payoh for non-stop traffic across the intersection. Improvements to the ground system are also included in the plans for Woodsville.



    The proposals will commence as soon as approval for fund is obtained from the Ministry of Finance. When completed, the proposals will relieve traffic congestion and reduce hazard to pedestrians at Woodsville.

TAMPINES WAY (BUSES)



    4. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for National Development and Communications when Singapore Bus Service buses will make use of Tampines Way for the convenience of the residents of Punggol Estate.



     Mr Lim Kim San: SBS will introduce a new double decker bus Service No. 86 to operate between Punggol Estate and Shenton Way, some time in June 1977. Seven double decker buses will operate this Service which will terminate at Tampines Way.
