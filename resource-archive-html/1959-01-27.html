    Sir, when the Bill was considered in Select Committee, representations were heard from the Chambers of Commerce, the Manufacturers' Association, and certain individuals connected with manufacturing in Singapore. The main

Column: 1911

objections were jointly from the Chambers of Commerce, and the grounds for the main opposition were as follows. First, they objected to the licensing of manufacture of almost every type, because they considered that that was too rigorous a requirement and created a state of uncertainty. Secondly, they considered that the 15-year life of the licence was too short and that there was no certainty that the licences would be renewed by a different government. They also considered that the power vested in the Minister was too excessive, and that there were no checks on the Minister in the form of any machinery for appeals. They considered that there was no safeguard for those who were in the course of setting up industrial projects. And finally some of them were opposed to the granting of monopolies to any one manufacturer. These points, Sir, were considered by the Government, and amendments were moved by me in the Select Committee to remedy these main grounds of opposition.

    The amended Bill, Sir, meets this opposition by the following methods: licensing has been abolished and registration has been substituted. The 15-year period has now been removed so that registration is for an indefinite period. Manufacture of every type is now not required to be registered; instead only the manufacture of those items specified in the Schedules is required to be registered. A Registrar has been provided for to administer the requirements of the Ordinance, and an appeal to the Minister over the Registrar has been provided for. Additions and variations to the Schedules are to be made by the Minister, but an appeal to the Assembly is provided for in the form of a provision whereby the Assembly can, by a motion, overrule the Minister. Projects already set up and are manufacturing cannot be refused registration. Projects in the course of being set up are safeguarded by a provision that registration for such projects cannot be refused except for those in the Second Schedule. The granting of monopolies by the Registrar will still be possible, but will now have to be gazetted

Column: 1912

so that the public are informed and they could make representations to the Minister, and therefore this granting of monopolies is subject to appeal to the Minister.

    These are the salient features of the amended Bill as it has emerged from the Select Committee. I have nothing further to add, Sir, and I beg to move.
