WITHDRAWAL OF BRITISH BASES IN SINGAPORE
(Request for Statement)


    1. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Prime Minister if he will make a statement on the outcome of his talks with the British Premier and his Ministers in London regarding the proposed withdrawal of the British Bases in Singapore; and what steps will be taken to ensure that the economy and defence of Singapore will not suffer as a result.

    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Mr Speaker, the British Government has stated its position in a Supplementary Statement on Defence Policy tabled in their Parliament on the 18th July this year.

    In the Far East, they have decided to reach a reduction of about half the forces deployed in Singapore and Malaysia during 1970-71. The reductions will be so phased that, by the early 1970s, the British forces still stationed in Singapore and Malaysia will consist largely of naval forces (including an amphibious element) and air forces.

    In paragraph 8 of the Supplementary Statement, they stated:

    `We cannot plan the period beyond 1970-71 in the same detail. The reductions over the next few years will be considerable: we are determined that they will take place in an orderly manner, which will enable our Commonwealth partners to adjust their plans, and will allow Singapore and Malaysia to make the necessary economic transition as smoothly as possible. We plan to withdraw altogether from our bases in Singapore and Malaysia in the middle l970s; the precise timing of our eventual withdrawal will depend on progress made in achieving a new basis for stability in Southeast Asia and in resolving other problems in the Far East.'

    Elaboration of the British Government's position can be found in the speech of the British Minister of Defence, who opened the debate in Parliament on the 27th July and also in the speech of the British Prime Minister when he closed the debate that

    In paragraph 9 of the Statement, the British Government states:

    `We are, therefore, planning to maintain a military capability for use, if required, in the area, even when we no longer have forces permanently based there.'

And in paragraph 10, they stated:

    `We do not need to settle the precise character, size and deployment of this capability now. It would be largely drawn, however, from the forces of all three Services which would be stationed in this country as a strategic reserve; and we should probably keep in the Far East some naval and amphibious forces.'

PROMOTION AND PROTECTION OF LOCAL INDUSTRIES


    6. Mr Bernard Rodrigues asked the Minister for Finance, with a view to promoting and protecting local industries whose production capacity can meet the demands of local consumption, if he will consider prohibiting the importation of products similar to those of such local industries.

    Mr Ramaswamy (for the Minister for Finance): The policy of the Government with regard to protection of local industries is quite clear. The Government is prepared to grant protection to industries if several factors are satisfied. Firstly, the industry must be in a position to supply a major part of the local market. Secondly, the industry should result in a net gain to the economy by way of foreign exchange savings, local income generated and local employment created. In addition, the industry should as far as possible be one with some export potential. The industry must show evidence of being

Column: 182

willing and able to compete in the export market. Thus it can be seen that the productive capacity of an industry alone is not sufficient to conclude that it should be protected. There are other no less important factors to be taken into consideration. In considering the actual measures to be adopted, we have learned through experience that prohibition of imports is not the best means.

    Firstly, such prohibition does not set an automatic limit to the extent to which local prices can increase.

    Secondly, prohibition of imports would mean that the local industry would be divorced from the competitive pressures of new designs and improved quality which are constantly being developed by industries abroad.

    Thirdly, prohibition creates a situation which opens the administration to business pressures and enticements. It also results in a very high premium for the imported products, which encourages smuggling.

    For these reasons the Government would prefer to grant protection in the form of import duties. There are, of course, cases where quantitative restrictions on imports are necessary in the initial stages of an industry's existence. In such cases, the Government is prepared to consider such measures.

    Summing up, the Government is prepared to consider measures to protect local industries from imports, but does not consider prohibition of imports as the best means of doing so.

This is provided for under clause 2.

Expenditure on urgent development projects for which there is no provision in the Development Estimates will be charged to the Contingencies (Development) Fund. This fund will have to be replenished each year with resources in the Development Fund.
