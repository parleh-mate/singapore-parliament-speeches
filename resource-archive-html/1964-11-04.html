MEDICAL REGISTRATION
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.32 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Health (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Health): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Amendment Bill to the Medical Registration Ordinance (Chapter 191 of the Revised Edition) seeks to amend the Ordinance so as to vary the composition of the Medical Council in Singapore.

    At present, the Ordinance prescribes that under section 4 the Medical Council shall consist of 16 members and under subsection 1 (e),

    'Three registered medical practitioners to be nominated by the Council of the University of Malaya and appointed by the Yang di-Pertuan Negara.'

    This Ordinance came into effect in 1963 but as from January 1962, the University of Malaya, Singapore Division, became known as the University of Singapore and the University of Malaya, Kuala Lumpur Division, became known as the University of Malaya.

    As the University of Malaya in Kuala Lumpur has started a Medical Faculty with its first intake of medical students in 1963, it is considered desirable that provision be made for representation of both Universities on the Medical Council. The Federal Minister of Health has been consulted and is in agreement that reciprocal amendments be made in the relevant Ordinances of both territories so that the following representation will be prescribed for both Medical Councils:-

    4 registered medical practitioners to be nominated in all, of which:-

    2 registered medical practitioners are to be appointed by the Yang di-Pertuan Negara on the nomination of the Council of the University of Singapore and another 2 registered medical practitioners to be appointed by the Yang di-Pertuan

Column: 179

Negara on the nomination of the Council of the University of Malaya.

    With the proposed amendment under clause 2 of the Bill, the Medical Council will have an increased membership of 17 instead of 16, hitherto.

    Question put, and agreed to.

    Bill accordingly read a Second time and committed to a Committee of the whole Assembly.

    Assembly immediately resolved itself into a Committee on the Bill.

    Bill considered in Committee; reported without amendment; read a Third time and passed.

HOUSING AND DEVELOPMENT
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.37 p.m.

    The Minister for National Development (Mr Lim Kim San): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Bill is to fulfil Government's promise to provide opportunities for the middle and lower income group to own homes. The Housing and Development Board's five-year programme calls for the completion of 51,000 housing units by mid 1965. I am sure Members of this House will be glad to hear that we will have the 51,000 units of public housing by that date. The progress of our housing programme, according to schedule, has enabled the Government to give accommodation to the majority of applicants who urgently need houses. We have reached the stage where we can offer accommodation to any citizen of Singapore who is eligible to a house if he is not too choosey. However, there are quite a number of people with low income who want to, and should be encouraged to, own their homes too. Unfortunately the flats and houses built by private developers are priced beyond their means. We have therefore now started a scheme whereby two- and three-room flats are sold on instalment at the subsidized prices of $4,900 and

Column: 180

$6,200 respectively. At these prices our citizens can choose between buying a car or a house.

    The plan for the sale of low-cost flats was announced in February this year, and the Housing and Development Board, as agent of the Government, prepared a pilot scheme for the sale of 2,068 two- and three-room flats. The response so far has been most encouraging. Up to date about 800 flats have been balloted for. Enough publicity has been given to the scheme and I do not propose to go into details. In putting up this scheme it is our intention to see that no abuse arises out of it. Therefore, certain conditions and regulations have to be made pertaining to the sale of these flats. There are enough provisions in this Bill to prevent these flats which are sold on instalments from falling into the hands of speculators and profiteers. The flats are to be sold only to bona fide purchasers who wish to buy a house for their own occupation. There are also provisions in this Bill to ensure that the flats can be managed properly and economically after they have been sold. Under this Bill, a person is not entitled to purchase more than one flat, and if he does so, he contravenes the Ordinance, and on conviction will be liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months, or to a fine not exceeding $2,000, or to both fine and imprisonment. Any flat purchased in contravention of the Ordinance will not be registrable. But, on the other hand, provision is made in the Bill to exempt all bona fide transactions from the legal requirements of the Registration of Deeds Ordinance and the Land Titles Ordinance, as regards the registration of sale, mortgage, and re-mortgage.

    There is a restriction on transfers which could only be effected with the consent of the Housing and Development Board, and where the Board refuses such consent, it is bound to buy it up at a price to be determined by the Board. This price is, however, subject to appeal to the Minister, should the owner be dissatisfied with the price offered.

    Powers are given to the Board to require the owners of flats to form a

Column: 181

management corporation for the purposes of general maintenance of the common property, and where a direction by the Board to this end is not complied with, the Board may authorise any company to undertake the control, management and administration of the property. The Board may, however, exercise these functions where there is no management corporation or where such corporation has been wound up on the order of the Minister. The corporation would subsist by levying con tributions from the owners of the flats in the block.

    Powers are given in the Bill to enable the Housing and Development Board to determine the lease under various circumstances, such as non-payment of instalments, securing purchase of a flat on false pretext or the owner has violated the rules for the sale of flats. In order to regulate the sale of flats, the Board will be empowered to make rules for purposes such as methods of sale, qualifications of persons eligible, monthly instalments, period of payments, and so on.

    The opportunity is also taken in this Bill to make other amendments to the Housing and Development Ordinance which are considered necessary in the light of experience of the working of the Board since it was formed in February 1960. The repeal and re-enactment of section 12 will enlarge the scope of the Board's functions and enable it to build accommodation for sale and lease as well as for renting, and will also enable it to undertake urban development and to provide loans to enable persons to purchase houses. The Board will also be empowered to promote and float incorporated companies to carry out any of the functions laid down under the Ordinance.

    By an amendment to section 24, the procedure for the internal administration of the Board will be simplified by vesting it with powers to make standing orders in regard to the methods of making contracts, the delegation of powers to officers of the Board, the keeping of accounts, conduct and discipline of
