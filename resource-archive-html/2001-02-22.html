CENTRAL PROVIDENT FUND
(Profile of ineligible members for top-up)


    1. Mr S. Iswaran asked the Minister for Manpower what is the profile of Central Provident Fund (CPF) members who were ineligible for the recent top-up in CPF accounts from the Government and whether the grace period for eligibility can be extended, particularly for the elderly.

    The Minister for Manpower (Dr Lee Boon Yang): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Central Provident Fund Top-Up Scheme was announced by Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong during his National Day Rally Speech on 20th August 2000. The top-up was given in appreciation of Singaporeans who have accepted the CPF cut to help Singapore overcome the challenges of the economic crisis. Singaporeans aged 21 years and above who have contributed at least a total of $100 to any of their CPF accounts between 1st January 1998 and 31st December 2000 are eligible for the top-up. Those who did not already meet this $100 minimum contribution requirement at the time of announcement had

about 4 months to make voluntary contribution to qualify for the top-up. Of the 2.13 million Singaporeans eligible, 1.94 million (or 91%) qualified for the top-up.

    Of the 200,000 (9%) Singaporeans who did not qualify for the top-up, 69% are aged below 55, 8% are between the ages of 55 and 65, and 23% are aged 65 and above. 33% of them stay in 1- to 3-room HDB flats, in homes or are on public assistance, 27% in 4-room HDB flats and 40% in 5-room HDB flats or private housing. 55% are females. The CPF Board will conduct a sample survey to find out why these citizens did not participate in the scheme.

    The CPF Top-up Scheme has already taken into account the CPF contributions made over a generous 3-year period and 4 months was given for those who needed to make voluntary contributions to qualify to do so. There was thus sufficient time for Singaporeans to qualify for the CPF top-up. Nevertheless, the Government is prepared to consider allowing those who missed out on the first payment, a second chance to qualify for the full payment of the CPF top-up, nearer the date of the second payment.

POST-SECONDARY FOREIGN STUDENTS
(Part-time work)


    2. Dr Ker Sin Tze asked the Minister for Manpower whether he will allow post-secondary foreign students to do part-time work in order to relieve the labour shortage in certain trades.

    Dr Lee Boon Yang: Mr Speaker, Sir, since 1st July 1994, my Ministry had allowed foreign students above 14 years of age and who are full-time matriculated or registered students of approved institutions of higher learning, post-secondary, and secondary schools to work during their vacation without having to apply for a work permit.

    Since January 1997, we have also allowed full time foreign students in our tertiary institutions to work part time without the need to apply for a work permit. The maximum hours of work should be no more than 16 hours a week and these students should first seek the

Column: 1419

permission of their tertiary institution before working. This is to ensure that their studies will not be adversely affected by their part-time work. As the number of foreign students in tertiary institutions who intend to take up part-time work is relatively small, the exemption from a work permit is not so much to help ease our tight labour market but to provide foreign students an avenue to finance part of their studies in Singapore.

    With effect from 1st February 2001, foreign students from the following tertiary institutions: La-Salle-SIA College of the Arts, the Nanyang Academy of Fine Arts, the INSEAD, Singapore, and the University of Chicago Graduate School of Business in Singapore, have also been exempted from applying for a work permit if they intend to work part-time.

WRITTEN ANSWER TO QUESTION

GOVERNMENT-LINKED COMPANIES



    1. Dr Jennifer Lee asked the Prime Minister (a) how is the search for members of statutory boards and boards of Government-linked Companies (GLCs) conducted; (b) what are the criteria and guidelines for the selection of these board members; and (c) what are the numbers of men and of women on these boards.

    Mr Goh Chok Tong:

    A statutory board or GLC, in consultation with their respective parent Ministry or holding company, first identifies their requirements and the broad selection criteria for Board members. Based upon this, potential candidates from both the private and public sectors are then identified and approached. For public officers, inputs of the Public Service Division (PSD) are sought.

    The criteria used for searching for members of statutory boards and GLCs are determined by the vision of the organisation, the nature of business, and the leadership role demanded from the Board. Potential nominees are selected based upon their relevant knowledge and skills, and their ability to contribute. In addition, we take into account other factors, for example, ensuring that there is no conflict of interest.

    The Ministry concerned, or the GLC or its holding company, as the case may be, also seeks to achieve a fair representation of candidates from the public and private sectors, as well as ensure that the interests of major shareholder are adequately represented.

    As of 15th February 2001, there are a total of 2,175 directorship positions on GLCs (including statutory board companies), of which 176 (8%) positions are held by females while 1999 (92%) positions are held by males. As for statutory boards, there are 666 board positions, of which 54 (8%) are held by females while 612 (92%) are held by males.
