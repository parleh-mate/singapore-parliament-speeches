    Sir, I propose to speak both on the Ministry of Law and National Development. As far as the Ministry of Law is concerned, I shall start first with the laws of Singapore and give the House a short resume of events as they might help in setting out the present position.

    Prior to 1963, Singapore had its own Constitution, and laws made up of Ordinances passed by the Legislature, such parts of the English Common Law and certain English Statutes that were made applicable to Singapore before we joined the Federation of Malaysia in 1963. Whilst we were part of the Federation, on account of the distribution of legislative subjects between the Central and the State Legislatures, certain existing laws that came within the purview of the Central Government were repealed and at the same time laws were extended in the name of "uniformity" to Singapore even in regard to matters where the ipsissima verba of the legislation was in no way different, as evidenced in the Trustees Ordinance, or where the substance was identical, as in the Internal Security Act.

    There was a spate of subsidiary legislation made by the Central Government and extended to Singapore, some of which were again substituted for those which were already in existence. The Attorney-General's Chambers (or the State Advocate-General's Chambers as it then was) had been hard put to

keeping abreast with the changes of these laws, especially in certain instances where copies of the Federal Ordinances that had been extended to Singapore in place of existing Singapore legislation had not been made available even to departments of state that were concerned with the administration of these Ordinances. Such was the state of the laws that when banishment proceedings were taken under the Federation Banishment Ordinance which, except for one or two procedural requirements, were exactly the same as the Singapore Ordinance, the first of these cases actually resulted in the banishee succeeding on a writ of habeas corpus. The police, not having copies of the Federation Ordinance, had proceeded under the repealed provisions of the Singapore Ordinance and, in doing so, had failed to comply with certain peremptory procedural requirements that were in the Federation Ordinance but were not in the State legislation.

    Then, as if to add to the confusion, on the 9th August, 1965, came the separation of Singapore from the rest of Malaysia. Although the Separation Agreement had provided for the continuation of laws then existing until such time when they were changed by Parliament in Singapore, this only added to the difficulties which both the legal profession and those concerned with the administration of justice in Singapore were already faced with. The work of unscrambling laws that had been extended to Singapore by the Central Government then began.

    We had then in 1965, as we do have now, such portions of the Federal laws that had been extended to Singapore existing side by side with Singapore statutes that had been left undisturbed. In some cases, the extension of these laws by the Central Government in relation to Singapore had reversed the Singapore Government's policies, as, for example, the Public Authorities Protection Ordinance of the Federation of Malaya. Singapore had, before it joined Malaysia, removed from its statute books a similar Ordinance giving

In December 1965, the Republic of Singapore Independence Act was passed with retroactive operation to the 9th August of that year. Singapore then became a Republic with effect from the 9th day of August, 1965. When things got more difficult and more desperate, some of the leaders tried to assuage the growing resentment of their people by claiming that though colonialism had been got rid of, the source of their troubles has now been traced to a new menace - neo-colonialism. And until very recently, even though the colonialists had retired to temperate climates and the countries concerned had been recognised as independent and free nations and given seats in the United Nations, the anti-colonial struggle was, nevertheless, persisted in for many years after that. Or some of them tried to shore up their crumbling economic edifice by making regular pilgrimages to European and American capitals in search of loans, grants or anything that might be going free.

    It never occurred to them - though this thought is now fortunately dawning on the new breed of leaders emerging

in Asia and Africa and who I think will be the future leaders of Asia and Africa - that the cause for their failures and disappointments might lie nearer home in the illusions and fallacies they bred during the anti-colonial phase of their struggle.

    One of the unpleasant facts we in Singapore must face is that economic growth and industrial development cannot be achieved on the cheap. Industrial development requires at all times a sustained effort, a willingness to mobilise savings, a readiness to take risks, a propensity for innovation, a passionate vision of the future, a willingness to make the painful adjustments required by modernisation and, most important of all, a readiness on the part of the people as a whole to postpone immediate rewards for greater gains in the future. This is the premise on which we can build genuine economic and industrial development. If we do not accept this proposition as true, then I think we are wasting time; we are fooling the people when we talk of economic growth and industrial development. We must accept the fundamental belief that if we want economic growth and industrial development, we must be prepared to pay the difficult and heavy price that it demands.

    If we are not prepared to pay this price, if we are not prepared to accept this as a basis of our future policy, then there will be little or no economic development. The present leaders, especially we in Parliament, can, if we like, fool the people, as some leaders in newly-independent countries have done, into believing that we can bring them prosperity without tears and effort. And if we did, then in five or ten years, we would go the way of many independent countries - into bankruptcy and despair. Only in Singapore, we have not got the escape hatch that many countries in South-east Asia have. We have not got vast scrub-lands and lush jungles to which the dispossessed and impoverished can flee to scratch some sort of living, either by hunting or growing bananas

Column: 334

and tapioca. Economic collapse or stagnation for a densely populated, nonagricultural and highly commercialised island like Singapore means mass starvation. The Prime Minister has said repeatedly that in one square mile we sustain something like 7,000 people. If our commerce and industry come to a halt, the only way 7,000 people can survive is by eating one another! We cannot grow anything, we cannot plant vegetables, we cannot start agriculture. There are about 7,000 people in one square mile. So for us the price of collapse is total disaster. Our commercial and industrial economy is such that, like a cyclist, we have to keep moving in order not to fall off. We must always be on the move.

    I think the time has now come for us - employers, workers and the people in general - to develop a new outlook. The old slogans, the old cries and the old attitudes of 20 years ago have become irrelevant to the times as they are today and we are going to face totally different problems in the future. Therefore, we have to develop a new outlook, a new philosophy to meet the challenge of the new times. For want of a better name, I will call it the philosophy of development as contrasted with the current outlook which can be described as the philosophy of consumption - the outlook of the ant which builds or the locust which consumes. It was perhaps inevitable that under colonialism we should have developed to a high degree a consumption mentality without realising that in a healthy economy the capacity to consume should be related to the capacity to produce, because when the colonials were in charge of Singapore they never talked about development, for their own interest and exploitation. Therefore, for us the concept of development has never occurred. The only thing we could think of to make life worth while was to ask -"What can we consume? How much can we consume?" And that became the purpose of life. So under colonial rule the efforts of workers and trade unionists were directed towards one objective - to redistribute wealth (for that is a way of consuming) by way of

higher wages and better conditions of work.

    The objective of employers was to extract the maximum of profits in the shortest possible time and use these profits to consume more and make more profits. This was the basis of our economic activity. Consume - whether as workers, whether as employers, whether as professional men, whether as civil servants. We earned more to buy more, built bigger houses, bought bigger cars. We observed standards of life which were set by the expatriates who came here on the basis that they were in exile, that they should make as much money as they normally would in half the time if they were in Europe, and go back. So once the expatriates retired or were Malayanised, the new elite stepped in, not only into the office but also into the standard of life and style of life that the departing expatriates had set for them - the same pay, the same size of house, the same size of limousine - not knowing that that kind of life was not related to our own economy. It was not related to what we could produce what we could maintain. It was set by expatriates who felt that they had come to this country as a punishment and, therefore, they needed extra for their effort. But we nevertheless continue to get this compensatory pay that was reserved for exiled expatriates.

    But now that we are independent and are on our own, this kind of approach, the consumption psychology, is a sure and certain road to disaster. At least in the crucial years ahead the major objective to which all other issues should be subordinated is this: it is not redistribution of wealth but the creation of wealth that should be our major and relentless concern. This is a concept which has never occurred to us and which has never entered either into the minds of workers or employers. Anything that hampers and retards the creation of wealth must be checked, however unpopular and however painful this may be. But the hard facts of economic life leave us with no other choice. You cannot evade these hard

facts. The cost of evading them, as many countries have discovered, is certain disaster. This is obvious, if you keep on distributing and redistributing the same amount of wealth among more and more people - because in Singapore the population is increasing at a rapid rate - what you get is not a more prosperous people but more and more poor people.

    The problem of redistribution becomes real and meaningful only after you have created sufficient wealth, additional wealth. Then redistribution makes for a genuine increase in living standards. Raising wages and maintaining welfare standards unrelated to the level of economic development results in spurious prosperity. When wages go up in a poor country, as it has often been done, prices go up and the worker is precisely where he was before. It is no use trying to ward this off by price controls. Experience has shown that attempts at price controls under these circumstances in developing countries have merely paved the way for more corruption and rampant speculation.

    The only practical way of raising living standards and bringing about genuine prosperity is by creating more and more wealth. This means producing more and consuming less than we produce. We must save enough to invest in more factories, more enterprises to produce more wealth.

    All the affluent and advanced countries of today, whether Communist or non-Communist, had to go through a long period of heart-breaking toil, discipline and sacrifice - and very often unimaginable horrors associated with the industrial revolution in Europe - before they made the grade. Sometimes we forget that every country which is advanced, every country which is prosperous today, paid a heavy price. It was accompanied by exploitation, injustice, child labour, long hours of work. I would advise hon. Members to read the history of modernisation and industrialisation in Europe, in the Soviet Union, in America and in Japan.

They would then begin to realise what a heavy price, an almost intolerable price, people have had to pay for modernisation and advance.

    These countries which are now judged as affluent, as advanced, were lucky in that they embarked on modernisation and industrialisation when there were no trade unions and no Parliament. Modernisation in most of these countries was pushed through by dictators, autocratic monarchs or elites who were not dependent on popular vote. So they could extract the price for modernisation, sometimes an inhuman price for modernisation, knowing full well that there were no trade unions or Parliament to call them to account. In the Soviet Union, if you were to read its history in the early days of industrialisation, two million were deliberately left to starve to death. That was the price that even a workers' country demanded, that workers must pay for modernisation. In China more recently, there was a case where three and a half million people were deliberately allowed to starve to death because there was no choice. That was the only way that China could make the advance that she had made so quickly, especially in the fields of industrialisation and modernisation of agriculture.

    But we in Singapore have to proceed with our modernisation and industrialisation at a time when we have well-developed trade unions and a democratically elected Parliament. We must, therefore, undertake the difficult task of bringing about industrialisation and modernisation with popular support. This is our problem. For a small island we have to force the pace not under conditions where we have not got to bother about trade unions or Parliament, but where we have to bother about both. And maybe this is a good thing because then we may be able to avoid some of the unnecessary injustices, hardships and brutalities that accompanied industrialisation and modernisation in some of the now

The need for economic growth or development must become an insatiable hunger -if you like, a religion. If you read the history of industrialisation in Europe, for example, in Britain, especially during the Puritan and Victorian era, work became a religion. To save was to glorify God. That was the Protestant ethic. To live frugally, to practise thrift, to work hard - that was the belief that inspired the industrialisation, the entrepreneurs, the capitalists in Europe and most of Western Europe. If we want to develop a meaningful philosophy of development, then we must bring to this task the same measure of courage, dedication and discipline that we brought to the anti-colonial struggle.

    Whatever the other fears and doubts that the trade union leaders might have in regard to the proposed Employment Bill, I would urge them to consider seriously what it is that they want in terms of priority. Do they want economic development and economic growth, whatever the cost? If you read the Bill, however, it does not put up the cost so high as to make it intolerable. But do you say, "Whatever the cost, we want economic growth."? In a way, Sir, trade unionism and parliamentary democracy for us have been a distortion of history. As I have said earlier, in Europe industrialisation came first and out of the struggle parliamentary democracy and trade unionism grew naturally. So, as the economy developed, trade unions also developed and became more mature and more powerful. But here the history is just the reverse. We get Parliament and trade unions first. And then we start on economic development and industrialisation. There has been a reversal, a distortion of history, not

And therefore they must come to the problem of industrial and economic development with a sense of understanding and responsibility of what it is that is happening.

    As I have said, since we have to do this in a democratic way with the consent and support of trade unions and employers, we must develop this almost religious fervour for development. They do it in the Communist countries. They make work a religion. Stakonovite - a people's worker. They glorify work. If you are in China, or in Russia in the early days, any worker who said, "Why should I work 52 hours? Why not 50 hours?", would be considered a heretic. If you want to make the grade, if you show that only by working 52 hours you can catch up with the West, the Germans, the imperialists, then you work 52 hours. So you get a Stakonovite coming and proving that a man can work 55 hours, and he becomes a hero.

    It is only when leaders develop this philosophy and fervour that they can transmit some of their fervour to the people and make them work-minded and development-minded. Only then, when the leaders show this, will the people willingly put up with the difficulties and hardships necessary to complete the task of modernisation and economic development. But if the leaders are weak, if they have no faith in the necessity of economic development in the imperative need for economic development, if they are cowardly, if they choose the easy way out, then Singapore goes the way of many other new nations. But if they are courageous and determined, then I think that ten to twenty years from now, those who come after us will say, "Thank God, in the hour of need Singapore did not lack real leaders.
