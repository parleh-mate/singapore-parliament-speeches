SINGAPORE-JAPAN PETROCHEMICAL PROJECT
(Statement)


    1. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Prime Minister if he will make a statement on his recent discussion with the Japanese Prime Minister on the proposed $2 billion petrochemical project in the Republic.

     The Prime Minister: Mr Speaker, Sir, when I met Mr Fukuda, the Prime Minister of Japan, on Monday, 30th May, this year, all issues regarding the Singapore-Japan Petrochemical project in Singapore had already been resolved by officials of both Governments. Mr Fukuda confirmed that the project had been agreed to by, and will receive the support of, the Japanese Government. This endorsement and support was repeated by all the Japanese Ministers concerned, i.e., the Ministers for International Trade and Industry, Foreign Affairs and Finance.

    The following are the main points of the Agreement. The joint venture "upstream" company of the project will be owned on an equal basis by the Singapore Government and Japanese participants.

    Japanese participation in the "upstream" company will be through an investment company being set up in Japan The Overseas Economic Co-operation Fund (OECF), which is a Japanese Government institution for overseas development assistance, will invest three billion yen (about

Column: 52

$27 million), equivalent to 30% of the equity of the investment company. The remaining 70% equity will be taken up by private Japanese companies, including Sumitomo Chemical, Mitsubishi Petrochemical, Mitsui Petrochemical, Asahi Chemical, Showa Denko, Idemitsu and other Japanese petrochemical companies. In addition, long-term loans for the project would be provided by the Export-Import Bank of Japan both for equity participation by Japanese companies as well as for the purchase of machinery and equipment from Japan.

    The Singapore project will be the first petrochemical complex to receive OECF equity participation. The project has the support of both the Japanese Government and the Japanese petrochemical industry. It goes without saying that continuing cooperation between Singapore and Japan is a key factor in the success of the project.

    The petrochemical complex when completed in 1981 will consist of an "upstream" company and initially three "downstream" companies. The "upstream" company will own the naphtha/gas oil cracker, which is the core of the complex and other common-user facilities. The "downstream" companies will purchase ethylene and propylene from the "upstream" company for conversion into plastic resins and other end products.

    This project will make a significant contribution to our economy, both during construction and during operation. Much of the engineering, plant fabrication and construction work will be done locally. The project will require about 4,000 direct

PULAU UBIN AND PULAU TEKONG
(Medical facilities)


    2. Mr Teo Chong Tee asked the Minister for Health what measures will be taken to provide better general medical facilities for the people in Pulau Ubin and Pulau Tekong as such facilities are lacking in the two islands.

     The Minister for Health and Acting Minister for Social Affairs (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, at present, a trained Nurse Practitioner visits Pulau Tekong daily, except Tuesdays when she goes to Pulau Ubin, to provide outpatient services. She attends to about 20 patients daily at Pulau Tekong and 10 at Pulau Ubin.

    As the bulk of the cases at the two islands involves minor ailments and in view of their small population, the present medical facilities provided are considered adequate.

I would therefore like to advise the public to go to the outpatient dispensaries if they are suffering from minor ailments. In this way, they will not overload the staff at the Accident and Emergency Department and will be helping the Government to hold down the rising costs of running hospitals.

its tenants, whether it is a large limited company or a small limited company incorporated with limited liability under the Companies Act. (Complaints)


    10. Mr Tan Soo Khoon asked the Minister for National Development and Communications whether he is aware of the numerous complaints by telephone subscribers of wrong charging over the Subscriber Trunk Dialling (STD) system and if he will request the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore (i) to review the STD system and rectify any faults: and (ii) to allow subscribers the choice of whether or not they wish to be provided with the STD facility.

Column: 59



     The Senior Minister of State for Communications (Mr Ong Teng Cheong) (for the Minister for National Development and Communications): Mr Speaker, Sir, a total of 190,000 subscribers use the Subscriber Trunk Dialling (STD) system, 40,000 of whom are regular users.

    Each month the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore has to go through 275,000 exchange lines to bill users of the STD. Of these, there are on the average about 20 cases of wrong charging mainly due to human error. The error rate is therefore only 0.007%. In all these cases, the subscribers' bills are subsequently corrected accordingly. The TAS is monitoring this matter closely to reduce the number of faults and improve the quality of service.

    The present automatic STD system is convenient, efficient and cheaper for subscribers who want to make calls to Peninsular Malaysia. Without this automatic STD system, the manually operated service will not be in a position to handle the volume of telephone traffic to Peninsular Malaysia. The manually operated service is generally inefficient, uneconomical and requires more manpower.

    To eliminate wrong charging in the STD system, the TAS is investigating the use of sophisticated computer switching equipment which can print out all the details of STD calls, such as the destination of call, the called party number, date of call and duration of call. However, the deterrent factor of this equipment is its high cost.

    The TAS is also looking into the practicality of providing trunk-barring facilities to subscribers who do not want STD facilities. The TAS has been cautious in considering the offer of such an option because it results in the inefficient use of the automatic STD equipment and may overload the manual trunk service. If this happens, the manual service will deteriorate. To sustain an acceptable grade of service, the cost of providing the manual service will increase; and this additional cost would have to be passed on to the public, a situation which we would want to avert.

OIL PRICE INCREASE
(Effect on economy)


    13. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for Finance to what extent our economy will be affected by the proposal of Saudi Arabia to increase its oil price by five per cent on 1st July, 1977.

     Mr Barker: (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have been asked to reply on behalf of the Minister for Finance. There has been no official announcement by Saudi Arabia to increase its oil price by 5% on 1st July, 1977. There have been, of course, speculative press reports. Let us hope that these reports will not be borne out by events. Any further increase in oil prices will adversely affect the economic development of many non-oil producing countries like Singapore.

    In itself, a 5% increase is not substantial. But since more than 60% of our crude oil imports comes from Saudi Arabia, this will represent a further aggravation of the already high oil burden for Singapore. Posted prices for crude oil have gone up by 330% from US$3.01 per barrel in October 1973 to US$13.00 per barrel at present.

    The direct effect of an oil price increase would be greatest on the domestic oil bill. There would be an increase in the cost of such oil, with a substantial proportion of it having to be paid by our industries. Besides this domestic impact, the effect of a further oil price increase on the economies of the other countries, especially the advanced industrial countries, has to be considered. The economies of such countries are recovering, albeit slowly. Any adverse effects from an oil price increase on their economies would affect Singapore's exports to these countries, thereby affecting our economic growth.

    Given that we are fully dependent on external sources for oil like many other non-oil producing countries, it is our hope that the reports on Saudi Arabia's proposal will not materialise. If the effect of any price increase is to be at all ameliorated, there must be a conscious effort by Singaporeans to conserve

Column: 62

MOSQUE BUILDING FUND SCHEME
(Statement by the Minister for Social Affairs)


     Encik Othman bin Wok: Mr Speaker, Sir, the House may recall that in September, 1975, the Administration of Muslim Law Act was amended for the implementation of the Mosque Building Fund Scheme. The objective of the Scheme is to ensure a steady source of funds for mosque building purposes. Under this Scheme, all employers are required to pay a 50-cent contribution per Muslim employee per month to the Fund and to recover the contributions from the wages of their Muslim employees. Since the Scheme started in May, 1975, contributions to the Fund have averaged 108,000 persons monthly and the average monthly collection is $68,000.

    The first mosque built under the Scheme at Toa Payoh was officially opened on 8th April 1977 and the second mosque at Queenstown will be completed in September 1977. The third mosque at Jurong is scheduled for completion in early 1978 and sites have been reserved for mosques at Woodlands, Bedok/Chai Chee and Ang Mo Kio New Towns. A total of six mosques will be built under the first phase.

Over the course of the next few years, mosques costing approximately $1.5 million each will be built in HDB new towns in Tampines, Nee Soon, Bukit Batok and Clementi. This will be the second phase of the mosque building programme. It is obvious that more funds will be required to ensure the early completion of these mosques.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, it has been decided to increase the Mosque Building Fund contribution from 50 cents to $1 per month with effect from 1st July 1977. The increase

Column: 63

is necessary to ensure the availability of funds for the mosque building programme.

    The decision to increase the rate of contribution is made after consultations with Muslim Members of Parliament and the Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura. The Majlis reported receiving requests from Muslims to increase the rate of contribution to speed up the mosque building programme to meet the needs of Muslims who are resettled in the new towns.

NATIONAL MARITIME BOARD
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.
3.01 p.m.

     The Senior Minister of State for Communications (Mr Ong Teng Cheong): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Presently, ships working cargo on Sundays have to pay Sunday Labour Permit Fee which ranges from $60 to $200 according to the tonnage of the ship. The money collected is paid into the National Maritime Fund. The Fund is administered by

Column: 65

the National Maritime Board whose functions are mainly to develop and regulate maritime employment and to administer welfare and training schemes for seafarers.

    The Sunday Labour Permit Fee originated in the late 19th century and was meant to aid destitute seamen. It is an anachronism for a port like ours which now operates 24 hours a day and seven days a week. The present system of collection also has various shortcomings. At present, the National Maritime Board collects the Sunday Labour Permit Fee based on information furnished by the Port of Singapore Authority and the Marine Police. But the Board has no facilities to check whether ships are working cargo on Sundays, resulting in cases of evasion.

    Revenue from the Sunday Labour Permit Fee amounts to about $1/2 million every year and constitutes an important part of the Board's income, of which a substantial portion is channelled towards the welfare and training of seamen. On this basis, the original criterion of collection has not been lost. To retain this income but without the administrative shortcomings, the Board proposes to replace the Fee with a new levy to be known as the Maritime Welfare Fee. The new Fee will be levied on all vessels of 75 gross registered tons (grt) and above calling at Singapore on a per call basis. The proposed rates of the new Fee range from $15 to $100 per call per vessel according to the size of the vessel. Concessionary rates, of course, will be provided for. The Maritime Welfare Fee will be collected by the Port of Singapore Authority which has agreed to be the collecting agent on behalf of the National Maritime Board.

    The new Fee will have only a marginal effect on shipowners' operating costs. A study of selected ships has indicated that the imposition of the Maritime Welfare Fee on a per call basis will amount to only about 1% of a ship's daily operating costs. As most ships stay several days on calling in port, the effect would therefore be negligible. In fact, if we were to consider instances of specific
