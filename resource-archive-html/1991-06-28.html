RENTAL SCHEME FOR HDB FLATS
(Review)


    2. Dr S. Vasoo asked the Minister for National Development whether there are plans to review the rental scheme for Housing and Development Board flats.

     Mr Dhanabalan: Mr Speaker, Sir, my Ministry and the HDB are finalising a review of HDB rental housing policies. The key objective remains to encourage and assist as many HDB flat tenants as possible to own their flats. At present, 80% of the population live in owner-occupied flats. Only 8% of the population, or 52,000 families, reside in HDB rental flats. With some further assistance, more tenants will be able to own their own flats. HDB is currentlyworking out various measures to facilitate this.

Column: 96



    Despite Government's efforts to encourage and assist citizens to own their flats, there will remain a small number of families who will continue to need rental housing. The majority of rental tenants are housed in 1- and 2-room units built in the 1960s and 1970s. Some of the 1-room rental flats are obsolete and will be gradually phased out. The physical standards and quality of rental flats to be retained will be improved. This will be done, together with the upgrading of owner-occupied blocks, under HDB's Upgrading Programme.

    HDB is also considering a Transitional Rental Housing Scheme to cater to the needs of Singaporeans who need short-term rental housing. Such a Scheme will enable young couples who wish to set up their own families, and those awaiting completion of their home-ownership flats, find interim housing. The Scheme will be restricted to citizen families who are not existing HDB tenants and lessees, or not owners of private properties. Rental will be at market rates.

    Full details of the various changes affecting HDB rental tenants will be announced in due course.

    On 22nd November 1990, almost a month after UIC started negotiating with Mr Liem's private company on the two proposed transactions and before they agreed not to proceed with the sale and purchase of shares in MCH and MBH, Mr Lee Kim Yew, Chairman of UIC, sold his personal holdings of 25.3 million UIC shares to Mr Liem. of $77 million for its investment in the shares of two Japanese banks.

    We must, Mr Speaker, Sir, distinguish between the actions of UIC and of its individual shareholders, that is to say, Mr OeiHong Leong and Mr Lee Kim Yew. The question for the company is whether it has complied with the Stock Exchange's disclosure requirements by withholding the investment losses from the public until 12th March 1991. The SES considers that it did not.     A separate question is whether Mr Oei and Mr Lee, in their personal capacity as individual shareholders of UIC, infringed any legislative provision on insider trading. Section 103(11) of the Securities Industry Act states that it is a defence for a person charged with insider trading if he can prove that the other party to the transaction knew, or ought reasonably to have known, of the inside information before entering into the transaction. The rationale is that when both the buyer and the seller are then on an equal footing, the other party to the transaction, knowing the information, is not therefore at a disadvantage.

In fact, Sir, for reservists who are attending part-time night classes, if they have to attend in-camp training, the unit commander will be able to let them off at night to attend those classes provided there is no night time training schedule on that day. With these guidelines, MINDEF has been able to devolve the power of deciding who should be given deferment to the unit commanders because the unit commanders know their men. They are in the best position to decide whether a reservist's application for deferment has merit or not and whether the application can be approved after considering the training schedule that has been prepared.

    Sir, I would take this opportunity to let the House know how effective is this system of devolving it to the commanders to decide on the outcome of each application for deferment. Let me quote you some figures of applications for deferment from part-time students who had been called up for in-camp training. These are figures that I have managed to collect from three of our divisions. Out of 226 applications for deferment from part-time students, between the period June 1990 and May 1991, 151 were approved. In other words, two out of three such applications were approved. So it is not as if the SAF had not been sympathetic or supportive of these applications. We had approved two out of three such applications.

    Sir, in the final analysis, reservists should understand that deferment comes at a price, a price for SAF, on the one hand, and for the reservist himself. A reserve unit operates as a team. All members of the team should be present when it is undergoing training. In-camp training helps to buildup team work, mutual support and esprit de corps. When the reservist is absent from in-camp training, he has broken the chain. He has deprived a fellow reservist of a buddy in the training. He has weakened the platoon by his absence. Worse still, Sir, if he is an NCO or an officer, the soldiers under his command would have lost a familiar superior to guide and direct them.
