    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Minister for Labour and Welfare (Mr Lim Yew Hock): Mr Speaker, Sir, I rise to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, in moving the Second Reading of the Clerks Employment Bill yesterday, I had already covered most of the ground in respect of this Bill. I cannot think that hon. Members need to be convinced of the need for legislation to protect shop assistants. As a class of worker, they have never been very vocal in demanding this or that. Nevertheless, Sir, since assuming office as Minister for Labour, I have received a steady stream of letters from shop assistants outlining their grievances and asking that some sort of protective legislation be devised to assist them. These grievances, Sir, can be summarised very simply: that their contracts of service are so vague that they never know exactly what rights they have under them; that their employers make all sorts of deductions from their

salaries, especially when they wish to resign and they have asked that their wages be settled in full; that their wages are seldom paid regularly; that their hours of work are too long; and that holidays or sick leave are grudgingly given. CONTROL OF IMPORTS AND EXPORTS ORDINANCE
(Continuation in force)


    The Minister for Commerce and Industry (Mr J. M. Jumabhoy): Mr Speaker, Sir, I rise to move,

    That this Assembly, in accordance with subsection (2) of section 1 of the Control of Imports and Exports Ordinance (Chapter 214), resolves that the aforesaid Ordinance shall continue in force for a further period of one year commencing on the 8th day of December, 1956.

    Sir, on five previous occasions this Ordinance has come up before the Legislature of this colony for its continuance and has been approved each time for a period of one year. My motion now seeks to extend the continuance of this Ordinance for a further period of one year as from the 8th of December, 1956.

    This Ordinance gives the Minister for Commerce and Industry powers to make orders prohibiting the import and export of goods. However, let me assure Members that, in keeping with our free trade policy, these powers are used very sparingly, and except for a comparatively narrow field, most imports are placed on the open general licence and exports are freely licensed. The powers of control, however, come in useful in regulating imports for balance of payment reasons, especially from hard-currency areas, but here again all essential and many semi-essential goods are licensed freely.

    These powers also provide a useful check for purposes of disease control in connection with the import of plants, animals and meats.

     Hon. Members will recall that these powers were used a few months ago to prohibit the importation of pin-tables and similar amusement machines.

    In the export field the powers under this Ordinance are used to keep a check on the export of strategic materials and essential items in short supply in Singapore. At present, controls on exports are very few in number. It is, however, necessary for the Minister to have such powers of control, and the Trade Advisory Council agrees with this view. Sir, I beg to move.
