ANNUAL BONUS INCENTIVES
FOR FACTORY WORKERS


    2. Mr Yeo Toon Chia asked the Minister for Finance if he will request factory owners to set aside a certain percentage from the factory's annual profits so that the amount set aside can be given to workers as annual bonus incentives.

    The Minister for Finance (Mr Hon Sui Sen): Mr Speaker, Sir, section 46 (1) of the Employment Act, 1968, (No. 17 of 1968) already provides that the contract of service or collective agreement between an employer and his employees may contain a provision for the payment of an amount not exceeding the equivalent of one month's wages of an employee as bonus or ex gratia payment. In addition, section 46 (2) of the Act states that nothing in subsection (1) of section 46 shall be construed as preventing an employer at his discretion from paying as bonus or ex gratia payment an amount that exceeds the equivalent of one month's wages as an incentive to his employees to increase their productivity or as a reward for their services. Bonus up to one month's salary is now therefore a negotiable

Column: 1159

item between employer and employee. And it is not necessary to request factory owners to set aside a certain percentage from the factory's annual profits to be given as an annual bonus incentive. In fact, the wise factory owner will also usually make an allowance for incentive or reward bonus in his calculations without being told to do so, although some timely urging on the part of the unions always helps.

    I have already stated in the debate on the Government financial policy that when the effects of the accelerated military run-down have been clearly overcome, we shall have to consider how best workers should be enabled to share in any increased profits where they result from increased productivity or from a general expansion of the economy. One of the ways would be to make bonus payments negotiable beyond the present limitation of one month's salary. This has been legislated for in certain countries and is the practice in others. In our own situation, however, this could be against our main policy of attracting middle technology industries. We want more capital intensive high skills and high value added industries in Singapore. They come mainly from the Western industrial countries where the payment of bonus is not a practice. West European and American companies prefer to pay higher wages rather than bonuses. They want concealed benefits reduced to a minimum so that they know where they stand from year to year. This philosophy treats profits as the result of efficient management, and losses as the result of the ineffective use of expensive labour, since all employers pay their workers the same rate for the job. We shall make a decision after carefully weighing the pros and cons. Our decision must advance our purpose, namely, to raise the living standard of Singapore workers through higher skills and higher productivity.

ILLEGAL IMMIGRATION
(Control measures)


    3. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for Home Affairs how many illegal immigrants were detained during the period 1st January to 28th February, 1971; and what plans he has to stop the social and security threat posed by the unceasing inflow of such persons.

    The Minister for Home Affairs (Prof. Wong Lin Ken): Mr Speaker, Sir, the number of illegal immigrants detained during the period 1st January, 1971, to the 28th February, 1971, is exactly 119.

    The control of illegal entry into Singapore is under the constant attention of the Immigration Department. However, a fully effective air, sea and land control system to prevent illegal immigration entails a sizable increase in expense and manpower. Concerted action is being taken by the Police and Immigration Departments to guard the Singapore coast-line against acts of illegal entry. Special police constables are constantly being deployed around certain beaches as shore patrols, while the Marine Police patrol boats cover the coastal waters. In addition, spot checks and investigations and raids are

Column: 1162

conducted by the Immigration Department and the Police from time to time to detect illegal immigrants.

    When illegal immigrants are arrested, action is taken forthwith against them under the Immigration (Amendment) Act, 1970. Traffickers of illegal immigrants are severely dealt with under the Act.

    The inflow of illegal immigrants continues. However, the number of immigrants entering Singapore illegally does not constitute a social threat. As regards security, the position is well under control and there is no threat to our security.

    Mr Ang Nam Piau (Thomson)(In Mandarin): Sir, can the Minister for Home Affairs say from what countries most of the illegal immigrants come?

    Prof. Wong Lin Ken: Sir, I cannot give the actual number of illegal immigrants from any particular country. But most of them come from Indonesia and Malaysia.

    Mr Ng Kah Ting: Sir, will the Minister be kind enough to give the number of illegal immigrants who were detained during the period as asked for in this question?

    Prof. Wong Lin Ken: I thought I had given the figure earlier. The number of illegal immigrants detained during the period is exactly 119.
