HOUSING AND DEVELOPMENT BOARD FLATS
(Revision of sale price)


    2. Mr Ong Kian Min asked the Minister for National Development whether the Housing and Development Board will revise the sale price of new HDB flats in view of (i) the drop in values of resale HDB flats and private properties, and (ii) the present economic conditions, in general.

    Mr Koo Tsai Kee (for the Minister for National Development): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government is committed to provide affordable housing to Singapore citizens, especially first-timers and low-income families, despite the current economic conditions.

    HDB considers several factors in setting prices of new flats. The main one is affordability. HDB prices new flats such that 90% of Singaporean households can afford at least a 3-room flat, and 70% at least a 4-room flat.

    HDB also takes into account individual attributes of the flats such as location, proximity to MRT/LRT stations, the view from the flats, and special design features and finishes. New flats tend to have better designs and finishes, and are located in towns with better facilities and infrastructure.

    HDB does not consider the prices of private residential properties when it sets

    At present prices, new HDB flats continue to be heavily subsidized. The overall sales proceeds do not cover their total development costs. The loss incurred by HDB has to be funded by a Government grant.

    Mr Low Thia Khiang (Hougang): Sir, in view of the drop in prices of resale flats, does the HDB take into consideration the so-called market subsidy in setting its price?

COMMITTEE ON SINGAPORE'S COMPETITIVENESS
(Motion)


    The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Lee Yock Suan): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move:

    `That this House endorses the strategies and policies set out in the Report of the Committee on Singapore's Competitiveness, contained in Paper Misc. 3 of 1998, as the basis for strengthening Singapore's competitiveness.'

    In May last year, the Committee on Singapore's Competitiveness (CSC) was formed to critically assess Singapore's economic competitiveness over the next decade, taking into consideration global trends and development of existing and emerging competition. The CSC was tasked to identify problem areas and propose appropriate strategies and policies with a view to maintaining and strengthening Singapore's competitive position. The Committee has completed its work and released its Report on 11th November 1998. Copies of the Report have been circulated to hon. Members.

    When the CSC first began its work, the focus was on the next ten years. The onset of the regional economic crisis in July 1997 has, however, altered the economic landscape significantly. Given our open economy and significant

Column: 1210

linkages with the region, Singapore too has not been spared from the contagion despite our sound economic fundamentals. The sharp currency realignments have also weakened our competitiveness. The scope of the CSC Report was therefore expanded to propose measures to help the economy cope with the crisis. The Report hence includes both short- and long-term recommendations to improve our competitiveness.

Short-term Measures

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the regional crisis has significantly impacted our economic performance. Between 1st July 1997 and 21st November 1998, the S$ to US$ exchange rate and the Singapore stock market have fallen by 12% and 30% respectively. The GDP growth rate year-on-year has slowed from 7.8% in 1997 to 6.2% and 1.8% respectively in the first and second quarter of 1998. Third quarter growth was -0.7%, the first decline since 1985. On a quarter-on-quarter basis, the economy has been shrinking for two successive quarters. For the whole of 1998, Singapore's GDP growth is expected to be around 1/2-1%.

    Businesses have been hit, some sectors more than others, and more than 20,000 workers have been retrenched in the first three quarters as a result. The seasonally-adjusted unemployment rate has risen sharply from 2.3% in June to 4.5% in September this year. The coming quarters will be difficult for Singapore. We will continue to face weaker demand for our goods and services from the region which is struggling to recover from the severe economic crisis. The slowing momentum in the US and the EU will dampen demand further. As external demand accounts for more than two-thirds of our total demand, 1999 economic growth is expected to remain weak. MTI's preliminary growth forecast for 1999 is between -1% and 1%.

    A key concern is that the sharp currency realignments have eroded our cost-competitiveness. Our relative unit labour cost (RULC) has been rising gradually but steadily over the years. When the region was prospering, our rising RULC was a concern but not a critical problem. Now

Column: 1211

economic conditions have changed drastically in the region and we are competing for a global economic pie that is growing more slowly.

    Feedback from EDB indicated that some companies were facing pressures to pare prices or relocate production to other countries because of the cost differentials between Singapore and the regional countries. Our total exports to the US have fallen by 8.2% in the first 9 months of this year while all the other regional countries still managed to register positive export growth to the US. This is due largely to the weak performance of our electronics industry. Our market share of electronics exports to the US has dropped from 9.3% in the second quarter of 1997 to 7.3% in September 1998.

    For the short term, the key concern for Singapore is how to restore our competitiveness, help our companies to tide over the crisis and minimise further job losses. We also need to continue attracting investments to create new jobs for Singaporeans.

    Besides raising productivity which takes time, there are two main approaches to restore cost competitiveness in the short term. One approach is through currency depreciation. While MAS has been managing the S$ more flexibly in view of the regional crisis, competitive devaluation is not recommended. A weak S$, in the context of an open economy heavily dependent on imports, will translate into larger imported inflation. This would in turn erode any competitive gains from a currency devaluation. A depreciating currency would also erode confidence in the S$ as a store of value, impact our position as a financial centre and diminish the value of S$-denominated assets held by Singaporeans.

    The other approach which the CSC has recommended is to reduce business costs directly. This addresses the cost competitiveness head-on, without the disadvantages brought about by competitive devaluation. The Government took the same approach to tackle the 1985 recession, with great effectiveness.

    In June the Government accepted the CSC's recommendation of a $2 billion package of off-budget measures which have been implemented. Since then the economic situation has deteriorated further and the contagion has spread to Russia and Brazil. The CSC has recommended that the Government should now implement a much larger cost and tax cutting package worth about $10 billion, equivalent to about 7% of our GDP, to help our companies compete during this difficult period. All the key components of business costs should be addressed, including wages, foreign worker levy, rentals, charges for services like electricity, telecommunications and port services, vehicle-related overhead costs, as well as Government taxes. Here I refer Members to pages 3 and 4 of the CSC Report which set out the details of these recommendations.

    These measures should be implemented expeditiously to regain our competitiveness, minimise further job losses and position our economy to face intense competition as regional competitors struggle to export their way out of their economic difficulties. Besides the cost-cutting package, the CSC has also recommended measures to improve credit availability, seek out markets beyond the region, attract more businesses to Singapore and build up our capabilities.

Long-term Capability Building Measures

    At the same time, we must not lose sight of the need to address longer-term challenges and take steps now to strengthen our competitiveness beyond the crisis.

    We need to take into account the long-term global trends which will have an impact on Singapore. Continued economic liberalisation, globalisation and rapid technological change will shape the competitive economic environment of the future. These global forces will not wait for the economic crisis to blow over. RESEARCH AND DEVELOPMENT


    15. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Education (a) whether the National University of Singapore and the Nanyang Technological University have given due emphasis to research and development activities; (b) how much has been allocated to research and development endeavours in the budgets of both universities; and (c) to what extent these research and development efforts have been translated into commercialised products.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    NUS and NTU are both committed to the promotion of research and development activities. They believe that the reputation of a university rests not only on teaching but also on its research excellence, and that a strong research standing attracts good academic staff and students. The universities have generally focused their research efforts on niche areas, including multi-disciplinary research, in which they can make an impact and which are of strategic relevance to Singapore and the region.

    In FY97, MOE allocated the universities a budget of $94.5 million to be used on research activities, including the purchase of equipment and recruitment of supporting manpower. This is almost 9% of the total budget allocated to the universities, and does not include the salaries of the academic staff which are covered under the universities' normal allocations. The universities also have access to research funding from other sources, eg NSTB, MOH, MINDEF, various foundations, and the industry. In total, they obtained another $68.8 million.

    There are some 19 national research institutes and research centres hosted by our universities. These institutions focus mainly on research that has high national relevance. NUS and NTU also have faculty-based research centres which nurture new niche areas in research.

    As part of our vision for Singapore to be a centre of excellence in education, our universities, together with various Government agencies, have recently initiated international collaborations at an institutional level. Cooperative arrangements have been formed with top institutions like MIT and Johns Hopkins. These seek to uplift the quality of research and teaching at our universities and to help them reach world-class status.

    At the individual level, the researchers in our universities have fostered strong links with industry and Government agencies. There are also numerous projects conducted jointly with researchers from top institutions around the world, eg Stanford University, University of Cambridge, Tsinghua University.

    The number of postgraduate research students has more than doubled in the past 5 years from 1551 in 1994 to 3956 this year. This is due to the aggressive recruitment drives undertaken by the universities to bring in the best students from Singapore and around the world. Selected undergraduates are also exposed to research work through specially tailored programmes in order to cultivate their interest in research at an early stage.

    The universities have made concerted efforts to ensure that their R&D works are commercially viable or academically relevant. Both have institutional outfits that promote commercialisation and technology transfer, namely the Industry and Technology Relations Office in NUS and the Innovation Centre in NTU. Over the past 3 years, they have been granted 65 patents and have set up 20 spin-off companies. Successes in commercialising staff research cover a broad range of areas from waste water treatment to orthopaedic implant products to sensor systems. Beyond commercially oriented research, there is also a significant level of basic research which encompasses the breaking of new frontiers in knowledge and innovation.
