    The Minister for Health (Mr A. J. Braga): Mr Chairman, Sir, I am ready to answer the request made by the Member for Paya Lebar (Mr Lim Koon Teck) in his motion yesterday. He intimated to me after the adjournment that he would like a little more information about the tuberculosis scheme which we intend to carry out in our Ministry. It is intended that all known cases of tuberculosis be followed up so that we can be sure that they are obtaining adequate treatment either as in-patients or as out-patients. As soon as such an organisation which I explained yesterday has been established, then it is worth while intensifying in Singapore our case findings. Effective tuberculosis control is likely to be an expensive undertaking, and for this reason it is most important that we should have a clearly defined estimate of the extent of the problem which faces us, and this is where Australian assistance comes in. We have asked for a team of experts with equipment for mass miniature radiography to undertake a short-term survey to cover about 50,000 persons. Subject to further consultation with the Australian experts, it is proposed to select three sample areas in Singapore - two in the city area and one in the rural area. Such a survey of complete groups to a total of 50,000 persons will take at least six months and the results will give us a very much clearer knowledge of the real incidence of tuberculosis in a densely populated and less densely populated area. When this intensive survey is over, the Australian experts will withdraw, leaving their equipment for our future use. Thereafter we shall continue with the case-finding programme, inevitably at a much slower pace but as quickly as available staff and funds will permit. There is no intention to nationalise tuberculosis. We shall welcome and need all the help we

Column: 980

can get from S.A.T.A. and from private practitioners, and there is no intention to dictate or interfere with treatment being given by non-government practitioners. We wish only to ensure that if a person has tuberculosis, he is given treatment and to ensure also, if he is infective, that the public are adequately safeguarded from his spreading the infection.

    It is interesting to note that a study group on tuberculosis control sponsored by the World Health Organisation met at the end of November 1955. The technical report of their findings has just been published. In that study group were represented experts from ten countries, and in their report they stated, inter alia, that "The establishment in each country of a Central Tuberculosis Register covering notified and re-notified cases will be of great value for the study of the epidemiology of the disease." and that "Complete pulmonary examinations of sample groups are necessary to obtain a reliable index for the prevalence of tuberculosis." It is also interesting to note that their recommendations represent, in fact, the programme which we have now formulated in Singapore.
