CIVIL LAW (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

10.06 a.m.

    The Attorney-General (Mr E. P. Shanks): Mr Speaker, Sir, I rise to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Bill relates to the assessment of damages awardable where death is caused by some wrongful act or negligence.

    Section 12 of the Civil Law Ordinance provides for damages to be proportional to the loss resulting from the death so that at present if the person killed has been sufficiently provident to provide for his widow and children by insuring his life, the amount

Column: 1927

payable under that insurance is deductible from the amount the Court assesses as being the worth of the deceased. Such damage is not a punitive penalty of negligence but the result is the curious position that it is cheaper to kill the provident than the improvident; or, putting it in another way, the wise precaution of the provident man in insuring himself for the benefit of his widow or children may be of no real benefit to them; they may not benefit from the premiums he has already paid. That this was inequitable was recognized in the Federation some three years ago, and in England as long as fifty years ago. It is, both I and the Bar Committee think, time that Singapore gave recognition to the providence of insurance in this respect.

    The other amendment proposed by clause 2 (b) of this Bill provides for dependants to be able to recover funeral expenses which they themselves incurred where they are parties to the action for their recovery. At present, such expenses are only recoverable where the action is brought on behalf of the estate. This is a further amendment that has already been made law elsewhere and is, I think, a desirable change to make in our law.

ENTERTAINMENTS DUTY
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

10.41 a.m.

    The Acting Financial Secretary (Mr Oon Khye Kiang): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, the Entertainments Duty Ordinance was first enacted in 1950 and came into force in October of that year. It is proposed to amend certain sections of the Ordinance in the light of eight years' experience.

    It has been found that the definition of "proprietor" in section 2 of the Ordinance has proved inadequate for the purpose of charging principals in the Courts in respect of the breach of the law, as it is often difficult to ascertain who the manager of an entertainment is. Under the Public Entertainments Ordinance, it is incumbent on any person who organises a public entertainment to obtain a police licence, and it is proposed that such licensee should also be held responsible in respect of any offence against the Entertainments Duty Ordinance, and any rules made thereunder. The proprietor is so defined as to include the licensee of a public entertainment.

Column: 1938



    The second major amendment relates to section 4 of the Ordinance. It will be recalled that at the meeting held on the 18th December, 1951, the former Legislative Council passed a resolution under section 4 (1) of the Ordinance levying half-duty in respect of live entertainment. It is proposed to incorporate this provision in the proposed amendment in clause 3.

    In section 2 of the Ordinance, entertainment is defined as including "any exhibition, performance, amusement, games or sport, to which persons are admitted on payment". Since the enactment of the above resolution, the various Singapore amusement parks, known as the "Worlds", have paid full duty in respect of admission charges. Whilst an amusement park is certainly an entertainment as defined in section 2 of the Ordinance, there is some doubt whether it might not be held to be included among those forms of entertainment which, by virtue of the proviso to the resolution referring to live entertainment, are charged duty at half-rate. It was certainly not the intention that the amusement parks should pay only half-duty, and the opportunity is therefore taken to clarify the position.

    The position of the open-air cinemas has been under examination. At the moment, there are 22 such cinemas operating in the rural areas and three in the city. They cater mainly for persons in the lower income group, such as farmers, fishermen, manual workers and students. The popular prices are 50 cents, 30 cents and 20 cents, on which duty leviable is respectively 15 cents, 10 cents and 5 cents. The operator is usually left with a nett of 15 cents to 35 cents to meet his overhead expenditure, which may come to $3,000 or $3,500 per month. The open-air cinemas do not receive income from advertisements as do the other big cinemas. They are subject to the vagaries of the weather. On the other hand, they are meeting the demand for entertainment which will not be supplied by the ordinary cinema operator. In that sense they are providing a social amenity to the community. They are in most cases working under difficult conditions and often suffer losses. It is

Column: 1939

therefore thought that there is a case for relief in this instance, and the Government has therefore decided to grant relief to the extent of remitting half-duty in respect of these open-air cinemas. I propose, Sir, to move an amendment to section 4 of the Ordinance in Committee. A notice to this effect has been given.

    A dance, as such, is not liable to entertainment duty, but it is however necessary to ensure that, if the proprietor of a restaurant or ballroom charges a fee for admission, and thereafter stages a floor show or several floor shows lasting for more than half an hour in the aggregate, duty should be charged upon the admission fee. The proposed amendment in clause 4 relating to section 10 of the Ordinance is designed to this end.

    At present, not all officers of the Customs Department have authority to inspect places connected with entertainments where tickets are surreptitiously sold in advance for entertainments to be held later. Senior officers of Customs are permitted to inspect only "places of entertainment" or "places ordinarily used as places of entertainment". All other places even though they are connected with entertainments are immune from inspection. In addition, the Entertainments Duty Ordinance requires such officers to inspect places of entertainment while the entertainment is in progress, thus making it difficult to detect offences which are usually committed shortly before the performance. As it is imperative to associate the junior Customs officers in these inspections, the proposed amendment in clause 5 is designed to enable an officer of Customs to enter and inspect at all reasonable times any place of entertainment, any place ordinarily used as a place of entertainment, as well as any other place he reasonably suspects to be connected with an entertainment. Similarly it is proposed in clause 7, which amends section 15 of the Ordinance, to extend the powers given to a senior Customs officer to an "officer of Customs" in respect of seizure of documents. The inspection of places of entertainment necessitates almost simultaneous checks at the admission gates (usually two or more) the

box office as well as the manager's office from where documents relating to the entertainment can be obtained. Revenue officers of the Customs Department should therefore be given powers to seize such documents.
