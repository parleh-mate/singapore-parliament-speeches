METRICATION
(Progress)


    1. Mr Teong Eng Siong asked the Minister for Science and Technology

what progress has been made in the implementation of the metric system, and which Government departments and businesses in the private sector have converted to metric.

    The Minister for Science and Technology (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, since the White Paper entitled

Column: 1147

"Report on a Study of the Proposed Conversion to the Metric System in Singapore" was submitted to Parliament in November, 1970, we have achieved as much progress as we could. On the whole, the measures we have recommended in the White Paper have been adopted. The Metrication Board has concentrated its initial efforts in introducing metrication to Government departments and statutory bodies. It has more or less been completed, and the Metrication Board is now directing its attention to the private sector, including the industries and the retail trade.

    Among the measures which have been adopted for metrication is documentation by the Trade Division, the Department of Customs and Excise, and the Statistics Department, which requires traders to declare the goods and have their excise duties assessed in metric units. The Post Office too is currently operating in metric units.

    Another programme implemented and which has great impact upon the general public is the introduction of metric units to the various aspects of road transport. The Public Works Department, the Registry of Vehicles, the Police Department and private organisations, such as the Automobile Association of Singapore and the National Safety First Council, were all involved in the planning of a programme for the smooth introduction of speed limits and distance markers in metric units.

    Recently, motorists came into contact with metrication when they began to buy petrol in litres. A campaign is currently being planned in August to make motorists accustomed to reading speed limits in kilometres per hour. For this campaign we are seeking the co-operation of petrol pump stations.

    In many government departments, fees and charges for the various services rendered are now based on metric units. The Public Utilities Board, for example, now charges for water in cubic metres. Care has been taken to ensure that these changes do not result in any increase at the expense of the general public.

    In departments where technical matters are involved, the change to metric is a little slower. But the Public Works Department, the Housing and Development Board, and the Public Utilities Board have all finalised their programmes with a target date of 1975, by which time they hope to he completely metricated.

    The bulk of the metrication programme is still in the planning stage. Nevertheless, packaging of paints in metric units was introduced in July, 1972, and the Metrication Board is likewise looking into similar conversions for other packaged goods.

Moreover, it will need time to educate the lay public on the metric system. At present, all that the Commissioner for Labour can do is to order the employer to pay a salary in lieu of notice of termination which can only amount to a maximum of four weeks' pay depending on the length of service of the employee concerned. The Minister will be empowered to order the reinstatement of the employee or to order the employer to pay compensation if, after due inquiry, he is satisfied that the dismissal was wrongful. Responsible and fair-minded employers will have nothing to fear, as the amendment is directed against the irresponsible amongst them.
