    6.     Mr Ong Pang Boon: Mr Speaker, Sir, up to 24th July, 1970, a total of 13,311 applications for Certificates of Suitability had been received. Breakdown figures by institutions of higher learning are not kept, as most applicants apply for admission to more than one institution in a single application. Only seven cases out of a total of 13,311 applications, or 0.053 per cent, were rejected on security grounds. The number of certificates granted and subsequently withdrawn is nil.

    The requirement of the Certificate of Suitability under the provisions of the Internal Security Act is to ensure that institutions of higher learning are not turned into a hotbed of subversive political activities which are not conducive to the pursuit of knowledge. As explained by the Minister of Defence in a press statement issued in March 1967, the Government adopted this requirement with two objectives in mind. The first was to prevent a high concentration of Communist-indoctrinated students collecting together in a tertiary institution with the intention of not only controlling the students' activities, but also intimidating the teaching staff and in the end taking over the running of the

Column: 176

institution. The second purpose was to deter students from getting mixed up in Communist United Front activities.

    I would, however, like to make an observation on the so-called "students' inherent right to higher education". The primary consideration for admission to a higher institution of learning is the possession of academic standards acceptable to the institution concerned. This implies that not all students who seek admission can be admitted even though they may have the minimum qualifications. Furthermore, the number of students that can be admitted to an institution of higher learning depends on a number of factors, such as accommodation, staffing and finance, which in turn depend on the economy of the country and its manpower requirements. There can, therefore, be no inherent right to higher education for anybody.

Moreover, the banking legislation in Malaysia has also been amended. It is therefore appropriate that we should now enact our own legislation to include modifications which would overcome the shortcomings of the present legislation.

    The Banking Bill now before Members incorporates the basic features of the current Banking Ordinance, but it also introduces a number of new provisions, the most important of which are designed to ensure better and sounder banking practices and to better safeguard depositors' interests. They include restrictions on the amount a bank can grant to a single customer, a prohibition from engaging in trade, restrictions on the extent a bank can acquire equity capital and immovable property, and on the amount of loans extended for the purpose of purchasing and improving immovable properties.

    More comprehensive definitions appear for "bank" and "banking business". A new concept of "capital funds" has been introduced so that a healthy relationship between a bank's lending power and its net worth in Singapore is maintained. "Capital funds" mean, for a bank whose head office is situated in Singapore, its paid-up capital and reserves and, for a bank whose head office is outside Singapore, the net head office funds and such other liabilities as the Commissioner may decide.

a licence. The minimum capital requirement has been raised from $2 million to $3 million. In the case of a foreign bank, this has been raised from $5 million to $6 million with a requirement that the net head office funds in Singapore should be at least $3 million. At present foreign banks are required to hold $2 million approved assets locally. It is not considered that these requirements will cause any difficulty to banks already licensed, but a transitional period of two years, which can be further extended by approval of the Minister, is being provided for compliance with the requirements. It is further proposed to require banks and their branches to pay licence fees annually.

Every bank shall also send to the Commissioner particulars of all advances, loans and credit facilities granted to any of its Directors, any firm in which any of its Directors is a partner, or to any related corporation, etc. The Commissioner may prohibit such further grants of advances, loans or credit facilities or impose restrictions on the grants unless he is satisfied that these would not be against the interests of the depositors of the bank.

    Part V of the Banking Bill spells out restrictions on certain types of business and prohibitions on others. These prohibiting clauses are new and are designed to safeguard depositors' interests. They have been recommended by the International Monetary Fund, and are found in the banking laws of a number of other countries. Briefly, they are that no bank will be allowed to grant to any single customer any credit facility which is at any time more than 60 per cent of its capital funds, though this can be increased to 100 per cent with the approval of the Commissioner.
