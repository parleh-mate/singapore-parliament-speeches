GOVERNMENT'S POLICY
TOWARDS EXPATRIATES INTERVENING
IN LOCAL POLITICAL AFFAIRS
(Statement by the Prime Minister)


    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Sir, I apologise to the Member for Anson for having been absent from the Chamber yesterday at 2.30 p.m. but I have explained to him that my Personal Assistant was ill. There was an oversight.

    Sir, the Government's policy towards expatriates intervening in local political affairs was considered and stated on the occasion when a Professor of the University of Malaya inadvertently wandered into the field of political controversy.

    In the case referred to by the Member for Anson, I have received a long letter from the lawyer concerned and another from his client. The lawyer explained that the offending statement attributed to him in the Press was, in fact, a Press news item and not a statement by him. The point that non-citizens should not enter the political arena which includes trade union disputes - particularly where the Communist-controlled unions are involved - has already been made and understood.

    Briefly, we have stated that Government does not approve of non-citizens interfering with local political affairs. The Government would strongly urge non-citizens who are British European expatriates to stay out of the local political arena and let none of their acts, either intentionally or otherwise, be construed as intervening in local political matters. This principle applies to a man whether he is a Professor at the University, a lawyer in the profession or merchant in commerce.

    The Prime Minister: Sir, the dividing line between being just the agent for an employer and being a participant is quite a clear one. A lawyer who issues a statement in the name of his client is only an agent and the client takes the consequences. I think the Member for Anson is aware - particularly in the case of Press statements - that no advocate and solicitor can issue statements in his personal capacity or in his firm's name, because that means, amongst other things, breaching the rules of etiquette with regard to advertising. Had this statement been attributed not to the lawyer but to the client, a completely different position would have arisen. He would have been a pure agent. But where, as in this particular case, the Press report stated that he as the lawyer had said so after he left the Ministry of Labour, obviously he did it in his capacity as the lawyer and not as the agent, then the Government drew certain inferences from it. As I have explained, he has in a very long letter elaborated on the circumstances of how the statement came to be reported and I have no intention of opening the matter unless he wishes to do so publicly.
