TELECOMMUNICATIONS AND INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY INDUSTRY



    1. Mr Chng Hee Kok asked the Minister for Communications and Information Technology whether the Government will adopt a hands-off approach in the development of the telecommunications and information technology industry, especially in the industry's pricing of services and marketing.

    The Minister for Communications and Information Technology (Mr Yeo Cheow Tong): Mr Speaker, Sir, in a fully liberalised and competitive market environment, the Info-communications Development Authority of Singapore, or IDA, will be moving towards regulating with a lighter touch. RESTRUCTURING STATUS OF POWER INDUSTRY


    6. Mrs Lim Hwee Hua asked the Minister for Trade and Industry if he will give an update on the status of the restructuring of the local power industry, particularly with regard to the introduction of competition to both electricity generation and supply, and whether there is any intention to limit the number of players in each segment.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Mr Speaker, Sir, since 1995, the Government had taken several steps to restructure the electricity industry, starting with the corporatisation of the Electricity and Gas Departments of the Public Utilities Board (PUB) to form Singapore Power Ltd (SP) on 1st October 1995. To facilitate competition, Singapore Power was structured as a holding company with five separate subsidiaries, namely two generation companies (PowerSenoko and PowerSeraya), a transmission & distribution company (PowerGrid), a supply company (Power Supply) and a piped-gas company (PowerGas).

    Another power station being built in Tuas was put under a separate company, Tuas Power Ltd (TP), owned directly by Temasek Holdings, so that it could compete against the SP generation companies. To facilitate competition in the generation and supply sectors, the Singapore Electricity Pool (SEP) was established as an exchange for trading electricity.

    The Government decided in March 1999 that the next step in restructuring the electricity industry was for Singapore Power to fully divest its generating

Column: 823

companies to Temasek Holdings in 2001. This will bring about ownership separation of the generators, which are the contestable part of the market, from the transmission and distribution network, which is the non-contestable part. All generators will have a level playing field. The transmission and distribution network will be regulated tightly as a natural monopoly. In the year 2001, there will be four competing generation companies, PowerSenoko, PowerSeraya, Tuas Power and SembCorp Co-gen.

Review of Industry Structure

    Many more countries have now liberalised their electricity services. As a result, electricity prices have dropped significantly. Consumers have benefited with greater choice, better product innovation and better service quality.

    To ensure the competitiveness of our electricity sector, MTI will further liberalise the electricity market with the aim to put in place a fully competitive market. We have engaged a consultant to carry out a comprehensive review of the electricity industry and to recommend how competition in the sector could be further enhanced. Companies that want to generate or supply electricity in Singapore can apply to the PUB, the Regulator, for a public licence. Background

Tail-End Home Detention for Penal Inmates

    Tail-End Home Detention is not new. Home Detention has been implemented at the tail-end of the detention period for drug inmates and Criminal Law Detainees as well as Reformative trainees released onto statutory supervision. Countries like the United Kingdom, Sweden and Australia have successfully implemented Home Detention. It serves as an avenue for the earlier release of prisoners who are most amenable to rehabilitation. Its implementation serves a number of purposes.

    Firstly, allowing the offender to be closer to his family and the community will help him to better reintegrate into

Column: 894

society after incarceration. The deterrent element is still retained as offenders will be subjected to restrictions and surveillance via electronic tagging. Secondly, as this scheme allows offenders to remain in the community, it keeps the families intact and allows offenders to be gainfully employed. Reaction to our announcement of the Scheme, especially those from families of offenders, has been positive.

    Clause 10 of the Bill introduces a new Part IVA in the Prisons Act to provide for the Tail-end Home Detention Scheme. The Scheme will allow selected offenders serving jail terms of six months and above to be released early on home detention up to a maximum of six months. These offenders must have served at least half of their imprisonment term before they become eligible.

    The selection of eligible inmates will be based on a stringent set of criteria. Only offenders who are assessed to be least likely to pose a risk to society will be considered for the Scheme. Prisoners serving life imprisonment, offenders of serious or violent crimes, and drug and sexual offenders will not be eligible. Clause 24 of the Bill provides for a new Schedule which lists the type of ineligible offenders. In addition, eligible offenders will go through rigorous assessments to determine their suitability for emplacement onto the Scheme. These assessments will be conducted by the internal Prison committees as well as an independent Advisory Committee, made up of distinguished members of the community. The conduct, attitude and response to rehabilitation and the risk factors of eligible offenders will be scrutinised.

This minimum age provides a safety net for young offenders and protects them from exploitation of unscrupulous adults who may want to make use of children to carry out illegal activities. It also allows for early intervention by social and other authorities before a child progresses into a hardened offender.

    Although the minimum age of culpability is 7 years old, children are not subject to the same justice system as adults. The juvenile justice system adopts a restorative model that

EDUCATION SCHEME OF SERVICE


    6. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Education (a) what is the rationale and special justification for the recent upward salary revision for the teaching profession despite the current need for wage restraint in both the public and private sectors; and (b) whether his Ministry has introduced any new or innovative measures other than salary increases to enhance the status, working conditions and job satisfaction of the more deserving school teachers so as to attract the right individuals to join the teaching profession as a long-term career.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    The revisions made to the Education Scheme of Service since 1st January 2000 was not a general salary revision. Instead, new intermediate salary grades were added to the scheme of service. The objective was to improve the structure of the service to allow enhanced promotional opportunities and more timely promotional opportunities to improve retention. These changes were part of civil service-wide changes to the structure of Division 1 services. New salary grades were introduced for both the General Education Officer (GEO) and the Senior Education Officer (SEO) salary schemes. Previously, classroom teachers were only eligible for one promotion to the career grade of GEO 1A and GEO 2A. With the new intermediate GEO salary grade, they now have an additional promotion opportunity, ie, 2 promotions, before they reach the career classroom teacher grade. GEO officers can also look forward to faster and earlier first promotions. The new SEO grade was introduced to offer better salary progression in the Upper Timescale levels before officers reach the Superscale levels.

    The Ministry agrees that we need to provide an overall conducive environment to help attract and retain teachers. It is not salaries and promotions alone that determine retention ability. We have a strong focus on training and development. The Ministry encourages and supports its officers to upgrade themselves professionally. The National Institute of Education has recently announced a comprehensive framework for continual training of teachers. We have also implemented various schemes such as the Study Leave Scheme and the Professional Development Leave Scheme to enable officers to upgrade themselves academically through full-time graduate and postgraduate programmes. Financial assistance in the form of sponsorships, half-pay leave and interest-free study loans are available. Since 1998, an average of 230 teachers per year have been granted study leave to pursue a first degree or a Master's degree.

    In recent years, various awards have been instituted by several organisations and the nation to recognise outstanding teachers. These awards, viz the Outstanding Youth in Education Award, the Caring Teacher Award, the Inspiring Chinese Language Teachers award and the pinnacle award, the President's Award for Teachers - are reflective of the community's recognition and nation's regard for the important role that teachers play. There is no greater affirmation for our many dedicated teachers than when the community seeks to recognise their good work.

    MOE has also implemented several measures to alleviate teachers' workload. These include the creation of School Administrator and Operations Manager posts at all schools to perform administrative support functions, the streamlining of work procedures (e.g. payment of school fees by GIRO, central issuing of student identification passes), and leveraging on IT to improve work efficiency. Since 1998, MOE has allocated more than 1,500 additional teaching posts and more than 380 administrative and support posts to schools.
