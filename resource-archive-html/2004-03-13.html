     Consumer Credit Laws

 

     Miss Penny Low (Pasir Ris-Punggol):    Sir, in Singapore, consumer goods purchased with credit arrangements by the merchants may be categorised into three main common categories.  First, credit sales; second, conditional sales; and third, hire purchase.  In all three types, the merchant either supplies the credit himself or arranges for a finance company to supply the credit.  I may add also a fourth category, called the "credit sales", where the credit is arranged by both the consumer and the merchant independently of each other. 

 

     At present, all four types of consumer credit transactions are governed by different regulatory regimes.  Credit sales and conditional sales are unregulated.  Hire purchases are regulated mainly by the Hire Purchase Act under MTI.  Credit card sales are regulated by MAS who exercises jurisdiction only over the banking institutions and, hence, the credit part of the transactions rather than the underlying sales transaction.  The result is a confusing array of rules and regulations applying to different types of transaction and inconsistent protection to consumers.  For example, consumers who wish to select the best form of credit to purchase the consumer goods cannot make comparisons that are meaningful enough for them to make a decision.  As such, they are, in fact, at the mercy of the merchants and there is unfair advantage at work.

 

     In the UK and Australia, such regulations have been reformed.  In fact, they have rationalised some of these transactions so that the same regime applies to all three types of transactions.  The areas that have been rationalised include linked-transactions and by this the sales and credit elements are conditional upon each other.  They have also joint liability.  This is especially important for

Column: 1520

hire purchase where consumers may have only one agreement with the finance company.  Some hire purchase agreements are not governed by the Hire Purchase Act and it contains exemption clauses which severely limits liability for delivery and condition of goods.

 

     Third, they also have similar regulatory regime to apply to all three types of consumer credits that include things like disclosure regime, method of calculating interest rates, restrictions on administrative default and penalty charges, advertising and marketing regime, etc.  I suggest that the consumer credit laws be rationalised so that different parts of the consumer credit industry may compete on equal footing.  The result would be a more efficient market and a much more level playing field for both the consumers, the merchants and finance companies at work.
