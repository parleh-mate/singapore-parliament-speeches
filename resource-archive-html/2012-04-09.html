Government Tenders

(Equal opportunities for local and foreign tenderers)

1 Mr Baey Yam Keng asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Finance how Government procurement guidelines ensure that neither local nor foreign suppliers are favoured during invitation, assessment and award of tenders.

The Minister of State for Finance (Mrs Josephine Teo) (for the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Finance) : Mr Speaker, Singapore is a signatory to the World Trade Organization's Agreement on Government Procurement or GPA. Our obligations both under the GPA and our Free Trade Agreements require that local and foreign suppliers be given equal opportunity to participate in Government tenders. The Government's procurement rules are aligned with Singapore's international obligations. All Ministries, Organs of State and Statutory Boards are required to adhere to them.

Page: 8

The Government's procurement rules also require that every tender be assessed fairly and awarded to the proposal that meets the tender requirements and gives the best value-for-money, based on both price and non-price considerations. There should be no discrimination in favour of or against foreign suppliers.

Mr Baey Yam Keng (Tampines) : Thank you, Mr Speaker. I thank the Minister of State for the reply. I would like to ask the Minister of State how would the Government procurement guidelines provide a level playing field for all types of businesses, whether foreign or local, and among the local companies, SMEs versus Government-linked companies. And how does the Government assess or allow tenders by smaller enterprises which may not have the strong financial background but have the requisite track records and capabilities?

Mrs Josephine Teo : Mr Speaker, the Government's procurement regime is an open one that places emphasis on fairness, transparency and value-for-money. It is also important to note that, wherever possible, Ministry of Finance encourages the use of outcome-based specifications in Government tenders. This allows bidders to put in their best proposals regardless of their own size or track record. The other aspect that is important is that bids are evaluated holistically based on their abilities to meet the requirements and objectives of the tender. For example, the total life-cycle cost is considered instead of just price alone. Award of contracts is on the basis of the best value-for-money offer. Specifically, to address Mr Baey's concern of whether the playing field is level for companies that are smaller in size, I am pleased to share with him that if we looked at the tenders that the Government has awarded, about 20% of them go to businesses with net tangible assets of less than $250,000. These would typically be small and medium enterprises. If we were to consider the contracts where the tender value is $100,000 or below, two in three of those contracts typically go to the small and medium enterprises with net tangible assets of $250,000 or lower. The evidence does suggest that our procurement regime is supportive of a whole range of enterprises, regardless of their size.

We also recognise that there may be occasions where the tender value is quite large. Smaller suppliers may need to enter into joint collaborations or consortia in order to compete for those projects fairly. The way to deal with that is to share pertinent information on public sector pipeline projects in advance, so that the suppliers have better visibility and to team up with potential partners. For example, IDA in their annual briefing to the infocomm industry will let suppliers know in advance of the kind of projects that they are looking to put out for businesses to tender for.

In addition, the Government also pushes alerts on business opportunities to suppliers via the GeBIZ system. There is one component called "Really Simple Syndication" (RSS). This is specifically designed to allow even smaller enterprises to access opportunities. When projects are published on RSS, email alerts on SPRING's Enterprise One Portal, which links to SMEs' database, are also made available. That is what we have been doing.

Mr Seah Kian Peng (Marine Parade) : Mr Speaker, I would like to ask the Minister of State whether these same guidelines apply to statutory boards and Government-owned companies where Government owns a significant shareholding. I ask this because not so long ago, I read in the newspapers that Changi Airport Group in the design of the new Terminal 4, that local architect firms were excluded, and that caused a ruckus within their industry. I know there was a subsequent clarification but I wanted to hear the Ministry's response to this and, specifically, whether these guidelines also apply to statutory boards and Government-owned companies.

Mrs Josephine Teo : Mr Speaker, I thank Mr Seah for his question. These guidelines apply to all Ministries, statutory boards and Organs of State. As for companies, they operate on commercial principles and it is up to them to decide on what basis their procurement is best proceeded with. In the case of the Changi Airport Group, I think they have made a very clear statement that where Terminal 4 is concerned, all local as well as foreign architects are welcome to participate. I think there is no need for us to speculate further. They have made their intentions very clear and in actual fact, in terms of implementation, all architects have been invited to submit their proposals.

Mr Speaker : Mr Baey Yam Keng, last question.

Mr Baey Yam Keng : Sir, the Minister of State has made mention to tenders above certain value and the requirements on asset size and paid-up capital. Can I check what is the tender value that Government agencies use as a benchmark, beyond which they would require such financial status?

Mrs Josephine Teo : Mr Speaker, I should just clarify. I did not say that there were any specific requirements. At the end of the day, Government looks at the criteria holistically and, depending on which supplier is best able to provide the value for money, as well as to meet the requirements of the tender, they will be considered.

Productivity Targets for Lift Upgrading Programme (LUP) Contractors

(Shortening completion time for works)

2 Ms Foo Mee Har asked the Minister for National Development (a) how long does it take to upgrade one lift under the HDB Lift Upgrading Programme (LUP); and (b) if he would consider setting tighter productivity targets for LUP contractors to shorten the time needed for onsite construction work and minimise inconvenience to residents.

The Minister of State for National Development (Mr Lee Yi Shyan) (for the Minister for National Development) : Sir, under the Lift Upgrading Programme (LUP), it takes about six months to overhaul ”€ Ms Foo used the word "upgrade" ”€ an existing lift; and about 16 months to build a new lift shaft with a new lift in it.

For overhauling an old lift in an existing shaft, it is a relatively simple job. The replacement of lift cable, motor, lift car and control systems, etc, can be done rather quietly and speedily. Even including testing and commissioning, most of the overhauling can be completed within six months.

Page: 10

For building of new lifts in their lift shafts, the work is more complicated. Firstly, HDB's appointed contractors would need to work with other agencies to detect and divert underground services such as telecoms, power supply, water and sewage pipelines as necessary. This phase alone could take up to four months. Secondly, the appointed contractors would commence the construction work proper starting with micro-piling, and then lift and lift shaft installation, lift testing and commissioning. This phase will take about a year.

When LUP is implemented in a precinct, HDB will ensure that the residents will continue to have access to their lifts. This is why HDB would construct the new lifts first before proceeding to overhaul the existing lifts. Taken together, for each block on the ground under LUP, the duration of the upgrading programme could take about two years.

To minimise inconveniences to the residents, HDB has taken active steps to shorten the time needed for onsite construction work. For example, HDB cut the time needed for construction by two months when it introduced the use of steel lift shafts in 2007. Steel lift shafts are pre-fabricated and can be assembled on site more quickly than concrete lift shafts. More recently, HDB achieved a further two-month reduction, from six months to four months, in the service diversion phase of LUP works, by streamlining the process involving multiple agencies.

HDB will continue to look at ways to expedite LUP works without compromising quality and safety, bearing in mind that the works are often carried out within a tight site area and in close proximity to residents. In some situations, HDB will also need to phase out the work, so that residents can continue to enjoy the same level of lift service while LUP works are ongoing, and to minimise noise and dust to residents.

Ms Foo Mee Har (West Coast) : Mr Speaker, I thank the Minister of State for his reply. I would like to ask two supplementary questions relating to comments made by my residents undergoing LUP at Pandan Gardens. Many of the residents ask me how come it takes two years to upgrade a block when we can build condominiums in the same time. The crux of the issue is about how work is being phased. According to my own observations in the estate, the work being done in upgrading the lifts in the precinct – let us say the eight blocks involved – basically the work is done in batches. They start digging in one block, they finish and they go to the next block, and they go one round before they come back to the first block to do the next step. If it is done like that, it would take a long time, probably two years, end-to-end for the whole project. My question to the Minister of State is whether HDB can consider the work being compressed so that works can start on one block, complete the works there before proceeding to the next block, so that inconvenience to residents is minimised. I would also like to ask a question on pre-fabrication. The Minister of State talked about pre-fabrication being used for the lift steel shafts. What other opportunities are there for HDB to explore so that a lot more work can be completed offsite?

Mr Lee Yi Shyan : Mr Speaker, I thank Ms Foo for her questions and her observations. Indeed, I sympathise with some of these observations that Members have gathered from the local execution of the LUP projects. I, myself, have some LUP works ongoing in my constituency, and I get very similar feedback from residents. Compared to a condominium or new flats built on a greenfield site, the LUP work has to be constrained by existing tight site and hours of construction and to minimise inconveniences and noise pollution to the residents. The Member made the observation about whether we could − instead of treating the whole precinct as one site – carry out work block to block. HDB is studying this approach. The reason why it is currently done this way has to do with the contract that is given. Contractors would typically hire a team of specialists doing certain functions, and they would go from one block to another block sequentially. If not well managed locally, these sequential activities can add up in terms of total duration.

In terms of pre-fabrication, HDB is making use of prefab technology as far as possible. I am hopeful that HDB will continue to make improvements by better onsite house-keeping and project management to make sure that activities are more tightly integrated on the ground, and where feasible to complete work in one block before moving to another block.

Mr Low Thia Khiang (Aljunied) : Mr Speaker, Sir, two supplementary questions. First, what is the progress of LUP? How many more blocks are in balance, still awaiting LUP? Secondly, the question is whether HDB is on track to meet the Government's target to complete the LUP so that all eligible HDB blocks will have lift access on every floor.

Mr Lee Yi Shyan : We expect to complete the LUP by end of 2014.

Impact of JTC Corp's Industrial Property Divestment Plan on Costs for Small, Medium Enterprises (SMEs)

4 Mr Teo Siong Seng asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) what is the impact of JTC Corp's plans to divest its industrial property portfolio on local SMEs; (b) whether there are plans to review the divestment plan; and (c) whether the Ministry would consider providing sufficient affordable industrial land or premises to enable promising local SMEs to continue operating and developing their capabilities to the fullest in Singapore.

The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Lim Hng Kiang) : Mr Speaker, Sir, in 2005, the Government decided to divest JTC's generic multi-storey, multiple-user factory space, or what is commonly known as flatted factory space. As explained in my speech during the Committee of Supply debate last month, there was already a competitive market for flatted factory space in the private sector. JTC's market share then was only 18%.

JTC's tenants were enjoying lower rentals than others. This created an unlevel playing field for other SMEs that were not JTC's tenants. Furthermore, JTC's lower rentals were undercutting private sector developers. If JTC had continued to remain in this segment of the market, it would crowd out the smaller developers. The divestment was therefore necessary to, first, level the playing field for all SMEs and, second, to promote the vibrancy of this segment of the industrial market.

The divestment of JTC's flatted factories has affected only a small proportion of SMEs because JTC's market share, as I mentioned just now, was only 18%. To ameliorate the impact of the divestment on the affected SMEs, JTC worked with the buyers to impose rental caps, for a period of up to three years. SMEs affected by the first phase of divestment in 2008 paid, on average, 6% to 25% less than market rates when they renewed their tenancies in the first three years after divestment.

The increase in rentals after this transition period may appear large to some SMEs, but this is partly because they had enjoyed lower-than-market rentals in the earlier three years.

MTI and JTC have been working to keep industrial space affordable. First, JTC continues to develop innovative infrastructure projects, some of which help reduce business costs for SMEs through innovative designs or the up-front provision of capital-intensive facilities that can be co-shared. Examples of such projects include the Business Aviation Complex and the upcoming MedTech Hub and Surface Finishing Complex.

Page: 13

Second, we ensure that there is a sufficient supply of industrial land to meet SMEs' demand for industrial space. Through the Industrial Government Land Sales (IGLS) programme, about 20 hectares of land was released every half year in 2010 and 2011. This is about 30% higher than in 2008 and 2009. In the first half of 2012, an even larger amount of land – 24 hectares in total – has been made available through the IGLS programme. For this IGLS programme, we also released several small sites with shorter tenure, to satisfy the demand of industrialists, including SMEs, who require customised facilities and thus prefer to build factory space on their own. With shorter tenure, the sites will also be more affordable.

To ensure a timely supply of space, we have also shortened the project completion period for IGLS sites, from eight years to between five and seven years.

These measures will help ensure that our industrial space remains affordable. Industrial rentals have, in fact, moderated recently on the back of incoming supply and weakening demand. Industrial rentals grew by only 0.4% between 3Q2011 and 4Q2011, compared to 5% for the same quarters of the previous year. We expect rentals to continue to moderate as more supply comes on-stream, and with the weaker economic climate.

Mr Teo Siong Seng (Nominated Member) : I thank the Minister for the explanation. Would JTC consider for the remaining of the factory space, instead of divesting to REITs, to sell to the factory owner?

Mr Lim Hng Kiang : We are at the tail end of the divestment process and these are just smaller flatted factories. We are considering either trade sales in which we sell individual buildings or if the owners and occupiers are able to form bodies, we would be happy to look into selling to them too.

Government's Target Inflation Range

5 Ms Tan Su Shan asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) whether the Government has a target inflation range; (b) if so, does it differentiate between domestic and imported inflation pressures; (c) whether a strong Singapore dollar policy leading to domestic inflation pressures is a concern; and (d) what is being done on the supply side to ease these pressures.

Mr Lim Hng Kiang : Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore's monetary policy is centred on managing the exchange rate of the Singapore dollar. This makes sense given the small and open nature of our economy. Our exports and imports of goods and services are nearly four times as large as our GDP. The exchange rate therefore has a significant influence on domestic prices, and plays an important role in determining economic activity. While MAS does not have a formal inflation target, the MAS Act clearly states that MAS' objective is to maintain price stability for sustained economic growth over the medium term.

MAS and MTI do differentiate between domestic and imported inflation. We keep close watch on consumer price developments from both sources. First, imported inflation from abroad. The prices of goods we import from other countries, for instance, food and oil, can change significantly due to events beyond our control, such as poor harvests, natural disasters, or geopolitical tensions. Second, there are domestic sources of inflation stemming from changes in the cost of factor inputs. For example, when the cost of hiring a worker goes up, businesses may charge higher prices for goods and services to maintain their profit margins.

Page: 14

A stronger Singapore dollar helps to cap both imported as well as domestic inflation. As the majority of products we consume and use in production are imported from overseas, a stronger exchange rate will reduce the Singapore dollar cost of these imported goods for both consumers and producers. Moreover, a stronger Singapore dollar will help to moderate domestic inflation by dampening the external demand for our goods and services, in turn lowering the demand for domestic resources, such as labour and land. As a result, rentals and wages would rise more modestly, reducing Singaporeans' demand for goods and services. This is similar to how interest rates impact domestic prices in larger economies.

Apart from consumer prices, Singaporeans have also been concerned about asset prices. The increase in asset prices is due to strong demand and tight supply of completed units. The low domestic interest rates, which follow global rates because of our open financial system, have also partly supported the demand. We have implemented fiscal and prudential measures to cool the property market. Where supply constraints can be ameliorated to relieve cost pressures in the housing, commercial and industrial property segments, we have also done so directly.

In addition, the Government is partnering Singapore workers and companies to raise productivity. Firms are in a challenging environment, where foreign worker levy and other costs are impacting their bottom line in the short term. Higher productivity allows firms to pay higher wages while remaining profitable without relying on large price increases. There are already several schemes for firms investing in machines that enhance productivity and innovation as well as to encourage further training and up-skilling of our workers.

These supply side measures from increasing the availability of property supply to enhancing productivity will help us manage domestic inflation over the longer term.

Finally, the Government also provides cash grants directly to help mitigate the impact of rising costs on households. For example, the "Grow & Share" package and the U-Save rebates. Last year alone, resident households living in HDB 3-room flats received Government transfers worth more than $2,000 per person on average. This amounts to around 10% of household income per household member. For resident households living in HDB 4-room flats, the transfer was around 7% of their average household income per household member. Although these grants do not reduce inflation, they help to offset the higher cost of living that our households experienced.

Under the new definition, an arbitration agreement which is concluded orally, or by conduct, or through any other means, if its content is recorded in any form, it will be recognised as an arbitration agreement within the context of the International Arbitration Act. In our consultation last year, a majority of arbitrators and practitioners were of the view that the broadened definition should be adopted.

Second, recourse against negative jurisdictional rulings by an arbitral tribunal. Clause 4 repeals and re-enacts section 10 to provide recourse against negative jurisdictional rulings by an arbitral tribunal. Such a ruling is one where the tribunal holds that it does not have the jurisdiction to hear a dispute. Academics and practitioners have called for reform in this area. Subject to the arbitral agreement, the tribunal will be able to award simple or compound interest, on sums claimed or costs awarded in the arbitration, from such a date, at such rate and with such rest as it sees fit. Mr K Shanmugam : Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time." This Bill enacts the Foreign Limitation Periods Act. It adopts the recommendations of the Singapore Academy of Law's Law Reform Committee's 2011 Report on Limitation Periods in Private International Law. The Report was prepared by Prof Yeo Tiong Min, Senior Counsel and Dean of the SMU Law School. Practitioners, arbitrators and academics contributed during our public consultation on this Bill. We thank all who have contributed.

Let me first outline the principles underlying the Bill. It deals with the application of limitation periods. In common law jurisdictions, limitation periods prevent actions from being brought after a specified period. That is the case under our Limitation Act.

Traditionally, the common law characterises time bars as procedural in nature. Therefore, according to the principle that a forum applies its own procedure, the forum would apply its own time bars, even if a foreign country's law is applicable to the issue at hand. So you could have a Singapore court applying Singapore time bars to an issue which is substantively governed by French law. The effect of the time bar, when it is applied, is to terminate the action in the forum.

To use the words of the Canadian Supreme Court in Tolofson v Jenson [1994], the time bar is "determinative of the rights of both parties". It is not part of the rules which "make the machinery of the court run smoothly." It is therefore incongruous to say, on the one hand, that an issue is governed by a foreign law and, on the other hand, to apply the forum's time bar to that issue.

The UK, Australia and Canada have introduced legislation to reform the common law position. The basic position is, if an issue is governed by a foreign law, that foreign law's limitation period will apply; and the forum's limitation period will not apply. Such an issue has not yet arisen in our courts. Nevertheless, we think it prudent to clarify the law in this area.

Clause 3 lays down the general rule that, when the law of a foreign country applies to a matter before the Singapore court, that country's law relating to limitation will apply, and Singapore's law relating to limitation will not apply.

Clause 3(2) in particular addresses the double actionability rule governing tort claims. The double actionability rule states that a claim in tort must be actionable under the law of the country where the tort is committed as well as the law of the forum. The rule exceptionally allows for the law of a more closely connected country to displace either or both laws if that is more appropriate. Clause 3(2) provides that, in both cases, the applicable limitation period is that of the applicable foreign law.

Clause 4 provides exceptions to the general rule. Clause 4(1) and (2) provide that clause 3 will not apply to the extent that it is against public policy. This includes situations where undue hardship will be caused. Clause 4(3) provides for any suspension or interruption of a limitation period on the ground of a party's absence from a jurisdiction to be disregarded. This would prevent forum shopping by plaintiffs, to deprive defendants of the benefit of limitation periods. This is similarly subject to a public policy exception in clause 4(4).

Page: 74

Clause 5 provides that, when a foreign court disposes of a matter by reference to a limitation period, it is deemed to have disposed of the matter substantively. This will preclude further litigation on the matter.

Clause 6 defines the meaning of laws relating to limitation, and clause 7 provides for the Act to bind the Government.

Clause 8 provides for consequential amendments, in particular it provides for the Act to apply to the International Arbitration Act.

The choice of Singapore as an arbitral seat will no longer automatically require the adoption of Singapore limitation periods. That will help enhance Singapore's status as a neutral arbitral seat.

The Bill was strongly supported by the legal fraternity during consultation and during the Arbitration Dialogue 2011 that my Ministry held at Maxwell Chambers last November. Sir, I beg to move.

Extension of Government-Paid Adoption Leave

17 Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports whether he will consider increasing the Government-Paid Adoption Leave from four weeks to 16 weeks for mothers who adopt babies below 12 months of age, as like maternity leave given to mothers who have their own babies.

Mr Chan Chun Sing : Adoption leave was introduced in 2004 to provide time for parents to bond with their adopted child. It is not a mandatory provision. Employers who voluntarily grant adoption leave to their employees can claim reimbursement of four weeks' salary from the Government. Employers are not precluded from granting a longer period of adoption leave, as long as there is mutual agreement between both parties.

The maternity leave prescribed under the Child Development and Co-savings Act, on the other hand, is part of overall efforts to incentivise more births. It provides for 16 weeks of maternity leave for citizen births to married couples. The 16-week maternity leave allows a mother to bond with the child, as well as physically recuperate from childbirth before returning to work. The Government reimburses the employer for eight to 16 weeks of the leave, depending on the birth order of the child.

As the objectives of adoption and maternity leave are different, it is not necessary to extend adoption leave to 16 weeks. Nevertheless, the Government will continue to encourage employers to support adoption leave applications by their employees, as part of family friendly practices. Adoptive parents can also tap on the six days of statutory paid childcare leave (for parents with a Singaporean child below seven years), as well as six days of statutory unpaid infant care leave (for parents with a Singaporean child under the age of two).

Strengthening the Social Entrepreneurship Sector

18 Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports whether his Ministry will formalise a directory of social enterprises and consider providing more sources of structured funding and assistance to enhance the structure and capability of the social entrepreneurship sector.

Mr Chan Chun Sing : Social enterprises are businesses with a social mission. MCYS offers funding support to "Work-Integration" Social Enterprises through the ComCare Enterprise Fund (CEF). These are enterprises that provide training and employment opportunities for individuals from needy or disadvantaged groups, such as persons with disability, ex-offenders, youths-at-risk, persons recovering from psychiatric illnesses and so on.

We have recently expanded the scope of the Fund. In addition to funding new social enterprises, existing social enterprises with a good track record can also apply to expand their business and employ more beneficiaries. In January 2012, we had also launched CEF Schools and CEF Young Start-ups, which are initiatives to fund and encourage youth social entrepreneurship.

Beyond grant financing, MCYS also supports local social enterprises through public education, capability building and talent development. To raise awareness of social enterprises, MCYS will continue to work with partners such as the Social Enterprise Association to educate consumers and help social enterprises better profile their products and services. We will also develop a social enterprise website, which will include a directory of social enterprises across various industries, listing of resources available to social enterprises, as well as general information on social enterprise. This will be launched in the second half of 2012.

MCYS will be exploring an internship programme for social enterprises with the local Institutes of Higher Learning (IHLs). This will help to raise awareness of opportunities in social entrepreneurship among youths, and provide first hand work exposure in a social enterprise. We will also look at ways to increase accessibility to training, and to bring expertise from the private sector to assist social enterprises with their business capability.
