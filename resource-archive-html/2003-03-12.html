    The Minister of State for National Development (Dr Vivian Balakrishnan) (for the Minister for National Development): Mr Speaker, the objective of the resale levy is to reduce the housing subsidy enjoyed by those buying their second subsidised flat from the HDB. When second-timers sold their first subsidised flat, they would have cashed in the subsidy that they enjoyed on their first flat. The resale levy therefore helps to discourage over-consumption of public housing subsidies by these second-timer families. This ensures that housing subsidies are fairly allocated between them and first-timer families, who have more pressing housing needs. I must emphasise that flat owners are not required to pay the resale levy if they do not purchase a second subsidised flat from the HDB.

    The resale levy collected by the HDB is used to partially offset its expenditure on home ownership subsidies. The amount collected during the period FY 1992 to FY 2001 can be found in HDB's annual reports. The average amount collected per year is about $170 million. This is a small sum when we compare it to the net amount of $750 million per year that the HDB incurred over the same period to subsidise its home ownership activities.

    The resale levy is graded so that lessees of smaller flats, whom we recognise may have a greater need to upgrade, pay a lower levy rate. The subsidy enjoyed by these families is still attractive compared to the resale levy that they have to pay. And, in fact, this is why second-timer families return to the HDB for a second subsidised flat.

IRIS RECOGNITION SYSTEM


    3. Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) what is the reason for developing and testing an iris recognition system, since Singapore already has the Immigration Automated Clearance System (IACS) which is working effectively; (b) how much does it cost to develop each system; and (c) what will happen to the newly setup IACS.

    The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Wong Kan Seng): Mr Speaker, Sir, Mr Steve Chia asked why the Singapore Immigration and Registration is developing and testing an iris recognition system when the Immigration Automated Clearance System (IACS) is working effectively.

    Let me begin by explaining what the IACS is about. The IACS is an automated immigration clearance system that leverages on biometric technology to identify the travellers. The fingerprint of the traveller is stored in a smart card and then used to match the actual live fingerprint presented by the traveller during immigration clearance. ICAS not only does away with the need for an immigration officer to man each clearance counter, it also enhances security.

    The IACS is not a new system, as Mr Steve Chia seems to suggest. It was first implemented at Changi Airport in 1997. It was extended to Tuas and Woodlands Checkpoints in 1998 and 1999 respectively for bus travellers. The cost of implementing the ICAS is $13 million. We have not implemented the ICAS for motorcyclists after a trial in 1999, because the total clearance time could not meet our operational requirement.

    Sir, biometrics technology is a fast evolving field. International research has shown that iris recognition, which works by scanning and matching the human iris pattern, has a very high level of accuracy and is faster compared to fingerprint matching. Hence, SIR decided to conduct an iris recognition trial for about 3,000 motorcyclists on a voluntary basis at the land checkpoints from June to December

Column: 450

    This will not only reduce manpower, but more importantly, it will also enhance the level of security without increasing the clearance time. The trials at the Woodlands and Tuas Checkpoints cost about $300,000 each. SIR is now evaluating the results of the trial which seem to be quite encouraging.

    Singapore is not the only country that is exploring the latest biometrics technology to enhance security. Under the current security situation, countries such as the United States, Australia, Japan are also on the lookout for advanced biometrics technology for deployment at their checkpoints.
