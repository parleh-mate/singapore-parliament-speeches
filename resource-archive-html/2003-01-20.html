ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

RSS COURAGEOUS
(Collision with ANL Indonesia)


    1. Dr Ong Chit Chung asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence (a) what are the causes of the collision between RSS Courageous and merchant vessel ANL Indonesia and (b) whether follow-up actions are being taken to prevent such incidents in the future.

    2. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence (a) if he will provide a status update of the recent collision between RSS Courageous and ANL Indonesia; (b) what measures are being taken to assist the family members of the deceased, especially on a longer term basis; and (c) how will the Republic of Singapore Navy ensure that this accident will not leave a psychological scar on existing navy personnel and impact the recruitment of potential navy recruits.

    3. Mr Chiam See Tong asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence if he will explain how the collision between the naval vessel RSS Courageous and the container vessel ANL Indonesia occurred in Singapore waters recently.

    The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence (Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam): Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I will take the three questions by Dr Ong Chit Chung, Mdm Ho Geok Choo and Mr Chiam See Tong together.

    Mr Speaker: Yes, carry on.

    Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam: Sir, the collision between the RSS Courageous and the merchant ship ANL Indonesia was a most tragic event for the Navy, the SAF and Singapore. On behalf of the Singapore Government, I offer our deepest condolences to the families of 1SG Seah Ai Leng, 1SG Heng Sock Ling, 2SG Chua Bee Lin and CPL Goh Hui Ling.

Column: 1997

This has been a very difficult time for their families and we share in their grief and sorrow.

    It is to be expected that Singaporeans want to know what happened that night and what caused the accident. The investigation by the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore is ongoing. MINDEF's own Higher Board of Inquiry is still in the process of putting up its report. It would not be appropriate for me to give a full public account at this point. But I will give Members some facts of the situation leading up to the collision.

    RSS Courageous, an Anti-Submarine Warfare Patrol Vessel, was conducting a regular operational patrol in Singapore territorial waters. This is part of the Navy's mission of safeguarding our territorial sovereignty. RSS Courageous was in the waters off Pedra Branca. There has been a spate of violations of our territorial waters at Pedra Branca by foreign state vessels recently. There were a number of violations in the two weeks before the incident, and there have been several since then.

    On the night of the collision, ANL Indonesia was heading out of the Singapore Straits towards the South China Sea. RSS Courageous was sailing in the opposite direction. The two vessels were headed in the general direction towards each other. RSS Courageous had detected ANL Indonesia on radar. ANL Indonesia was also visually sighted by the duty watch personnel on board RSS Courageous. The two vessels subsequently came into a close quarter situation, and both took action to try to avoid a collision. But unfortunately the collision took place. ANL Indonesia rammed into the right rear section of RSS Courageous and a portion of the tail end of the Patrol Vessel was sheared off. This portion included the aft mess, where four servicewomen who were off duty at the time were resting.

    The Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore (MPA) is carrying out an independent investigation into the incident, and MINDEF has given MPA our fullest cooperation in this investigation.

The Commanding Officer and crew members of RSS Courageous have been interviewed by MPA. MPA expects to complete its full investigation by the end of the month and will make known its findings in due course. Separately, MINDEF has convened a Higher Board of Inquiry to determine what happened on the part of RSS Courageous. Until the various investigations are completed, we do not have a complete picture of the events that happened that night. At this juncture, it would not be appropriate for us to speculate on how and why the collision happened, or to apportion responsibility for the collision.

    I would like to reassure the House that MINDEF takes a very serious view of this incident and we will take close heed of the findings of the inquiries and rectify any problems that are identified. In the meantime, the RSN has taken immediate steps to reinforce the existing emphasis on safety and professional training. It is increasing checks on shipboard practices and operational training, and conducting a thorough review of its procedures and processes.

    Safety has always been a matter of high priority for the SAF. There are inherent risks involved in military activity, and we take every precaution to mitigate such risks with intensive and comprehensive training of our people and a thorough system for risk management. We cannot entirely rule out accidents no matter how safety conscious we may be, because other elements such as errors of judgement and mechanical failures sometimes come into play. Whatever the cause of the tragic accident involving RSS Courageous, the SAF will use the findings to make further improvements so that we further reduce the risk of another accident.

VESSELS PASSING THROUGH SINGAPORE
(Security measures against terrorist attacks)


    4. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) whether Singapore, as the world's largest container trans-shipment hub, is a prime target for acts of terror; and (b) what are the measures his Ministry is taking to enhance security and harden the area against terrorist attacks for vessels passing through the area.

    The Senior Minister of State for Home Affairs (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore has the world's busiest port. The Singapore Straits is also one of the world's busiest sealanes with about 140,000 vessels a year passing through it. It is Government's top priority to ensure that incidents similar to the suicide bombing of a French flagship, the Limburgh, off Yemen on 6th October last year do not happen here.

    Sir, although newspaper reports have highlighted the likelihood of terrorists seizing a ship and crashing it into another vessel or into a refinery or port, there is currently no specific intelligence of such an attack in our ports and the sealanes around Singapore.

    Singapore waters have been free of sea robberies inclusive of terrorism for the past 12 years as a result of relentless efforts put in by our security agencies. But we are conscious of evolving developments and are constantly looking for ways to protect ourselves against the terrorist threat. The Singapore Police Force, together with other relevant agencies, has audited and reviewed the security measures at our ports and other landing points. We have introduced enhanced security measures. These measures include ensuring security of our ports' perimeters, controlling access via security passes, checking of personnel, baggage and vehicles, closed-circuit monitoring of port facilities

guards. These are landward security measures.

    On the seaward side, the three lead agencies, ie, the Police Coast Guard, the Republic of Singapore Navy and the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore, work together and have introduced additional security measures to safeguard vessels in our waters. Sensitive areas such as the waters around petrochemical installations and the approaches to them used by tankers are declared out of bounds to all other traffic. YOUNG DRUG ABUSERS


    18. Mr Ahmad Khalis bin Abdul Ghani asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) whether the Ministry has a profile of youths at high risk of becoming new drug abusers; (b) what has been done to prevent these youths from becoming drug abusers; and (c) how successful have these measures been.

    Mr Wong Kan Seng:

    The typical young drug abuser used to be a male heroin abuser below the age of 20. In recent years, however, in percentage terms, synthetic drugs have displaced heroin as the main drug of abuse amongst young abusers below the age of 20. 61% of the young arrested last year were abusers of synthetic drugs, compared to 37% of them being heroin abusers.

    It is difficult to compile a profile of young drug abusers. This is because youths from all backgrounds find themselves tempted to try synthetic drugs. Such "club drugs" are touted as being hip and harmless when, in fact, they are harmful and potentially even fatal.

    The National Council Against Drug Abuse (NCADA) spearheads a wide range of preventive education programmes to spread the anti-drug message to a wide spectrum of our youths. Let me outline some of these measures.

    On the school front, CNB conducts talks to students on the dangers of drugs. CNB has also introduced anti-drug messages into the school curriculum. Visits are conducted to Drug Rehabilitation Centres (DRCs) to warn youths of the dangers and penalties of drug abuse. More recently, NCADA launched a new online preventive drug education tool called David's Diary. This has been well utilised and received by schools.

    NCADA also supports the PAL programme under the Singapore Anti-Narcotics Association which aims to reach out to out-of-school youths. In 2001, an estimated 600 youths received assistance under the programme. 140 eventually returned to school while others managed to find employment.

    As for synthetic drug abuse which is associated with the nightlife and entertainment outlets frequented by youths, NCADA, over the past two years, has partnered the management of clubs to conduct an annual "Clubs Against Drugs" campaign to communicate the anti-drug message to club-goers.

    I am pleased to report that these efforts have been successful. The number of youths below age 20 arrested for drug abuse has declined from the peak of 679 in 1993 to 203 in 2002*, a significant drop of 70%. The problem is under control. Nevertheless, MHA will continue to work with the community and the schools to instill an attitude of zero tolerance towards drug abuse amongst our youths.
