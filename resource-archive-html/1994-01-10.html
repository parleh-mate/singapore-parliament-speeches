PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS



    The President (accompanied by two A.D.C.s) entered the Chamber, accompanied by Mr Speaker, who was preceded by the Serjeant at Arms (without the Mace), the First Assistant Clerk, the Principal Assistant Clerk and the Clerk.

    On the Clerk announcing "The President", the President delivered his Opening Speech.



     The President (Mr Ong Teng Cheong): Honourable Members, the Asia Pacific region is prospering. The economies of ASEAN and East Asia are all experiencing dynamic growth. India and Vietnam are also opening up. Leaders of the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation group (APEC) held a successful informal meeting in Seattle in November 1993, and is

Column: 6

planning the next one in Indonesia this year. Our region is set to play an expanding role in the international economy.

    Singapore is prospering too. We enjoyed strong growth in 1993, and can look forward to further steady progress over the next few years. Our short term issues are well in hand. My Government_s focus is therefore on long term policies to position ourselves for the next stage of nation building.

    In economics, one major thrust is to venture abroad, to develop trade and investment links with neighbouring countries, and tap the vitality and opportunities in a fast-growing region. We will promote a regional outlook among Singaporeans, and remove obstacles that hinder our entrepreneurs and companies from going offshore. Our advantage is that we are familiar with both East and West, with the region as well as the developed countries. By investing in the larger economies around us, we can link our prosperity with their dynamism.

    At the same time, we must upgrade our domestic economy, to avoid losing business to other countries with lower costs and more abundant resources. We need to invest in the education and training of our people, attract a steady flow of talent from abroad, master new technologies, and promote key industries in the manufacturing sector. This will make Singapore a strong base to support our efforts in the region.

    We are restructuring our tax system to stay competitive. The Goods and Services Tax (GST) will come into effect on 1st April 1994. We will then reduce direct taxes in order to encourage enterprise. The comprehensive programme of offsets to the GST will protect the lower income groups, so that the new tax will not leave them worse off.

    The Government's asset enhancement programme will enable every citizen to share the fruits of Singapore's success. The HDB upgrading programme will benefit the 90% of Singaporeans who own HDB flats. The interim upgrading programme will improve the relatively younger HDB estates which need more

Column: 7

than routine maintenance. The sale of two- and three-room HDB rental flats to sitting tenants will enable even more Singaporeans to own their own homes. The sale of hawker stalls and HDB shophouses at a discount to sitting tenants will help hawkers and small businessmen to own rather than rent their commercial properties.

    Last year, the Government paid $200 into the CPF accounts of Singaporeans who contributed at least $500 each themselves to their CPF savings, through the CPF Share Ownership Top Up Scheme. We also sold Singapore Telecom Group A Shares at a substantial discount to our citizens. These measures helped many Singaporeans take a direct stake in Singapore's economy.

    The Singapore Telecom flotation was a resounding success. 1.4 million citizens bought Group A shares. Significantly, people are holding on to them as long term investments, instead of selling them immediately for quick profit. Other major privatisations will follow Singapore Telecom. The next likely one will be the PUB Electricity and Gas Departments in two to three years_ time. The Government will use these privatisations to enhance the assets of Singaporeans.

    Singaporeans are enjoying better health and living longer. But birth rates remain low, and the population is aging. These fundamental demographic changes have serious social and economic implications. We must adjust our policies to prepare for them.

    Longer life spans mean that people need to stay economically active longer, and retire later. Older workers will need retraining to learn new skills and fit into new jobs. So will those seeking to re-enter the workforce, for example, women who had previously stopped working to have children. The Skills Development Fund will expand retraining programmes for older workers to help them upgrade and adapt to new technology.

    We have passed legislation to raise the retirement age to 60. We will gradually raise it further, eventually to 67. However, we must do so gradually, to avoid

Column: 8

suddenly making the labour market inflexible, and to give employers time to adjust to new labour conditions.

    An aging population will need to spend more on health care than a young one. We have established policies to contain health costs, keep high quality basic health care affordable, and avoid the problems besetting health care systems in developed countries.

    Singaporeans must put aside enough for their old age. The CPF provides a sound foundation for this. The CPF Minimum Sum scheme, in particular, caters to members_ needs after they have retired. As people live longer, and as their standards of living rise, we need to revise this scheme to get members to set aside a larger Minimum Sum, beyond the normal annual adjustments for changes in the cost of living. A larger Minimum Sum will provide for a longer lifespan, and enable members to withdraw higher amounts each month after they retire at 60.

    We will raise the Minimum Sum gradually over a number of years. This way, workers who are now nearing 55, and have already planned on withdrawing their CPF balances in excess of the existing Minimum Sum, will only need to make small adjustments. But younger workers must start to make the full adjustment now. The Government will discuss the issue thoroughly over the next few months, and then decide on the best way to proceed.

    Last year, we had our first Presidential Election. We elected a President to safeguard the integrity of the civil service and protect our financial reserves. The elected President will make it harder for a government to go on a profligate spending spree, and squander our hard-earned reserves. But the elected President alone cannot guarantee our well being. Only the people can make sure that the government pursues prudent and responsible policies, by electing honest and competent Members of Parliament to form a good and clean government. For it is the government that creates the environment where people can generate wealth for themselves through enterprise, knowledge, skills and hard work.

Column: 9



    The Government's fiscal policy is to maintain a balanced budget. In good years, we will accumulate modest surpluses, to be held in reserve for economic downturns. Sooner or later we will face a recession like the short one we had in 1985, or like the prolonged current recessions in Europe and Japan. The surpluses will then enable us to reduce taxes appropriately in order to help business.

    However, we should not increase subsidies just because revenues are healthy. Subsidies must be justified by desirable objectives of improving the self reliance or long-term economic independence, not dependence, of those helped by subsidies.

    We will not subsidise services which benefit only those individuals who use them, e.g. PUB utilities. We will subsidise services where there is a public good, and the benefit is enjoyed not just by the direct user but by society at large. Examples are basic health care, education, public housing and infrastructure. Even then, we will avoid across the board subsidies, but focus the most subsidies on those whose needs are greatest.

    We should also charge for at least part of the cost of subsidised services, even though only a small part, so that people will appreciate the value of the service and not waste it. Wherever possible, our welfare schemes depend on self-reliance and savings, and not just on government taxation and direct transfers of income. We encourage self-reliance of the family and the extended family, by allowing CPF top-up for parents and Medisave payments for family members.

    The Government will also use part of its budget surpluses to fund asset enhancement programmes, like the CPF Share Ownership Top Up Scheme, and the Singapore Telecom Group A shares. Such programmes benefit the less well off proportionately more than the better off. However, they are not welfare schemes or entitlements. The Government can carry them out only so long as Singapore does well and accumulates healthy budget surpluses. Furthermore, the benefits must

Column: 10

be related to effort. For example, under the CPF Share Ownership Top Up scheme, CPF members had to contribute $500 to their CPF accounts before they could receive the $200 grant. These conditions will prevent asset enhancement programmes from degenerating into a system of handouts.

    The Government's policy is to rely on the free market wherever possible, except where the free market does not work properly, for example, in health care. The free market matches supply and demand of goods and services through the price mechanism. It channels resources to where they are most needed and valued, and enables the economy to respond quickly and flexibly to constantly changing circumstances. People have the freedom to decide for themselves how to spend the money they earn, and take responsibility for their own choices, instead of having the government step in to spend their money for them. As a result, they have the incentive to give of their best. Free markets promote efficiency, enterprise and growth, as governments the world over have discovered.

    But in Singapore, the free market is tempered by government intervention. The Government intervenes to give everyone equal opportunities and a fair start in life, and to provide a safety net of last resort to the small minority who need it. We do this although it reduces the incentive for Singaporeans to strive individually for themselves, because Singaporeans also need to cooperate with one another to build one nation together.

    Developed countries in Europe, Australia, New Zealand and Canada once proudly called themselves welfare states. Now they have to "revamp" their welfare systems, in order to remedy the disastrous side effects of state welfare: like weakened family bonds, diminished incentives to work, and impoverishment of the country's finances. But turning back is slow and painful. Their problems confirm that we have chosen the right approach. We must stay with what has worked, and not follow the mistakes of others.

Column: 11



    The international environment is becoming more competitive. Fast growth in the region means more opportunities but also formidable challenges. Countries with abundant land, low wages, and hardworking, intelligent workers want to plug into the world economy. They will compete against us for exports and investments. To stay ahead, we must anticipate long term problems, and strengthen our

Column: 12

will to achieve and excel. Every citizen will have his or her stake in our common future. So long as we are prepared to learn and to work our best, we can continue to be ahead. But we need the cohesion and determination to excel. Then we will thrive and prosper.

MINISTRY OF NATIONAL DEVELOPMENT
MR LIM HNG KIANG
Acting Minister for National Development


    MND will continue to improve the quality of the physical development of Singapore through rational far-sighted planning.

Planning and Developing A City of Excellence

    2. The Ministry has revised the Concept Plan to provide a long-range blueprint for Singapore's physical development into the 21st century. The Plan safeguards sufficient land for housing, commercial, industrial and infrastructural developments to support our economic growth. It also sets aside land for recreation and other social amenities to give Singaporeans a better living and working environment.

    3. To translate the Concept Plan into implementation plans for the medium term, URA will prepare Development Guide Plans (DGPs) for 55 local areas over the next five years. DGPs will provide a local perspective to the overall vision of the Concept Plan, showing clearly the development potential of specific areas.

    4. We will progressively release more State land for housing development by the private sector to meet the aspirations of Singaporeans. Similarly, we will make available State land for commercial and industrial developments to support our economic growth.

    5. We will embark on land reclamation works off the north-east coast and at Pulau Ubin and Pulau Tekong, to provide land for future development.

Asset Enhancement

    6. HDB plans to build 27,000 - 30,000 new flats a year over the next three years to meet demand for public housing. It will increase the number of flats to be built under the Design-and-Build scheme to provide more choices.

    7. The Main Upgrading Programme, to refurbish and improve existing flats in older housing estates to match the standards of newer estates, will be in full swing by 1994. The programme covers flats older than 17 years. HDB will announce, in batches, the precincts to be upgraded progressively.

    8. The Government will fully fund an Interim Upgrading Programme (IUP) to complement the Main Upgrading Programme, so that more residents will enjoy the benefits of upgrading earlier. The IUP will enable Town Councils to carry out selected improvements for flats in the 10 to 17 years age band, which would otherwise have to wait more than eight years for their turn under the Main Upgrading Programme.

HDB has introduced smaller 4-Room flats with simpler finishes which are significantly cheaper than the standard 4-Room flats. We will introduce new schemes to help lower income families to buy 3-room resale flats. We will also look into helping tenants of HDB 2-and 3-room rental flats to buy over their flats.

HDB will examine whether a similar scheme can be extended to sell industrial shops to sitting tenants. Many industrial estates, however, are not able to meet expanding needs for electrical power, higher floor loading and increased traffic circulation. Such estates will have to be upgraded or redeveloped before they can be sold.

MINISTRY OF FINANCE
DR RICHARD HU TSU TAU
Minister for Finance


Fiscal Policy

    The Government's fiscal and budgetary policies will continue to lay the basis for sustained, non-inflationary economic growth. They will promote a strong and dynamic private sector supported by an efficient and responsive public sector. They will strengthen national resilience, promote social cohesion and enhance the quality of life of Singaporeans.

2. The medium term budgetary objective is to achieve a balanced budget, with surpluses in good years to stimulate growth in lean years. All operating and development expenditures will be financed from operating revenues.

3. Expenditures. The major expenditures will be on investments in our future, i.e. education, health care, public housing, infrastructure and national security. The size of the public sector will be tightly controlled to free manpower for the private sector. Computerised cost information systems will be put in place in all ministries and departments, and more direct linkages established between budgets and outputs to cut waste and make public sector managers more accountable for their expenditures.

4. Revenues. The introduction of a Goods and Services Tax (GST) on 1st April 1994 will be a major tax reform. The GST rate will be kept at 3% for at least 5 years. Tax on companies will be reduced to 27% to stimulate growth and improve the investment environment. Personal income taxes will also be lowered to encourage effort and enterprise. To offset the impact

Column: 19

of GST on the lower income groups, a package of tax and non-tax rebates, grants and public assistance will be introduced.Nearly all households in Singapore will be better off after the GST is introduced.

5. We will continue to promote the external wing of the economy. Appropriate fiscal measures will be used to encourage Singapore companies and businessmen to trade and invest in the booming and growth economies of Asia. At the same time, we will help these companies to sink deeper roots into Singapore as they develop their overseas operations so as to prevent a hollowing out of our domestic economy.

Monetary Policy

6. Our exchange rate policy will continue to be to contain imported inflation, maintain price stability and support export competitiveness. Domestic inflation has declined from an average 3.1% per annum during 1989-91 to just over 2% per annum in 1992-93. Beyond the initial one-off effect of GST on prices, underlying inflation is not expected to pick up significantly in 1994.

Regulatory Policy

7. The hallmark of Singapore's development into a successful international financial centre has been the creation of a sound financial system with high prudential standards. We will maintain the integrity of our financial markets through continued supervisory vigilance. However, we will also constantly review and update rules and regulations, to sustain a conducive environment for our financial sector.

Privatisation Programme

8. The successful flotation of Singapore Telecom has created a large new class of citizen shareholders. The Government will privatise other suitable statutory boards and list them on the Singapore Stock Exchange. Singaporeans will be given opportunities to own shares and take a stake in Singapore's success.

MINISTRY OF TRADE AND INDUSTRY
Mr YEO CHEOW TONG
Minister for Trade and Industry


Economic Growth

    The Singapore economy grew strongly by 9.8% in 1993. The economic boom in Asia has cushioned us against the weak economic performance of the developed countries, our traditional export markets.

2. We must participate fully in the business opportunities in the region. Our competitive advantage remains unchanged: a competent and hardworking quality workforce and excellent infrastructure. We will focus on developing human resources so as to raise productivity and the quality of goods and services produced.

3. We will expand economic cooperation with countries in the region. Becoming an international business hub with first class services in finance, commerce and communications will help. We will closely support the efforts of the business community to build linkages and participate in key markets.

The Domestic Economy

4. We will be pro-active in our industrial development to counter the increased competition for manufacturing investments. Other developing countries offer vast resources at low rates. They are also catching up on infrastructural development. We must compete on the basis of capabilities. We will develop specific industry clusters to improve our competitiveness. We will invest selectively in strategic industrial projects with private sector partners.

5. In industrial promotion, we will shift from offering ourselves as an attractive offshore production and services centre to making Singapore a regional home base for MNCs and local companies. We will make Singapore a place for high-value added manufacturing activities and related services such as design, R&D and marketing. We will maintain our industrial base and maintain the manufacturing sector's contribution to GDP at around 25%.

6. We will continue to emphasize education and training. A skilled workforce can adapt better to the demands of new economic activities. More places in the universities, polytechnics and technical institutes will be created. Presently 16% of each cohort enters university. 7% of the workforce are graduates. By 2000, 12% of the workforce will be graduates.

7. We will continue to upgrade those who are already working. The National Productivity Board (NPB) will train 100,000 workers through structured on-the-job training by the year 2000.

NPB will continue to target specific skills enhancement programmes like the Training Leave Scheme, the Fast Forward Scheme and the Training Initiative for Mature Employees (TIME) at older workers to help them stay productive. We will continue to seek new ways to help workers participate in training programmes, and to encourage them to continually upgrade themselves.

9. We must produce high quality products and services. The National Productivity and Quality Council will spearhead a National Quality Strategy to inculcate a sense of quality consciousness in our people and implement initiatives to promote quality systems. The Singapore Institute of Standards and Industrial Research (SISIR), our national standards and certification body, will actively promote the SISIR ISO 9000 certification scheme for quality management systems. NPB will also help small retailers to

Enhancing Youth Participation

16. The National Youth Council (NYC) will extend and enhance its range of programmes and activities. It will foster greater youth participation in these programmes and serve as an important channel for youths to express their views and contribute to the nation.

17. PA will complete redevelopment of the Outward Bound Singapore (OBS) by 1995. The new facilities include multi-dimensional rock-climbing walls, training pools, rope challenge courses and seacraft. These new facilities will enable more youths to develop their sense of adventure and mental and physical ruggedness. PA will also enhance the Overseas Youth Programmes.

MUIS

18. The Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura (MUIS) will work closely with the community and other Malay/Muslim organisations to strengthen the religious values of Muslims in Singapore. The Government will, together with Yayasan Mendaki and other interested groups, work to improve the educational, economic and socio-cultural status of the Malay/Muslim community.
