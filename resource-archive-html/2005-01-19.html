     The Acting Second Minister for Finance (Mr Raymond Lim Siang Keat): Mr Speaker, Sir, I would like to respond and clarify certain points that Dr Wang Kai Yuen raised yesterday regarding budget surpluses and the GLCs.

 

     First, Dr Wang makes the point that the Government should tax the economy just sufficiently to cover its expenditure.  This is exactly what the Government has been doing.  In fact, we are one of the leanest governments in the world, by that measure.  Our public spending as a percentage of GDP is about 17%, compared to the OECD average of about 30-40%.  Our operating revenue is about 16%, compared to the OECD average of about 30-40%.  So we are already running a balanced budget which, by international standards, is already very, very lean.  In fact, in the last four years, our operating revenue  -  taxes, fees and charges  -  is less than our total expenditure.  And the only reason why we did not get ourselves into a structural fiscal deficit situation was the contribution that we had from our Net Investment Income (NII) from our reserves.  As Members know, the NII comes from the accumulation of reserves that were built up over the years.  And it is our policy of seeking to maximise the reserves that gives us the latitude and space to lower taxes, fees and charges aggressively while, at the same time, being able to meet our spending needs.

 

     Second, Dr Wang also claims that Temasek invests our reserves; whatever

Column: 304

reserves we have, we give it to Temasek to invest abroad.  And that by doing so, this makes it difficult for young enterprises to survive and prosper, as we are draining away domestic liquidity.  This is mistaken.  It is not the Government's policy to inject surpluses into Temasek for it to invest abroad.  Temasek's expansion abroad has not been fuelled by the injection of Government funds.  In fact, on a net basis, Temasek injects, in the form of dividends, funds to the Government each year.

 

     As to the point he made about young enterprises finding it difficult to survive and prosper, the critical test here for young companies is really the strength of their business ideas, and not just on the availability of domestic liquidity.  In fact, you can secure funding but there is no guarantee that you will be profitable as a company.  So liquidity and profitability are not quite the same thing.  I would like to assure the House that the Government is committed to ensure that there is a full spectrum of risk capital in Singapore so that the conditions are there where entrepreneurs and start-ups will have access to funding so that they can emerge and grow.  Mr Inderjit Singh, on Monday, gave a detailed account of some of the initiatives that we are taking to help to ensure that there is this access to funding.

 

     The third point that Dr Wang made was, an often repeated argument, that the GLCs crowd out the SMEs.  Analytically, this issue has less to do with ownership.  It is not really an ownership issue, but really an issue of size.  The GLCs within the domestic market are big and they provide strong competition to the SMEs.  If, today, we were to change the ownership of the GLCs, the competition for the SMEs will still remain.  The larger issue, the more important issue, is that in today's context, whether you are a GLC or SME, the competition that you face is

Column: 305

not just local, not just regional, but global.  And many of these GLCs are publicly listed and they have shareholders who are leading fund managers and financial institutions, and they hold the management and the board of the GLCs accountable for their performance, and they benchmark that against global peers.

 

     Finally, Dr Wang makes the point that it is not the business of statutory boards to generate surpluses.  I agree with him.  The Government is committed to ensure that we have a competitive tax structure so as to facilitate and encourage initiative and enterprise.  Besides the ongoing efforts to lower our taxes, we will ensure that we lower the burden on businesses, that wherever possible, we would lower fees and charges, set it at cost, unless higher costs are needed to discourage consumption.
