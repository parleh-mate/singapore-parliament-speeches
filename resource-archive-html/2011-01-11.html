BLOCKING PORNOGRAPHIC SITES

(Review of policy)

     1.  Mdm Cynthia Phua asked the Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (a) whether the Ministry will review its policy and consider pre-blocking pornographic sites on the Internet even though the blockage may not be foolproof; and (b) whether the Ministry will consider directing Internet Service Providers (ISPs) to provide the service of blocking pornographic websites for free.

     The Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (RAdm [NS] Lui Tuck Yew):  Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government has taken a practical light-touch approach to regulating the Internet.

 

     Under the Media Development Authority’s (MDA) Class Licence scheme, Internet Service Providers (ISPs) in Singapore are required to block access to 100 websites, which include pornographic sites.  This block on 100 sites is a symbolic statement of our community’s stand on harmful and undesirable content on the Internet.  ISPs and Internet Content Providers are also required to remove objectionable content upon notification by the MDA.

 

     In addition, ISPs provide optional opt-in filtering services that will filter out pornographic and other undesirable websites.  I strongly encourage parents to subscribe to such filtering services to protect their children from undesirable Internet content.  ISPs offer these filtering services at $3 to $5 per month and some filtering services are offered free of charge with certain subscription plans.  There are also filtering software that can be downloaded for free from the Internet.  The MDA is working closely with the

industry to raise greater public awareness of Internet filtering tools and services, and to make them easily available to the public.

 

     In line with the recommendations by the recent Censorship Review Committee, the Government will require ISPs to actively market their Internet filtering services to consumers at the point-of-sale and renewal of their Internet subscriptions.  This would be more beneficial than simply providing the filters for free by default, as parents can learn about and understand the benefits of such filtering services and exercise parental responsibility to protect their children.

 

     We recognise that given the borderless nature of the Internet, it is not possible to block every undesirable website.  As our hon. Member has pointed out, no blocks can be foolproof.  Hence, in addition to regulatory protection and filtering services, public education is critical, especially for our children, on the proper and safe use of the Internet.  The MDA has been working closely with partners from the public, people and private sectors to implement cyberwellness outreach programmes that promote the responsible and discerning use of new media.  The Inter-Ministry Cyberwellness Steering Committee (ICSC) that was set up in 2009 has initiated and funded cyberwellness programmes to promote cyberwellness particularly among our youths.

 

     This multi-pronged approach of regulation and cooperation between the Government, industry and the public has worked well in promoting the responsible use of the Internet.
