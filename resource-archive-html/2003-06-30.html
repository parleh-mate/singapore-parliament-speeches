ASSISTANCE FOR THE UNEMPLOYED


    1. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Acting Minister for Manpower, with the unemployment rate forecast to creep up and perhaps even surpass the levels experienced in the 1998 downturn, will his Ministry consider further interim measures to help the unemployed, at least for a period, subject to suitable means

    The Acting Minister for Manpower (Dr Ng Eng Hen): Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I would like to take Question Nos. 1 to 3 together.

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    Dr Ng Eng Hen: Because the Questions have been asked from various agencies, this reply is with the inputs from the Ministry of Finance, Ministry of Community Development and Sports, Ministry of the Environment and Ministry of Trade and Industry.

    As the Member rightly pointed out, since 1998, as economic conditions worsened, the Government appreciated that more would face hardship. In response, several measures have been introduced to help lower income groups and unemployed workers cope with the difficult times. These measures involved not just temporary financial assistance, but also job matching and placement services, and specific schemes and programmes to facilitate re-entry into the workforce.

    On financial assistance, the Government works closely with Community Development Councils (CDCs) and voluntary welfare organisations (VWOs) to tailor

the assistance to households in need. For instance, the unemployed who have a low household income can apply for interim financial assistance at their Community Development Councils. Short-term financial aid will be granted for six months, subject to a review every three months. In FY2002, 6,700 unemployed Singaporeans were helped by this scheme. 88% of them managed to get a job within three months and become financially independent.

    In addition, families who live in 1-, 2- or 3-room rental and purchased flats who have difficulty paying their rental, utilities or conservancy charges can obtain short-term relief to tide over temporary crises. They may apply for monthly subsidies for rent, utilities and service and conservancy charges, up to a maximum monthly cap of $90 for utilities and $35 for service and conservancy charges. For those lower income families that are unable to pay their utility bills - this is in reply to Mr Low Thia Khiang - PUB has already adopted the practice of thimbling their water supply instead of cutting it off completely since late 2002. This has enabled them to continue to have access to a minimum supply of water for basic needs. PUB will extend this practice until the economic situation improves.

    As for facilitating job placement, between December 2001 and last month, 152,000 visits for job assistance under MOM's Career Link and its Distributed Career Link Network were recorded; of these, 19,600 people were placed into jobs. Recently, we have seen an increase in take-up rate and this is encouraging. MOM also introduced a number of industry-targeted programmes to place and train or convert job seekers for jobs in growth or strategic sectors. More than 1,200 Singaporeans have benefited from such programmes so far. Further, training programmes are in place to ensure that workers' skill sets develop in tandem with the economy. About 80,000 workers have benefited from the Skills Redevelopment Programme.

    Dr Ng Eng Hen: Sir, MOM has been providing retrenchment advisory services to help companies explore feasible alternatives to retrenchment, as Ms Braema Mathi has suggested. We have asked

This Unit monitors retrenchment trend, advises companies on how retrenchments can be carried out smoothly and fairly to the extent possible, provides conciliatory assistance to resolve disputes, disseminates information relating to retrenchment through brochures and leaflets. CENTRAL PROVIDENT FUND
(Liberalisation on use)


    6. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Acting Minister for Manpower will the Government consider liberalising the use of Central Provident Fund (CPF) monies for purposes of (i) supporting overseas education of children who are not able to gain admission into local universities so long as any housing loan owing has been paid up by the CPF account holder; and (ii) subsidising the start-up of a small business (up to a comfortable limit as determined by CPF) as part of the Government's encouragement for self-employment by the unemployed.

    Dr Ng Eng Hen: Mr Speaker, Sir, the primary objective of the CPF is to help members save for their retirement, healthcare and housing needs. While often stated in these simple terms and often repeated, we should be reminded again that our CPF system remains a cornerstone on which we build our social and indeed economic well-being. We should resist strenuously attempts to adulterate the original aims and intents of the CPF system. Yes, from time to time, we may have to recalibrate the amount we put aside to meet these basic needs because there is a trade-off from too high a statutory burden for wages. We risk becoming less competitive and thereby restrict employment creation, we create less jobs. In other words, we should make sure that the majority of the population has enough to cover these basic needs and we should neither under or over-provide.

    The CPF Education Scheme was introduced in 1989 as a special concession to

Column: 2269

help lower-income families support themselves or their children through full-time local tertiary education. To allow CPF savings for overseas tertiary education would be a further deviation from the aims of the CPF scheme. It would also introduce opportunities for over-consumption. For example, an overseas engineering course could cost as much as $58,000 a year as compared to $5,650 at the NUS for the academic year 2003/2004.

    Further, if we did allow it, this availability of the use of CPF savings would put unwelcome pressure on parents to fund an ever-increasing aspiration of children to pursue a tertiary education. I am not for it. I would rather we hold the line than ask parents to do it. There are other avenues of scholarships and loan schemes available to good students who would benefit from an university education, anywhere.

    It is for this same reason of remaining pure to the original and focused aims of the CPF scheme that I am against allowing the early withdrawal of CPF savings for business start-ups. There is no certainty that all start-ups will thrive and indeed data proves the contrary, that the majority of start-ups fizzles out. We run the risk of many members losing their retirement savings for a few to make good.
