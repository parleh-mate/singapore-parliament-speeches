     Mr Lew Syn Pau (Tanglin): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am indeed honoured to be given the opportunity to move the Motion of Thanks to the President.

    Sir, I beg to move,

    That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the Opening of the Second Session of this Parliament.".

    The First Session of the Seventh Parliament was a relatively short but busy one. In his Address to Parliament then, the President had stated that his Govern-

Column: 44

ment's goal was to create a better life for all Singaporeans. A better life meant not just having material needs met but also enjoying meaningful and fulfilling lives. In Maslow's hierarchy of needs, this would mean not just having our physiological and safety needs met but also our needs for love, self-esteem and the esteem of others, and self-fulfilment.

    In the last 18 months, in the words of the President, "We have completed a substantial part of this programme." As we begin our Second Session now, it is useful for us to take stock of our progress and ask ourselves whether we are moving nearer towards our Vision for 1999, ie, attaining the standard of living of the Swiss, and becoming a city of excellence, a society of distinction.

    Economically, we have done well for the last three years, the average growth rate having exceeded 9%. More importantly, labour has been given its fair share of the economic growth. Nominal and real earnings went up by 10.3% and 7.9% respectively. The employers' share of CPF contribution has also been restored gradually. With another 1 1/2% increase in July this year, the employers' share of CPF would have been restored to 16 1/2%, still 3 1/2% short of its long-term goal of 20%. If the economic growth continues to be robust in the next few years, the Government should

Column: 45

restore it to 20% quickly. In this respect, the Government should be congratulated for having kept faith with the workers. Although the restoration has taken many years and is still not complete, it is moving in the right direction.

    The Government's action in this area will help to further strengthen the existing trust between the Government and the people. Trust, like glass, is very brittle and takes time to forge but can be shattered in an instant. This bond of trust and understanding between the Government and the people should not be taken for granted, but should continue to be treasured and nurtured.

    The challenge that we face today is how to maintain our impressive economic growth for the rest of this decade. In order to do so, Singapore must remain attractive to investors. We have been given 1A ratings by BERI as a very attractive place for investments together with countries like Japan, Switzerland, Germany, Taiwan and the Netherlands. In another report on world competitiveness by IMEDE in July 1989, we were rated first out of 10 NIEs. The important key factors taken into account are political stability, Government policies and a good labour force. Although our workforce has been rated the overall best in the world by BERI for 11 successive years, the Singapore worker still lags behind his counterpart in Japan, Switzerland and Taiwan for work attitude, and ranked 14th for technical skills. These two areas have also been identified by NPB's Productivity 2000 initiative as two of five main areas to focus on.

    Our workers are generally better educated than before. In 1989, 51.1% of the workforce had at least secondary education compared to 41.5% in 1980. To-date, 134,000 workers have completed the Basic Education for Skills Training (BEST) while another 22,500 finished the Workers' Improvement through Secondary Education (WISE) programme. These improvements, however, are inadequate to help us attain the status of a developed nation. Of even greater concern is the declining enrolment at our BEST classes. To reverse

Column: 46

the trend, the Government should play a more active role in promoting training, increasing training opportunities through part-time studies in educational institutions and self-learning programmes, giving greater recognition to workers who have succeeded and providing greater financial incentives. In this respect, I am pleased to see the Skills Development Fund setting aside $20 million a year for the next three years to develop better training programmes and facilities. Unfortunately, current tight labour market and high labour turnover have made it increasingly costly for employers to spend on workers' training. Nevertheless, employers should still proceed as the nation as a whole will still benefit as long as all the workers remain in Singapore.

    Positive work attitude has been identified as one of the key factors of higher productivity. Recently, I read in the newspapers with disbelief the case of an architect who resigned from his job so that he could concentrate on watching the World Cup matches. Many others took annual vacation to watch the entire series. Although I have nothing against soccer, I read with concern because this kind of attitude towards work, as if it was a hobby or a pastime, is undesirable, and which we cannot afford as a nation searching for excellence. It must be kept in check, otherwise the entire work ethos which we have been trying so hard to build will be slowly eroded.

    Indulgent parents who have succeeded economically are very much to blame for the poor work ethics of many of our youth. Parents should inculcate in their children from young the importance of working hard for a living and not relying on parental or state financial support, except as a last resort. These concepts should also be promulgated in our schools during moral and civics education.

    The temptation for parents to shower upon their children what they were once denied in their childhood is great, but should be resisted. The situation is aggravated by fewer children parents have today. The danger comes when a younger generation grows up taking many of the luxuries of life for granted; worse still, our

Column: 47

economic success for granted. Parents at home and teachers in schools should continue to imbibe in our youth the values of hard work, and set good examples themselves for their children to follow, otherwise we might never attain our Vision for 1999.

    A better life for all Singaporeans is not just giving people higher wages, better housing and greater comforts of life. As we move up Maslow's hierarchy of needs, creating a better life for all Singaporeans is not just providing the people with their basic physiological needs, such as food, or their safety needs, such as housing and CPF, it is also meeting their needs for love, self-esteem and self-actualization.

    According to Maslow, if both the physiological and safety needs are fairly well gratified, then there will emerge the needs for love, affection and belongingness. The Government has met most of the material wants of a large proportion of Singaporeans. Not too long ago, the Government even ventured into helping to meet the love needs of our young people by setting up match-making agencies, such as the Social Development Unit, the Social Development Section, the Social Interaction Unit and the Social Promotion Section. I am pleased to note that these activities have met with enthusiastic response. In our society, the thwarting of these needs has often resulted in cases of maladjustment and more severe psychopathology.

    The Government can meet the people's needs for self-esteem by being less paternalistic and giving them a greater say in running the country. The President has highlighted the importance of this issue when he addressed it at the Opening of the First Session as well as this Second Session of the Seventh Parliament. I am pleased to note that we have made further progress in this area in the last 18 months.

    The Feedback Unit has diligently sought the views of the public as well as special interest groups on most major Government policies announced. The Unit has also used these occasions to explain Government policies in order to help the people understand them better. Unfortunately, these good intentions have not

    In addition to the Feedback Unit's dialogue sessions, public hearings at Select Committees have also provided opportunities for interested Singaporeans to debate, give their views and attempt to influence Government policies before their formulation. Not everybody, however, will have their views accepted and incorporated in the final policy decisions. But they would have been heard and their submissions and views documented.

    The Feedback Unit sessions and Select Committee hearings have been well received and are seen by the people as a genuine attempt by the younger generation of leaders to listen. Our political democracy, after all, is about representation of the people by the people. During the last debate on the nominated MPs' Bill in Parliament, the First Deputy Prime Minister's concession to incorporate a self-destruct clause in the Bill is an excellent example in accommodation and consensus building. That act of compromise had great impact not only among MPs within this Chamber but also far-reaching ripple effect outside the House. The people outside saw how their future Prime Minister would lead and forge consensus in the face of controversy and conflict. It is an encouraging example which I hope other Ministers will follow under his leadership.

    We should continue to encourage participation on all important issues that affect the people's lives. We know we have succeeded when such participation becomes spontaneous and people speak up candidly, without fear, not with personal vested interest, but because they care about what happens to us and our future.

    In the next few months, Parliament will also see the first few nominated MPs.     Self-esteem and the esteem of others can also find expression in strength, achievements, adequacy, confidence in the face of the world, independence and freedom. These needs can be met not only through greater political participation but also participation in the fields of education, sports, culture and the arts. The Government should give greater recognition, attention, importance or appreciation to participants in all fields of endeavour.

    The satisfaction of the self-esteem needs can be and should be enhanced through less regulation and giving people more choices and options in life. One example where less regulation can give people more utility is the issue of sunken bars in swimming pools. I am told that in Singapore, this is not allowed because the Ministry of the Environment regulates the distance where preparation of food and beverages must be kept away from the edge of the pool. It even regulates the distance flora can be planted from the edge of the pool. The end result of all these regulations is that Singaporeans who want to enjoy sunken bars in swimming pools (and I can assure you that they are quite enjoyable) must visit our neighbouring countries or other developed countries whose standards of hygiene cannot be said to be inferior to ours.

    Another example where self-esteem can be enhanced is the issue of passports for reservists. The restriction on the validity of such passports to two years (formerly just one year) for reservists gives rise to feelings of discrimination, unfairness and resentment which the Government should resolve.

    As we progress and become more developed, the people would also want more choices in the area of education, both for themselves and for their children. Independent schools is one step in this direction. The establishment of a third polytechnic and the upgrading of the Nanyang Technological Institute into a

Right now, too many parents, some of whom can barely afford it, are sending their children overseas for further education, some because vacancies were not available, others because the courses of their choice were not available. Some parents do it in the belief that their children would get a more rounded education. We should therefore continue to upgrade both the number of places in educational institutions at all levels as well as the quality of our educational system so that parents need not spend their hard-earned retirement savings in order to give their children the right start in life.

    Singaporeans are also pleased with the many choices they have now in the area of land transportation, from more expensive private cars to taxis, buses and the MRT. The Government should work on providing the people with the choice of using bicycles, for example, from their housing estates to the MRT stations, by building bicycle sheds at or near the stations. Since the concept of a weekend car was mooted, the people are waiting in anticipation. The Government should expedite its study on the proposal.

    The choices available for the people in telecommunications are also varied and fascinating. However, many choices are still not affordable to the man-in-the-street. The Government's role in this respect is to encourage privatisation and greater competition. The greater efficiency and higher productivity that result should be translated into lower costs for consumers.

    In the area of broadcasting, however, the choices do not appear to be as wide. Allowing the use of personal satellite dishes would increase our people's choices tremendously. The Government's concern over the undesirable consequences of unrestricted external influence may be genuine but unfounded. Besides, our people travel so frequently abroad that any attempt to censor information may be futile. As technology advances and the satellite dishes become smaller and smaller, the cost of enforcement may also become prohibitive.

    Finally, a better life for all Singaporeans includes fulfilling their self-actualization needs. For some, this may mean engaging in cultural programmes and activities. For others, it may mean continuing education through BEST, WISE and eventually a part-time degree in university. If we are to be a centre of excellence and a society of distinction, our people must be able to find outlets to fulfil these self-actualization needs. It makes for happier and psychologically better adjusted people.

    Although many Singaporeans have their physiological and safety needs adequately met, there is still a group among the lower income whose needs may still only be partially met. I am pleased to note that the Government has pledged to help improve their living standards, increase their assets through HDB upgrading and provide for their children's education as well as subsidize their medical fees. The common problems encountered include infestation by rats and mosquitoes. In addition, their safety needs are greatly increased as more and more of their neighbours move away. I would like to urge the HDB to consider expediting these relocation programmes so as not to prolong the inconvenience of the lower income groups affected.

When Vision 1999 was announced as the PAP's election promise during the 1984 general elections, a lot of Singaporeans could not quite imagine what it would be like as it was still many years away. Many had not been to Switzerland and seen how the people lived. The mention of Switzerland probably conjured up visions of snow-capped mountains and white swans in blue lakes. Now, although we are not quite half way there yet, we have come round the bend and onto the long home stretch with a glimmer of light at the end of the tunnel. The reins of leadership will be changed this year, but we are safely on course. Our basic physiological and safety needs have been met. We must now work towards satisfying the people's higher needs and help them to find self-esteem and self-fulfilment. The people are looking forward with confidence and excitement.
