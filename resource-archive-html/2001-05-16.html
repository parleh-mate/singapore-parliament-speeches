DEATHS CAUSED BY MENINGOCOCCAL INFECTION


    4. Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for Health (a) whether he will comment on the two recent deaths in Singapore due to meningococcal infection as a result of close contact with pilgrims from Mecca; (b) how many such infections were there in Singapore over the past two years from 1999 - 2000; and (c) whether he expects an increase in the incidence of meningococcal infection due to the W135 strain.

    The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Health (Mr Chan Soo Sen) (for the Minister for Health): Mr Speaker, Sir, meningococcal disease is a contagious bacterial disease caused by the meningococcus bacteria. It is spread by person-to-person contact through respiratory droplets from the nose and throat of infected people. The disease is caused by a number of different strains of the bacteria, of which the strains A, B and C are responsible for about 90% of all cases. The disease is potentially fatal, with a fatality rate of about 5% to 10%, and this is despite the fact that the disease may have been diagnosed and treated early.

    In year 2000, the World Health Organisation reported an international outbreak of the meningococcal disease related to travel to Saudi Arabia, which was caused by the uncommon strain, W135. In Singapore last year, ie, year 2000, there were eight reported cases of meningococcal disease related to travel to Saudi Arabia, of which five were caused by the W135. In 1999, there were no cases reported at all. So we have cases only from the year 2000.

    In view of this international outbreak, in May 2000, the Ministry of Health and the Ministry of the Environment issued a joint press statement advising all travellers to

Column: 1657

Saudi Arabia to be vaccinated with the quadrivalent meningococcal vaccine. This provides protection against disease caused by the A, C, Y as well as W135 strains, instead of the vaccine against only strains A and C required by Saudi Arabia. The Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura (MUIS) was informed of this advice.

    Although vaccinated persons are protected against the disease, a small number of returning pilgrims who have been vaccinated may still carry the bacteria in their nose and mouth and transmit the infection to close contacts. The Ministry of Health would like to advise that returning pilgrims can reduce transmission to close contacts by simply observing good personal hygiene and by reducing direct close contact and exposure to respiratory droplets.

    So far in 2001, there were four reported cases in Singapore of meningococcal disease caused by the W135. Of the four, three of them were contacts of returning pilgrims from Saudi Arabia who had been vaccinated with the quadrivalent vaccine. The other was a foreigner with a travel history to Africa. Of the four, three patients recovered, while the fourth, unfortunately, developed serious complications and died.

The Ministry of Health and the Ministry of the Environment will again remind all travellers to Saudi Arabia to be vaccinated with the quadrivalent vaccine and advise returning travellers and their close contacts to seek immediate medical attention if they are unwell. All hospitals and medical practitioners have been asked to be vigilant of meningococcal disease in returning pilgrims and their close contacts.

Revision of Banking Secrecy Provisions

    The banking secrecy provisions of the Banking Act protect the confidentiality of customer information. Tight banking secrecy is important to maintaining the confidence of customers in our banking system. However, our present banking secrecy provisions have impeded banks wanting to take advantage of potential operational benefits and savings. For example, banks find it difficult to securitise mortgage loans, or to outsource data processing to third parties. MAS has reviewed the banking secrecy provisions in consultation with the industry. We have considered both the operational requirements of banks and the need to preserve customer confidentiality. The measures set out in the new section 47 strike a careful balance between these two sets of considerations.

These are set out in a new Sixth Schedule, which consists of two parts. Part I will contain many of the existing exceptions, and Part II will contain most of the new exceptions to the secrecy provisions. Objective

    The Commodity Futures (Amendment) Bill introduces new provisions to the existing Commodity Futures Act (Chapter 48A) to address the `bucket shop' problem. The Bill provides for penalties for persons and entities operating without a licence, and enhances the penalties for stipulated offences under the Act.

Background

    `Bucket shops' are firms that entice the public to open trading accounts with them and to grant them wide discretion to trade on the investors' behalf. Instead of trading with the investors' interest in mind, the contractual terms are such that they favour the firms. They may claim to effect transactions when they did not; or `churn', that is, make repeated transactions to earn commissions, without regard to the interest of investors. Investors are enticed with small initial profits but may end up losing their entire investment

    The Commodity Futures Act was first enacted in 1992. It covers commodity futures that are traded at the Singapore Commodity Exchange (SICOM), namely, rubber and coffee only. In the mid-1990s, `bucket shops' caused a stir with mainly spot or leveraged foreign exchange trading. This was stamped out when the MAS amended its Futures Trading Act in 1995. The situation was somewhat controlled until `bucket shop' operators came up with new schemes in the last 2-3 years. These `bucket shops' deal in other commodities such as pork belly, which are not covered under the current Commodity Futures Act.

Amendments to the Act

    The Commodity Futures (Amendment) Bill provides for the Commodity Futures Act to cover all commodities, instead of just rubber and coffee. Apart from commodity futures trading, the Amendment Bill also provides for other forms of commodity trading to be covered. These
