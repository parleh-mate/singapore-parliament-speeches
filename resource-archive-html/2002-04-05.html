OPENING OF PARLIAMENT
(Delay)


    1. Dr Tan Cheng Bock asked the Prime Minister why was the Opening of Parliament delayed for more than four months.

    The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Wong Kan Seng) (for the Prime Minister): Mr Speaker, Sir, there has not been any undue delay in the Opening of the New Parliament. The time between the General Elections and the Opening of Parliament

Column: 464

in the previous and the present instances was similar, being 144 days and 142 days respectively, or just about 20 weeks. In the earlier two general elections in 1988 and 1991, the interval was slightly shorter, about 18 weeks.

    Sir, a reasonable period of time between the General Elections and the Opening of Parliament is necessary to take stock of the tumultuous global and domestic developments, and to prepare policy positions and plans for our new term of office.

    But before the Opening of Parliament on 25th March, the Prime Minister, the Ministers, the PAP Members of Parliament reached out directly to our community leaders and the public to discuss the actions against the terrorists in Singapore, the new terrorist threats that we faced, communal and the tudung issues, as well as economic challenges we face. The Government also very quickly set up the Economic Review Committee to review our economic policies to revitalise the economy, and remake Singapore. So the Government has not been tardy since the General Elections. In other words, we have not been sitting on our hands.

    Dr Tan Cheng Bock (Ayer Rajah): Mr Speaker, Sir, can I ask the Minister whether we could convene Parliament one month after the General Elections, so that Parliament can be operationally ready in case there are urgent matters to discuss? I am sure Members of this House are fully aware that many issues happened after the General Elections, and this House has got no opportunity to discuss all these issues. Therefore, I would request that following General Elections in future, at least a month, we should all be sworn in and this House will be ready to tackle urgencies.

    Another question, Sir, is whether this is going to be the trend.

    Mr Wong Kan Seng: I do not think we can predict what would be the trend in the future because nobody can tell us what is going to happen in the future. While we can set some rules, I do not think we should be so rigid as to say that

Column: 465

we should meet immediately one month after the General Elections (GE). There could be many reasons why we cannot meet immediately. But if there are important or urgent reasons that we have to meet immediately after the GE, then there is no doubt that the Government will seize upon the opportunity to do so. But, here, we cannot commit that in future we will have a sitting one month after the GE. Much depends on the circumstances. I think the less we tie ourselves down with rules, the better it is. We should do what is necessary, what is useful, and what is important.

Many of the programmes that the Government put up before the GE are ideas that we should pursue, and we need time to thrash them out; we need time to think through the major issues, and in this instance, for example, the key challenges we face today are economic restructuring and the new threat of the terrorists. LAND RECLAMATION OFF PULAU TEKONG
(Impact on Malaysia)


    2. Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for National Development what is the situation regarding the land reclamation off Pulau Tekong and its possible impact on the Malaysians.

    The Minister for National Development (Mr Mah Bow Tan): Mr Speaker, Sir, there have been many reports in the Malaysian media alleging that our land reclamation works off Pulau Tekong and Tuas will impact Malaysia in various ways. We are surprised by these recent allegations since

Column: 466

our land reclamation policy is not new. Given Singapore's small size, it has been our long-standing policy to reclaim land from our territorial waters so as to meet the demands on land use. I will address these allegations in my response.

    First, there is concern that our works may encroach into their territorial waters, or somehow shift the boundary between Singapore and Malaysia. We have stated that the reclamation works are carried out entirely within our waters and that there is no encroachment. The reclamation works also cannot change the territorial boundary in the Johor Straits. This boundary was defined by fixed geographical coordinates since 1995 in an Agreement that was signed and ratified by Singapore and Malaysia.

    Another concern raised is that the reclamation works at Pulau Tekong will affect the shipping lane to Pasir Gudang Port by making the shipping lane narrower and shallower. Sir, this is not the case. The existing shipping lane to Pasir Gudang Port lies in the deep water channel between mainland Singapore and the islands of Pulau Ubin and Pulau Tekong. Ships going to our Sembawang Wharves also use this shipping lane. The current reclamation works at Pulau Tekong will not reduce or otherwise affect the existing width of this shipping lane. The shipping lane is not made shallower by the reclamation works. The Maritime Port Authority (MPA) regularly monitors the siltation levels within the shipping lane to ensure that the channel remains deep enough for navigation.

    In the case of Tuas, our reclamation works are about 7 km away from the shipping lane to Tanjung Pelepas Port, so there is no possibility that our reclamation would affect access to Tanjung Pelepas Port.

    I would also like to address allegations that the reclamation works at Pulau Tekong will increase the current flow in the Johor Straits, cause flooding in Johor and affect the fishermen's catches. Hydraulic modelling studies are done for our reclamation works to assess the

Column: 467

impact of reclamation on currents, tidal flows and water quality. They indicate that the reclamation at Tuas and Pulau Tekong will not have an adverse impact on the surrounding waters.

    Furthermore, water quality in the Johor Straits has also been jointly monitored on a regular basis by our Ministry of the Environment and Johor's Department of the Environment (DOE) since 1991. The results show that the water quality in the Johor Straits has remained unchanged even after reclamation works have started.

    Sir, Singapore has acted within its rights and obligations under international law in carrying out the reclamation works. During his visit to Malaysia in March this year, DPM Lee had indicated that if the Malaysians had concerns about the effects of the reclamation, they could send us a note and we would consider it. The Singapore Government is still waiting for a note from the Malaysians to understand their concerns more fully.

    Mr Mah Bow Tan: Sir, to answer Mr Iswaran's question, let me first explain to the House why we reclaim land.

    Singapore is a small country. We are about 680 sq km. We need land. When Habibie said that we are a little red dot, he was not too far off the mark. If you look at the map, you will see Singapore in relation to Malaysia and Indonesia. Malaysia is about 330,000 sq km. So we are less than 1%, about 0.2%, the size of Malaysia. Our population is growing. Our need for land for schools, factories, housing is growing. That is the reason why we have to reclaim land. Without reclamation, we would not have Marine Parade, we would not have East Coast Parkway, we would not have the airport.

    3. Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for Health how many patients, who have overstayed in acute hospitals, have been charged with higher fees since the policy was implemented from March 2002.

Member for Jalan Besar GRC, Dr Lily Neo, has asked how many patients who have overstayed in acute hospitals have been charged with higher fees. It is a policy that the Ministry of Health introduced in March 2002.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, to-date, only one patient has been charged full cost for four days of overstay in Class B2 ward of Singapore General Hospital (SGH). Despite financial counselling given to the patient, she chose not to be discharged home after she was certified medically fit. The reason she gave was that she did not want the hassle of returning to SGH for her daily outpatient radiotherapy treatment.

    Let me take this opportunity to explain our policy on overstayers in acute hospitals. Patients should be discharged from the acute hospitals when it is medically assessed that they no longer require inpatient medical care. Patients who still insist on staying in the hospitals should pay the full cost of their extended stay. In this way, we ensure that the heavily subsidised beds are taken up by patients who require inpatient care.

    In implementing this policy, I would like to reassure Singaporeans that we have put in place several safeguards. First,

Column: 472

our hospitals certify patients that they are medically fit for discharge based solely on medical grounds.

    Secondly, my Ministry is aware that some families may face difficulties in looking after patients at home upon their discharge for various social reasons. Some patients may also require step-down care. As such, the hospitals will assist the patients and their families to arrange for placements in step-down facilities, or home medical, or home nursing, for these patients. Patients and their families will be given sufficient time to make the necessary domestic adjustments. Full cost would only be charged from the seventh working day after the patients have been certified medically fit and after the hospital has placed them in appropriate step-down care facility, if necessary.

Collective ministerial responsibility

    Soo Khoon also suggested that we should let Ministers state their individual views freely before the final decision is taken on a subject.

    We do not let Ministers speak discordantly outside Cabinet because of the principle of collective ministerial responsibility. This principle is another cornerstone of Westminster-style Parliamentary democracy. It is, in fact, enshrined in Article 24(2) of our Constitution. There are two reasons for this principle.

    First, collective ministerial responsibility enhances the accountability of the Government.

    In our Parliamentary democracy, whichever party has a majority in Parliament will form the Government. But the Government, led by the Prime Minister and his Cabinet Ministers, is not at liberty to do whatever it wishes. It is accountable to Parliament.
