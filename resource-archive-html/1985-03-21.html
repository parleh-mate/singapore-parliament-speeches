ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

SINGAPORE COMMAND AND STAFF COLLEGE
(Attendance of Reservists)


    1. Dr Lau Teik Soon asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence why his Ministry is asking reservists to attend the six months full-time course at the Singapore Command and Staff College and whether this will not create undue disruption for both the reservists and their employers.

    The Minister of State for Defence and Trade and Industry (BG Lee Hsien Loong) (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, at present, reservists earmarked to be Battalion Commanders and Brigade Staff Officers attend the part-time Reserve Officers' Staff Course (ROSC). The course spans two years. It involves two in-camp training periods of 40 days each, with a considerable amount of home assignments during the rest of the two years.

    The ROSC can cover only the bare bones of the full-time Singapore Command and Staff Course (SCSC). This cannot be helped because of the current limitation of 40 days of reserve training a year. There is time in the ROSC only to teach procedures and tactics, the essentials for the higher reserve appointments. The SCSC does much more than this. It provides for personal development and rounded exposure, which will also benefit the reservist in his civilian employment. It gives reservists and regular officers on the course a chance to interact intimately, to develop personal ties and camaraderie, even as they learn from one another.

    The six months of full-time SCSC may appear disruptive to employers and reservists, but it need not be. SCSC is good management training. The course develops a man's ability to make decisions within strict time limits, to sort our priorities, to cope with uncertainty and false or incomplete data, and to revise assessments and decisions as new information comes in. The absence of the reservist from his office is offset by what he and his

Column: 1074

employer gain in leadership and management development. Many other far less intensive executive development programmes take more than six months.

    Mindef will continue to conduct ROSC courses to generate the required number of reservist commanders and staff officers. But the reservists and their employers are urged to consider the full-time SCSC instead.

     Dr Lau Teik Soon (Serangoon Gardens): Sir, can I ask the Minister of State whether the Ministry will consider granting scholarships to reservists who wish to study full-time at the National University of Singapore?

     BG Lee Hsien Loong: Mr Speaker, Sir, nearly every male citizen who studies in the National University of Singapore is a reservist. Our scholarships are limited, and our priorities are to grant scholarships to those reservists and also serving officers who will come back and serve with us as regulars and make a, contribution to the SAF. So if any reservist wishes to come back to the SAF and make it a career, and if we think that he is suitable, and he thinks that he is suitable for the SAF, we shall be very happy to take him and to award him a scholarship.

SMALL CLAIMS TRIBUNALS
(Progress)


    2. Mr Goh Chee Wee asked the Minister for Law, since the inception of the Small Claims Tribunals, (a) how many cases have been brought to the Tribunals; (b) what are the nature of disputes; (c) how many cases have been settled by the Tribunals; (d) how long it takes to have a dispute heard and judgement made; (e) whether the procedures are simple enough for ordinary citizens to lodge their claims; and (f) whether there are manpower and other constraints that impede the smooth functioning of the tribunal services.

     The Minister for Home Affairs and Second Minister for Law (Prof. S. Jayakumar) (for the Minister for Law): Mr Speaker,Sir,as the Small Claims Tribunals came into operation only on 1st February this year, it is really too early to make any appraisal.

Column: 1075



    Since its inception, 381 claims have been filed with the Tribunals. These claims comprise disputes in respect of the following: sale of goods cases, 139; provision of service, 235; others, 7. All these claims, Sir, which were filed in the Tribunals, were first processed by the Registrar or the Assistant Registrar of the Tribunals to see whether the claims could be settled through consultations between claimants and respondents without a hearing before the Referee.

    Of the 381 claims, 81 were settled through consultations led by the Assistant Registrar. Three cases were referred to the Referee, of which one has already been settled. The remaining cases are under consideration. Sir, it takes about three to four weeks from the time a claim is filed to the time the parties appear before the Registrar or Assistant Registrar for consultations, and four to five weeks from the time the case is filed to the time the parties appear before the Referee for hearing.

    Compared to the procedures in the Magistrates courts, the procedure in the Small Claims Tribunals is extremely simple. A claimant merely needs to fill a one-page form and service of the claim and other notices is done by the Tribunal.

    Staff of the Small Claims Tribunals Registry will assist, wherever necessary, and services of interpreters are also provided. The Small Claims Tribunals are presently functioning smoothly with the manpower provided.

AYER RAJAH EXPRESSWAY
(Progress)


    4. Encik Abbas Abu Amin asked the Minister for National Development if he will inform the House of the progress of the Ayer Rajah Expressway and details of areas affected for road widening.

     Mr Teh Cheang Wan: Mr Speaker, Sir, the Ayer Rajah Expressway (AYE), when completed, would run from Shenton Way in the east to Jurong Town Hall Road in the west, over a distance of 13 km. It forms the westward extension of the East Coast Parkway.

    The Expressway is being built in two phases. Phase I of the project between Shenton Way and Alexandra Road is now under construction. This stretch of the Expressway will be completed by early 1986.

    The second phase of the Expressway is now being designed. This phase of the project will involve the widening and upgrading of Ayer Rajah Road and Upper Ayer Rajah Road to expressway standards, with flyovers at major road intersections. Construction work is scheduled for commencement in late 1985 and completion by mid-1987.

    Seventeen lots of private land, 11 lots of State land, and some HDB land were affected by the first phase of the AYE. For the second phase of the Expressway, the PWD is unable to furnish details of the land that would be affected, since the design has not yet been finalized. However, a large proportion of the land off Ayer Rajah Road and Upper Ayer Rajah Road are State land and statutory board land which are occupied by Government institutions, such as the Kent Ridge Hospital, Science Park and National University of Singapore. Land required for this phase of the Expressway has already been set aside when these projects were built.

REVIEW OF DIESEL TAX ON TAXIS


    5. Mr Goh Chee Wee asked the Minister for Communications and Information, Second Minister for Defence and Second Minister for National Development (a) whether he will consider reviewing the quantum of diesel tax to be imposed on taxis if, after the fare increases, the demand for taxis drops significantly to the extent of adversely affecting the income of taxi drivers; and (b) if his Ministry will suspend the release of more taxis on the road until the supply and demand pattern has become clearer after the fares revision.

     The Minister for Communications and Information, Second Minister for Defence and Second Minister for National Development (Dr Yeo Ning Hong): Mr Speaker, Sir, the objectives of the taxi fare adjustment are to slow down the excessive demand for taxis brought about by unrealistically low fares and, at the same time, to provide the average taxi driver with an increase in take-home pay.

    The Member for Boon Lay's first question is concerned with the income of taxi-drivers, whether they will be affected by the new fares. We are confident that their income will increase because we believe the demand for taxis will not drop more than 15% after the fare adjustment.

    Sir, I understand the apprehension of the taxi-drivers at the moment. That is why we have asked the Ministry of Finance for a six-month period before the diesel tax is introduced. Let me say that before the period of six months is up, taxi-drivers will be able to see for themselves the full benefits of the increased fare. There is no better argument than for the taxi-drivers themselves to see the increased fare coming in. I am confident that this increase in fare will be the case.

    Sir, having said that, I can assure the House that if the objective of the increased earnings for taxi-drivers is not met, my Ministry is prepared to review the matter. It is always Government's practice to closely monitor a situation after a change has been made. We shall monitor the travel pattern in taxis over the coming months. However, I am confident that the objectives will be met.
