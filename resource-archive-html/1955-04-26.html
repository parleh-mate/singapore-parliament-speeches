DEBATE ON GOVERNOR'S ADDRESS


    The Chief Minister: Mr Speaker, Sir, the,motion that stands in my name is,

    That this Assembly expresses its thanks to His Excellency the Governor for his Address delivered on Friday, 22nd April, 1955.

    On this motion, Sir, it is traditional that the policy of the Government is debated, its point of view is amplified, and the Opposition has an opportunity to attack.

    At this point in our history, Sir, it is perhaps appropriate to look back a little to see how and why our policy has been

Column: 31

evolved the way it has shaped in the Address. Hon. Members have heard that at the beginning Singapore was a village situate where this Assembly House now stands, and today it is the tenth busiest port in the world with a population of more than a million. In between, Sir, we have established a gold-mining town economy. The law of the jungle translated into business terms was our basic philosophy of economy, and the god of money was the god of gods. Our gold, Sir, is our geographical position - we are at the crossroads of the world; and also in the flood of expatriates from many lands who flocked to this territory to make their fortunes. The English expatriates, Sir, whom Sir Stamford Raffles called "merchant adventurers of our nation", using their military and their political power, backed by their economic resources and experience, maintained a rigid and overwhelming domination over the Asian expatriates to whom was relegated the role of worker ants. These are the people who became the merchant princes, the taipans of the East, with the Government officials their willing satellites. They found it convenient, Sir, for the better control of the mass of Asian labour, without which this port would not have developed, to permit some few Asians to achieve prominence in the economic field - and only in the economic field. GOVERNMENT CLINICS AND MEDICAL INSTITUTIONS IN CONSTITUENCY OF TANGLIN


    13. Mr John Ede asked the Minister for Health if he will detail the Government clinics and other medical buildings situated in the constituency of Tanglin; how many are being built within the constituency in 1955; and where they are to be sited.

    Mr A. J. Braga: There are two maternity and child welfare clinics in the constituency of Tanglin, one at Holland Village and the other at Race Course Village respectively.

    No provision is made for further buildings in this constituency in 1955.
