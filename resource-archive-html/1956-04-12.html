    Mr John Ede (Tanglin): Mr Speaker, Sir, there is much to commend in the broad principles laid down in the White Paper: for example, that all schools should receive equal treatment and that all regulations should apply equally to all schools which are financed or aided by Government; that teachers with the same or equivalent qualifications and experience should be paid the same salary; and that every parent should have the right of choice regarding the first and second languages which his children should learn. These principles will be acceptable to the majority. But when we begin to examine the White Paper to see how some of these principles are to be carried out in practice, we find that the Government skates somewhat uncomfortably over the problem. This White Paper, in fact, is the work of politicians rather than of educationists.

    The main problem, of course, is that of language. The All-Party Committee plunged into trouble when they recommended in Section I:

    "That at least two of the following languages, English, Malay, Mandarin and Tamil should be the media of instruction in their respective schools."

    If you are to take that statement literally, it would mean that students should be taught all the subjects in the curriculum in one language and then all over again in the second language. That, I presume, is not what the Committee meant, but that is what they said, and they then tried to extricate themselves from the difficulty by recommending bilingual teachers so that instruction in certain subjects could be switched from one language to another, the students having equal facility in the two languages concerned, presumably, in the main, English and Mandarin. The White Paper skates over the impracticability of this politicians' solution to all our ills, merely stating that there must be a primary followed by a secondary language of the parents' choice, and that students must be able

to leave the primary school with a competent knowledge of one language and a fair working knowledge in the second language. The implication is that if you Malayanise the textbooks and prune the syllabuses of all non-essentials, whatever that may mean, this can be accomplished. But what happens after that? Are two languages to be taught almost side by side in the secondary schools, technical schools and so on? The White Paper, for very good reasons, remains silent on this point.

    Sir, let us get down to what can and what cannot be done in our schools - educationally, I mean, and not politically. Firstly, it may be possible, up to a certain point, to give instruction in two languages in the primary stage, but the shortened hours of work, due to the doubling of morning and afternoon schools, is a terrible handicap. But when you reach the secondary stage, there can only be one medium of instruction if the most important part of a student's life is going to be properly utilised in turning him from a comparatively ignorant lad of 12 or thereabouts into a maturing young man of 16 to 17. There is so much to be crammed into those four years that there must already have been planted in him one language in which he thinks and writes and speaks without hesitation; and this language must be the one through which he will imbibe all that he must learn in his secondary school life. By all means let him have a second language, learn it properly as a subject and pass examinations in it, but it is out of the question to allow it to be used as a second medium of instruction at this stage of his education. It just will not work and the Government, I feel, has not adequately faced up to this fact.
