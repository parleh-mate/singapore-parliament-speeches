VISIT OF MALAYSIAN PRIME MINISTER TO SINGAPORE

(Outcome)

    3. Dr Ong Chit Chung asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs what is the outcome of the recent visit of the Malaysian Prime Minister to Singapore and the discussions that took place between both sides, in particular, how does it impact Singapore-Malaysia relationship.

     The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Prof. S Jayakumar):  Mr Speaker, Sir, the visit by the Prime Minister of Malaysia Abdullah Badawi on 12th January 2004 was his introductory visit, in keeping with the ASEAN tradition of such introductory visits.  PM Abdullah, of course, needs no formal introduction as PM Goh and many other Singapore Ministers have known him for many years.

 

     Being an introductory visit, it was not expected that both sides would have substantive discussions to resolve any outstanding issues.

 

     However, PM Goh and PM Abdullah did use the opportunity to have frank and friendly exchange of views on the state of bilateral relations.  They resolved to put bilateral ties back on the right track.  They agreed that good relations are important, not just for the benefit of Singapore and Malaysia, but also for ASEAN, and that both sides should explore ways to maximise the common areas in order to strengthen our cooperation.

 

     Members will recall that at our sitting on 5th January 2004, I said that there are deadlocked issues from the old era of Dr Mahathir which both sides will have to address and address them in a way that does not reopen the acrimonious paths which we have travelled previously. In the new era, we have to start a new chapter in bilateral relations without being saddled by the deadlocked issues of the old era.

     "I told him frankly how I saw we could move our relations forward.  I've been at this for many years now, discussing issues with Prime Minister Mahathir, and over the years, the issues which were discussed as a "package" and later on as individual items outside the package had ended up in what I told him as "dead knots".  It's like Gordian knots; you can't untie them.  So I suggested that maybe for the future, we should refer these "dead knot" issues to third parties to arbitrate or to adjudicate.  Then we can look forward to co-operation in many areas and if there are new issues, we will try and resolve them before they become tied in knots which we cannot disentangle.

CHALLENGES OF "NEW WORLD"

     4. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) if he will elaborate on the challenges of the "New World" that the Prime Minister referred to in his recent CNN "Talkasia" interview; (b) whether Singapore has embarked on a new economic blue-print or white-paper to meet these challenges; (c) what measures and programmes are being contemplated to inform and educate Singaporeans, especially the workforce, on the opportunities and the threats of the

"New World"; (d) what are the new programmes to foster the growth of local world class companies and will this become our preferred national strategy.

     The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo):  Mr Speaker, Sir, with the end of the Cold War, countries like China and India are re-joining the global marketplace with a vengeance. Technology has facilitated the movement of goods and capital, of people and ideas. With tariffs coming down world-wide, manufacturing for example has become global in nature. For simpler products, China has now become a giant factory for the world. Services are also increasingly outsourced with India becoming a major supplier. Businessmen have to consider different global options carefully before deciding where to invest. Capital has never been more mobile. All this presents Singapore with new challenges, both threats and opportunities. There is no doubt that the competition has become more intense and positioning Singapore well for the New World PM Goh talked about is critical to the well-being of our economy. The Economic Review Committee under Deputy Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong addressed these challenges, and we debated at length the recommendations in this House last year.

 

     On economic structure, our tax and wage systems are being reformed in a fundamental way to make our economy more competitive.  We have been bringing down our direct taxes, liberalising our economy, especially the services sectors, and making the labour market more flexible.  Our FTAs help to expand our external economic space.  We are promoting entrepreneurship and innovation on a broad front, with particular emphasis given to smaller companies.  The education system is

Column: 266

being revamped to prepare our students for the economy of the future.  WDA has been formed to facilitate job matching and worker retraining.  We are also investing significant resources in R&D and the build-up of our scientific manpower.

 

     We have been highlighting these challenges to Singaporeans through speeches, media campaigns and public forums. However, there is no greater incentive to learn than the pressure of necessity itself.  The economic difficulties of the last few years have done more to alter our mindset than any particular publicity exercise.  Just recently, NTUC Secretary General Lim Boon Heng told me how workers now welcome overtime work when only a few years ago there were complaints of too much overtime.  IE Singapore has reported a significant increase in interest by small Singapore companies in overseas business missions. Many young Singaporeans are now prepared to work overseas.  It is right that we are always worrying about the future, because the greatest danger is complacency.  We must not let up on our efforts to alert Singaporeans to the new trends in the world.

 

     We have many programmes to foster the growth of world-class companies.  These are broadly in three main areas: capability development, market opportunities and finance.

 

     For capability development, IE Singapore helps companies with branding and design and the training of manpower for internationalisation.  Smaller companies identified by SPRING to have growth potential beyond Singapore are passed on to IE Singapore.  A*STAR, EDB, IE Singapore and SPRING have also come together to provide comprehensive assistance to companies in the areas of innovation, technology development, operations upgrading and overseas expansion under a scheme called Growing Enterprises with Technology Upgrading (GET-Up).

Second, as for access to market opportunities, we have a number of schemes.  Let me highlight just a couple of them.  The S$30 million iPartners Programme encourages companies to form alliances in venturing overseas.  A market leader becomes the anchor company, drawing on board other companies with complementary products or services.  SPRING has recently launched the Business Alert Internet portal, which alerts local enterprises to some 90,000 business opportunities worth S$120 billion each year, including information on Government procurement opportunities in the US.  Under the US-Singapore FTA, Singapore companies can compete on an equal basis with US companies for tenders of value greater than US$56,000 put out by the US Federal government.   In future, this service will also include tenders by the governments of Australia, New Zealand and other FTA partners.

CHANGI AIRPORT

(Competitiveness)

     5.  Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Minister for Transport what are some of the new measures and steps that his Ministry is taking to keep Changi Airport a competitive and leading airport in terms of (i) uniqueness; (ii) costs; and (iii) service levels in the midst of growing competition from Asian capitals developing their airhubs and establishing budget airlines.

     The Minister for Transport (Mr Yeo Cheow Tong):  Mr Speaker, Sir, as I informed hon. Members late last year, my Ministry's goal is to anchor our existing airline partners here, attract new airlines to use Changi Airport, and position Singapore for the long term.  These fundamentals have not changed.

 

    

Column: 270

Let me describe some of the key measures to achieve these objectives.  Changi Airport has to maintain and even improve upon its unique "Changi Experience" to its passengers and airlines.  Passengers using Changi enjoy speedy immigration and other processes, a lively and bustling shopping mall, friendly service, a wide range of passenger facilities and first-rate infrastructure.  Surveys have shown that passengers do value this very pleasant experience.  To continue to enhance this "Changi Experience", we will continue to innovate and improve our facilities and services. Terminal 2 (T2), which opened in 1990, is now undergoing its first major upgrading at a cost of some $240 million.  The upgrading works will be completed by the end of next year.  The construction of Terminal 3 (T3) is on schedule.  When completed in 2008 at a cost of $1.75 billion, T3 will provide capacity for 20 million additional passengers in an exciting setting.  Plans are also underway for the major upgrading of T1, to revitalise it and enhance the range of services available there.  We expect to begin upgrading works at the end of 2005, and to complete them in 2008.

 

     The upgrading of T1 and T2 will rejuvenate these buildings and give them a modern, fresh look with its own individual distinctive character.  In addition, the current retail areas will be enlarged and re-configured to offer passengers an even wider range of shops and merchandise.  They will be given more interesting and appealing shopfronts.  There will also be better integration of the shops, F&B outlets and new passenger facilities.  All these will give passengers a new and even more interesting and exciting "Changi Experience".  The new T3, with its outstanding architectural and interior design, will also provide passengers a brand-new and enjoyable experience in air travel.

Performance-based approach to fire safety

 

     I shall first touch on the proposed performance-based approach to fire safety. 

 

     The Fire Safety Act provides for the fire safety regulations and effective enforcement of fire safety standards.  Currently, fire safety standards for buildings in Singapore are governed by the Code of Practice for Fire Precautions in Buildings, commonly known as the "Fire Code".  The Fire Code, first introduced in 1974 and last revised in 2002, prescribes specific fire safety requirements and measures without stating the intention or desired outcome. 

 

     The prescriptive approach is rigid and restricts flexibility in building designs, as requirements specified in the Fire Code have to be strictly adhered to.  For example, the types of construction

Column: 335

     Such rigid requirements have led to feedback and requests from building designers and owners for more flexibility.  In fact, some have hired Qualified Persons to propose alternative solutions that still meet the fire safety standards, and sought waivers from compliance with the Fire Code requirements.  Examples of buildings where performance-based solutions have been used include the Singapore Expo and the Esplanade.

 

     In recent years, the trend overseas is towards adopting a performance-based approach towards fire safety.  Countries that have adopted the performance-based approach include New Zealand, Australia, Sweden, USA, UK and Japan.

 

     In view of the feedback and developments in other countries, SCDF consulted the relevant professional bodies, such as the Singapore Institute of Architects and the Institute of Engineers, Singapore, as well as the academia, and has recommended the adoption of a performance-based approach towards fire safety in Singapore.

 

     The Fire Code will be amended to incorporate the performance provisions, which would specify the desired fire safety objectives.  It would then be up to the fire safety engineers to devise fire safety solutions that meet these objectives.  The proposed fire safety engineering approach will not compromise the current high standards and levels of fire safety, and is in line with the Government's approach to reducing over-regulation and be business-friendly.  In some instances, there could be cost savings as well.

Duties and responsibilities of Fire Safety Engineers

Introduction

 

     This Bill before the House seeks to repeal and re-enact with amendments the Accountants Act (Cap. 2), in conjunction with the establishment of the Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority, or ACRA.

Public Accountants Oversight Committee

 

     ACRA will be responsible for administering the Accountants Act. This includes the registration of public accountants, accounting corporations and accounting firms, as well as the regulation of the auditing practice.

 

     Part II of the Bill sets up a Board committee, to be called the Public Accountants Oversight Committee, or PAOC for short. The Committee will comprise representatives from the public and private sectors, including at least one public accountant. The PAOC will assist and advise the main ACRA Board on matters pertaining to the regulation of public accountants. The main ACRA Board will focus on strategic and policy-related issues.

 

Regulatory framework for public accountants, accounting corporations and accounting firms

 

     Parts III to VII of the Bill strengthen the regulatory framework for the audit profession in Singapore.

 

Accounting firms

 

     First, this Bill sets up a Register of Public Accounting Firms, in line with the regulatory framework already in place for individual public accountants and accounting corporations. It allows ACRA to take disciplinary action against errant accounting firms. This is consistent with the practices in leading jurisdictions, such as the US and UK.

 

     Second, the new Accountants Act will require only two-thirds, rather than all, of the partners of an accounting firm to be public accountants. This allows firms that engage in tax or corporate secretarial work, in addition to audit work, to register as accounting firms. As long as two-thirds of the partners in their firms are already registered as public accountants, partners

who do not perform audit work no longer need to register as public accountants.

All accountants, including public accountants, will be free to use titles indicating membership of professional organisations or accountancy degrees. As a safeguard against fraudulent representation, the Accountants Act will prohibit a person, body corporate or firm who is not registered by ACRA from practising as, or holding out to be, a public accountant, accounting corporation, or accounting firm.

 

Registration criteria for public accountants

 

     The registration criteria for public accountants will continue to include qualifications, practical experience and membership in a professional association. With regard to membership in a professional association, the Institute of Certified Public Accountants of Singapore, or ICPAS, will be the only professional association recognised for this purpose, at least for the time being.

 

     ICPAS will transit from a statutory body to a non-statutory society for the accounting profession in Singapore. This is consistent with the practices in other leading jurisdictions such as the UK, the US and Australia, and will be similar to the approach that we have adopted for other professionals, including the architects, doctors and engineers. The Bill transfers ICPAS's assets, liabilities, employees and members to its successor entity. To further facilitate the transition, the current ICPAS Council will serve as the interim governing committee of the successor entity until new elections are held.

Two-tier disciplinary process

     Our preliminary study is that jurisdictions with such laws have found it difficult to enforce such a law.  Successful prosecutions have been few and far between.

PERMANENT RESIDENCE FOR FOREIGN SPOUSES

     2.   Ms Braema Mathiaparanam asked the Minister for Home Affairs in the last three years (2001, 2002 and 2003), (i) how many Singaporeans have applied for permanent residence or citizenship for their foreign spouses; (ii) how many were successful; and (iii) what are their educational and gender profiles.

     Mr Wong Kan Seng:

     In the last three years, the average annual numbers of Singaporeans applying* for Citizenship and Permanent Residence (PR) for their foreign spouses are 2,400 and 10,400 respectively.  About 90% of these applications were submitted by male Singaporeans for their foreign wives.

 

     About 50% of the PR applications and 85% of Citizenship applications received were approved.  The approval rates have been increasing over the last few years.

 

     Of the successful cases, more than half of these foreign spouses have completed at least Secondary level education. About 30% of them hold at least a tertiary qualification (Diploma, degree or other professional qualification).


* Some of these are repeat applications from applicants who were rejected earlier.
