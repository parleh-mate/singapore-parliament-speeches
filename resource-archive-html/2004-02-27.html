HEALTHCARE COSTS

2.  Dr Lily Neo asked the Acting Minister for Health what are the steps taken or will be taken by his Ministry to help contain the healthcare costs in the public sector.

     The Acting Minister for Health (Mr Khaw Boon Wan):  Mr Speaker, Sir, managing medical inflation so that healthcare costs do not run wild is one of my Ministry's priorities.

 

         It is, however, not possible to eliminate medical inflation and keep healthcare cost constant.  This is because healthcare is both labour and capital intensive and it will not be realistic to expect healthcare workers to have no wage increases.

 

Column: 407

       However, it is possible to manage healthcare costs with a view to keep medical inflation within affordable limits.  But it requires major and continuous efforts, from all the stakeholders in the healthcare sector: doctors, hospitals, patients and their loved ones.

 

        To understand this phenomenon, we need to first know the key drivers behind medical inflation.  There are several.  But I will highlight three: shortage of skilled manpower, rapid introduction of new medical technology and rising expectation from patients.

 

        While skilled manpower and new medical technology have led to significant improvements in medical outcome, the results come with a large price tag.  At the same time, increasing affluence of Singaporeans and greater awareness of technological advances in medicine have led to higher expectations from patients in terms of the care and kind of treatment they should receive.  

 

         Against this backdrop, the challenge is to keep pace with medical advancement, but at a rate which the society as a whole is willing to pay.  Inevitably and realistically, some sort of rationing has to be done and patient expectations need to be managed.

 

         It is little wonder that rising healthcare cost is a global phenomenon.  Indeed, relative to many other developed countries, Singapore has fared not too badly.

 

        I will not be able to give a comprehensive account of all our steps in managing medical inflation.  But broadly, I will group them into six major thrusts.

 

        First, emphasise personal responsibility in healthcare.  Government can only provide healthcare services, but good health has to be attained by individuals.

The most effective way to manage medical inflation is to keep Singaporeans healthy.  That is why it is so important for all Singaporeans to lead a healthy lifestyle, thus avoiding illness and hospitalisation.  On this score, as Members of Parliament, we have an important role to play.  I think we should walk-the-talk and be shining examples of what healthy lifestyle is all about.  Incidentally, this Sunday, I will be taking part in a 5-km run to raise money for SSO.  Mr Speaker and our colleagues, especially those in the GPC on Health, are most welcome to join in the run.  We will kill several birds with one stone - promote health and support the arts and sports.

 

        Second, increase supply of skilled manpower, especially specialists and nurses, to match rising demand.  Without this, any prolonged shortage must inevitably lead to excessive wage increases.  We are thus expanding our training capacity, but meanwhile we will mount external recruitment to augment our local supply.

 

      Third, our healthcare providers, especially the hospitals, must squeeze as much productivity as they can out of their resources.  They must strive to produce more with less.  This is a continuous endeavour.

     3.  Mr Iswaran asked the Acting Minister for Education in light of the recent incident at Kent Ridge Secondary School, where students were found with pornographic VCDs and the incidents were reported to the Police, what guidelines on how to handle such incidents have been issued to schools by the Ministry.

positive, proactive approach schools should adopt in handling discipline, as part of their broader efforts to help our young become responsible, thinking citizens.

  

   The guidelines recommend the development of a coherent, whole-school framework for managing discipline.  This comprises measures ranging from the preventive to the corrective  -  in other words, from educating the students to counselling them and to taking disciplinary actions. The guidelines  provide schools with examples of programmes and strategies that they can adopt  to manage discipline in their schools.  They also advise schools on how they can work in partnership with parents, and obtain the assistance of external agencies, such as the VWOs, self-help groups and the Police. 

 

   While the MOE guidelines help achieve  a broad consistency of principles and approaches in managing discipline amongst  our schools, each school has to decide on  its own specific  approaches. This will depend on the nature of its student body and the history of student discipline in the school.

 

   

    Schools also have to decide  on the actions most appropriate in each situation, such as  counselling the students involved, disciplining them within the school, referring them to other agencies for further counselling and help, or reporting the matter to the Police. In particular, a decision on whether to call in the Police has to be made by the principal,  and that will depend on the severity of the offence, the scale of the problem,  and the willingness of the students involved to cooperate. Importance of Sound Public Finance

One of the most important contributions any government can make to economic growth and resilience is to put public finances on a sound footing.  A balanced budget and a trim and efficient public sector will make for stable macroeconomic conditions and a pro-business climate. Only then can the private sector grow and create wealth for the people.

Fiscal prudence has been a hallmark of Singapore's economic management.   Some people accuse us of being too prudent, but we must continue to maintain fiscal prudence, despite stronger spending pressures and tighter budgets.  We will spend more in a downturn if we need to, even if it means going into temporary deficit, as we have done in the past two years. STUDIO APARTMENTS FOR THE ELDERLY

     12.   Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for National Development (a) how many studio apartments for the elderly have been built by HDB since the introduction of the scheme; (b) how many have been taken up; (c) whether his Ministry intends to continue to provide this form of housing for the elderly; and (d) what other measures will be introduced to cater to the housing needs of the elderly.

     Mr Mah Bow Tan:

     Since the launch of the Studio Apartment (SA) scheme in 1998, HDB has built a total of 936 SAs in standalone blocks under the pilot phase. About 96% of the units are currently occupied. The remaining units were returned to HDB by their former owners. HDB will re-offer them for sale to eligible applicants.

 

     In view of the favourable response to the pilot batch of SAs, HDB will be building new SAs to meet the housing needs of the elderly. These will be integrated with other HDB flat types in the same block to encourage more social interaction between the elderly and younger residents. 300 such units are being built at Selective En-bloc Redevelopment Scheme (SERS) replacement sites in Geylang, Bukit Merah and Queenstown. They are currently being offered for sale to SERS lessees and the balance units will be offered to the public at the end of 2004.  HDB will gauge the response to these new integrated SAs before deciding on its future plans for SAs.

 

     Besides SAs, the elderly in Singapore have other housing options. They can buy various types of HDB flats according to their preference and financial situation.  Through HDB's upgrading programmes, HDB flats are made more elderly friendly to enable our elderly to remain in their own homes.  Elderly households with lower incomes can apply to rent a flat from HDB at subsidised rates.  We will continue to regularly review our housing policies for the elderly to ensure that they are relevant and sufficient to meet the needs of our ageing population.

TAXIS

     14.   Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for Transport what is being done by his Ministry to monitor and improve the service of taxi companies and taxis, especially the "On Call" taxis which pick and choose fares.

     Mr Yeo Cheow Tong:

     The Member has raised an issue which is close to the hearts of many taxi commuters. I can assure Members that taxi service quality is also a key concern of my Ministry and the Land Transport Authority (LTA). 

 

     Today, we have some 40,000 taxi drivers driving over 19,000 taxis on our roads. I am pleased to say that on the whole, our taxi drivers have upheld excellent service standards.

 

Column: 507

     Some commuters have complained that taxi drivers are abusing the "On Call" signs to pick and choose fares.  To put this matter in perspective, let me explain that 62% of our taxis are fitted with automatic "On Call" signs.  This means that the "On Call" signs of these taxis are controlled by a central dispatch system, and cannot be manipulated by taxi drivers themselves. As for the rest, we have strict measures to curb such possible errant behaviour by drivers.

 

 

   Members would recall that last June, LTA put in place the Vocational Licence Points System (VLPS) to regulate the conduct of taxi drivers.  Under the VLPS, the penalties (ie, fines and demerit points) for various offences are spelt out in a transparent manner, together with guidelines on suspending or revoking vocational licences arising from the offences. Abusing the "On Call" sign to refuse a customer is an offence that LTA views seriously.  Under the VLPS, taxi drivers who refuse to pick up customers without a valid reason will be fined $100, and given 3 demerit points. A taxi driver's vocational licence will be suspended once he accumulates 6 or more demerit points.

 

 

    I would also like to assure the Member that LTA will continue to monitor the Quality of Service (QoS) performance of the taxi companies closely, under the Taxi Operator Licensing regime, to ensure high standards of service to commuters. 

 

     Finally, let me add that we should not judge all taxi drivers based on the errant behaviour of a few. There have been many reported instances of taxi drivers who have gone the extra mile to provide good service to their commuters.  One example, which was published on the Forum page (19th December 2003), is of a taxi driver who drove all the way from Jurong to Pasir Ris to return a purse and a bag of groceries that a passenger had earlier left behind in his taxi.  Many other grateful commuters have similarly written letters to the Forum page to commend taxi drivers for their service.  I would like to urge commuters to come forward to compliment taxi drivers where this is due, as this is yet another way to spur service improvement in the sector.

     Dr Ng Eng Hen:

     In 2003, a total of 4,034 foreign workers (excluding foreign domestic workers) filed salary claims against their employers, about half the number (7,424 foreign workers) in 2002.  Most of these claims related to salaries owed to foreign workers in the construction industry.  

 

     The NMP asked how many were successful in their claims. I will provide the outcome of those claims dealt with in 2003. As of December 2003, a total of 4,479 foreign workers, including those cases carried forward from 2002, had their claims dealt with by the Ministry.   Of the 4,479 workers, 2,070 of them (46.2%) had successfully settled

Another 591 workers (13.2%) were able to reach a private settlement with their employers following consultation with the Ministry, or withdrew their cases for various reasons such as their claims were not contractually provided for in their employment contracts or covered by the Employment Act. 

 

     Investigations revealed that 1,141 workers (25.5%) had no valid claims.  Seven foreign workers (0.1%) referred their claims for adjudication by the Commissioner for Labour.  The remaining 670  workers (15%) had encountered difficulty in securing their payments either because their employers were facing cashflow problems due to irregular progress payments from the main contractors or  their employers had ceased operations or had become insolvent.  Most of these cases were from the construction industry which was badly affected by the economic downturn. 

 

     As for foreign domestic workers, 231 filed salary claims with the Ministry against their employers, as compared to 214 cases in 2002.

TEN MILE JUNCTION

     16.   Dr Teo Ho Pin asked the Minister for Law (a) how many tenants have terminated their leases at Ten Mile Junction at Bukit Panjang;  (b) what was the  loss incurred by the Government in that development;  and (c) what are the future plans for Ten Mile Junction.

     Prof. Jayakumar:

     Since its opening in 1999, 32 tenancies have been terminated at the Ten Mile Junction or TMJ.  Over the same period, 6 new tenancies were signed.  As of today, there are 6 tenants in TMJ occupying about 40% of the available floor space, including a supermarket and a number of tuition centres.

     The Member has asked what was the loss incurred by the Government in this development.  Since its opening in 1999, TMJ's annual rental revenue less running costs has averaged about $60,000.  As the complex was designed as an integrated mixed development with commercial, infrastructural and residential uses and since the major residential component has not been built, it is premature to estimate what the returns from this investment will be.
