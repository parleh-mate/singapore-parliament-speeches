We can identify its foreign policy by the statements of principles and objectives propounded by the leaders of a country. These are generally very reassuring and infused with high moral purposes, but these do not necessarily constitute the foreign policy of the country involved.

    Then there is another sense in which we can talk of a foreign policy, in that we can abstract it from the concrete decisions and actions taken by a country on specific international issues. From these we can abstract what the country's foreign policy is. In other words, there is a foreign policy of words, of principles, and there is a foreign policy of deeds. For practical purposes, the foreign policy of deeds is a more reliable guide to the intentions of a country than its declared and invariably reassuring principles. I am not saying that the enunciated foreign policy principles of a country are irrelevant or that they do not have any bearing on its foreign policy. They can and very often do. They denote what a country would like to do, though in the world of hard realities it may be forced to do things which do not quite harmonise with its proclaimed principles.

    It is therefore necessary for Singapore, which is venturing for the first time into the jungle of international politics, to bear in mind always these two kinds of foreign policy. We should be on the look-out for possible discrepancies between deeds and words in the foreign policy of a country. Only then can we devise our own foreign policy which is sound and realistic. For example, we should not be lulled into a false sense of security just because a country professes undying friendship and high regard for us or proclaims peace and goodwill to all men. We should satisfy ourselves first that these proclamations accord with what it does.
