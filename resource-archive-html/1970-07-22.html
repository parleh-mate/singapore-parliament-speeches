MARIJUANA
(Measures to end smuggling, peddling and use)


    1. Mr Yeo Choo Kok asked the Minister for Finance whether he has received reports that marijuana is obtainable at some roadside coffee-stalls and other places in the Republic; and what measures are being taken to put an end to the smuggling, peddling and use of the drug.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Police is aware that marijuana (or ganja as it is known locally) is being peddled at some sarabat and eating stalls in certain areas, such as Geylang Serai, Joo Chiat, Tanjong Pagar, Bukit Timah, Wayang Satu. Measures to prevent the peddling, usage and smuggling of this drug have been intensified.

    The Police is responsible for the suppression of internal trafficking and consumption of drugs, while the Customs Department concentrates on international trafficking and smuggling.

    In 1969, 62 persons were arrested for possessing ganja: 96 persons were arrested during the first six months of this year. The Harbour Division of the Customs Department, during the first half of this year, made several large seizures of ganja (cannabis sativa) from

Column: 109

coastal vessels. On shore, Police and Customs squads have also stepped up the frequency of raids on known and suspected places where pushers and smugglers congregate. Seizures of ganja for the first six months of this year amounted to 192.9 pounds as compared to 61.9 pounds for the same period last year.

    A couple of years back, cannabis plants were found to be cultivated even on the island itself, but land patrols by Customs have effectively eradicated these illicit farms. The Primary Production Department has also co-operated by educating local farmers to the danger of cultivating this plant through rural newspapers and other publicity campaigns. The success achieved by the Customs and the Primary Production Departments is reflected in the fact that whilst 548 cannabis plants were seized during the first six months of 1969, only one plant was seized for the first six months of this year. Constant vigilance is also maintained at the Causeway and Airport to prevent the entry of this deleterious drug.

    Recently, a Seminar on Narcotics Control was held in Singapore under U.N, sponsorship. The Seminar recognised that lack of information on the dangers of drug abuse increased the risk of habituation. To counter this, plans are under consideration for the dissemination of information on the dangers of drug-taking.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Education (Dr Lee Chiaw Meng) (for the Minister for Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, since the beginning of this year, 35 in-service training courses for teachers in the four language streams have been conducted.     The bonus scheme had not been finalised when the Member of Parliament for Telok Blangah raised that question in the last session of Parliament. My Ministry is now ready to implement the bonus scheme which will replace the incremental benefit scheme.

    Courses will be conducted from time to time on a variety of subjects designed to increase the quality and efficiency of qualified teachers in Government and Government-aided schools in the four language streams.     To ensure that the greatest benefit is derived from the bonus scheme, teachers are selected for the approved courses under a certain criterion.     Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, the complaints over telephone services raised by the Member for Jalan Kayu may be explained as follows:

    The increasing difficulty in getting through to a telephone number at the first attempt is possible during peak periods, especially between the hours of 10.30 a.m, and 12 noon and again between 2.30 and 4 p.m. If a line is engaged, naturally the second caller cannot get through until the first conversation is over.

    Yet another reason is the capacity of the automatic exchange. The present demand for telephone facilities has far exceeded planning forecasts. Although the Singapore Telephone Board received the World Bank's approval in December 1969 (only six months ago) of a loan of $33 million (US$11 million) for its $163 million Development Plan for six years (1970-75) with a planned target of 14 per cent per annum rate of growth, this has now been found to be inadequate and the revised 1971 Development Budget which was originally fixed at $29.4 million has just been raised by 30 per cent to $38.3 million.

    In respect of the second complaint that there "appears to be an increasing number of erroneous calls being put through", as we are operating an automatic telephone exchange, such errors can largely be caused by the person who dials the telephone. Therefore, it is the human error rather than the fault of the automatic exchange.

Sometimes the Singapore Telephone Board will not even be able to meet the request for telephones, because the applicant happens to live in an area which is rather inaccessible and where it would be just uneconomic to provide such a service.
