    The following Question stood in the name of Mdm Ho Geok Choo -

    1. To ask the Minister for Education in light of the report that there has been an increase in the number of students seeking counselling services in our schools, will his Ministry provide (i) some of the analyses and findings for the students wanting to seek such services and (ii) the number of such students with percentage breakdown and their profile.

    Assoc. Prof. Chin Tet Yung (Sembawang): Question No. 1.

    The Minister for Education (RAdm Teo Chee Hean): Mr Speaker, Sir, from the counselling cases reported by schools, about 6% of students have been referred for counselling, with secondary school students accounting for slightly more than half of these 6%. Of these 6%, 4% were seen by school personnel, including Teacher Counsellors, and 2% were referred to the Ministry of Education's specialists and external professionals. The two main types of problems referred for counselling were discipline-related problems, eg, truancy and late-coming, and emotion problems, eg, coping with grief and loss issues, boy-girl relationship problems.     Order read for Resumption of Debate on Question [9th July, 2002],



    "That this House, being aware that Singaporeans are suffering from the effects of a recession, mindful that many Singaporeans, in particular the unemployed, are having difficulties making ends meet and agreeing with the Prime Minister that this is not the best time to increase bus and MRT fares, (a) urges the Public Transport Council to review its decision on the increase in public transport fares; and (b) further urges the Government to take steps to ensure that the prices of public services are not unreasonably increased during these difficult times.". - [Mr Tan Soo Khoon].



    Question again proposed.



    Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong (Tampines): Sir, according to the latest Annual Report of the Public Transport Council, the PTC is driven by the mission to balance the need for public interest to be safeguarded and for public transport operators to remain financially viable. With the fare hikes, coming at a time when Singaporeans are hit by job losses and pay cuts, the feeling on the ground is that the PTC has put the interests of the public transport operators over that of the public. The PTC has lost sight of the larger social mission of public transport.

    Most Singaporeans use public transport today because cars are priced out of their reach, with the Certificate of Entitlement and the taxes on road usage. Public transport is the only affordable means to get to where they need to go, to work, to the market, to get home. It gives them access to employment and, hence, income. As an MP, I sometimes hear jobless residents not wanting to take jobs far away from their homes because it means taking two buses and an MRT ride, which means waking up at 5.30 am to get to work on time. The cost of travelling itself can also be a disincentive, especially if the take-home pay of the job offered is already low.

    The pricing of public transport has a major impact on the lives of disadvantaged people. The household expenditure surveys conducted by the Department of Statistics consistently show that it is the lower-income group, especially those in 1- to 2-room flats, who spend most on public road transport, compared to the other groups. Indeed, the latest survey shows that public road transport takes up 6.8% of the household expenditure of those living in 1- to 2-room flats. This is higher than the average 4% for all households. Any price changes will hit the lower-income most. And we also know that this economic downturn has hit them the hardest. The Government says that it helps them with the Economic Downturn Relief Scheme, the New Singapore Shares and other such financial schemes. But these are often once-off affairs, or for the EDRS, up to three months. But the psychological blow, not to mention the financial impact, of a price increase for something as basic to them as public transport is felt on a regular basis.
