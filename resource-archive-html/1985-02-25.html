ELECTION OF SPEAKER
(Tribute)

3.26 pm

     The Leader of the House (Mr S. Dhanabalan): May I, on behalf of the Members of this House, extend to you our congratulations on your re-election as Speaker.

    Sir, you have by now been elected for the fifth time as Speaker of Parliament. You are now in your 1&h year as Speaker, the longest of any Speaker of our Parliament. I do not see your record being broken for a long time to come.

    You have presided over proceedings in this House and ensured order, decorum and fairness in our business. You have helped in ensuring that debates shed light and not merely generate heat. Some may say that this makes for a dull Parliament. Parliamentary debate is a serious

Column: 8

business. We are here to explain, probe, question, defend and criticise laws and policies. What we do here affects the daily life and fortune of our citizens to an extent that many of our citizens may not realize. Thus, while wit, humour, anger (real or feigned) all serve to liven up debate, Parliament is not an institution to provide entertainment. You have been greatly instrumental for upholding the high prestige and distinction of this House.

    Sir, I cannot let this occasion pass without a few words about the Member for Tanglin who was Leader of the House for 16 years. He has quietly and with a deceptively casual air been a firm Leader of the House. At the last meeting before the dissolution of Parliament, he; asked:

    "Have you not had enough of me as Leader of the House?"

    Well, the Prime Minister has heard his hint to want to step down as Leader and I now find myself as the Leader. We shall miss Mr Barker as Leader of the House, but Members can rest assured that he will continue to give us the benefit of his experience and wisdom. It is not by chance that I asked for him to be seated next to me.

    There is a clearly perceptible change in the complexion of this, the sixth, Parliament compared to the fifth Parliament. We have in the Government benches 24 new Members. We have also two Members from the Opposition parties, the first Opposition Members to be elected in a general election since 1963. To all Members I can do no better than to repeat the advice of the previous Leader of the House, and I quote: "No Member will stray from the straight and narrow path if he always lets his conscience be his guide."

    Members should speak their minds freely, having the courage to agree and disagree without playing to the gallery. Ministers and office holders expect the most rigorous examination of their policies. They will have a ready ear for suggestions and constructive criticism. They do not claim infallibility. But Members must expect that we will give no quarter in defending policies we believe to be vital for our nation's well-being.

ADJOURNMENT TO A LATER DAY
(Motion)


     The Leader of the House (Mr S. Dhanabalan): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I seek your consent and the general assent of the Members present, under Standing Order 33, to move that, at its rising today, Parliament do stand adjourned till Friday, 1st March, 1985, at 11.00 am and that at the sitting on 1st March, 1985, the moment of interruption shall be 4.30 pm.

     Mr Speaker: I give my consent. Is it the pleasure of hon. Members that the Motion be moved?

     Hon. Members indicated assent.

     Mr S. Dhanabalan: Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move,

    That, notwithstanding the provisions of Standing Order No. 1, at its rising today Parliament do stand adjourned till Friday, 1st March, 1985, at 11.00 a.m. and that at the sitting on 1st March, 1985, the moment of interruption shall be 4.30 p.m.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, this motion is to change the present sitting times of Parliament in the first instance for the meeting on 1st March, 1985, but I shall be giving notice for a motion to change the sitting times for future sittings of Parliament. This change is necessary because we intend to give television coverage of Parliamentary sittings. Parliamentary proceedings will be recorded in full and every evening of the day when Parliament sits, a 30 to 45-minute programme of the day's business will be telecast by SBC. These telecasts will be in English and in Chinese,

    The general public have been informed about Parliamentary proceedings through the press and through SBC newscast. Except for ceremonial occasions, like the President's Address this evening, the people do not have a direct visual and aural impression of Parliamentary debates. When it is possible for TV to bring events occurring in remote places under very difficult circumstances right into the living room, Parliament cannot continue to ignore taking advantage of TV to communicate with the people. Parliaments in other parts of the world have already started using television. Television coverage will inform Singaporeans not only of the content of Parliamentary proceedings

Column: 12

but also the cut and thrust of debate or the lack of it. Reading speeches in cold print is not the same as actually seeing and listening to an argument or an idea being put across. The printed word cannot convey the sincerity, passion and quality of the argument. To produce the kind of programme that we envisage, SBC will need time to edit and summarize four to five hours of parliamentary business into a 30 to 45-minute programme.

    Under the present Standing Orders, the House rises at 8.00 pm or 8.30 pm if there is an Adjournment Motion. This gives no time for SBC to edit and produce a programme to go on the air between 9,00 pm and 10.00 pm. It is therefore necessary for sittings of Parliament to be changed. It is proposed that Parliament sits from 11.00 am to 1.00 pm with a break from 1.00 to 2.30 pm for lunch; sittings will resume at 2.30 pm and finish at 4.30 pm. If there is an Adjournment Motion, sittings will carry on to 5.00 pm.

    Sir, we may have to modify these times in the light of actual experience. When we are more certain about the most suitable time, we can change the Standing Orders.

     Mr Speaker: Does any Member want to join in the debate?

     Hon. Members: No.

    Question put, and agreed to.

    Resolved,

    That, notwithstanding the provisions of Standing Order No. 1, at its rising today Parliament do stand adjourned till Friday, the 1st of March,1985, at 11.00 am and that at the sitting on 1st March, 1985, the moment of interruption shall be 4.30 pm.

PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS



     The President (Mr C V Devan Nair): Honourable Members, the political leadership of Singapore has passed on to a new team of younger leaders. In Cabinet, 9 out of 12 were elected after 1968. In Parliament, of the 77 Government MPs, 61 were elected after 1968, and 24, or nearly one-third, in the recent General Election.

NEW GOALS

    My Government's first duty is to safe-guard what Singaporeans have achieved.

    Its second duty is to improve upon what has been achieved by building a better society.

    In pursuing these higher goals, my Government will pay particular attention to those who find it difficult to keep up with the pace of change - the 15 to 20% in the lower income group and the aged.

    For the next five years, my Government's objectives will be:

    1. to reach an accord on the character of the society people desire, and involve the young in working towards its achievement;

    2. to strengthen the driving force of our progress, namely, the urge each citizen has to develop his potential to the fullest and to achieve his rewards in accordance with the worth of his contribution to our economy or society;

    3. to target for economic growth rates of between 5-7% per annum, and an increase in the productivity rate of between 3-5% per annum;

Column: 14



    4. to aim for 80% home-ownership by 1989, as the next stage towards a 100% home-owning society by 1999;

    5. to help working mothers by providing well-run nurseries, creches and play schools for their young children;

    6. to provide support for a socially-active life for the aged.

Greater Participation by the Population

    My Government will seek the participation of our people both the young and the old in these tasks. Everyone has an honoured place in Singapore society. Everyone has a valuable contribution to make. All must participate in the big decisions which will re-shape their lives.

    We are examining ways to devolve control of the now towns by investing authority over the management in town councils or estate committees, which may include Members of Parliament and community leaders. Such autonomy should lead to speedier response to local problems and needs. Shared responsibility is good for neighbourliness and community solidarity. Each new town can develop its own distinctive character.

Equal Opportunities For All

    A better life for all requires the creation of more wealth. Governments do not create wealth. They can only create the pre-conditions for fruitful efforts. These pre-conditions are an efficient and honest administration, a strong defence, political stability, social and racial harmony, good industrial relations, and a free market economy. Under these conditions, equal opportunities to every citizen for education and health will lead to the maximum individual effort and the highest team spirit.

100% Home-Ownership Society

    81% of our population live in public housing. 60% own their homes. Few countries in the world have achieved such a high percentage of home-owners. We can do better. At the end of five years, we are confident we can increase the percentage of HDB home-owners in our population to 80%. By then, close to 90% of our population will live in HDB flats.

Column: 15



    The HDB will build 180,000 units in the next five years. About 113,000 out of 500,000 HDB households are in old rental flats. Only 17,000 of the rentals have registered to buy their own. The rest may need help to buy their flats. This Government will offer self-help schemes which with an extra effort in thrift and self-discipline can enable them to own their homes. In the next five years, we hope annually 7,000 to 10,000 families who now rent flats can become home-owners.

Working Mothers and Upbringing of Children

    Women have become important contributors to our economic growth. There is a danger that their children may not receive enough parental care and guidance to grow up as moral and upright citizens.

    Foreign maids whose social backgrounds are different from ours, cannot be the imparters of cultural values to our young. The best care comes from parents and grandparents. The next best are creches and child-care centres under the charge of professionally trained child-minders. An important part of community development for a generation of working mothers is to provide high standards of care for pre-school children.

Column: 16



Special Attention for the Aged

    Our senior citizens, those over 65 years, need better facilities to keep them socially active and healthy. Their numbers are increasing. The young must adjust their attitudes to make our senior citizens feel that they are participating members of society, and not left to be objects of charity, outside the mainstream of life.

Achievable Goals

    As we achieve our goals we shall develop and mature as a society. Our people can be encouraged to find satisfaction and fulfilment beyond material pursuits. A more vibrant cultural environment can make living in Singapore more fun. This we can achieve if we set higher goals for ourselves, if we dare to achieve. Our performance in the last 25 years gives us confidence that we can succeed.

ADDENDA TO PRESIDENTIAL ADDRESS AT THE OPENING OF
THE FIRST SESSION OF THE SIXTH PARLIAMENT ON

MONDAY, 25TH FEBRUARY, 1985

A BETTER SOCIETY


    Our people have achieved their basic needs. Now we can set out to achieve higher goals.

    Our people can enjoy a better quality of life by 1999. There will be no unsewered households, slums or environmental pollution. Every family will own a home for we shall help the 15-20% lower income group to do so.

    Roads will be free-flowing. The MRT will be complemented by an efficient bus and taxi service. A new city will rise from the reclaimed land at Marina Bay.

    Advances in technology will bring about a new future of comfort and convenience. We are about to enter a new age of instant information.

    There will be ample facilities and support for music, the arts, for sports, recreation and leisure activities. Life in Singapore can be fun.

    Singaporeans will participate in and actively run their own community services. They will manage their own towns. They can place the collective interests of society above self.

    Singaporeans will be knowledgeable, well read and rounded. They can be gracious as well as socially disciplined and confident. They can find fulfilment in non-material pursuits. They can have the attributes of a mature people: cohesive, tolerant, charitable and compassionate. The old will not feel unwanted and the young will grow up as upright citizens.

    Singapore can be a cultivated society. The pursuit of excellence, especially in our schools and universities, will be the distinctive hallmark of Singaporeans. Talents of the people will be developed to the fullest. For that we need a creative, stimulating and culturally vibrant environment.

    The Government will be honest and effective. The people will be hardworking, keen to upgrade their skills and to increase

Column: 18

their productivity and to be rewarded accordingly.

    Based on our record, we can, if we work together, realize these goals of a better society.
GOH CHOK TONG
First Deputy Prime Minister and
Minister for Defence

MINISTRY OF NATIONAL DEVELOPMENT


Building a City of Excellence

    We will plant more freeflowering trees and shrubs to make the city cool, green and bright with flowers. Fruit trees will be cultivated in public housing estates and private condominiums. More play-grounds, neighbourhood parks and town gardens will be developed. We will build four new regional parks at Bukit Batok, Sembawang, Pasir Ris and Eng Neo.

    We will build a city served by expressways for free-flowing traffic. In the next five years, the Public Works Department will build the Bukit Timah Expressway (linking the north to the south), the Ayer Rajah Expressway (connecting the east to the west) and the Central Expressway (joining Seletar to Jurong through the urban corridor).

    We will prepare the plans and lay the foundations for the creation of a new city on the reclaimed land at Marina Bay while continuing the redevelopment of the existing Central Area. We will preserve our heritage in conservation and rehabilitation schemes in areas of architectural and historical merit.

Homes for Everybody

    Ours will be a society where all families not only own their homes, but have the chance to upgrade to bigger and better homes. We will introduce schemes to assist and encourage the 15-20% lower income groups to become home-owners.

Column: 19



    By the end of the decade, public housing will accommodate close to 90% of the population. In the next five years, the HDB will build 180,000 units, mostly of bigger flats to meet the demand for upgrading.

    The HDB will work closely with grassroots organizations and residents to promote and foster the development of cohesive communities in our housing estates.

Construction Sector

    We will continue to upgrade the construction industry by promoting mechanisation and system building. A corps of well-trained, highly skilled and well-paid construction workers will be built up for the construction industry.
TEH CHEANG WAN
Minister for National Development

MINISTRY OF DEFENCE


Introduction

    Singapore's defence policy is peace through strength. We will be friends with any nation which respects our interests and our right to exist. Our defence doctrine

Column: 21

is Total Defence encompassing the whole population and all aspects of defence: military, economic, psychological, social and civil.

    For the next few years, MINDEF will be concentrating especially on the operaional readiness of the SAF reserves, the absorption of new hardware, and the non-military aspects of Total Defence.

Readiness of Reserves

    The reservist components of the SAF have been building up steadily. Not only are there more and more reservists, but in an emergency, the troops can be mustered, armed and committed to battle in the shortest possible time.

    Of particular concern will be the training of units and the grooming of commanders and staff. The time available to train a reservist is short - at most only 40 days a year. The SAF will be systematically exploring better ways to train reservists and reserve units. Training circuits will be designed to practise individual military skills in the minimum possible time. training villages will be constructed so that units can conduct realistic and worthwhile field exercises in land-scarce Singapore.

    Many reservists have attended upgrading courses together with regulars. They now occupy most of the key appointments in their units. More will follow.

Absorption of New Hardware

    Despite our many full-time National Servicemen and reservists, the SAF can never expect to prevail in combat by force of numbers. Our troops must be better trained, better led, and better equipped. We must become a capital and skill-intensive armed force, and not a manpower-intensive one.

    We must exploit high technology to the full. The SAF will have to assimilate each individual piece of technology, like the E-2Cs, and fit them all together into a potent winning combination. To do this, the SAF will build up its manpower base, preparing its technical staff and developing its operational doctrines, and exercising its combat units.

Column: 22



Total Defence

    As we master the problems of military defence, we shall give more emphasis to the non-military aspects of Total Defence.

    The Public will be involved in practical ways. More and more HDB residents will learn the basic skills of civil defence - first aid, firefighting and rescue work. Ways will be worked out, and exercised, to allow the SAF to tap the vast resources of the civilian economy - vehicles, equipment and services - and put them to military use should the need arise. Total Defence is much more than a slogan.

Conclusion

    We have no enemies today, but we cannot wait for some to appear before starting to prepare our defences. A threat to our security can develop within months. An armed force takes many years to train and nurture. That is why we have been building the SAF ever since Singapore became independent. That is why we are secure today, and why we shall continue to strengthen our defences in the years to come.
