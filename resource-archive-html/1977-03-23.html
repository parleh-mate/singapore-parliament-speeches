    1. Dr Lau Teik Soon asked the Minister for Foreign Affair's if he will give his comments on the story reportedly carried in The Sunday Times of London that the detention of the local journalists and the statement about the involvement of the Editor of the Review have "seriously jeopardised its (Singapore's) international reputation", and whether in his view foreign correspondents resident in Singapore have

any role to play in the foreign relations of the Republic.

    The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Mr S. Rajaratnam): Mr Speaker, Sir, I think the hon. Member is referring to an article by a Mr William Shawcross in the Sunday Times of London of 13th March. In answering the hon. Member's question I am in danger of breaching a new convention which certain sections of the Western Press have developed in regard to criticism. If a pressman attacks a politician, that is free speech. But if the politician concerned hits back, then a hue and cry is raised that freedom of the press is in utter jeopardy. A point that has been lost sight of in recent press criticisms about the Singapore Government is that though we have, as a

Column: 1523

Government, often exercised our right to tell off certain foreign pressmen when we think them misguided, we have never arrested or deported any of them or banned their publications merely for critical and even ill-informed articles about Singapore.

    We have done so only when, from being mere observers and reporters, pressmen have become active partisans in our domestic affairs or, as a number of them did, got involved with subversion and the espousal of its cause.

    Sir, with regard to Mr William Shawcross' observation in the Sunday Times that the arrest of Singaporeans who have admitted to working with the communists has blackened Singapore's international reputation, it has about as much substance as that paper's efforts a few years ago to sell the Slater-Walker team to the world as model of 21st century entrepreneurs.

    Admittedly, Sir, the reputation of the Singapore Government has sunk rapidly in the eyes of a handful of British publications, like the Sunday Times, The Times, the Far Eastern Economic Review and the Economist. But these publications do not represent world opinion and our reputation with some 1 50 nations is as sound as ever before.

    Some hon. Members: Hear, hear!

    Mr Rajaratnam: No government or international financiers have told us officially or unofficially that they have lost confidence in the Singapore Government as a result of the recent arrests in Singapore. So I do not know what Mr Shawcross is talking about.

Member means when he asks whether "foreign correspondents resident in Singapore have any role to play in the foreign relations of the Republic."     My guess, Mr Speaker, is that the Western newsmen sensed from the evidence given to them that an intense grilling of Mr Arun and Mr Raman, far from weakening the Government's case, would only have strengthened it. Had there been an intense cross-questioning by foreign pressmen of Arun and Raman, what would have in fact emerged was that we had detained a journalist who had knowingly played along with the communists, and a lawyer with affiliations with British Marxists, not averse, according to Mr Raman, to using violence to bring down the PAP Government.
