SALE OF CHILLED MEAT IN WET MARKETS

(Provision of chillers)


    1. Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for the Environment whether his Ministry will facilitate the sale of chilled meat by retrofitting wet markets with chillers for use by meat sellers.

    The Minister for the Environment (Mr Yeo Cheow Tong): Mr Speaker, Sir, as announced on 23rd April 1999, chilled fresh meat supplied to wet markets will have to be sold in display chillers. This will ensure that the meat sold in the wet markets is safe and hygienic.

    Stallholders selling pork in wet markets will have to install display chillers by 1st November 1999. Those selling poultry have one year to put in their display chillers and those selling mutton and beef 18 months to do likewise.

    The stallholders will have to buy the display chillers themselves. The cost of the chiller, at between $3,000 and $6,000, is not exorbitant. The investment in a chiller is a business decision that stallholders need to make if they wish to remain in the business of selling meat.

    So far, three finance companies have informed my Ministry that they are prepared to offer loans of between 80% and 95% of the cost of the chillers, with repayment over one to four years and interest rates of between 3.5% and 4% per annum.

    Four suppliers of chillers have also agreed to allow interest-free instalment payments.

    The Government on its part will spend some $6.5 million to upgrade electricity supply in the wet markets to enable the chillers to operate.

    Dr Lily Neo (Kreta Ayer-Tanglin): Sir, may I ask the Minister if ENV would assist those meat sellers in our wet markets who

have financial difficulties to retrofit and install the chillers in their stalls?

    Mr Yeo Cheow Tong: Sir, as I mentioned just now, those stallholders who are facing difficulties paying for the chillers can obtain loans to buy the chillers. They can get the loan from the finance companies and, as I mentioned, some of the companies are prepared to offer 4-year loans at fairly attractive interest rates of 3.5% to 4%.

    Dr Lily Neo: In such circumstances, would the Minister consider installing the chillers for them and allowing them to pay by instalments?

    Mr Yeo Cheow Tong: Sir, it is best for the stallholders to decide which particular chiller best meets their business volume. If they are prepared to pay by instalments, they might as well get the loans directly from the finance companies. Assuming they get a bigger chiller which costs $6,000 and they pay over four years or 50 repayments, I think the monthly instalment is very affordable.

    Dr Lee Tsao Yuan (Nominated Member): Could the Minister give some ballpark figures of the cost of the chiller or the monthly instalments vis-a-vis the monthly rental or the average income of these stallholders?

    Mr Yeo Cheow Tong: Sir, in fact, the plan to move to the cold chain has been in discussion for quite some time and the recent episode of JE and the Nipah virus highlighted the need for us to speed up this plan. Why do we need to go to the chilled meat and the cold chain system? Under the old system, the animals were slaughtered and they were straightaway brought to the markets, which means there was very little time for the proper analysis to be carried out as to whether that pig was in any way infected. By having the chilled meat system, it allows the abattoirs to hold on to the meat after slaughter and it ensures that the blood and the internal organs can be properly analysed. By having the chilled meat system we will in fact be in a better position to assure Singaporeans that the meat that they buy from the markets is well inspected and found to be free of diseases.

    Mr Mah Bow Tan: Thank you. Sir, in March this year, during the Budget debate, Prof. Chin and Mr Chay Wai Chuen raised similar queries about the penalties for ERP violations.     I then informed the House that the Land Transport Authority (LTA) was working out a scheme to allow motorists who commit these ERP violations to pay the ERP charges and an administrative surcharge. I am pleased to announce that the LTA has finalised the details of this scheme.

    Under the revised system, motorists who forget to insert their Cashcards into the IUs or have insufficient balance in their Cashcards when passing through ERP gantries, will be required to pay the applicable ERP fee plus an administrative surcharge within two weeks of the violation. It also makes motorists more conscious of the cost of motoring as the deduction is immediate each time the vehicle passes under a gantry. Finally, the

In other words, and this is in response to Mr Tay's question, the ERP has had a positive impact on business, not only in direct cash terms but also in terms of faster travel times that lead to overall lowering of transport costs. This ultimately helps us to maintain our competitiveness as an efficient international manufacturing, logistics and business centre.

    As our vehicle population expands, in order to cater to growing demands from business and to meet the aspirations of our people, the challenge to keep congestion at bay will become increasingly more difficult. Our choices are limited. We can build more roads, but we will have to sacrifice land for housing, schools, industries and recreation. This is not an option for most Singaporeans. We can restrain car growth. Again, this is not an option if we are to give young Singaporeans and those who can afford their chance to own a car. Finally, we can manage the traffic on our roads, by encouraging better use of our limited road space through ERP. This is the preferred option. With ERP, motorists are more careful about using their vehicles. They have several choices. Do they have to drive? Can they take a bus or train? Can they share a car with friends or colleagues? Or can they switch their travel to a less congested time? If they really need to drive at peak hours, they are free to do so, and in return for a small fee, they enjoy a smoother and faster ride.

State Of Discipline In Schools

    The overall state of discipline in our schools is good. Serious cases of breach of discipline are infrequent.



    MOE has implemented the Behaviour Tracking and Analysis (BETA) system in schools since 1995. The system collects data on the state of discipline in individual schools. For primary schools, misdemeanours such as not doing assignments and disruptive behaviour, and attendance-related offences, constitute 84% of all the recorded offences. In the secondary schools, 74% of the offences were attendance-related, namely, late-coming and truancy.

    The BETA records show no significant statistical change in the offence rates from 1995 to 1997. "Offence rates" refers to the number of offences per 1000 pupils. The data for 1998 show the number of serious offences such as gangsterism and possession of weapons remaining close to that of 1997, and the number of cases of substance abuse falling from 272 in 1997 to 40 in 1998. However, the incidence of smoking has risen from 14 per 1000 pupils over the three years 1995 -1997 to 19 per 1000 pupils in 1998.

    The overall offence rate for boys has remained stable over the years, but a worrying trend is the increasing offence rate for girls in the secondary schools. Though lower than the male offence rates, the female offence rates have increased by 6% to 9% per year over the last 4 years, going up from 300 in 1995 to 456 in 1998.

    A committee chaired by the Senior Minister of State for Education, Dr Aline Wong, was set up in May last year to look into the discipline situation among girls in schools. The committee is finalising its recommendations.

    

Measures To Address Discipline in Schools

    The Ministry's philosophy is that to care is to discipline. Schools by and large take a whole-person approach to discipline. They have many programmes and support structures to help promote discipline. Besides the creation of the posts of Discipline Master, Head/Pupil Welfare and, most recently, Operations Manager, there is also the Voluntary Special Constabulary Scheme where teachers assume the mantle of law enforcement officers to help reinforce an orderly school environment.

    Teachers have been equipped with basic counselling skills and many spend much time counselling errant students. Such efforts are complemented by programmes like life skills and peer mediation.

    However, discipline of students is not just the responsibility of schools. Parents must remain primarily responsible for their children, particularly outside of school hours. While the Ministry does not have data on ill-discipline outside school, schools do work closely with the Neighbourhood Police Posts through the NPP-School Link-Up Scheme. Many schools also mount before-and-after-school programmes to keep their students gainfully engaged in school activities.

INCIDENCE OF CRIME BY ILLEGAL IMMIGRANTS



    14. Mrs Lim Hwee Hua asked the Minister for Home Affairs whether the incidence of crime by illegal immigrants or overstayers has risen over the last twelve months and what steps are being taken to keep such incidence in check.

    Mr Wong Kan Seng:

    The incidence of crime by illegal immigrants and overstayers has remained low. In 1998, the number of crimes committed by immigration offenders was about 250. This is about 0.5% of the total number of seizable offences reported in 19981. Of the 1929 foreigners arrested for crimes in 1998, 210 (10.9%) were illegal immigrants and 46 (2.4%) were overstayers. These immigration offenders were mostly involved in minor offences, such as shoplifting.

    It is not practical to devise specific measures targeted only at the immigration offenders involved in crime. Hence, my Ministry's approach is to deter and take tough enforcement action against all immigration offenders in Singapore. In this regard, we have enhanced the penalties in the Immigration Act. Immigration officers have stepped up their vigilance at our checkpoints, the Coast Guard has intensified its patrols and surveillance of suspicious activities at sea, and the Police has conducted islandwide raids at suspected places of refuge. This has led to a record high of 23,000 in the total number of illegal immigrants and overstayers arrested in 1998.

    The Home Team will maintain its enforcement efforts to ensure that the immigration offender situation and the associated crime problems remain firmly under control.

FOOT AND MOUTH DISEASE


    2. Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for National Development whether there is any danger of the foot and mouth disease spreading to Singapore from Malaysia in view of the recent outbreak of the virus in Negri Sembilan.

    Mr Lim Hng Kiang:

    The foot and mouth disease (FMD) is a serious viral disease affecting cloven hoof animals such as cattle, pigs, sheep and goats. However, FMD does not affect humans and is, therefore, not a health risk to the general public. I would like to clarify that the Foot and Mouth Disease is totally unrelated to the human disease called Hand-Foot-and-Mouth disease.

    We have managed to keep Singapore free from FMD over the years even though it is endemic in the region. To maintain Singapore's FMD free status, the Primary Production Department (PPD) allows cloven hoof animals to be imported from FMD free sources only. As for Malaysia, PPD only allows imports from accredited farms in the FMD free areas. Upon arrival in Singapore, the animal consignments are inspected for signs of disease. In addition, animals, such as dairy cattle, brought in from FMD free countries for breeding and production are required to go through at least 30 days of on-farm quarantine in a specified isolated area.
