LEASES OF AGRICULTURAL LAND


2.35 p.m.

    The Prime Minister: Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move.

    That this House considers that in any legislation in respect of the grant of leases of agricultural land, provision should be made in the case of working coconut and rubber estates, whereby the tenant should be given the option of either a lease at economic rent or a licence to occupy the land for specific user such as rearing of poultry or pigs, with the right to possession and enjoyment to the coconut or rubber remaining in the landlord.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the House will recall that two Bills - the Agricultural Leases Bill and the Control of Rent (Amendment No. 3) Bill - went to Select Committee and, arising out of the representations in the Select Committee, a curious phenomenon has come to light on which considerable ingenuity and time will have to be spent to work out the rights of the interested parties.

    Out of an estimated 30,000 acres of the Master Plan's agricultural belt, an estimated one-third, or 10,000 acres, is

Column: 1632

covered by coconut and rubber plantations. In nearly all these plantations there is, de facto, a situation where the landlord has let out not the land but a licence to tenants or squatters to rear pigs, poultry or fish, whilst retaining possession and tapping his rubber or collecting his coconut. Therefore, the provision to allow a tenant to acquire a lease from the landlord at an economic rent, subject to certain maximum safeguards on behalf of the tenant, will work an obvious injustice in that, first, the tenant never had enjoyment of either the coconut or the rubber, and his rent (the base upon which the maximum economic rent is tied), because he did not enjoy the fruits of the coconut and the rubber, was a nominal one. Therefore, to grant the tenant a lease would be to exclude the landlord from possession and enjoyment of his crop - be it rubber or coconut - and give the tenant something which he never had, never was entitled to, and never expect-ed to be entitled to. Therefore, it is being considered now - and I am recommending this to the House - that in any legislation in respect of the grant of leases of agricultural land, provision should be made, in the ease of working coconut and rubber estates, whereby the tenant should be given the option of either a lease at economic rent, or a licence to occupy the land for specific user such as the rearing of poultry or pigs, with the right to possession and enjoyment to the coconut or rubber remaining in the landlord. In other words, what we hope to achieve would be, in effect, to give the tenant a licence which is irrevocable for a specified term of, say, 12 years, but the licence will specify the user to which the licensee is entitled. I mean, if he has been rearing chickens or pigs, he will continue to rear chickens or pigs, but the landlord will continue to collect his coconut and rubber. The alternative is for the tenant to pay the whole economic rent and he collects the rubber and the coconut.

    I am unable to say that we can produce legislation which will please all parties, but I do suggest that if we are to do justice in this case, this is the only way in which we can ensure

Column: 1633

that conditions are created in which farmers are given if not security of tenure, then security of user of land for specified purposes to carry out activities in agriculture - be it poultry-farming or pig-rearing - and, at the same time, allow the landlord to continue to derive that interest in the land which he always had, namely the collection of rubber and coconut.

    It is the intention of the Government to allow the two Bills to lapse. The House will be prorogued sometime in the near future. In fact, it was prorogued last year after the May sitting. But for various reasons which have to do with legislation, we have taken this sitting in June. After the prorogation, it is hoped that new Bills will be introduced which will incorporate these new clauses. By tabling this motion, I am, in fact, inviting a debate - I hope a constructive one - as to how we can equitably work out our policy of giving encouragement to agriculture but under terms and conditions which would not impose an unnecessary radical change in the proprietary position of land-ownership or the enjoyment of the fruits of the land.

    I would like to mention here that several representations have been made in the Select Committee, a number of which are unacceptable to us as the Government. I can only say now that we cannot hope to push such legislation through without, in principle, accepting the fact that there is an interference of the right of ownership to land, and that by this legislation we are, in fact, interfering with the absolute right of ownership to land. There have been representations from private estates and the Land and Housing Development Association saying that to push through these new provisions would, in fact, be to foist statutory tenants on landlords, that they had bought land which had no tenants prior to 1947, and that therefore they would be deprived of a reasonable expectation of their ability to get rid of their tenants as and when they wished to build. I am afraid that, in principle,

Column: 1634

we cannot consider all such representations if we are to pursue the objects of the Bill, but we can, to a certain extent, meet periphery representations as to the workings of the Bill.

    For instance, representation has been made on the point that for the Bill to have the phrase "economic rent" and, at the same time, to tie down economic rent to a maximum of a certain percentage over present rent is, in fact, a contradiction in terms. Logically it is. Certain amendments in this respect to make it less illogical but nevertheless still practical are being considered. On the side of the tenants, they also would not like any maximum mentioned, not for the reason that they think the maximum is not to their advantage, but that they feel there would be a tendency for landlords always to insist on the maximum in negotiating for leases. Both the representations - the one from the landlords that the maximum rent being tied to present rent and economic rent is illogical, and the other one from the tenants that maximum rent should never be mentioned at all because it would incite landlords to demand the maximum - are being considered, and provision will be introduced in the new Bill which will, we hope, meet the objections from both parties insofar as they can be reconciled. There was also representation that there should be not just one arbitrator who is a Government official, but that there should also be a tribunal - one representing the landowners and one representing the tenants. And it is proposed to introduce a provision whereby in cases where the arbitrator deems it necessary, he can call upon the services of the tribunal to assist him. ROAD TRAFFIC BILL
Order for Second Reading read.

2.51 p.m.

    The Minister for Labour and Law (Mr K. M. Byrne): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have His Excellency the Yang di-Pertuan Negara's recommendation for the Road Traffic Bill, and I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Hon. Members will know that in accordance with the policy of the Government, action is being taken to integrate the functions of local authorities in Singapore with those of the Government. In regard to the Bill which is now before the House, we are concerned with the integration of the various functions and processes which relate to the administration of road traffic in general and to the office of the Registrar of Vehicles in particular. The responsibility, Sir, for these functions and processes is at present shared between the Government and the local authorities. It will in future lie solely with the Government. This entails a transfer to the Government of the responsibility for all those functions previously exercised by the City Council and the Rural Board.

    The existing Road Traffic Ordinance provides for the registration and licensing of motor vehicles and is not concerned with the registration and licensing of non-motor vehicles. At present, it is the City Council which is the licensing authority for the purposes of registration and licensing of motor vehicles, and the executive officer responsible for the various details of registration and licensing is the Registrar of Vehicles, an officer of the Council. The functions relating to the licensing of drivers is at present vested in the Police. The

Column: 1637

existing Road Traffic Ordinance also contains provisions governing the use by vehicles of Singapore's highways, as, for example, the provision of parking places; the emplacement of road traffic signs; and the restrictions placed from time to time on the use of roads. In connection with the operation of these provisions, the highway authority is the City Council, and the Police, who have the general responsibility for the operation of these provisions, are required to exercise such responsibility in consultation with the City Council.

    The existing Municipal and Local Government Ordinances contain provisions for the registration and licensing of non-motor vehicles such as carts, trishaws and bicycles. There are also provisions regulating the conduct of personnel who drive such vehicles, as well as provisions which regulate the operation of public service vehicles, as, for example, hired cars or taxis as they are commonly known.

    Now, Sir, may I briefly state the situation which will arise on integration? The City Council will cease to exist. Responsibility for the Registry of Vehicles will he transferred to the Deputy Prime Minister's portfolio. The Municipal Ordinance and the Local Government Ordinance will be repealed, and it will be clearly necessary for alternative legislation to be enacted to replace all those provisions, under which the Registrar of Vehicles functions at present, in respect of non-motor vehicles and of public service vehicles. I must explain, Sir, that although the direct effect of integration will be the transfer of the Registry of Vehicles from the City Council to the Government, there have also been other operations which, although they have no direct bearing on integration, have nevertheless been undertaken in the context of such integration. First, the responsibility for the issue of ordinary driving licences will be transferred from the Police to the Registrar of Vehicles. Secondly, whereas at present the responsibility for enforcement of the provisions of the Road Traffic Ordinance is shared between the

Column: 1638

Police and the Registrar, this responsibility will in future devolve wholely on the Police. Thirdly, the function of compounding traffic offences will be transferred from the Police, who now exercise it, to District Judges and Magistrates.

    The Minister is empowered to make rules to prescribe, inter alia, the offences which may be compounded and the amount which may be offered by way of composition in respect of any such offence. It is proposed to set out in such rules a scale of amounts which may be offered by way of composition for such offences, and, within the limits specified, District Judges and Magistrates will have discretionary power to offer the offenders such amounts as they think fit with a view to compounding the offences in question. For any compoundable traffic offence which is repeated, the scale of composition rates will he increased for a second offence and for third and subsequent offences.

    I would add, Sir, that in amending the Road Traffic Ordinance the opportunity has been taken to bring certain traffic offences in line with the legislation in the United Kingdom and in the Federation of Malaya. It is also proposed to introduce a system of service of summonses by registered post, which is designed to conserve Police manpower. Further, the provisions of the Road Traffic Ordinance are to be extended to two areas, the Naval Base and Pulau Bukom, with the concurrence of the authorities concerned.

    At this juncture, I would like, for a moment, to revert to the subject of the registration and licensing of non-motor vehicles which was previously carried out under the provisions of the Municipal Ordinance, but which, as the Bill now before the House envisages, will in future be carried out under the provisions of the Road Traffic Ordinance. Now, Sir, a perusal of Part I of the existing Road Traffic Ordinance will show that the Ordinance already provides satisfactory machinery for the

Column: 1639

collection of revenue by way of registration and licence in respect of motor vehicles. It seemed both simple and appropriate to adapt this machinery for the collection of revenue in respect of non-motor vehicles as well. Under the existing provisions of the Municipal Ordinance and Local Government Ordinance, non-motor vehicles (except bicycles) were either registered annually or taxed annually, or both. In future, it is proposed that these non-motor vehicles (except bicycles) will be treated exactly in the same way as motor vehicles, that is to say, registered once and for all and licensed annually. The method adopted in the Bill was generally to remove the word "motor" from the provisions of Part I of the Road Traffic Ordinance, which, ipso facto, makes that Part applicable to all vehicles. The level and quantum of licence fees will be designed so that these fees will be equated to the tax previously paid.

    The licensing of public service vehicles - taxis and buses - is a quite separate exercise, and a new Part II has been introduced in the Bill for this purpose. This new Part reproduces the corresponding provisions of the Local Government Ordinance.

    With regard to the transfer from the Police to the Registrar of Vehicles of the function of issuing driving licences, the deletion of references to the Deputy Commissioner of Police, who was the previous licensing officer, and the substitution therefor of references to the Registrar of Vehicles in sections 13 to 21 of the Road Traffic Ordinance (which correspond to clauses 13 to 21 of the Bill) has given effect to the intention.

    The definition of roads in section 2 of the Road Traffic Ordinance has been amended, as hon. Members will observe on a perusal of the definition of "road" in clause 2 of the Bill. A "road" now includes any road in Pulau Bukom and any road within the Naval Base. As I explained earlier, the effect of this definition will be to extend the provisions of the Road Traffic Ordinance to these two areas.

Members to clauses 24 to 28 of the Bill which relate to reckless or dangerous driving; driving without due care or reasonable consideration; causing death by reckless or dangerous driving; driving while under the influence of drink or drugs; and being in charge of a motor vehicle when under the influence of drink or drugs. These provisions, as I mentioned earlier, have been brought into line with Federation and United Kingdom legislation. It is worthy of note that a specific penalty is now provided for causing death by driving a motor vehicle recklessly or at a speed or in a manner which is dangerous to the public.

    Sir, as hon. Members can see from the Explanatory Statement, this Winding Up Bill seeks to wind up the Certificates Fund constituted under the Victory Savings Certificates Ordinance, 1946.

    The principal Ordinance was passed on 10th October, 1946, and under its provisions the Certificates Fund was set up to borrow money to provide for the

Column: 1658

reconstruction of capital works after World War II. Certificates were sold at a discounted value of $15 each, and when they reached maturity in 10 years from the date of issue, holders were entitled to $20 per certificate. Redemptions were allowed any time before the expiry of 10 years and the surrender values of a unit certificate were graduated from $15 when less than one year to $20 on completion of 10 years. Certificates were sold from 1946 to 1950, when sales were terminated. The total sale of the certificates was only $809,542.50.

    Some of the buyers of the certificates started handing in their certificates for redemption within a year after the certificates were introduced, and after 1948 the amount of certificates presented for redemption was more than the amount sold. As stated, the date of maturity of certificates is 10 years from date of issue. Since the last certificates were sold in 1950, all the certificates sold have matured and no certificates will earn interest after 1960. Therefore, it is now proposed to wind up the Fund.

    The Bill contains provisions which are largely procedural. Clause 5 provides that not later than six months after the coming into operation of the Ordinance, the assets of the Fund shall be converted into cash and used to settle all outstanding principal and interest payments on unredeemed certificates. Notice is to be given to holders of unredeemed certificates so that they will have ample warning that the Fund is going to be wound up.

    If there is any dispute regarding any payment or repayment, the Minister may pay the amount into court or may make other appropriate provision for them.

    After all claims have been paid or provided for, the balance, if any, is to be paid into the Consolidated Fund.
