(Paper Misc. 3 of 1979)

2.30 p.m.

     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Education (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move the Motion standing in my name,

    That this House having noted the Report on the Ministry of Education, 1978, contained in Paper Misc. 3 of 1979,

    (1) supports the revised structure of education which will allow each pupil to Study at a pace suited to his learning capacity and which will make him as bilingual as he can be,

    (2) approves flexibility in implementation particularly to accommodate the wishes of parents,

    (3) recommends periodic adjustments and changes to the revised structure which experience and advice of principals and teachers show to be desirable in the long term interests of pupils.

    The Report of the Education Study Team and the Prime Minister's comments on it have received extensive coverage in the local media. It is therefore not necessary for me to summarise the findings which I assume are familiar to Honourable Members. Instead, I want to explain the basic approach which the team adopted in examining a complicated and vexatious problem. We are not specialists in education. While education has its professional aspects about which the layman is not well informed, education impinges on everyone. To start with, we have been through the mill ourselves, though at different times in the past. Nearly all of us have children, grandchildren or relatives in schools. We are not therefore totally ignorant of the problems of school children.

    Our approach, the starting point of our investigation, or what in mandarin is aptly called chu fa dian (    ), is to find out the reasons why problems keep on recurring year after year, despite the efforts of successive Ministers of Education to solve them. The chu fa dian (    ) of the Education Study Team is best summarised in the motto of the London School of Economics: Rerum Cognoscere Causas - to know the causes of things.

    There is nothing special about this. Doctors use it regularly on patients - if their diagnosis is correct, the patient is cured unless the ailment is incurable, such as lung cancer. Engineers and mechanics regularly observe the motto of the London School of Economics when they have to repair a motor car, a piece of machinery or equipment.

    But in human affairs, to recognise the causes of things does not come naturally, especially in important matters of state. Take, for instance, what is probably the most important economic and social problem facing the world today - inflation. The causes of inflation are simple. Yet government leaders in some industrial

Column: 6

countries pretend otherwise and believe that they can fight inflation by persuading trade union leaders not to press for higher wages and businessmen not to raise prices. They do not seem to be aware that they are attacking the symptoms of the malaise, not the root causes. They are therefore not likely to succeed.

    I believe that the Team has identified the real causes of trouble in our education system. There are three:

    (i) The languages of instruction in our bilingual system are not spoken at home for the great majority of school children.

    (ii) The rapid switch from the Chinese stream to the English stream made necessary the mass production of teachers for the English stream schools, to the detriment of the quality of teaching.

    (iii) Our system of education, lasting twelve years, has been tailored to suit the brightest 12% of pupils.

    The inevitable result is high wastage rates in our schools, both primary and secondary. Automatic promotion was practised in primary and secondary schools up to 1975. and 1977 respectively. This meant that as the slow learners moved from lower to higher classes, they understood less and less of what was being taught. Automatic promotion in Primary and Secondary schools was abolished in 1976 and 1978 respectively and retentions enforced on a large scale. This did not produce the results hoped for. Retention inflicts a psychological trauma on the child and when this is repeated two or three times, it would be an exceptional child who does not lose interest in learning, suffer a loss of self-esteem and self-confidence and develop character defects as a consequence of these.

    Once the basic causes have been identified, the solutions almost suggest themselves. We must accept the principle of teaching children of different learning capacities at different rates. The Report recommends different streams of education to suit the slow, average, above average and outstanding learners. I do not wish to discuss

the details of this proposal. All I need to say is that while the principle is clear enough and the policy correct, there are formidable problems in implementation. Nevertheless, I believe that over the years they can be overcome, for reasons which I shall explain later.

    I want to discuss a number of issues not mentioned in the Report but which have a bearing on the subject. The first is: "Are parents acting rationally in switching their children from the Chinese, Malay or Tamil streams to the English stream in increasing numbers over the last two decades?" To answer this question, one must set up some criteria of rationality. I propose two simple criteria:-

    (1) Will it enable a child to earn more after he leaves school?

    (2) Is he likely to be literate in at least one language, i.e. can he read local items in the newspapers and write simple letters?

    As regards the prospective earning power of pupils in the Chinese language stream and the English language stream, the general belief is that there is no doubt an English language education confers a decided advantage. There is ample evidence on this in relation to university graduates. But we do not know how large is the differential and whether the advantage of an English language education over other language streams holds true down the line.

I asked the Manpower Division from MINDEF last year to retrieve information from the computer files. The information has been summarised in tables (Cols. 75 - 76) which have been distributed to Members. These tables describe the median monthly income of reservists according to their educational level and occupation groups in the English stream and the Chinese stream. 84,022 reservists were included in this

It includes a large number, some 7,000, about whom descriptions of occupation were insufficiently clear for inclusion in the five occupational grades used in these tables.

    What about the second criteria of literacy? There has been, to my knowledge, no comprehensive study of literacy levels, however defined, achieved and retained by school leavers. The general belief is that those who have passed 'O' and 'A' levels and above remain literate in at least one language in the sense that they can read newspapers. The position is uncertain about those who have not completed 'O' levels. A Ministry of Defence study, cited in the Team's report, concluded that only a small proportion of school dropouts from the English stream possessed a satisfactory working knowledge of English. Psychologists in the Ministry of Defence have made case studies of poorly educated National Service soldiers with low rank and in low vocations. They have either failed their PSLE or have not gone beyond Secondary 1 or Secondary 2 at best. The Chinese stream fared better than the English stream in the ability to converse in the first language taught in schools. There is also some evidence that they are more likely to read newspapers than those who failed the English PSLE. But the number of soldiers included in such case studies is too small for any firm conclusion to be drawn.

This study will investigate the languages and dialects they speak at home, at work, the newspapers they read, the radio programmes they listen to, the TV programmes they watch and the books they read, if any. The study will relate these factors to educational standards achieved in schools, the occupation and income of reservists and their social and economic background.     There are two further comments I want to make on this subject. The first is that retention of verbal or written skills in any language after school depends on how often it is used. Therefore, the language used in daily work is of critical importance. So far as non- manual jobs are concerned, it is likely that English will be more widely used both in conversation and correspondence than other languages. Especially is this so in the service industries, We are not certain what the position is in the manufacturing industries and maintenance and repair installations which employ large numbers of blue-collar workers. If the languages taught in schools are not used there, then regression must take place.

    The second point I want to make is that, in future years, the importance of English is likely to increase rather than diminish. The service industries such as business houses, banks, hotels, supermarkets and the entertainment and tourist industries have shown a robust growth rate in past years which is likely to continue in the future. In the manufacturing industries, as we move to higher levels of technology, knowledge of English will become increasingly important. For instance, in aircraft maintenance which involves lengthy specialised training in different fields, such as instruments, avionics, airframe, weapons, hydraulic systems and jet engines, reference to manufacturers' manuals on specifications, design and drawings is a routine practice both in the training schools and in the repair and maintenance bases. Even with the simple type of aircraft we use, the volume of documents is immense, costing Mindef several million dollars for each make of fighter aircraft. Competence cannot be achieved without a good working

knowledge of English since it is not feasible for us in Singapore to translate these documents into other languages.

    There is a wider perspective to this. This relates to our competitive position, both in Southeast Asia and in relation to countries like South Korea, Hong Kong and Taiwan. Among the ASEAN states, as our wages increase year by year, we cannot remain competitive in labour-intensive industries for very much longer. But the edge we have over all of them, except for the Philippines, is the large number of Singaporeans who can understand and use the English language. This would give us an advantage in the service and high technology industries which we shall retain for a long time.

    As regards fast-developing countries, such as South Korea and Taiwan, we must not forget that we are more dependent on foreign technology and the multi-national companies than they are. This is because we do not have domestic markets of the size that they enjoy and hence are more dependent on export markets than they are. The multi-nationals not only bring technology but also their world markets with them. Multi-nationals need not only workers on the factory floor but also supervisory staff and middle level management, technical and engineering personnel in large numbers. Proficiency in English coupled with a sound system of workshop, technical and engineering education will ensure that our competitive advantage will remain.

    Having said this, I do not want to give the impression that there is no future at all for those who are not literate in English. The tables I referred to earlier, show that at the lowest levels of education, the Chinese stream fared no worse than the English stream. If their spoken language is adequate, this will widen their job opportunities. There are many well-paid jobs; SBS drivers, for instance, which offer no premium on linguistic competence.

    I now turn to the subject of streaming. It became obvious to the Education Study Team in the course of its investigations that

putting through children of varying abilities at the pace suitable to the brightest 12% was the main reason for high wastage in both primary and secondary schools. Once you accept this argument, you must also accept the logical consequences which inevitably follow. We must teach children at different rates according to their capacity to absorb learning. We discussed this subject with some of the professionals in the Ministry of Education and school principals as to how best to achieve this aim. And the scheme outlined in the Report is the result of our joint thinking. It represents a major innovation in Singapore, or so we thought.

    My instinctive response to innovations, particularly in such a sensitive subject as education, is one of caution. Nevertheless, the Team decided to go ahead because we could see no other solution. After I moved to the Ministry of Education, I discussed the subject with my colleague, Dr Tony Tan. We decided that we should study other education systems, particularly those faced with the need to impart bilingual competence to school children. We thought that Switzerland could be a useful subject of study, and Dr Tony Tan proceeded to collect information about the Swiss system. The exercise turned out to be an interesting one. The newspapers have given an account of the Swiss system and Members may be interested in a review of the essential points.

    Switzerland is a confederation of 19 cantons and six half cantons, each of which possesses considerable autonomy. Education is under the control of cantons which run their own schools, have their own school legislation, curriculum and teacher-training institutions. There is no national Ministry of Education in Switzerland. But over the centuries, a common pattern seems to have emerged, though there are wide differences in detail between the cantons.

    The system studied is the one operating in Berne, the largest canton in Switzerland. It is a predominantly German-speaking Canton.

    Education commences in primary school at the age of seven and is compulsory for nine years. In the first four years, the lower

Column: 13

level primary, the medium of instruction is in high German. Promotion is virtually automatic, retention being rare.

    After Primary 4, children who want to go beyond primary school sit for an entrance examination set by a secondary school. Children are then streamed on the basis of examination results.

    Only 50% succeed in getting entrance into a secondary school, out of which only one-fifth qualify at the end of two years to enter a Gymnasium. Those who do not get admission into a secondary school spend another five years in a primary school when their academic education ends. In the secondary school, a second language, French, is introduced from the beginning, and a third language, Italian or English, two years later. Completion of education in secondary schools does not qualify a student for entrance into a University. They can go for technician training, the way our 'O' level students do at the Polytechnic.

    Only those who succeed in getting into a Gymnasium can go to a University. Only 10% of a Primary I cohort succeed in getting into a Gymnasium. Virtually all Gymnasium graduates go to a University.

    There is provision for secondary school pupils to transfer to a Gymnasium, as I said, through an entrance examination at the end of the second year. But the number who succeed is small. Standards set at the Gymnasium are extremely high, the course lasting eight years.

    I understand that Berne follows the German system of education. It makes no pretensions to being egalitarian. It recognises that children have different learning abilities and they are streamed accordingly. During our investigations, the Study Team was unaware of the Swiss or German system of education. We studied the Australian and New Zealand systems. Since both of these were modelled after the British pattern, just like ours, we could find no guidance there. It comes as a great relief to me that the solution we worked out from first principles did not turn out to be such a major innovation after all.

    Honourable Members would have noted that the streaming proposed in the Report is less severe than the one practised in Berne. We expect no more than 20% to be streamed in the largely mono-lingual stream as compared with 500/0 in Berne who do not go beyond primary school in academic education. There are reasons for this. It is not that our children are cleverer than Swiss children. First, we have to take into account the wishes of parents. Parents in Singapore have great expectations of their children. There is a Chinese saying which expresses this very well - wang zi cheng long (    ) - "hoping that one's child will become a dragon."

    To deprive half the children, all potential dragons, of any chance of getting secondary school education will not be acceptable to Singapore parents, We have therefore allowed part of the slow learners - those who would pass the Primary School Leaving Examination after two or three tries - an extended primary school education with eventual entry into secondary school. Most of these will not reach 'O' level, but will move on to vocational training. However, they would have acquired sufficient competence in the English language to widen their opportunities for employment. For reasons which I have discussed earlier, this is advantageous not only to the children but also to our economic growth. The service component in our economy is proportionately much larger than that of South Korea and Taiwan and accordingly our requirement for those with a command of the English language is greater.

    I have referred earlier to the practical problems of streaming. It will be based largely on performance in schools. In addition, we intend to use intelligence tests with the object of detecting those children who perform poorly but may have the potential for better performance in later years - the so called late-developers. This instrument of assessment can never be perfect. Educational psychologists have told me that it will take some years to construct and validate these IQ tests. Under these circumstances, we cannot have complete

confidence that justice will be done in every case.

    It is therefore wise to accommodate the wishes of parents to a wider extent than is customary in a well established system. If a parent disagrees with the assessment of the school authority that the child should be streamed to a mainly mono-lingual stream or other low-level streams in primary and secondary schools and is prepared to sign a statement to that effect, then I propose that the child be allowed to continue in the higher stream despite the adverse assessment of the school. Similarly, there may be parents who want their children to be educated to the highest standards possible in Chinese, Malay or Tamil and not to join the main stream of education in which standards in these languages had to be lowered in the interest of higher competence in English. The Ministry would arrange for special classes to be conducted in these languages.

    The third part of my Motion refers to the need to adjust the new education structure, which experience and advice of principals and teachers show to be desirable in the long-term interest of pupils. It is not possible in a major undertaking of this kind to foresee in detail all the problems and difficulties that lie ahead. We only know that they are bound to arise. It is important that the problems and difficulties be identified and dealt with before they reach crisis proportions. This requires a more efficient and sensitive management system in the Ministry of Education than now exists. It is particularly important that we get the opinion of school principals and teachers on the way the new system functions. They are the ones who are doing the job; they are in daily contact with school children, and they know what is happening.

    There has been widespread public interest in the Report and the Prime Minister's comments on it. While this is gratifying, I would like to sound a note of caution against expecting too much too soon. There are no magic cures for our troubles, no quick-fixes and no short-cuts. Ours is probably a unique
