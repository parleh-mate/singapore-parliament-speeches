    Order for Second Reading read.

2.44 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Members will recollect that in the Budget Speech the Minister for Finance mentioned that the rates of stamp duty for a number of items, most of which have not been revised since the 1930s, would be revised to bring them in line with the present value of money and to suit present day conditions. From the revenue angle, the most important item of stamp duty is on conveyances on property, accounting for 40 per cent of the stamp revenue during 1966. The existing rate is $2 for every $100 or part thereof. It is proposed to base the duty on a sliding scale. Where the value of the property is up to $30,000, there will be no change in the existing rate and, as such, purchasers of Housing and Development Board flats or other low-cost houses will not have to pay higher stamp fees. Where the value of the property is above $30,000, and not more

Column: 789

than $50,000, there will be an increase of 50 cents for every $100 or part thereof on the value of the property exceeding $30,000. In respect of property exceeding $50,000, there will be an increase of $1 for every $100 or part thereof on the value of property exceeding $50,000. It is quite reasonable that purchasers of property of higher value pay more.

    At present the rate of duty on the transfer of shares and stocks is 15 cents for every $100 or part thereof where the name of the transferee is filled prior to the execution of the transfer, in other cases, the rate is 20 cents for every $100 or part thereof. It is proposed to increase the stamp duty to 20 cents and 30 cents respectively.

    The present fee of 10 cents per share certificate will be increased to $1 for every $1,000 or part thereof of the par value in respect of new share certificates. This revised rate will also apply to certificates issued in respect of bonus and rights issues. The higher fee is justified because, in subsequent transactions, very often shares change hands without registration, resulting in a loss of revenue. At the same time, the existing flat rate of 50 cents on a contract note will be replaced by an ad valorem duty. A duty of $1 for every $1,000 of the value of the shares and stocks will be payable.

    The stamp duty on cheques will be raised from 10 cents to 15 cents and on receipts from six cents to 10 cents.

    Apart from these, there are small increases in the rate of stamp duties on mortgages, leases, insurance, appraisements, etc.

    (i) bonds on superannuation annuity - the rate is 25 cents for every $100;

    I shall now deal with the important, amendments. Section 14 of the Ordinance is amended by the addition of a new section - section 14A - to allow for goods manufactured, assembled or produced in Singapore which are not subject to excise duties and which have been exported to be re-imported without payment of import duty. The importers will, however, have to satisfy the Comptroller of Customs that such goods have not undergone any processing or manipulation outside Singapore.     Under the existing Ordinance, drawback of duty is allowed on goods, subject to import duty, but not on goods, subject to excise duty. Clause 18 of the Bill extends drawback facilities to locally-manufactured products which may be subject to excise duty.

    At the moment, the power to levy or vary the special tax on diesel-engined vehicles lies with Parliament. Clause 6 is intended to transfer the power to the Minister. This will enable any changes to be made speedily by a Ministerial order.

CINEMATOGRAPH FILM HIRE
DUTY (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.13 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Bill now before Parliament proposes two amendments to the Cinematograph Film Hire Duty Act, 1966. An amendment to section 10 to limit the amount of gross receipts which may be deducted by a film renter for the purposes of film hire duty assessment, and the other is an amendment to section 16 to enable senior officers of Customs to compound offences under the Act where the amount involved does not exceed $100.

    Film hire duty is assessed at 60 per cent of the gross receipts of a renter derived from the renting of films. For the purpose of duty assessment, a film renter is allowed to deduct from the gross receipts, proceeds derived from the sale or hire of advertising materials or accessories used in connection with the distribution or hire of films. There have been instances of renters deducting unreasonably large amounts in this respect, thus reducing the amount assessable for duty. As a remedy to such abuse, the amendment to section 10

Column: 798

of the Act limits such deductible amounts to 10 per cent of the gross receipts or $50, whichever is the lesser.

    At present, senior officers of Customs are empowered to conduct court prosecutions on behalf of the Comptroller of Customs and Excise for offences under the Act. Experience has shown that it is desirable to provide powers for such officers to compound offences involving sums of less than $100 to avoid taking cases to the court. This is effected by the proposed amendment to section 16 of the Act.

    Question put, and agreed to.

Soon after the separation in 1965, we decided to establish our own Registry of Ships, and the Merchant Shipping (Amendment) Act of 1966 was accordingly promulgated. Under this piece of legislation, ownership of Singapore ships was confined to Singapore citizens and companies incorporated in Singapore and having their principal place of business in Singapore. Indeed, Singapore citizens and companies were compelled under the law to register their ships as Singapore ships. While, on the one hand, an obligation was imposed upon Singapore citizens and companies to register their ships with us, on the other hand, no corresponding incentives were offered. It was not surprising, therefore, to find that after one year of the establishment of our Registry, as at 31st October, 1967, there were only 298 ships on the Register totalling some 123,000 gross tons. Of the 298 ships, 244 or 82 per cent were tugs, barges, dumb lighters and other small harbour craft.

Members may recollect that the then Deputy Prime Minister, now the Minister for Science and Technology, stated in Parliament during the course of the Budget debate in December last year that legislation would be introduced this year to encourage more ships to fly the Singapore flag.

    The Bill before this House seeks to amend the Merchant Shipping Ordinance to enable Singapore to be a party to the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea, 1960, and to establish an open Registry of Ships. At present, Singapore is a party to the 1948 Safety Convention. This Convention prescribes certain requirements relating to the construction and survey of ships, provision of life-saving appliances and radio equipment, safety of navigation, carriage of grain and of dangerous goods. Signatory governments are obliged to apply these prescribed standards to their ships. The 1960 Convention is in scope and content essentially a revision and extension of the 1948 Convention which it replaces. Being a major port and having a vital interest in the development of merchant shipping, it is necessary for Singapore to accept the 1960 Convention. The first part of this Bill, therefore, contains provisions whkh will enable Singapore to give effect to the provisions of the 1960 Convention. This is achieved by deleting Part III of the existing Ordinance which deals with passenger ships only and whith contains outdated provisions, and substituting therefor a new Part III. Under this new Part III, ships are required to hold Safety Convention certificates, and provision is made for the issue of the appropriate certificates for both passenger ships as well as cargo ships. Power is given to the Minister to make the necessary regulations to implement the provisions of the 1960 Convention.

    The second part of this Bill seeks to amend the existing Ordinance to

Where previously ownership was confined to Singapore citizens and companies, this clause now provides an opportunity for foreign shipowners to place their ships under the Singapore Register.

Hence the Bill provides that the copyright in a musical work or gramophone record is not infringed by the Government when it causes the musical work or gramophone record to be heard in public by means of a radio or television broadcast. Clause 6 of the Bill further provides that the copyright in a musical work is not infringed by the Government when it makes a record, tape or other device in respect of that work solely for the purpose of being used in radio or television broadcast.
