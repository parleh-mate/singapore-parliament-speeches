SINGAPORE'S FINANCIAL POSITION
(Statement by the Minister for Finance)


2.32 p.m.

    The Minister for Finance (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I wish to report to the Assembly on certain aspects of our financial position.

    When we assumed office, we made a detailed investigation of the finances of the country. The Government has reported that a deficit of $14 million has to be disposed of. The sum itself is not very large but what was of greater import is that for some time past current expenditure had been financed out of the reserves of the country. This points to a basic imbalance between revenue and expenditure which needs looking into.

    The General Revenue balance now stands at about three months' revenue. It is therefore a matter of common prudence that the revenue balance shall not be permitted to go much below this limit unless new sources of liquidity are readily available. It has therefore been necessary to take a number of steps some of which have been severe in character.

    The Government has frozen all vacancies arising in the establishment without making a distinction as to the reasons for such vacancies, whether they arise from retirement or resignation, or whether they are newly created posts. Special Expenditure has also been frozen so that a careful scrutiny can be made to weed out items which are not absolutely necessary. A Cabinet committee was formed, consisting of the Prime Minister as Chairman, and the Deputy Prime Minister and myself as the other members. We have gone through applications from Ministries and departments and have set up the most stringent standards.

    Recently all Ministers have been asked to scrutinise the state of the vote and to report on what economies are possible. This whole process of freezing and vetting of expenditure constitutes a major operation. As a result, substantial economies have been effected and the total amount of reduction has reached the figure of $25.7 million. Of this, $20.1

Column: 527

million were reductions in the Ordinary Estimates and $5.6 million were savings in the Development Estimates. This is a considerable achievement and has been made possible through the co-operation of all departments of Government.

    I wish to pay a special tribute to senior Treasury officials who have had to put in long and punishing hours of work in the course of this exercise. I also want to acknowledge the full-hearted support of my colleagues in this economy drive. My colleagues and I work as a team and it is because we work as a team that it has been possible to reduce the estimated expenditure by this substantial amount.

    It will be idle to pretend, however, that the slashes in the economy drive of this magnitude can be brought out painlessly. Everyone is aware of the pained reaction to the cuts in variable allowances. Because of the freeze on vacancies, many Ministries have to work short-handed and this means that everyone works harder. Then many of the schemes which have been entered in the Estimates after years of tussle with the Treasury have now to be jettisoned or at least postponed.

    The operation has not been a painless one but it is necessary, in the interest of financial solvency, that one sets one's face sternly against the cries of anguish coming from the stricken.

    I am now in the happy position to report to the Assembly that, as a result of our exertions, the finances of the Government have now attained a healthier state.     So far as the Government is concerned, the public debt has been very small. This has its virtues. At the same time, it must be borne in mind that there is no merit in not borrowing money from the public if this means retarding essential development schemes and services which are necessary to the growth of the economy. More especially where public savings are available, it is foolish to deny these sound opportunities for investment in this country - investments which would promote further expansion in various sectors of the economy.

    So far as this Government itself is concerned, the position is that the total public debt stands at $115 million. Of this, sinking funds have been accumulated which amount to about $75 million. There is therefore a net public debt of $40 million or some $25 per head of population. There are very few governments which have a public debt as low as this. These latter total some $190 million of which $172 million are still outstanding. The Government itself emerges as an overall creditor.

    I make this brief review of Singapore's overseas balances in order to underline two points. The first is that, as a great trading centre, we are a part of the world economy, and have important financial and economic links in many parts of the world. Our continued prosperity depends on strengthening these links, and if we disrupt these links, we do so at our own peril. The second point is that we have invested large sums abroad. In return, there has been a substantial investment of foreign capital in our country, though the totals have not been reliably computed. It is almost certain that after taking these into account, we emerge very much on the credit side.

    Since the end of World War II, it has become fashionable among the modern industrial nations of the world to speak of the need to assist under-developed regions, especially by way of capital investment. But in Singapore the relationship is exactly the reverse of what has now come to be accepted as normal. I do hope that when we have occasion to ask for loans from abroad, we shall not be treated as poor relatives in quest for assistance, for our moral position in this is surely very strong.

    The amendments, Mr Speaker, Sir, to the Criminal Procedure Code proposed by this Bill may conveniently be dealt with under four heads.

    Firstly, this Bill seeks to increase the jurisdiction and powers of the Criminal District and Magistrate's Courts.     The Minister for Labour and Law (Mr K. M. Byrne): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The objects of this Bill are simple and straightforward. The Bill is designed to indemnify those persons who, acting in good faith, dealt with property erroneously assumed to be enemy property and also to provide for the disposal of in, come derived from enemy property, and any copyright of an enemy nature infringed owing to the War. The Bill is based on the United Kingdom Enemy Property Act, 1953, and follows in the main the Federation Enemy Property Ordinance, 1959.

    The United Kingdom Act has three main purposes:

    1. To indemnify persons who dealt with property erroneously assumed to be enemy property, provided they were acting in good faith and in purported compliance with the Trading with the Enemy legislation. Titles to such property actually sold are validated but the real owner's rights to recover the property are protected when it is still in the custodian's hands or when it was leased to an ostensible owner without payment.

    2. It provides for the disposal of income from enemy property. This income is used, first, to pay interest in respect of property erroneously taken into custody. The balance of the income is paid to the exchequer.

Column: 605



    3. It provides protection in the case of infringement of German-owned copyright for the use of German-owned patents and designs and for transactions involving reparation materials sent from Germany to the United Kingdom, where no legislative saction or authority existed for such infringement, use or transaction.

    The main reason why claims may arise is because of the peculiar difficulty in war time of determining what was enemy property and what was not. The definition of enemy property depended upon the residence of the owner regardless of his own nationality, because if the owner was under enemy control, his property might be available to the enemy. Consequently when owners moved between enemy and neutral countries, or companies operating in enemy territory were controlled by other companies in neutral territory, there was difficulty in determining the status of their property. The rule was adopted that property should be treated as enemy property when there was any doubt as to its true status. In these circumstances, mistakes were inevitable.

    While the factors leading to the passing of the United Kingdom Act are similar to those in Singapore, there are other factors to be taken into account. The most important is the fact of the Japanese occupation and the confusion cause by it, together with the considerable amount of legislation passed in Singapore to deal with its after-effects.

    Part I of the Bill therefore specifically includes the Custodian of Property Proclamation, the Custodian of Property (Vesting) Ordinance, and the Custodian of Property Ordinance as legislation authorising dealings with enemy property, or property which was assumed to be enemy property. The Bill will provide indemnities for dealings in such property which was in fact not enemy property. It has not been necessary to make special provision in respect of patents in industrial designs, because registers of patents and designs in Singapore are mere extensions of the English registers. Consequently,

Column: 606

the protection afforded by the United Kingdom Enemy Property Act against claims by the owners of patents and designs, whose rights were infringed during the War, apply equally to individuals in Singapore and in the United Kingdom. The provisions of the United Kingdom Enemy Property Act relating to copyrights and disclosure of trade secrets have been included in the Bill.

    In the United Kingdom when property was erroneously taken into custody and later restored to the owner, interest was paid to cover the period of seizure by the Custodian. With regard to Singapore, however, it is proposed that interest should be paid only from the date of resumption of Civil Government on the 1st April, 1946, or the date when the property was taken into custody, whichever is the later. This proposal is in line with the policy adopted in connection with the moratorium. During the Occupation and until Civil Government was resumed, it was not possible to administer enemy property nor to return to owners property erroneously taken under control. Just as some of the loss caused by the Occupation was placed upon the owners as a result of the moratorium, so some of the loss in respect of property seized by the Custodian should also fall on the owners. It is proposed that interest shall be payable, following the United Kingdom precedent, only on property that was never enemy property at any time. Interest on the property of allies and others taken into custody when their countries were occupied by the enemy is not payable to the owners. The reason for this decision is that, in very many instances, the amount payable would be trivial and the work of calculating the interest for varying periods on small but numerous sums would be disproportionately costly.
