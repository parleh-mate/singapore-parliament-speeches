(Appointment of Commission of Inquiry)


    1. Mr David Marshall asked the Prime Minister when will the Commission of Inquiry on Political Nepotism be appointed.

    The Prime Minister: Mr Speaker, Sir, I refer the Member for Anson to my answer to the question of his on the 15th of January. I stated then that it would not be possible at any one time to have more than one High Court Judge available to conduct a Commission of Inquiry. It is the Government's intention to proceed with the first Commission of Inquiry as soon as a Judge is available. I have been informed by the Chief Justice that a Judge will be available towards the end of March or the beginning of April when another Judge On leave will be returning. Then the first Commission asked for by the

Column: 4

Member for Anson will be set up to inquire into the Secondary IV Examination boycott by some Chinese Middle School students, and to determine to what extent it was brought about by exploitation of students by Communists and their open front organisations.

    The second, and less important inquiry, is to investigate the events which developed into open insubordination and defiance of the Board of Management by some 130 out of 480 workers of the People's Association, and to establish the connection between these persons and their leaders who are members of the Barisan Sosialis who had been on the Board of Management.

    The third Commission is to inquire into all specific allegations of political nepotism and improper political interference with the Civil Service and employees of Statutory bodies, and to make recommendations provided that all Members who have voiced these allegations in the Assembly would undertake to repeat these allegations before the Commission and to give evidence of such allegations on oath or affirmation.

GOVERNMENT POLICY ON "MASS GRAVES"
OF PEOPLE MASSACRED BY JAPANESE


    4. Mr Tee Kim Leng asked the Prime Minister whether the Government has adopted any policy regarding the reported discovery by the Singapore Chinese Chamber of Commerce of the "mass graves" of people said to have been massacred by the Japanese in early 1942.

    The Prime Minister: Mr Speaker, Sir, questions of war reparation, including compensation for atrocities and war damage, were the responsibility of the United Kingdom Government who in

Column: 13

1951 signed a treaty of peace with Japan. The Government has written to the U.K. Commission asking the U.K. Government to take up the matter with the Government of Japan to seek amends and atonement for the atrocities that they committed.

    The U.K. Commission has referred the matter to London and sought further information as to the scale of the present discovery of the intended remains. They have enquired whether the number of the persons involved may run to several thousands or something much less. They would like to know whether this evidence is related to some hitherto unrealised atrocities or of one already known. The U.K. Commission wrote to say that they believed that this recent discovery is evidence not of an atrocity as yet unknown but is connected with the massacres that had taken place, and were known to have taken place, though the actual sites of the graves may not have been known in detail.

    There are no reliable figures as to the total number of persons who were massacred by the Japanese invading forces in 1942. Some estimates put the figure around 10,000, and some much more, but certainly thousands of families have beloved ones who were missed shortly after the Japanese invasion. All of them were taken away from screening camps and were never heard of or seen again.

    While it may be difficult to ascertain the actual identity of the persons who had been massacred or their immediate dependents and families for compensation to be made direct, there is no doubt that the people of Singapore, as a whole, suffered by these massacres. It is our view that atonement should be made to the people of Singapore collectively.

    The Government is also considering the acquisition of the site where these bones have been discovered in order that a suitable memorial can be put up in, memory of the men who have been massacred. If the area is suitable, a green belt to provide for a small park may also be designated.

    Sir, the introduction of this amending Bill has three main purposes. These are, firstly, to make the Defence Forces Record Officer, instead of the Commandant, Singapore Military Forces, responsible for all matters pertaining to reservists; secondly, to provide for the enlistment of youths between the ages of 14 and 17 years as drummer boys and band boys; and thirdly, to establish a benevolent fund for regular member of the Singapore Military Forces.

Column: 45



    In accordance with the provisions of section 12 of the Ordinance, the executive command of all members of the Singapore Military Forces is at present invested in the Commandant, Singapore Military Forces. These members consist of two categories of personnel. They are (a) regulars on "army service" and volunteers doing "colour training" service on the one hand, and (b) those regulars and volunteers who have completed their service of active service and are not on the reserve on the other. Whereas the Commandant, Singapore Military Forces, is the correct authority to exercise command over all serving regulars and volunteers, the authority responsible for dealing with all matters pertaining to reservists should properly be the Officer in Charge of Records as in the British Army. In the case of the local forces, the officers holding the appointment of Officer in Charge of Records is called the Defence Forces Record Officer. This officer's duties in connection with reservists include, amongst other things, looking after their past records of service and recording their up-to-date addresses; the re-engagement of a soldier (about to go on the reserve) for further "army service" if regulars, or for further "colour training" service if volunteers; transferring a reservist from the reserve to the active list; granting a reservist permission to leave the State up to a period of two' years; permitting a reservist to wear uniform on special occasions, etc. The Defence Forces Record Officer is also responsible for paying pensions to retired service personnel and is the "Pensions Authority" under the Personal Injury (Civilians) Scheme, 1941, for paying pensions to war widows and dependants and for paying disability pensions to disabled service personnel. In order to place reservists under his control, an amendment is required to section 12 of the Ordinance. This is what clause 4 seeks to do.

    The amendment to section 12 of the Ordinance will entail consequential amendments to section 2, subsection (1) of section 11, section 42, and subsection (1) of section 43 of the Ordinance so

These amendments will also entail consequential amendments to the Singapore Military Forces (Reserve) Rules, 1957. These will be effected later.

    As the Defence Forces Record Officer has since 1st September, 1960, been carrying out the duties already referred to in connection with reservists, it is necessary to give retrospective effect to this by ante-dating the Bill to 1st September, 1960. This is what clause I seeks to do.

    Clause 5 amends section 40 of the Ordinance to allow for the change of designation of the Officer in Charge Records from his former designation of "Officer in Charge Forces Record" to his present designation of "Defence Forces Record Officer". This amendment has already been incorporated in the Schedule to the Departmental Titles (Alteration) Ordinance, and the opportunity is therefore being taken in this amending Bill to make a similar amendment to the Singapore Military Forces Ordinance.

    Clause 3 amends section 10 of the Ordinance to allow for the enlistment of drummer boys and band boys between the ages of 14 and 17 years since the present provisions of the Ordinance provide only for the enlistment of those who have attained the age of 18 years. The intention in recruiting such boys is to ensure that the Singapore Military Forces Staff Band will have a regular flow of trained musicians available to take the places of those bandsmen who will leave the band when the latter's seven years' active service is over.
