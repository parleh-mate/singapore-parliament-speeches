RICE STOCKPILE

    7. Mr William Tan asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry if he will make a statement regarding the rice stockpile.

    Mr J. M. Jumabhoy: Mr Speaker, Sir, I am glad to avail myself of this opportunity to make a statement on the rice stockpile though I am sure that most of what I am going to say is already well known to hon. Members.

    The stockpile of rice is part of the Singapore defence scheme and is maintained in the island to cover an emergency. I am sure hon. Members will appreciate that it is imperative that Government should be in a position to issue rice to the public and to the essential services in the event of any emergency either here or in the rice producing countries which results in a temporary shortage of rice. I might add here in passing that the Federation of Malaya also holds a similar stockpile.

    The basic principle behind the stockpile is to ensure that adequate supplies of fresh rice are always available. It is necessary therefore that the stockpile be rotated and this is achieved by requiring

Column: 687

importers of rice for local sale to purchase a certain amount from the stockpile on a ratio depending on the grade of rice. The ratio is two to one for imported whole-grain rice and three to one for broken rice.

    It will be readily seen that the whole success of this stockpile scheme is dependent on regular rotation of the stockpile to ensure freshness of the rice. A new scheme has been introduced recently which aims at a minimum off take from the stockpile of 2,500 tons per month. This will operate for a trial period of four months. Most importers of rice have individually undertaken to draw from the stockpile up to a total of 2,500 tons a month. The principal association of rice importers in Singapore has also collectively guaranteed that offtake would not fall below the minimum figure of 2,500 tons per month. This measure of co-operation by the trade will assist both the trade and the Government.

    As a concession to those importers of whole grain Thailand rice who have given this undertaking, they will be permitted to import up to three times the quantity which they have undertaken to purchase from the stockpile. Hon. Members will observe that the effect of this concession is to lower the stockpile obligations of the importers from two to one for whole grain and three to one for brokens to a flat rate of three to one. Additional imports from Thailand and from other sources will be subject to the normal stockpile requirements.

    I should conclude here by repeating that the stockpile is operated strictly on a "no profit no loss" basis.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, I wish to assure hon. Members that unless radical changes in Government's economic policy are undertaken quickly, time is running out for us in Singapore and soon we will be facing a situation much worse than what we have recently seen, in the form of gangsterism, because of unemployment, and other anti-social activities.

     Hon. Members will recall that I outlined Government's adoption of a policy of selective protection for local industries in this House on the 13th of August this year, when this Bill had its First Reading that day. I stated in this connection that outside manufacturers wishing to manufacture their products here will, as a general rule, be required to come to an arrangement with the local manufacturer or intending local manufacturer, if any, of the same product or failing which with interested Singapore citizens. I further indicated that such an arrangement should either be on a partnership basis with at least 49 per cent of the shares held by the local interest or on the basis of the local interest manufacturing under licence of the outside manufacturer. If after a reasonable time there is a lack of response from local interests or if such an arrangement cannot be made to my satisfaction, the outside manufacturer will be given permission to set up his own undertaking.

Singapore as a wide open market may suddenly find restrictions placed in their way. They would find that the local manufacturer who up to now was treated by the Singapore Government as an orphan, would now be in a position to capture the Singapore market from them.

Thus it is expected that large sums will henceforth be invested by local capital in industries.

    The result of all this would be that some of the outside manufacturers who up to now have been satisfied with supplying Singapore from outside may either have to gradually give up the Singapore market or be forced to open a factory here.

    With the strong consumer preference in Singapore for imported goods, these outside manufacturers opening a factory here and manufacturing their well known brands and marketing them in Singapore again, could have a very tragic effect on local investors. Local investors who might have in the meanwhile invested huge sums and would just be getting on their feet could suddenly find the whole ground cut from underneath their feet by these outside manufacturers and their investment vanish into thin air.
