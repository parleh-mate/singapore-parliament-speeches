RECENT DEATHS OF NSMEN

     1.  Assoc. Prof. Paulin Tay Straughan asked the Deputy Prime Minister and

Column: 2506

Minister for Defence in light of the tragic deaths of two full-time National Servicemen involving reversing army vehicles, what additional measures have been put in place to prevent such accidents from happening again.

     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence (Mr Teo Chee Hean): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am saddened by the loss of Lance Corporal (LCP) Eugin Wee Yong Choon on 28th January 2011. I would like to express my deepest condolences to the family.

 

     Assoc. Prof. Straughan referred to two incidents. The first incident occurred on 3rd July 2009 at Seletar Camp. A Motor Transport Officer was pinned under a landrover and succumbed to his injuries in the hospital. A Committee of Inquiry convened to investigate the incident found that the driver of the vehicle had been negligent in his duties as a driver, and had failed to follow the correct vehicle mounting and starting procedures. The Police also conducted a separate and independent investigation. The driver of the vehicle was subsequently charged in Court for causing death by doing a negligent act not amounting to culpable homicide. He pleaded guilty and was fined $5,000 and disqualified from driving all vehicles for four years.

 

     Following this incident one and a half years ago in 2009, MINDEF and the SAF conducted a review on the procedures for vehicle mounting and starting and concluded that they were fundamentally sound, and the incident could have been prevented if the driver had followed them. The SAF has continued to reinforce these procedures and remind its vehicle commanders and drivers to adhere strictly to them before mounting and starting any vehicle.

 

     The most recent incident involving LCP Eugin Wee occurred on 28th January 2011. The SAF immediately ordered a

Column: 2507

safety time-out that same day, for unit commanders to go through with all vehicle commanders and drivers vehicular safety drills and procedures. The SAF does this when there is an incident of note because these are teachable moments when safety procedures are reinforced and lessons learnt are shared, so that every person is aware of the relevance of procedures and safety consciousness. And each person knows that safety is in his hands and that what he does or does not do can have serious consequences on his buddies or himself.

 

     Mr Speaker, MINDEF takes the safety and well-being of our servicemen very seriously. The Army safety system is not simply one which is drawn up on its own but it is also certified in accordance with the Occupational Health and Safety Assessment Series (OHSAS). Adherence to OHSAS provides a comprehensive system of safety management based on a set of standard procedures designed to systematically and methodically manage risks in our work processes. For those who are not familiar with it, OHSAS is an internationally recognised system created by a group of world-leading bodies and consultancies who are the authorities of standards. These include countries like Ireland, Australia, South Africa and Britain.

 

     The Army constantly upkeeps our OHSAS standards and undergoes annual inspection and audit by an independent external agency. In addition, the Army also conducts internal safety management system audits on core Army units. The prescribed safety procedures are hence fundamentally sound but we have to continue to do better to make sure that they are followed.  This includes regular reminders to those responsible for operating vehicles and special reminders during such teachable moments.  However, safety procedures cannot cover every specific condition that may arise. Hence, it is also important that every

LATEST MEASURES TO COOL PROPERTY MARKET

     2.  Er Lee Bee Wah asked the Minister for National Development (a) what is the compelling factor that prompted the Government to implement the latest property cooling measures; (b) whether the Government analysed buyer data to ascertain whether foreigners or Singaporeans were responsible for the speculative buying; and (c) why the release of state land for housing developments have not had the intended effect.

     The Minister for National Development (Mr Mah Bow Tan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the property market was very buoyant last year. To meet the strong demand, the Government had increased the supply of land for private residential development. In 2010, the Government sold land that can potentially yield about 13,300 units. For the first half of 2011, the Government launched sites under the Government Land Sales programme that can yield another 14,300 units. As at the beginning of this year, there were about 33,000 unsold units, or roughly equivalent to three years’ take-up of private residential units, if we go by the average annual take-up rate over the past four years.

 

     Despite the large supply in the pipeline, market sentiments had remained bullish, with signs of renewed exuberance in the market towards the end of last year. The majority of the buyers were Singaporeans. We were particularly concerned that low interest rates plus excessive liquidity in the financial system, both in Singapore and globally, could further drive up demand for private housing. This, in turn, could cause prices to overshoot economic fundamentals. The interest rates would also not continue to stay low. Purchasers who over-extended themselves financially could be caught

Column: 2511

     Therefore, the Government introduced additional measures in January to pre-empt a property bubble from forming. These measures aimed to temper demand, encourage greater financial prudence among property purchasers and discourage short-term speculation.

 

     We will continue to monitor the property market closely and take further steps, if necessary, to promote a stable and sustainable property market.

     Er Lee Bee Wah (Ang Mo Kio):  Mr Speaker, Sir, I would like to ask the Minister what is the intended end-result of the latest cooling measures.  Would the Minister like to see property prices stabilise or go down?

     Mr Mah Bow Tan: It is not the intention of the latest measures to crash the market, certainly, but rather to cool the market. Given the current situation of strong economic growth, low interest rates and a lot of liquidity in the market, it is not unnatural to expect that property prices would remain strong. But we do not wish to see prices shooting up so fast as to be unsupportable by the fundamentals in the market. At the same time, we also do not want to see a lot of short-term speculation in the market. Our measures in January were pre-emptive in nature because we wanted to act before a bubble forms. They were calibrated so as to cool the market and not to crash the market. They were very targeted at the short-term speculators and not at genuine home-buyers. If we meet these objectives, then I think the measures would have done the job. It is a little too early to say at this point in time what the outcome of these measures are, what the results are, because it has only been slightly over a month since we introduced the last set of measures. We will continue to monitor the

     The increases were necessary to meet rising costs and, more importantly, because polytechnics continue to invest in capability-building initiatives in order to deliver high-quality education to Singaporeans. Where justified, it is preferable to have regular but small fee increases rather than a significant hike in any one year. Even after the current increase, the Government continues to heavily subsidise the cost of polytechnic education.
