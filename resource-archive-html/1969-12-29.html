WORKMEN'S COMPENSATION
ORDINANCE
(Amending legislation)


    1. Inche Abdul Aziz Karim asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour if he will consider introducing legislation to amend the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance (a) by extending the applicability of the Ordinance to all categories of employees earning $750 or less per month; and (b) by increasing the numbers of months of earnings and the maximum amounts prescribed for compensation in section 8 of the Ordinance where injury sustained results in the death or in permanent total disablement of the employee.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Labour (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour): Mr Speaker, Sir, manual workers are eligible for workmen's compensation under the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance, 1954, irrespective of the amount of their monthly earnings. It is only in respect of non-manual workers that the ceiling of $400 earnings per month is fixed so that non-manual workers earning more than $400 per month will not be eligible for workmen's compensation.

    If the Ordinance is amended in the manner suggested by the Member for Kallang, it would have the effect of denying workmen's compensation for manual workers should their earnings exceed $750 per month. As for non-manual workers, statistics for the 11 months of January to November 1969 show that the number of non-manual workers who were paid workmen's compensation was 14 out of a total of 7,952. This works out to 0.17 per cent, the rest being all manual workers. Raising the eligibility of non-manual workers for claims of workmen's compensation would, therefore, have little

Column: 300

or no practical effect since it is well known that manual workers are exposed to greater risks to their personal safety in the course of their employment than non-manual workers.

    Under section 8 of the Ordinance, the amount of workmen's compensation for victims of accidents resulting in death is calculated on 36 months of their monthly earnings or $7,200 whichever is the less. Where the victim is permanently disabled, the workmen's compensation is calculated on 48 months of his monthly earnings or $9,600 whichever is the less. The maximum payable for death or permanent disablement of the employee is based on an earning of $200 per month. This figure is above the average monthly earnings of workers in Singapore which, according to a sample survey made in 1968, amounted to $180.60 per month. Amending legislation will be made to revise the scales of benefits should the average monthly earnings of workers in Singapore exceed $200.

    Inche Abdul Aziz Karim (Kallang): Does the Parliamentary Secretary value a man's life only at $7,200 or 36 months' wages?

    Mr Sia Kah Hui: I think that is laid down as compensation; that is, a man's life-span of work expected of him. But I am sure, if the Member has any suggestion to make, we will be very glad to receive it.

    Mr Sia Kah Hui: Sir, depending on the individual case, I am sure the deceased's family is at liberty to seek workmen's compensation or to seek redress in the High Court.

    Inche Abdul Aziz Karim: Does the Parliamentary Secretary not agree with me that a workman would get more

LABOUR SHORTAGE
(Survey of situation in Singapore)


    2. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour (a) whether there is a shortage of skilled, semi-skilled and unskilled labour in Singapore recently due to the Government's policy of rapid industrialisation; (b) what steps have been taken or are being taken to remedy the situation; and (c) if he will consider an overall survey to ascertain the actual situation.

Column: 302



    Mr Sia Kah Hui (for the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour): Mr Speaker, Sir, according to the categories and numbers of persons currently registered for employment in the Employment Exchange, there appears to be a shortage of skilled labour and to a lesser extent semiskilled labour in Singapore. However, there is no shortage of unskilled labour. Out of a total of 52,115 persons registered for employment at the Employment Exchange as on 30th November, 1969, 1,475 were registered as skilled workers, 3,259 as semi-skilled workers and 28,854 as unskilled workers. The remaining persons were registered as commercial and domestic workers.

    The steps taken by the Government to meet the immediate shortage of skilled workers include (i) encouragement to industrial concerns to undertake their own training programmes to meet their demands for skilled workers; (ii) permission for industrial concerns to bring in skilled labour from outside Singapore; (iii) provision of special courses to upgrade and retrain workers in the Vocational Institutes and the Industrial Training Centres by way of day-release and evening classes. To meet the requirements of industries for skilled personnel on a long-term basis, Government has taken measures to restructure the educational system so that there will be an increasing number of school-leavers in the coming years with vocational and technical training.

    From time to time, various organisations, including the Ministry of Labour, the National Productivity Centre, the Technical Education Department of the Ministry of Education, and the Economic Research Centre of the University of Singapore, have carried out their own surveys to ascertain the manpower requirements in certain specific areas or industries. At the moment, the question of an overall survey is not an urgent one. If at some future date the situation demands it, Government will consider making an overall survey to ascertain the skilled manpower requirements of Singapore.

(1) Definition of "sexual sterilization"

    This amendment relates to the definition of "sexual sterilization". As defined in the original text, the surgical removal of the female or male reproductive organs would all come under the purview of this Bill. It was pointed out that this was too broad a definition. The definition has, therefore, by the amendment, been restricted to exclude the removal of such organs which are essential for reasons of medical treatment. The suggestion was made by Professor L.C. Green, former Professor of Law in the University of Singapore, in a written representation to the Select Committee.
