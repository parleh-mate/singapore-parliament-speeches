    Order read for resuming adjourned debate on Question [14th December, 1965],

    `That the following Address in reply to the speech of the Yang di-Pertuan Negara be agreed to:-

    Question again proposed.

    The Minister for Education (Mr Ong Pang Boon): Mr Speaker, Sir, the enforced emergence of Singapore as an independent and sovereign State is an event that has far-reaching consequences on the lives of the people of our country. The chain reaction started by this dramatic event has begun, and has become apparent in the allocation of expenditure in the first Budget of independent Singapore. Foreign affairs and defence are responsibilities which we can no longer avoid. These must be paid out of revenue. In the present state of affairs, it must mean that other fields of responsibilities cannot claim as large a share of the budget as had been the case in the past. Thus, the amount devoted to education next year has dropped from about one-third of the budget to 24 per cent. However, it must be noted that there has been a drop only in proportional terms of the whole budget. The actual amount allocated for education in absolute terms has increased, being $129 million or an increase of $16 million more than this year. In other words, education is still amply provided for, and will continue to make

progress. This is because the Government fully realizes that investment in education is crucial to economic and social progress, and that "no single factor is more likely to bring about a more fair and just society than equal opportunities for education for all."

    Before I outline the Government's education programmes for the future, let me briefly summarize our past achievements. By the end of this year, the present Government will have spent nearly $654 million on education since 1959. In return for this big outlay of public funds, we have built up an education system that has become the envy of many less fortunate countries.

    We have achieved universal free primary education without making it compulsory. There are enough school places to admit every child aged six plus, not only now but for many more years to come. Every child of school-going age is assured of a place in a school using the medium of instruction of his parent's choice. Once admitted, he is assured of ten years of primary and secondary education, finishing at the minimum age of 16 with at least a Secondary Vocational School Certificate. Of those who obtain the School Certificate, no less than 25 per cent are admitted to Pre-University classes to prepare for the Higher School Certificate and entry to the universities and the Polytechnic.

    Our school enrolment has shown spectacular increases. In the primary schools, the enrolment in Government and Government-aided schools has increased from 261,000 in 1959 to 360,000 in 1965, or an increase of about 38 per cent. Secondary enrolment has increased in an even more spectacular manner, having risen from 44,000 in 1959 to 113,000 in 1965, that is, an increase of 157 per cent. We should note with satisfaction that our total primary and secondary enrolment of 473,000 represents about 25 per cent of our entire population.

    This expansion in enrolment has been made possible by heavy capital outlays on the construction of schools. In this year alone, 15 new schools and four

major extensions to existing schools have been completed. All in all, the Government has built 72 new schools of all types, with 1,700 classrooms - excluding special rooms like the teachers' common room, the school hail, etc. - affording more than 125,000 places. These schools have been constructed at an average rate of one school per month.

    In order to ensure that no child in Singapore shall remain illiterate on account of financial difficulties, free primary education has been introduced for children born in Singapore or whose parents are Singapore citizens. In addition, there is provision for 15 per cent of the secondary enrolment to be free. About 17,000 pupils have been granted free secondary education under this provision on account of their financial background. Malays who are Singapore citizens enjoyed special assistance in education. Malay children have been guaranteed free education from primary schools to the University. All in all, about 35,000 Malay pupils are enjoying free education at all levels. Annually, large sums of money have been allocated for scholarships and bursaries. In this year, the Government has spent $1.6 million on scholarships and bursaries, and this amount has enabled 40,266 poor students to study in the secondary schools, universities and the Polytechnic. In addition, the Government has also spent $259,000 in the provision of free text-books.

    In the past five years, the Government has fulfilled its objectives with regard to primary education. From now onwards, the emphasis on education will be:-

    (i) the improvement of the quality of education at all levels;

    (ii) the expansion of educational facilities in the secondary and tertiary levels;

    (iii) diversification of educational development which is essential for the development of an industrial society; and

    (iv) the further development of education for national unity and tolerance in a multi-racial, multi-lingual and multi-cultural society.

Work will also begin on two new primary schools under the 1966 school building programmes in Toa Payoh and MacPherson South Housing Estates.

    The construction of secondary schools will receive special attention next year. In the course of next year, 13 new secondary schools and nine major extensions to existing schools will be completed, thereby providing an additional 36,000 places for secondary students. Furthermore, work on eight new secondary schools under the 1966 building programme will commence early next year. The building programme next year will ensure that every child who is now in primary school and with the eligible qualification will be assured of a place in secondary school.

    Under the Second Development Plan, the Government will also build a number of secondary schools to be known as Junior Colleges specially for Pre-University students. This will make possible the optimum use of teaching staff and laboratory facilities and relieve the secondary schools of the pressure for accommodation up to the Secondary IV level. Construction of one of these Junior Colleges will commence early next year. The Junior Colleges will admit Pre-University students from all the language streams, thereby bringing integration one stage nearer.

    In the next few years, more attention will be paid to integrating our higher institutions of learning into the national community so that they will the better serve society. Universities represent the apex of the educational system in a country. The role and character of universities have varied from place to place and from time to time. Universities are simultaneously conservative and progressive institutions - conservative in the sense that they preserve the best in the culture of a society, and progressive in the sense that they introduce new ideas, new discoveries, and new inventions, usually for the betterment of society.     2. "The answer depends on whether the School Management can fulfil the requirements as laid down in the Government Grant-in-Aid Regulations 1957, with which all schools are expected to comply. For example, the school must, among other things, hold a legal title to the land and have the amount of its own contribution in hand. If the School is in a position to comply with the Regulations. I see no reason why its application for capital grant for building cannot be considered together with others.

    As a matter of fact, Pin Ghee High School is more a Primary than a Secondary school. In 1957 it began with one Secondary I class; by 1964, it had only 6 Secondary classes. The academic standard achieved has been appalling: in 1964, out of 36 candidates who sat for the Government Secondary IV Examination, only 6 passed, of whom 5 passed with Grade III and 1 with Grade I certificate. Since 1957, when this school began its first Secondary I class, it has been encouraged to improve its school buildings and equipment. The Government approved in 1962 capital grants amounting to $5,241 for the school to purchase science equipment and furniture in order to help the school develop gradually its secondary classes into a proper secondary school. But unfortunately, the Management Committee of the school not only had not been able to make any improvements, but also allowed the school administration and discipline to deteriorate. For instance, the school, under its former Management, was known to have sold textbooks at list price, thus making a profit of some $9,000 a year from students, and yet it owed several bookshops a total of $18,000 in September 1964. No serious attempts had ever been made to improve the school buildings, which still consist of 7 blocks of wooden structures with asbestos and attap roofing. Repeated requests by the health authorities to improve its health requirements had not been fully complied with. Only one wooden plank room is available for an all-purpose laboratory; Chemistry, Physics and Biology experiments are all conducted in it. The secondary department of the school is notorious for indiscipline. In November 1964, a pupil was caught intimidating other pupils to subscribe to Barisan Sosialis Party Fund. More recently, school monitors, two of whom were known to have attended meetings organised by the Barisan Sosialis for its cadres, defied the authority of the principal and engaged in producing and

distributing illegal handbills giving a distorted picture of Government policy towards Chinese education.

    Unless the School Management Committee can work selflessly and genuinely for the future well-being of the school, I do not see how all the present indiscipline, poor management and political manipulation by the Barisan Sosialis are going to help develop this school.

    Work has already begun on the construction of a secondary school at 10 m.s. Changi Road at a cost of $1.1 million plus an additional $250,000 for furniture and equipment. It will cater for students living in the Kampong Chai Chi, Changi, Tampines, Pulau Ubin and Pulau Tekong areas who now have to travel long distances to attend school.
