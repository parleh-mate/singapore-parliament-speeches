COMPENSATION FOR MASSACRES
BY JAPANESE ARMY
IN SINGAPORE


    1. Mr Tee Kim Leng asked the Prime Minister if he will make a statement on his recent discussions with the Japanese Prime Minister on the question of compensation for the massacres by the Japanese army in Singapore in the last war.

    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Sir, on the 25th of May, I paid a courtesy call on the Prime Minister of Japan and in the course of our conversation, he referred to the unfortunate incidents which took place during the war in Singapore in which our civilians were massacred by the Japanese army. He expressed his sincere regret for what had happened. I told him that there were strong feelings on the subject and that it was a very emotional issue. I told him also that the Japanese Government and people will have to make atonement to the people of Singapore. My conversation was an informal one because foreign affairs as distinct from trade relations are the responsibility of the United Kingdom Government, and as I have stated in answer to another question by the Member for Pasir Panjang, the question of war reparation including compensation for atrocities and war damage was the responsibility of the United Kingdom Government which in 1951 signed a treaty of peace with Japan.

    4. Mr Tee Kim Leng asked the Minister for Education to state (a) the method adopted in transferring out from a school, teachers therein who are related to each other; (b) how many such teachers have been transferred this year; and (c) how many have not been transferred.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, before I answer the question proper, may I be permitted to explain as follows? On the 24th August, 1959, a circular was issued to all school principals requiring them to declare a list of staff employed in each school of those who happen to be close relatives, defined as: - spouses, parents, brothers, sisters, or children. The purpose of the circular was to ensure fair and proper running of schools by breaking up teams of close relatives working in the same school, as far as possible.

    In reply to the first part of the question as to the method adopted in transferring out teachers who are closely related in accordance with the circular: in the case of Government schools, such teachers are generally posted to different schools by the Ministry itself. In the case of Government aided schools, the transfers are usually arranged mutually between teachers and principals of schools concerned, subject to approval by the Ministry.

    In reply to the second part of the question: as most transfers of this nature were effected during the two years 1960 and 1961, there were only four transfers for this year.
