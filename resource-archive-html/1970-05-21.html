WORKSHOP INSTRUCTORS
AND STUDENTS
(Insurance coverage against physical injury)


    2. Mr Ch'ng Jit Koon asked the Minister for Education whether there is any insurance coverage against physical injury resulting from accidents for workshop instructors and students in industrial training centres and vocational institutes doing practical subjects.

Column: 34



    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Education (Dr Lee Chiaw Meng) (for the Minister for Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, I propose to answer this question in two parts.

    First of all, concerning the insurance coverage against physical injury resulting from accidents for workshop instructors, there is no separate insurance coverage against physical injury resulting from accidents for them in the industrial training centres and vocational institutes. But being public officers, if they sustain injuries in the course of discharging public duties, they are covered under two schemes.

    The first scheme is the Pensions Ordinance, 1956. Under Regulation 19 of this Ordinance, the quantum of compensation where payable as a result of injury is in addition to any pension, gratuity or other allowance granted under the same Ordinance. Subject to the provisions of the Ordinance, the quantum of the annual allowance payable as compensation ranges from 5/60ths (for slight impairment of earning capacity) to 1/3rd (for total destruction of earning capacity) of an officer's emoluments at the date of injury.

    The second scheme is the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance. Under this Ordinance, where a public officer (including a technical teacher, workshop instructor, workshop attendant, and workshop assistant) is classified as a "workman" and is in receipt of a salary not exceeding $400 per month, he is entitled to be compensated under the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance provided he is not paid a pension or gratuity for such injury. The rate of compensation is governed by the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance.

    The second part of the question deals with insurance coverage for students. The Ministry assists students in taking personal accident insurance coverage when they are sent for practical training to any industrial establishment, job sites, and schools. Students are advised but not compelled to participate in the insurance scheme. So far all students

Column: 35

concerned have joined the scheme, as the rate of premium is relatively low, i.e. $1 per student per month for a capital sum assured for $10,000.

    In the case of injury caused by the negligence or omission of the teacher or instructor in the vocational institutes, the Government and the teacher or instructor concerned are jointly liable, and it is for the injured party to determine which party or both parties to sue for damages. However, the school authorities will not be liable if all necessary precautions and safeguards have been taken to prevent accidents in schools. Adequate action has been taken to ensure that safety precautions are observed in all schools, vocational institutes and industrial training centres.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the onus of providing insurance coverage against accidents not caused by negligence of the school authorities in industrial training institutions should rest on the parents, and I do urge parents to take insurance coverage for their own children.

    Mr Ch'ng Jit Koon (Tiong Bahru)(In Mandarin): Sir, the Parliamentary Secretary has stated that teachers of Government schools sent to the vocational institutes as instructors are covered under two schemes. But what about those teachers in the Government-aided schools?

    Dr Lee Chiaw Meng: Mr Speaker, Sir, if they draw a salary of less than $400 a month, they will come under the Workmen's Compensation Ordinance. If they draw more than that, I presume they will sue the school authorities for damages.

TIMBER LORRIES
(Cause of traffic congestion and hazard to road users)


    3. Inche Rahmat Bin Kenap asked the Minister for Defence if a survey has been made by the Police as to whether timber lorries using busy roads, like Nicoll Highway, in and near the

Column: 36

City centre during peak periods are a cause of traffic congestion and a possible hazard to other road users; and whether and what remedial action will be taken.

    The Minister for Defence (Mr Lim Kim San): Mr Speaker, Sir, it is true that timber lorries travelling along the busy roads in and near the City centre during peak periods are a cause of traffic congestion and also a possible hazard to other road users, as they are huge vehicles carrying heavy loads and moving at a slow speed not exceeding 20 miles per hour. These lorries transport timber logs from the railway yard at Keppel Road to the various sawmills, most of which are situated in the Kallang Road and Lavender Street areas, and must inevitably travel through the City centre. It would not be conducive to our timber trade, however, if transportation of timber logs to sawmills were to be restricted to outside the peak periods of the day.

    The Traffic Police are watching the situation very carefully, and drivers of timber lorries are advised to keep to the left-hand side of the road so as not to hinder vehicular traffic and also endanger other road users. The Traffic Police are also taking stringent action against these lorry drivers for failure to comply with the traffic laws for speeding, for causing obstruction, and having smoky exhausts. For the whole year of 1969 and since the beginning of this year, no case of traffic accident involving timber lorries occurred in the City area.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, an area South of Kranji industrial estate is being developed by the Jurong Town Corporation for re-siting sawmills at present situated in various parts of the Republic. Those sawmills in the City area which will be affected by the urban renewal scheme will also be given alternative accommodation in this area. Once this project is completed, timber lorries will not and need not use the already heavily congested roads in and near the City centre.

HAWKERS
(Eligibility for public assistance allowances)


    4. Mr Ch'ng Jit Koon asked the Minister for Social Affairs whether hawkers are eligible for public assistance allowance and, if not, why.

    Mr E. W. Barker (Acting Minister for Social Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, hawkers are not eligible for public assistance allowances as long as they are holders of a current hawker's licence. A licensed hawker is presumed to be in regular employment, and it is regretted that the Social Welfare Department cannot consider such cases for public assistance. This presumption is necessary as it is not possible to assess the actual income of hawkers, particularly the itinerant ones.

    The Department will, however, consider applications for public assistance from hawkers who are temporarily incapacitated by illness, provided the applicants can produce the necessary medical certificate.

    Hawkers who have become unemployed and have surrendered their current hawker's licences to the Hawkers Department will be eligible for public assistance.

PARLIAMENTARY PENSIONS (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.50 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    There is one underlying objective in the recent exercises of increasing Members' allowances and Ministers' salaries and of providing for their pension. That underlying objective is to remove the financial disincentive in getting men of ability and integrity to undertake political life.

    The proposed increase in Members' allowances and Ministers' salaries is a further step in this direction. The first step was made when the Government passed the Parliamentary Pensions Act in June last year.

    In moving the Second Reading of the Bill, the Minister for Finance said, and I quote from Hansard, Vol. 29, No. 1, col. 18:

    `We cannot expect to get future leaders of calibre on the cheap, whatever was the case in the past, and it is as well that the people

Column: 41

of Singapore realize and accept this fact. Unless Singaporeans want to settle in the future for a mediocre crack-pot or corrupt leadership, they must not believe they can continue to expect men of ability in future to suffer severe financial sacrifices in pursuit of a hazardous political career. In my opi nion, a mediocre crack-pot or corrupt political leadership will ruin this country in an incredibly short time, and such ruination could be final, complete and permanent, as Singapore has no natural resources or a large economic base on which to effect a recovery.'

The Minister for Finance concluded, and I quote:

    `And to ensure the continued prosperity and success of Singapore, the cost of the pension is a small price to pay.'

    The Parliamentary Pensions Act has now been in force for about a year. In this time, it has been found that some of the provisions of the Act are too restrictive and need modification.

    For instance, the Act provides that to qualify for pension, a Member would have to put in at least ten years' reckonable service and be not less than fifty years of age. This means that a Member would have to serve not less than two full terms of five years each in order to qualify for pension. A Member who serves for two terms, any of which is less than the full term of five years, would therefore not be eligible for a pension unless he is elected for a third term.

    In Australia, a Member of Parliament can qualify for a pension at the age of forty years, while in New Zealand Members of Parliament are entitled to pensions after nine years' service and on attaining the age of fifty years. In Canada, on the other hand, a pension is paid to a Member of Parliament who serves more than two terms.

    It is therefore proposed to amend the Act so that a Member who has put in nine years' reckonable service, instead of ten, would qualify for pension. The qualifying age for the grant of pension is also being reduced from fifty years to forty-five years.

    At present, only a Speaker who is also an elected Member of Parliament may qualify for pension, and it is proposed to amend the Act so that a Speaker who is not an elected Member may

Column: 42

also qualify for pension. It is only reasonable that a person who serves in Parliament as Speaker, whether or not he is an elected Member, should be eligible for a pension for the service he puts in.

    There may also be cases where persons who are Members of Parliament subsequently cease to be Members without having served the minimum period to qualify for pension but who, nevertheless, on ceasing to be Members, may hold public offices such as High Commissioners or Ambassadors for several more years. Such persons should be adequately provided for in their declining years. It is therefore proposed to amend the Act so that a person who has completed less than nine years' reckonable service in Parliament, but the aggregate of whose service in Parliament together with the service in a public office makes up nine years, would qualify for pension under the Act. For this purpose, "public office" is to be defined as the office of High Commissioner or Ambassador or such other office as the President may, from time to time, declare in the Gazette.

    This Bill, when passed, will be made retrospective to 1st July, 1969, the date when the principal Act came into operation, to enable the provisions of the principal Act and the amending Act to dovetail.

    Question put, and agreed to.

CIVIL LIST AND PENSION BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.01 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    It has been the tradition in many countries for the Legislature to provide by law for a Civil List for their Presidents. And this tradition is enshrined iii our Constitution, Article 4 of which

Column: 44

reads: "The Legislature shall by law provide a Civil List for the President." Part I of this Bill, therefore, seeks to make such provision in accordance with the Constitution.

    Members will note from the Schedule to the Bill that the President will draw an annual salary of $48,000 which is tax free. In addition, he will get an annual entertainment allowance of $10,000, also tax free. The expenses for maintaining the President's household, together with salaries for his personal staff, come to $474,000 a year. By clause 3 of the Bill, the amount payable under this Schedule is charged on and payable out of the Consolidated Fund.

    Opportunity is also taken in the Bill to provide for the President's pension when he retires. At present, there is no statutory provision for the payment of a pension to a former President. All civil servants are given pension rights; in Singapore, all persons in employment have at least the protection of the Central Provident Fund to provide for them and their families in their retiring years; and, since July last year, even politicians who have been M.P.s can have pensions provided certain conditions are met. It is only reasonable therefore that our President, who holds the highest office in the land, should be superannuated for his services on his ceasing to be a President.

    Part II of the Bill, therefore, provides for the payment of pension or gratuity to "former" Presidents. Clause 8 of the Bill provides that a former President, who has ceased to hold office, shall be granted a pension or gratuity of such amount and under such circumstances as Parliament may determine. No specific period of service as a President is prescribed, and the Bill leaves it to the discretion of Parliament to determine the amount to be paid. This discretion will give flexibility to meet changing circumstances, and Parliament can be relied upon to determine an amount that would allow a former President to maintain a standard of life in keeping with the exalted office he has held.

    Question put, and agreed to.

MERCHANT SHIPPING
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.12 p.m.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Yong Nyuk Lin): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The main object of this Bill is to enable Singapore to adopt the International Load Line Convention, 1966. The Load Line (i.e. Plimsoll Mark) indicates the maximum depth to which a ship can be loaded. At present, the Load Line Convention, 1930, applies to Singapore, and is implemented by United Kingdom legislation which was extended to Singapore. The new International Convention of 1966, which came into force in July 1968, takes into account modern technological advances in shipbuilding and provides for tankers of over 600 feet in length. The new Convention also provides certain advantages - for example, it allows deeper loading which results in about 4 per cent increase in carrying capacity, and therefore earning capacity, for some tankers. It is necessary for Singapore to accept the 1966 Convention so that Singapore-registered ships can enjoy the benefits of deeper loading, and furthermore ships calling at Singapore are often loaded in accordance with the 1966 Convention.

    For the purpose of acceptance of the Convention, the Merchant Shipping Ordinance needs to be amended. The

Column: 47

existing load line provisions are deleted and new provisions substituted. Members may wish to refer to relevant provisions contained in clause 23 of the Bill which, inter alia:

    (a) prohibits ships from sailing without Load Line certificates;

    (b) empowers the Minister to exempt ships under certain circumstances, and to appoint classification societies to issue Load Line certificates; and

    (c) empowers the Minister to make regulations for the purpose.

    Another amendment provides for the licensing of all harbour craft operating for trade or business within Singapore territorial waters. At present harbour craft which are registered either in Singapore or elsewhere are exempted from licensing. This means that such craft are not subject to control and can therefore avoid safety and other requirements. There have been instances where condemned craft sold for breaking up as scrap were subsequently registered under foreign flags and being operated as harbour craft. This is clearly undesirable on grounds of safety. The object is achieved by amending section 453 of the Ordinance, vide clause 36 of the Bill.

    The rest of the provisions in the Bill deal with amendments recommended by the Law Review Commissioners, and are mainly amendments consequential on constitutional changes.

    The purpose of the Second-hand Dealers (Amendment) Bill is to empower the Police to enter and search the premises of unlicensed second-hand dealers. The Ministry of Social Affairs is the administrative authority for the Second-hand Dealers Ordinance, whilst the Police are responsible for licensing and control of such dealers. The Ordinance now only empowers the Police to enter and search without warrant the premises of licensed second-hand dealer shops when they have reason to suspect that stolen or lost property is kept therein. The Police have no such power, however, in respect of premises where second-hand goods are sold without a licence. To justify their entry into such unlicensed premises, the Police have to rely on the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code. The procedure is cumbersome in that the Police must obtain, in the first instance, a search warrant from a Magistrate's Court. If there is insufficient time to apply for a search warrant, a list of the articles stolen or missing must be taken down in writing with a declaration stating that a robbery or theft has been committed and, moreover, the person who lost the goods or his representative must accompany the police officer in the search. Powers of summary search are limited to certain special circumstances, for example, where the premises to be searched are used by any person who has been convicted of receiving stolen property or harbouring thieves.

    Sir, it is therefore proposed to amend the Second-hand Dealers Ordinance in order to give the Police power to enter and search without warrant any shop where there is reason to suspect that any person is dealing in second-hand goods without a licence and to seize such goods found therein.

    This is a simple piece of social legislation necessitated by the inconsiderate behaviour of some smokers in public places, such as cinemas and theatres. I think Members will agree that it is a matter of survival for ourselves that our society should be a closely knitted one with special emphasis on the strict exercise of social discipline by its members. Such a pattern of life makes it imperative for every member of society to value the comfort and convenience of his fellow citizens as much as his own, and that he should completely and voluntarily refrain from acts that make life miserable for others.

    In matters such as smoking in public, the ideal solution is for the smoker to exercise self-discipline and to see to it that his smoking does not cause any discomfort to persons around him. However, this has not always been the case. Only after traditional forms of public exhortation have failed to produce the desired effect is the law being invoked to give a helping hand, in fact, the Ministry of Health has given attention to the problem of smoking in cinemas and theatres since 1966. The voluntary ban on smoking in certain cinemas which started in 1967 has not been very fruitful in that, in practice, the patrons of the cinemas concerned have simply and invariably ignored the management's request to refrain from smoking.

    It must be pointed out that smoking under crowded conditions within enclosed spaces or in air-conditioned rooms causes a nuisance and irritation to the other occupants, especially the

Column: 58

non-smokers. Members of this House must have had the unpleasant experience of leaving the cinema or the theaters after the day's last show with their clothing and even the articles inside their pockets reeking of nothing but tobacco smoke. Further, the smoke screen in the auditorium not only interferes with proper viewing, but also makes the eyes smart. This Bill, however, by prohibiting smoking in cinemas and theatres, and certain other places that may be specified by the Minister, is designed to remove this source of nuisance and irritation, thereby enhancing the pleasure and comfort of the patrons of the cinemas and theatres as a whole.

    The Bill seeks to prohibit smoking in the auditorium of any cinema or theatre and in any specified building at any time during which these are open to the public. The expressions "cinema", "theatre" and "specified building" have been carefully defined. The prohibition on smoking is confined only to the auditorium of cinemas and theatres to which members of the public are admitted upon payment of admission fees, and only during the periods when these are open to the public. The premises of clubs, associations and the like have been expressly excluded from this prohibition. In order to obviate the need for an amending Act at a future date, the Minister is empowered, under clause 3, to specify in the Gazette any particular building or class of buildings or part thereof to which members of the public have access as being a place or places in which smoking shall not be permitted either permanently or for such period or periods as he may deem fit.

    The penalty for smoking in prohibited places is a fine not exceeding $500 and enforcement action will be by means of ticketing. This device has been found to be very suitable, effective and expeditious in dealing with persons caught littering in public places under section 26 of the Environmental Public Health Act, 1968.

Column: 59



    The main task of enforcing the prohibition will fall on the public health auxiliaries attached to the Public Health Division and police officers. Managers of cinemas, theatres and other specified buildings are required to display notices in four languages in conspicuous positions stating that smoking has been prohibited and the penalty provided therefor.

    Responsibility has also been placed on managers and certain authorised employees of these places to request any persons smoking therein to cease smoking forthwith, and if such persons fail to comply with their request, to require them to leave the place forthwith without entitlement to refund of ticket money. Should the management encounter any difficulty, they may seek the assistance of any police officer or a public health auxiliary. Provisions have also been included for the punishment of any person who abuses, hinders or assaults the manager or any authorised employee.

There is nothing more effective than the reproachful eye of the public to prevent a smoker from causing nuisance to his fellow citizens. With the law spelt out clearly on their side, the non-smoking public can now stand up to assert their right to more comfortable conditions in cinemas, theatres and other specified places.
