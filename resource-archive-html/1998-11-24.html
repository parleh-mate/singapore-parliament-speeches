EMISSIONS OF CARBON DIOXIDE PER CAPITA

(Singapore's ranking)


    1. Mr Simon S C Tay asked the Minister for the Environment how Singapore ranks in the emissions of carbon dioxide per capita, in comparison to other countries and what efforts are being made to reduce the emissions.

    The Senior Minister of State for the Environment (Encik Sidek bin Saniff) (for the Minister for the Environment): Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore ranked 24th in the world in terms of per capita carbon dioxide emission in 1995, based on a comparison between our carbon dioxide emission figures and data compiled by a US-based agency. Our per capita emission is lower than countries such as the USA, Canada, Japan, Germany, Denmark, the UK as well as Australia.

    In Singapore, power generation accounts for half of the carbon dioxide emission, and industries and road transportation account for much of the remaining 50%. A wide range of measures taken over the years has helped to keep down carbon dioxide emissions from these sources.

    In power generation, new technology and greater efficiency in generation have meant more electricity being produced from the same unit of fossil fuel. As the use of natural gas in place of fuel oil can reduce carbon dioxide emission by up to 25%, we are planning to use more natural gas for power generation in the future.

    Much effort has also gone into improving energy use efficiency. The Public Works Department has laid down energy conservation standards aimed at promoting energy-saving building designs. The Public Utilities Board conducts regular educational programmes and training courses on energy conservation. It also conducts energy audits to help the industrial and commercial sectors identify oppor-

Balance of cost cuts

    Three quarters of this cost saving package - $7.5 billion worth - comprises wage costs, through the CPF reduction and the NWC recommendations to reduce wages. In the public discussion of the CSC Report and also here over the last two days, the question has often been asked whether wages are bearing too large a share of the burden, and whether the Government can do more. Some have even suggested that the Government match the reduction in wage costs dollar for dollar.

    This is not possible. To understand why, we need to examine the composition of our GDP. We can think of GDP as the sum of all the incomes generated for the domestic production of goods and services. You add up all the money earned from productive effort and that is our output for the year. If you use this "income approach" to GDP, then you can divide GDP into three components:

    (1) Wages (including salaries and CPF) which make up 43% of GDP.

    (2) Indirect taxes (e.g. GST, ARF of cars) making up 10%.

    (3) The remaining 47% of the GDP is gross operating surplus, ie. earnings of companies and statutory boards, including depreciation. So wages, 43%; indirect taxes 10%; and the balance, gross operating surplus, 47%. So any cost reduction package will have to come out of either wages, indirect taxes, or essentially company earnings (companies meaning, including statutory boards).

    The economic crisis is already squeezing company earnings. Our aim is to help companies improve their bottom lines. Getting companies to bear a large share of this cost reduction would defeat this objective. So most of the cost reduction must come from the other two components of GDP, ie, wage costs and

Column: 1460

Government levies. Since total wage costs are much larger than Government charges, wages must inevitably account for a large part of the total cost reduction package.

    Of course companies doing well may pass on their cost savings to consumers, or invest them in business expansion or worker training. This includes, of course, statutory boards and Government-linked companies, such as JTC and Singapore Telecom. However, statutory boards and GLCs cannot simply run a deficit in order to give money away. Santa Claus has not come to town. They must operate as efficiently as possible so as to keep costs to a minimum. They also have a responsibility to operate commercially, just like any other company. Even hospitals must run efficiently and break even, given the cost recovery targets for the different classes.

    Once we deviate from this cardinal principle, and our statutory boards and GLCs ignore their bottom lines to operate contrary to commercial and economic logic, they will lose discipline, and become bloated and inefficient. Service will deteriorate. Eventually they must either go bankrupt and close down, or else the Government must bail them out, and raise taxes to pay for their deficits . This is exactly what has happened in many other countries.

    The $3 billion reduction in Government taxes and charges in this package is substantial, especially coming on top of the $2 billion announced in June, which was completely borne by the Government and which, judging by the debate over these last two days, has been largely forgotten. We already expect a budget deficit of 1% of GDP this year. Ministry of Finance estimates the deficit to grow to 3% to 4% of GDP next year. This is a sharp contrast from our usual surplus budgets.

And the next time we run into rough weather, which will happen sooner or later, we would have no more safety net, no more rabbits to pull out of the hat. We must therefore get back to prudent, balanced budgets as soon as possible.
