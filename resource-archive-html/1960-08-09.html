No doubt the chicken is not as good as the mother hen, and it is no use, Sir, laying eggs which are not in popular demand. White Leghorn eggs do not sell readily in the local market for some reason or other. Because they cannot sell their eggs, the farmers themselves show no enthusiasm for white Leghorn egg-layers. The Government research station at Sembawang has succeeded in producing a crossbreed that will lay 180 eggs per year. It is twice the output of the local fowl. These eggs fulfill the local demand for colour and flavour and, therefore, find a ready market. Moreover, the new strain is smaller than the Leghorn or the Hampshire. It costs less to rear them and, at the same time, they are productive for their size. But egg-laying, Mr Speaker, is not only a science. It is also an art. The Member for Joo Chiat will confirm this. The pullets of hens have to be persuaded to increase the production of eggs. One way of coaxing the hens to lay more eggs is to keep them in artificial daylight, sort of making them work overtime. But there are some hens which will just not lay at all, unless there are male birds or cockerels around to coax them. Even hens have their vanity. The Member for Jon Chiat claims to produce 280 eggs per year. Sir, that is something to cluck about. He is in the championship class. We do not know how he manages it, but the Government research station at Sembawang would very much welcome his services, and experimental hens at the station will no doubt look forward to a demonstration from the Member for Joo Chiat.
