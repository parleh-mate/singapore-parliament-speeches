    On the first question, the Political Study Centre is meant for civil servants, not only those in the Administrative services but also Government employees

    Dr Goh Keng Swee: I am much obliged, Mr Speaker, Sir. When we formed the Government, one of the difficulties that faced us was that we inherited a civil service which, by and large, was incapable of making political judgment. Our local civil service, though technically competent, had always been under the illusion that because they should be non- political in character they must avoid all interest in political affairs to the extent of even being ignorant of political issues. For instance, the opinion has always been expressed that, as civil servants, they must not indulge or dabble in politics, as if politics is a kind of parlour game intended for the amusement or diversion of people. The way we look at it is that political affairs is not a parlour game. On the contrary, it may be a matter of life and death whether or not we can build up a democratic system of sufficient strength to withstand the stresses that our country has to meet. This is not such a simple matter as some Members of the Opposition may think. It is not sufficient just to say that the civil servants are there to carry

out the policies and instructions of Ministers. When they carry out policies, they necessarily must have a wide sphere of discretion. It is not possible for Ministers to give directives in detail to enable the civil servant to meet any possible eventuality. If the role of the civil service were just to carry out instructions in a mechanical way, that is, to be guided purely by the letter of the General Orders or by such circulars and directives as may be issued, you can be sure that the Government would not be able to make any impact upon the people. This lack of experience or lack of involvement or lack of thinking on the part of the civil servants on political issues has been detrimental to themselves, because they have not been able to see how they have been separated from the masses, why that is a bad thing and why it is necessary for them to know what the masses think about political affairs, their hopes and aspirations, so that the execution of Government policy can be made more effective.

    As regards the school principals, it is obvious, Sir, that they have a vital role to perform in the task of nation-building. They are the key persons who will mould the form and content of the thinking of the younger generation. And like the civil servants, the school principals have not given any thought to the political issues of the day, nor have they made any effort to understand the general political background not only, in respect of Singapore and the Federation of Malaya but also the general resurgence of nationalism in Asia in the post-war years. These are matters they must appreciate, and it is the purpose of the Political Study Centre to run courses of instruction for these groups of Government employees. As I have said, the effect on them has been very marked and immediate. I myself have gone to the Political Study Centre after sessions and held informal discussions with civil servants and school principals on what they thought of these courses; and apart from a few diehards who cannot understand what it is all about or who cannot appreciate the necessity for re-thinking of the basic issues, the general effect has
