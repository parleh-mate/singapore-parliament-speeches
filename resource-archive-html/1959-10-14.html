    Dr Goh Keng Swee: Mr Speaker, Sir, Personal Secretaries (Political) are political appointments. They hold office at the pleasure of the Government in power. They presumably will be removed from office should at the next election another Party form the Government. There is therefore a basic distinction between the Political Secretary and the career civil servant whose duty it is to serve impartially and to the best of his ability any elected government. The post of Political Secretary does not therefore offer a career and, because of this and the difference in functions and responsibilities from those of the career civil servants, is not subject to the disciplinary provisions of General Orders. The Political Secretary is a member of the political party in the government of the day. As such his public actions and utterances, be they made either in his individual or official capacity, have an important bearing upon the policy of the government. It is therefore his responsibility to clear with his Minister whether his actions or statements are consistent with government and party policy.     Sir, this is the first opportunity the P.A.P. Our basic attitude and philosophy on this question have been stated and restated on innumerable past occasions when the Party was in the Opposition. It has not been made earlier because up to the third meeting of the Internal Security Council on the 8th October it was not possible for us to make any statements on this matter. first meeting of the Internal Security Council, the provisions contained in sections 65 and 66 were considered, and the powers of the Internal Security Council were noted and placed on record in simple language as follows:-

    (i) "The competence of the Singapore Government to formulate and implement the policy affecting internal security is subject to the overriding powers of the Internal Security Council."

    (ii) "In addition to consultation and decisions on questions of policy, the Internal Security Council is competent to make executive decisions for the maintenance of internal security in Singapore, and any such decision which has been communicated to the Singapore Government must be given immediate effect."

    (iii) "The Singapore Government is competent to take executive action on its own authority in matters of internal security. For its part, the Internal Security Council is competent to take decisions in these same matters, and in so far as it does those decisions, by virtue of section 66, paragraph (2), endorse, supplement or override any decisions which the Singapore Government may itself have taken and constitute binding directives with which the subsequent decisions and actions of the Singapore Government must conform."

    (iv) "The Singapore Government should not take any action in any field which would be contrary to policy already agreed by, or decisions already taken by, the Council or would be likely to raise policy issues, or would have repercussions affecting internal security, without prior reference to the Council."

A fact worth noting is that in both the Federation and Singapore, the same British trading interests have investments. It is obviously in the interest of not only the British but also of Malaya to remain as one economy.     Coming to the details of the Bill, the main provisions relate (1) to amending the penalties for soliciting in public places for the purpose of prostitution or any other immoral purpose, and (2) to strengthening legislation relating to the possession, custody or control of any knife, offensive weapon, explosive or corrosive substance.

    Clause 3 of the Bill increases the penalty for soliciting by prostitutes for immoral purposes from the present fine of $20 or 14 days' imprisonment for the first offence and a fine of $50 or three months' imprisonment for subsequent offences, to a fine of $100 for the first offence and a fine of up to $500 or six months' imprisonment for the second and subsequent offences. It is well known that the activities of secret society gangsters are frequently centred on the proceeds of prostitution. The so-called protection of these loose women is a fruitful source of revenue to gangsters, who do not hesitate to assault those persons who will not contribute to their own protection. It is with the object of not only driving prostitutes off the streets in our campaign against vice but also of depriving gangsters of this fruitful source of revenue that we now propose to inflict these heavier penalties upon those who indulge in soliciting for immoral purposes.

    Section 4 of the Bill is designed to strengthen the existing legislation in respect of possession of corrosive or explosive substances and of offensive weapons which can be used to smash or to stab. The weakness of the present legislation is that the Police can only prosecute persons for possession of these offensive weapons when they are apprehended in a public place. The Police found it very difficult to prove ownership of caches of secret society offensive weapons when they are discovered in houses and other premises. Under the amending legislation, it will be an offence for a person to have acid or an offensive weapon on his person or in his possession or custody or control, unless that person can prove that the acid or weapon is intended for a legitimate purpose. Much ingenuity has been shown by gangsters in converting domestic or commercial implements

Column: 708

of everyday usage into formidable weapons which can be used to maim or kill. The parang, bearing scraper, and the ordinary screwdriver have all been used in the past to inflict terrible injuries on those who dared to oppose the gangsters. Under this proposed legislation, the possession of these implements will be made an offence, unless the person having or possessing them can prove that they are for legitimate purposes. The law-abiding householder or artisan need have no fear that he will be prevented from having in his possession such implements for genuine domestic purposes or for purposes of his trade, but resolute action will be taken against thugs whose sole aim is to terrorise or to kill.

These vicious implements can be easily carried and concealed, but with no more than a flick of the wrist they are converted into an instrument of death. The effect of this section of the Bill will be to make it illegal for these weapons to exist on this island. Members will see that penalties for having or possessing an offensive weapon, which I have just mentioned, includes a heavy fine or imprisonment for the first offence; and if the offence is repeated, a heavy prison sentence can be inflicted without option of the fine.
