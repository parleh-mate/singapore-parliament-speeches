(As reported from Select Committee)

Column: 9

a raging controversy between the Singapore traders on the one hand and the Singapore manufacturers on the other. This Bill seeks to regulate the manner in which a Tariff Advisory Commission is to conduct its investigations on any proposals to impose tariffs in Singapore. As such, therefore, the Bill seeks to change from time to time the free port status of Singapore. There was an expectation then that a controversy might rage between the upholders of a 100 per cent free port status of Singapore on the one hand, and the manufacturers who want to see new industries as fast as possible on the other hand.

    When the Bill was sent to Select Committee, the usual processes were set on foot, including advertisements in the papers asking for views of interested parties. A strange thing then happened. There was absolutely no response to these advertisements and the closing date for reception of memoranda had to be extended. Finally, two parties made their submissions, namely, the Joint Chambers of Commerce, who were somewhat apprehensive of the provisions of this Bill, and the Singapore Manufacturers' Association, who welcomed the general provisions of the Bill.

    We had a fairly cordial and friendly discussion with the interested parties in Select Committee, as a result of which two amendments were introduced and approved by Select Committee. May I draw the attention of the House to these two amendments? They are in clause 6. There is an addition to clause 6 and the additional sentence reads as follows - I should mention that clause 6 allows the Commission to co-opt, on an ad hoc basis, two or more persons with special knowledge of the subject at the inquiry. An amendment was made to this clause to allow and, in fact, to make it compulsory that one of these persons shall represent industrial interests and the other shall represent trading interests. This amendment was made after the Select Committee heard the views of the representors and agreed that they had a strong case. So clause 6 contains this additional sentence which reads:

    `At least one of such persons shall be a person representing industrial interests and

one other shall be a person representing trading interests.'

This gives effect to the proposal tabled by the Member for Tanglin who desires that the Commission should investigate, among other things, the effect of tariffs on the general level of employment in Singapore. And this, I think, Members of the House will agree is a completely unexceptional request and accordingly the Government agrees to the inclusion of this amendment.

    The other clauses do not call for any comment from me at this stage as they have been dealt with adequately, I think, on the Second Reading.

    You will recall, Sir, at the Budget session last year I mentioned that the integration of the City Council into the Central Government was a major administrative reform undertaken by the People's Action Party Government. I touched at some length on the make-up of the City Council and the manner in which it was proposed to integrate it into the Central Government. Since 1st January, 1961, the physical integration of the City Council other than the three trading departments has been in progress, and administratively and operationally they have been absorbed by various Government Ministries. This major administrative reform is in accordance with the pledge of the Party to bring about a smoother and more efficient public service to serve the people of the State. In the process, duplication of functions that had previously

Column: 17

existed has been removed, and it is expected that in the long run economies will be effected and efficiency raised.

    Sir, the Bill before the House is complementary to the Public Utilities Bill which was earlier moved for a Second Reading by the Prime Minister, and it provides for the transfer to the Government with retrospective effect from the 1st January, 1961, of all assets as well as liabilities and employees of the City Council and of the Rural Board other than those transferred to the Public Utilities Board for the running of water, gas, and electricity undertakings. These are contained in Part I of the Bill. It also enacts in Parts II to VI and VIII all provisions of the Local Government Ordinance, 1957, relating to streets, sewage, public health, etc . - matters which hitherto have been within the jurisdiction of the City Council and the Rural Board - and vests the necessary statutory powers in Government officers.

    The only new legislation is in Part VII which establishes District Offices and gives certain powers to District Officers.

    The enactment of this Bill will formally complete the Government's policy of integrating local authorities into the central administration. Since it is proposed to refer the Bill to Select Committee for closer examination and since the Explanatory Statement gives the objects and reasons of the Bill, I shall confine my remarks to two important aspects of the Bill: namely those concerning personnel and finance.

    The Bill provides that all employees of the City Council who are not transferred to the Public Utilities Board shall be transferred to the service of the Government at the same rate of pay or as near as may be the same conditions of service as those on which they were employed by the City Council. Upon the coming into operation of this Ordinance, these employees will become Government servants and will legally be governed by Government regulations and by the Constitution. But it is our intention, in so far as salaries are concerned, that all serving employees on the permanent establishment of the City

Column: 18

Council shall be allowed to retain, if they so desire, their existing salaries and salary scales on their transfer to the Government service.

    With regard to other terms and conditions of service, it is hoped, in consultation with the Staff Unions concerned, to bring about a set of conditions of service that would be acceptable and which would be patterned on the terms and conditions now applicable to Government servants. It is envisaged that in the process certain minor adjustments and changes may be necessary.

    The Bill also provides that persons transferred from the City Council who are already members of the Municipal Provident Fund will continue in membership of that Fund. In addition, such persons who are eligible for membership of the Fund at the time of transfer to the Government service will retain that eligibility. Officers of the City Council who are not members of the Municipal Provident Fund nor are eligible for membership thereof will be governed by the Pensions and Central Provident Fund Ordinances in every respect as they are applicable to Government servants. Persons appointed after the coming into force of this Bill will, of course, be governed by the Pensions Ordinance and the Central Provident Fund Ordinance. The management of the Municipal Provident Fund will be transferred to the Government together with all its assets and liabilities, and the accounts thereof will be kept by the Accountant-General. The assets and liabilities connected with the public loans of the City Council, including the sinking fund for those loans, are to be transferred to the Public Utilities Board under the Public Utilities Bill, but the Government will contribute to the Board in respect of those portions of outstanding public loans which were used to buy assets to be transferred to the Government under this Bill.
