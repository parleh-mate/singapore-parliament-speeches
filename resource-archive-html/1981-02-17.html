    'That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the opening of the First Session of this Parliament.".'.

    In clear and lucid terms, the President has outlined the three major tasks ahead of the Government on the evening of 3rd February, 1981.

    First, the importance of building a modern industrial society, based on sophisticated economic activities and a new relationship between management and labour. Second, Singaporeans must be made to recognise and support the pivotal role that defence and national service plays in underpinning Singapore's continued independence and prosperity. Third, the Government must complete the most crucial phase of the process of self-renewal of Singapore's unique political leadership system.

    Against a backdrop of a rather bleak and uncertain world economic climate, the President's warning to Singaporeans to be prepared for slower growth and some unemployment in the coming years ahead is indeed most timely. As far as the world economic situation is concerned, there is little we can do about it. But we can do something and the best that we can do is to make the best of what we have so that we can reduce or soften the impact of world recession on our economy.

    What better way to do this than by forging ahead in restructuring our industrial society and so move on towards the next stage of modernisation. This is the logical step forward in our process of industrialisation which was initiated and nurtured in the mid 1960s when the main thrust of industrial development was the provision of an infrastructure vital to the success of our conversion from an economy dependent upon entrepot trade to one based on industrialisation. The 1970s saw further development, with growth and expansion hinged upon the promotion of both local and foreign investments. Now, in the 1980s, we bring this modernisation process yet a step further

with the emphasis on higher technology and higher value-added products. With many developing countries competing in limited markets, countries with natural resources and large labour pools, we have no option but to speed up our modernisation process through industrialisation. Indeed, this will be the binding force of all Singaporeans.

    The conversion from low-skilled to high-skilled production entails the mobilisation of hitherto labour-intensive industries into capital-intensive ones, based on the use of more sophisticated machinery and backed by extensive efforts. This should also be complemented by an efficient service sector. Rigorous scientific education and practical industrial training will have to be stepped up.

    Our employers, management and labour cannot afford to sit back to enjoy the fruits of success of the previous decade. Instead, they will have to enter the next decade by coming together even more closely to form a new kind of relationship of co-operation. This, the President has delineated to be of vital importance in the face of poor economic growth in the uncertain future. I quote, "They must never work against each other. Old habits of confrontation between management and labour must cease." The now relationship has to be "one where both management and labour pull, and are seen to be pulling together." Team spirit must see us through the next few years.

    Now, if you look at the present scene, I suspect that the old, and maybe even the current relationship between management and labour is still largely based on management's assumption that most workers have no interest in work and they are a lazy, spoilt, unco-operative and soft lot of people, interested only in money. Hence, only through increments of money or through threat of loss of money can workers be made to work. This attitude towards workers appears to be similar to that generated by Mr F.W. Taylor, an American mechanical engineer of the late 19th century in the United States, when factories were beginning to

convert from "many labourers and few machines" to "many machines and few labourers." Mr Taylor is better known as the Father of scientific management. At that time, Mr Taylor discovered that the foremen and the workmen of the late 19th century had very low educational levels and, indeed, virtually all shop employees were technologically illiterate; some were so illiterate that they were unable to decide how work should be carried out. Hence, he evolved a system where planning of work was passed on to various specialists and the foremen and workers were expected just to carry out what they were told to do, and this remained the norm of American management practice for a long time. However, important changes took place in the American society which made some of his management practices rather obsolete; for instance, the rise in the level of education in America today and the higher standard of living of the Americans. Besides, industrial unionism had progressed to such an extent that management had to bargain collectively on many matters which were previously solved by unilateral action. Americans today are seriously considering modifying their management system.

    Singapore today is just about converting from "many workers and few machines" to "many machines and few workers". We must avoid having the same attitude Taylor had towards workers in the nineteenth century. A conservative and distrusting relationship between management and labour will only breed suspicion between the two. We must be prepared to change and change fast. We have procrastinated long enough.
