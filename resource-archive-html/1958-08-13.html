ACID BOMB THROWING


    2. Mr Lee Choon Eng asked the Chief Secretary if he is aware of the increasing use of acid bombs by gangsters in gang clashes in which innocent persons have been blinded or injured; and what measures the Government has taken or proposes to take against the use or possession of acid bombs.

    3. Mr John Ede asked the Chief Secretary to state the number of cases of acid throwing which have taken place since 1st January, 1958, the number of convictions in such cases; and whether this form of crime is on the increase.

Column: 549



    Mr David: With your permission, Sir, I will answer questions Nos. 2 and 3 together.

    The crime of using acid to cause hurt is on the increase. Between 1st January and 10th August this year, there were 15 known instances of such use. During the same period, three persons were convicted of offences under the Corrosive Substances Ordinance, 1955. Under this Ordinance, the carrying of acid for the purpose of causing hurt is subject to a maximum penalty of ten years imprisonment and using it for the same purpose a maximum penalty of imprisonment for life and whipping. Acid is very widely used for legitimate commercial purposes and it is extremely difficult therefore to prevent small quantities falling into the wrong hands. The Government is considering, however, whether any practical and effective measures are possible to control its possession and storage. The Government is also considering the imposition of the death penalty for the use of acid to cause hurt.

    In the days when we had a Legislative Council it was customary to refer to the "laying before" that Council of subsidiary legislation, and the form of words then appropriate still appears in many Ordinances of the Colony.     When a crime is committed, whether it be a gang fight, an assault or a murder, those who are responsible do not commit their crimes before the eyes of the police so enabling the police not only to arrest the offenders there and then, but also to furnish eye-witness evidence to bring a charge before the Court. On the contrary, they take very good care to ensure that there are no police officers present before they embark on a crime. The police consequently only obtain knowledge of the crime some time and, in certain cases, a quite considerable time, after it has been committed. The perpetrators have disappeared and the only method of detection open to the police is to question those members of the public who may have been witnesses to the crime or for some other reason have knowledge of it. This interrogation, coupled with any deductions which can be made from any material evidence to be found at the scene of the crime, is the only way of obtaining information which may lead to identifying the criminals. They have in addition their own knowledge, culled over a period of time, of the members of the secret societies involved - who are the fighters and so on - but that is of little value when it comes to the second stage of the proceedings, which is to bring the accused persons before

The reason is a simple one - that those concerned are too frightened of reprisals from other members of the gang if they furnish any information which would lead to the arrest of a member of the gang.

    These gangs have in short succeeded in establishing a very widespread form of terrorism which inhibits the law-abiding citizen from carrying out his duty in assisting in the enforcement of the law. Even if they are prepared, as they sometimes are prepared, to provide information in secret in such a way that they are satisfied that it will not become known that they have done so, nothing will induce them to stand up and give their evidence in open court or in the presence of the accused persons.
