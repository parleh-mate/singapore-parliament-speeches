ORAL ANSWER TO QUESTION
GOVERNMENT-LINKED COMPANIES
(Divestment programme)


    1. Mr Leong Horn Kee asked the Deputy Prime Minister (a) if he will give an update on the Government's divestment programme for Government-linked companies (GLCs); (b) whether the large Government shareholdings in GLCs have hindered their efforts to grow in the region; and (c) whether the Government

Column: 993

has any plans to reduce these shareholdings, especially in companies like Singapore Telecom and DBS Bank.

    The Deputy Prime Minister (BG Lee Hsien Loong): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government's programme to divest its shareholdings in the Government-linked companies is progressing smoothly. We conducted the initial public offering of Singapore MRT last year, bringing the Government's shareholdings down from 100% to 62%. The Government also divested Semac and Indeco Engineers completely in 2000 and 2001 respectively. Looking ahead, PSA Corporation and Singapore Power are slated for listing, while the three power generation companies (Power Seraya, Power Senoko and Tuas Power) will also be divested.

    With regard to our GLCs' ventures abroad, many have been successful. PSA Corp, for example, has gone as far as China, South Korea, Middle East and Europe. SingTel has footprints in Asia, including Thailand, Taiwan and India. DBS bank is in Thailand, the Philippines and Hong Kong.

    In some regional ventures, the Singapore Government shareholding in GLCs has been an advantage. It has given the GLCs' partners and host governments confidence that this is a bona fide operation with a reputable, long-term shareholder, which will uphold high standards of integrity and not cut corners for quick returns.

    But not all regional ventures of GLCs have been smooth and successful. Many companies treat certain industries as "strategic" and fundamental to their national interest and economic development. But they are cautious about allowing foreign players to enter or buy into their domestic market, especially players with significant foreign government ownership. In banking, for example, regulators typically apply stringent standards and single shareholding limits. In telecommunications, many countries limit foreign participation in their domestic market.

Column: 994



    Where the Singapore market has substantial and visible shareholdings in a company, regulators, political leaders, the press and local constituents tend to perceive the GLC to be Singapore Government-controlled, operating on an agenda that overrides normal commercial considerations. Such perceptions can unnecessarily politicise and complicate mutually beneficial commercial deal strategic alliances or mergers and acquisitions. From time to time, GLCs, like SingTel and DBS, have encountered such problems in their regional ventures.

    As a policy, the Government does not interfere in the strategic and commercial decisions of GLCs. All GLCs must operate strictly commercially, and are expected to yield proper economic returns. They are supervised by their boards of directors and accountable to all their shareholders, including the non-government shareholders who often own the majority of the shares.

    However, if these perceptions exist, we need to address the issue. In many industries, the scale of economic activity is increasingly global. To be competitive and have growth prospects, companies need to transcend national boundaries, especially in a small economy like Singapore. The recent wave of mergers and acquisitions in industries, like banking and telecommunications, reflects this.

    The Government has stated that its shareholdings in GLCs are not sacrosanct. With very few exceptions, GLCs are in play, free to acquire or be acquired in accordance with commercial dictates. If anybody wants to bid for them, whether the bidder is foreign or Singaporean, friendly or hostile, we will consider the proposition seriously. We cannot afford to take a parochial, xenophobic approach.

    Companies, like SingTel and DBS, are large players in Singapore, but small by world standards. To become significant players, they must, over time, evolve into more than Singapore companies. This is why we have lifted requirement for banks and telecommunication companies to be majority Singapore-owned.

Column: 995



    The Government presently owns 78% of SingTel, and 37% of DBS. We do not regard these as strategic stakes, to be permanently maintained. We are prepared to reduce significantly our shareholdings in SingTel and DBS over the medium term. How this should be done will be studied further. It can be by dilution through a merger, private sale to institutional investors, a SOTUS-type asset enhancement exercise for citizens, or some other means. Any divestment should naturally take place at the right price, and under the right market conditions.

    For SingTel, we will remove the "special share" which gives the Government the right to veto major decisions in the company. Instead, we will meet our national security needs through appropriate safeguards.

    For DBS, we will need to ensure that the Singapore character of the bank is preserved, following any divestment by the Government. It is important that we will be able to rely on the local banks to keep Singapore's financial system on an even keel in a crisis. The Banking Act and MAS Regulations help to ensure that the local banks retain their commitment to Singapore, even if they form strategic linkages or merge with foreign partners.

    First, MAS approval is required before single shareholdings can exceed three threshold levels, 5%, 12% and 20%. Second, MAS requires at least half of the board of directors of the bank to comprise Singapore citizens or permanent residents. This specific requirement may need to be modified, for example, in a merger with another party of comparable size, which is able to contribute to the bank and its continued growth and commitment to Singapore. But even in such a situation, MAS will continue to ensure a strong and meaningful Singaporean presence on the board of the merged entity.

    I hope that this statement of our intentions will dispel some of the misapprehensions about the nature of the Government's shareholdings in GLCs, and give them a freer hand to pursue M&A deals with prospective parties.
