NATIONAL SERVICEMEN
(Injuries and suicides)


    2. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence if he will say (a) how many of the 102 national servicemen who suffered injuries whilst under training in 1985 suffered them as a result of being compelled to continue with the exercises after they had complained that they were unable to continue; (b) how many such cases have come to light so far this year; and (c) how many cases of suicide were there in the army in 1985 and so far this year.

     The Minister of State for Defence (BG Lee Hsien Loong) (for the Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am answering on behalf of my Minister.

    In 1985, 102 servicemen suffered injuries in training which led to varying degrees of permanent disability. There is no report that any of them have been compelled to continue with their training despite their being unable to do so. So far this year, there has also been no such case reported of any national serviceman suffering injuries resulting in permanent disability as a result of such compulsion.

    Mindef does not condone ill-treatment of servicemen. If any Member of Parliament is aware of any ill-treatment case, I would urge him to inform Mindef about it as soon as possible, giving details of the case, so that Mindef can trace and investigate it promptly. If the Member for Anson wishes to seek further clarification on a specific case, he should table a question giving the particulars of the serviceman involved so that a proper answer can be given based on the facts of the case.

    Coming to suicides, there were six cases of suicide in the SAF in 1985. None of them were found to be service related. So far, there have been three cases of suicide in the Armed Forces this year. Two of them were not service related while the reasons for the third case have not yet been established as investigations are still in progress. Members will, of course, be aware that in suicide cases, in addition to Mindef's own investigations, Coroner's inquiries are also conducted.

HEAVY DEFENCE EXPENDITURE
(Rationale)


    3. Mr Chiam See Tong asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence what is the rationale, taking into account the current economic situation in Singapore, for spending so heavily on defence when Vietnam is not in a position to launch any attack on any other country in the next decade and China has abandoned its policy of exporting revolutions abroad.

     BG Lee Hsien Loong (for the Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Mindef budget for financial year 1986 was thoroughly discussed in this House in the Committee of Supply during the Budget debates. Both the amount to be spent and the need to maintain our defence expenditure in a recession were explicitly covered. There were no dissenting views. It is a long-standing policy of the Government to put aside 6% of the GDP each year for Mindef. As the economy has grown over the years, the amount we can afford has gone up. This has enabled us to build up the SAF and equip our national servicemen with better modern equipment. Last year, because of the recession, the economy contracted. This year again, growth was expected to be negative. 6% of the estimated 1986 GDP therefore represents a smaller absolute amount than 6% of the estimated 1985 GDP. As a result, there is a reduction of $172 million in the Mindef budget this year. The Services will have to strive hard to maintain their readiness and accomplish their missions with fewer resources.
