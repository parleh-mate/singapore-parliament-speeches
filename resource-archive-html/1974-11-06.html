ENLISTMENT (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.55 p.m.

    The Minister of Defence (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Special Constabulary comprises volunteers and part-time national servicemen. Its members are paid nominal allowances for their services during duty hours. Their employers are under no obligation to pay them the difference between their civilian pay and the allowances paid by the Police for their services. To assist the members of the Special Constabulary the Police Force has, as far as possible, arranged their duty hours such that they suffer minimal disruption in their civilian occupations or suffer loss of personal emoluments. However, in view of a severe manpower shortage, the Police Force intends to utilise the Special Constabulary more intensively. This may result in loss of pay to members of the Special Constabulary unless the Government is in a position to compensate the losses through make-up pay, as is being done in the case of national service reservists.

    Just like the Special Constabulary, the People's Defence Force is also composed of national servicemen and volunteers. Recently the Force was reorganised to enable it to undertake additional operational duties. Members of the People's Defence Force are only paid allowances during their training periods. Unless they are national service reservists, their employers are under no obligation

Column: 1092

to pay them any make-up pay. As a result they too would lose in terms of pay if they are not compensated.

    It is intended to remedy the situation through this Amendment Bill. The Bill seeks to repeal and re-enact section 24 of the Enlistment Act (i.e, the present provision providing for make-up pay). Apart from making provision for makeup pay for the categories who are already eligible, the Bill requires employers to pay make-up salary to both the volunteers and national servicemen (i.e, those who are performing part-time service) in the People's Defence Force and the Special Constabulary when they are called up for training or reserve service and to volunteers in these Forces when they are mobilized. Employers may deduct the amount paid from their payroll tax. Those employers who are not liable to pay payroll tax, and those whose payroll tax is insufficient for such deductions, may claim reimbursement from Government. Self-employed members of the People's Defence Force and the Special Constabulary can claim reimbursement also from Government.

    The Bill also provides that employers of volunteers in the People's Defence Force and the Special Constabulary are to continue to make Central Provident Fund contributions to the extent that they would be liable had the volunteers not been mobilized for full-time service or called up for training.

    The Bill repeals section 13 of the Vigilante Corps Act and with this repeal, volunteers in the Vigilante Corps will not be liable for full-time mobilization. There are two main reasons for repealing section 13. First, it is unfair to impose the liability of mobilization on volunteers in the Vigilante Corps. Secondly, it is not intended to mobilize the volunteers anyway, as there are large numbers of national servicemen doing part-time service in the Vigilante Corps.

    The other important points in the Bill relate to reinstatement in employment, prohibition against dismissal and leave of absence when performing service. The provisions in respect of these matters are found in sections 21, 22 and

    The Singapore Council of Social Service (Amendment) Bill was introduced in Parliament on 23rd October, 1974. This Bill, now before the House, seeks to make amendments to the Singapore Council of Social Service Act, 1968, in the light of the experience gained by the Council in administering the Act over the years.

    The Council was formally established with the following objectives:

    (a) to co-ordinate the voluntary welfare activities in Singapore not only between its member organisations, but also with other organisations;

    (b) to maintain and improve standards of voluntary social work and to encourage voluntary welfare efforts in fields not fully covered by Government;

    (c) to assist member organisations in their efforts to promote welfare activities in Singapore;

    (d) to initiate, assist and organise forms of relief and schemes of social service;

    (e) to disseminate information to members of the public and interest them in social welfare matters and to encourage their participation in these matters; and

    (f) to make recommendations to Government regarding legislation in the field of social welfare.

    In the course of administering the Act to achieve these objectives, the Council has encountered some problems and this is why my Ministry has proposed the amendments in this Bill. I shall now elaborate on these problems.

    Past experience shows that many purely religious and non-welfare related organisations have sought affiliation with the Council.

    However, the Council's main interest and focus is on welfare and social service. It is therefore proposed that the Singapore Council of Social Service Act be amended to limit purely religious and non-welfare related organisations from seeking affiliation to the Council. Such organisations which seek to carry

out welfare and social service activities and wish to affiliate with the Council must establish a separate structure for social and welfare activities and could then seek affiliation in their own right.

    Under current legislation, any person, firm, corporation or organisation which subscribes not less than $25 per year may be an associate of the Council. Likewise, any organisation affiliated to the Council must subscribe not less than $10 to the Council. The Council has, among its associates and member organisations, a wide-ranging group of bodies, including many well-known firms and corporations. The present subscription of $25 per annum for associates can be considered negligible. My Ministry therefore proposes an amendment to allow the Council to charge any subscription it thinks fit for associates as well as for member organisations.

    There are many people who are interested in welfare work but who, for some personal reason, do not wish to be attached to any particular welfare organisation. Such people should be encouraged, nevertheless, to contribute towards the work of the Council. It is proposed that the Council be empowered to appoint such people as honorary members, in addition to the patrons-in-chief and patrons whom the Council is already authorised to appoint. In this way, more people could contribute actively towards the work of the Council. Such honorary members will, however, be restricted from holding office and voting in the Board of Management of the Council.
