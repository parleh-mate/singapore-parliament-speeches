PUBLIC UTILITIES BOARD
(Results of salary consolidation exercise)


    3. Mr Low Yong Nguan asked the Prime Minister (a) if he will ascertain from the Public Utilities Board (i) how many officers of the Board have had their take-home pay reduced in the recent salary consolidation exercise; and (ii) how many officers have had to pay arrears to the Municipal Provident Fund in excess of their monthly salary; and (b) if he is satisfied that as a result of the exercise there have been improvement and simplification in the salary structures.

    Mr Lim Kim San (for the Prime Minister): Mr Speaker, Sir, the exercise on consolidation of salaries was to integrate the basic salary with the variable allowance into an all-in salary; in other words, what were allowances before have now been incorporated into the salary proper. As donation and contribution to the Provident Fund will be based on the consolidated salary, some 1,389 out of a total of 2,756 officers will have their take-home pay reduced. There is, however, an increase in the gross emoluments drawn by these officers because of the larger amount of donation made by the Board to the Provident Fund. For the 30-month period from 1st January, 1969, the total increase in the Board's donation to Provident Fund would amount lo 51,350,700, averaging about $40,000 a month.

    The result of the exercise on consolidation has been a reduction of 88 basic salary scales of senior, junior and subordinate officers grades to 25 key scales.

FOREIGN UNDESIRABLE PUBLICATIONS
(Measures to stop circulation)


    7. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for Culture what measures have been taken against the circulation of foreign undesirable publications, which are poisoning the minds of our young children.

    The Minister for Culture (Mr Jek Yeun Thong): Mr Speaker, Sir, by "foreign undesirable publications", I presume the Member for Thomson has in mind the Chinese chain comic books imported mainly from Hongkong. These books, as you can see from the samples here, are poor in quality, crude in production and seem to contain nothing except violence, bloodshed, ghosts and demons, and even sex. We consider them to be very harmful to the young children and have always been trying to slop them from being imported into Singapore. Our methods include Post Office and Customs interceptions and periodic surprise raids on bookshops known to be specialising in this kind of comic books. I myself have personally made appeals to the Singapore Chinese Booksellers' Association to restrain their members from importing such books. But there are always the black sheep in the flock. We have tried several ways to stop them, but they are always one up on us. I presume the profit that they derive from selling such books must be quite substantial.

    Moreover, our criteria on these books are different from those in Malaysia. This difference has been exploited by the operators in recent months. They first import the books quite openly into Malaysia and then smuggle them into Singapore through our Woodlands

Column: 57

check-point where we had no inspector to check on book parcels. However, since March this year, measures have been taken to remedy this. Since then, more than l9,000 copies of these books have been confiscated and destroyed.

    We will continue to keep a close watch on these operators and, if necessary, very stern action will be taken against them. But this alone is not enough. We want the help of parents, teachers and the civic-minded public. They should report to us whenever they find these books being sold in the bookshops.

BUILDING OPERATIONS AND WORKS OF ENGINEERING
CONSTRUCTION REGULATIONS


2.56 p.m.

    The Minister of State for Labour (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Labour): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move the motion standing in the

Column: 59

name of the Minister for Labour, namely,

    That this Parliament, pursuant to subsection (3) of section 66 of the Factories Act (Chapter 123, 1970 Edition), resolves that the Building Operations and Works of Engineering Construction Regulations, 1971, made on the 18th day of March, 1971, and published as Notification No. S 96 in the Gazette Subsidiary Legislation Supplement No. 21 of 26th March, 1971, as contained in Paper S.L. 16 of 1971, be approved.

    Sir, over the past few years, my Ministry has been very perturbed and greatly concerned at the steady rise in the number of accidents occurring in the building and engineering construction industry. Statistics show that 279 accidents occurred in the industry in 1969, and in 1970 this figure rose to 376, a rise of nearly 35 per cent.

    In the first six months of 1971, reports of 166 accidents have been received. What is even more tragic is the number of persons killed as a result of these accidents. In 1969, 17 persons were reported killed in the building and engineering construction industry. In 1970 it was 19. Until June this year, nine fatal accidents have been reported.

    The above figures illustrate the urgent need for the adoption of higher standards of safety measures in the building and engineering construction industry. But experience has shown that mere exhortations and appeals to the goodwill of the employers and the good sense of the workers to be more safety conscious have been of little avail. My Ministry is therefore of the view that definite guidelines are necessary. The Regulations now before the House not only strengthen the provisions of the Factories Act relating to the health, safety and welfare of the workers, but also provide for safety measures of special application to the building and engineering construction industry. The cardinal principle enunciated in these Regulations is that the required safety measures for any project or construction work should be incorporated right from the design stage and not left to be added on as an afterthought or when accidents

Column: 60

have already occurred. The basic provisions for safety should therefore be `i major consideration at all stages and levels of planning and implementation.

    The Regulations also stress the need for qualified and competent persons to erect or set up the requisite safety measures and to make frequent inspections to ensure that these are properly maintained and correctly used. The Regulations not only require the employer to provide safe working conditions but also make it the duty of every employee to co-operate in carrying out the Regulations.

    It is hoped that with the implementation of these comprehensive, unequivocal and stringent Regulations, workers in the building and construction industry will enjoy a greater measure of safety at their working places and also enjoy a higher standard of health.
