INCREASE IN UNEMPLOYMENT AND STEPS TO REDUCE IT


    2. Mr John Ede asked the Minister for Labour and Welfare what is the estimated increase in the number of unemployed persons in Singapore since April 1955, and what steps are being taken to reduce unemployment.

    Mr Lim Yew Hock: Mr Speaker, Sir, in answer to a similar question in this House in February 1956, I explained that accurate figures for the numbers of unemployed persons in Singapore are not available. For this reason I cannot state the number of unemployed persons in April 1955 any more than I can state the increase or decrease in the number since that date. As recently as last January I informed this House that a study of the various factors which affect employment in Singapore did not suggest that there had been any appreciable change in the unemployment situation during the year 1956. I believe that that still substantially represents the position today. However, I must inform the House that recent reports do seem to indicate a decline in employment in certain industries. For example, there has been a reduction of 10 per cent to 15 per cent in the numbers of workmen employed in the saw-milling and rubber packing factories. Employment in biscuit factories has declined by about 25 per cent. These changes appear to have been due to Federation of Malaya duties and smaller quantities of low-grade rubber imports.

    The retrenchment of about 400 industrial workers at the Naval Base has been well publicised.

    Information collected during the Labour Department's census of manual workers in employment at the end of March has not yet been fully compiled, so that I am unable to state whether there have been increases in other industries to compensate for the decrease I have mentioned. Members will appreciate that in a competitive trading centre like Singapore, there are inevitable

Column: 1924

ups and downs in particular industries. It may be that when the census is complete, it will show a contraction in the employment of manual workers over the last six months; but I am satisfied that the net change will not give rise to undue alarm.

    Employment exchanges' records show counts of persons actively seeking employment assistance at various times as follows: 




April 1955 - 7,031.

April 1956 - 8,122.

April 1957 - 8,074.


    I am bound to point out that the figure for March 1957 was 8,858. Last month there was a sharp fall which will probably only be temporary, as a result of recruitment for census duties.

    To sum up, there has probably been an increase in the number of unemployed persons in Singapore since April 1955, both as a result of school leavers coming on to the employment market and a recent fall in the volume of employment. I estimate that the total is of the order of one per cent of the total number of persons in employment.

    The Government's aim is to pursue a policy of full employment. Public works are maintained at as high a level as resources of the building construction industry allow. These works not merely provide temporary employment for those engaged in actual construction, but permanent employment for those who maintain and operate the schools, hospitals and public buildings which are erected. Government is anxious to create the necessary conditions under which existing industries will expand and new industries can be attracted to Singapore. The Industrial Promotion Board has been set up to assist in this process.

    3. Mr Lee Choon Eng asked the Chief Secretary-

    (a) whether the Government recognises the fundamental right of a Trade Union to go on strike;

    Mr S. T. Stewart: Mr Speaker, Sir, with regard to (a) the answer is Yes.

    With regard to (b), there was no official strike of the temporary clerks. What happened was that on the 29th of April two temporary clerks, whose services were to be terminated along with others the following day, absented themselves without permission from their offices and picketed the Government offices, Empress Place. It is understood that they were joined there by four other clerks all of whom had obtained leave of absence. Their action did not have the support of the only union which is recognised as representing the clerical classes, and they did this at a time when the union representatives were negotiating for an extension of the notices terminating their service. The two clerks who had absented themselves did not

Column: 1926

turn up at their office on the 30th. There was therefore no question of extending their notices.

    With regard to (c), Sir, as I have already stated, there was no official strike. To the extent that those who stayed away without leave did not have their notices extended, there was disparity of treatment, but I have already indicated the reason for this in my previous answer. There was, however, no difference in treatment in the matter of the review by the Public Service Commission. SCHOOL BUILDINGS IN RURAL AREAS
(Steps to reduce costs)


    9. Mr John Ede asked the Minister for Education what steps have been taken to reduce the cost of new school buildings in the rural areas; and whether he proposes to erect such buildings with materials similar to those used for the Singapore Infantry Battalion buildings at Ulu Pandan.

    Mr Chew Swee Kee: Mr Speaker, Sir, through the combined efforts of the Public Works Department and the Advisory Panel on School Buildings, the standard of construction, finishings and fittings in our new schools has been reduced to a minimum. INFLUENZA EPIDEMIC
(Statement)


    10. Mr John Ede asked the Minister for Health whether he will make a statement on the causes of the recent influenza epidemic.

    Mr A. J. Braga: Mr Speaker, Sir, I propose to make a full statement on the influenza epidemic to this House before the business of the day. The statement no doubt will answer many points that may be in the mind of the Member for Tanglin. But to answer this particular question briefly, the cause of influenza is generally by a virus known as Type A or Type B, it was originally thought that the cause of the present epidemic was by Type A, but recent indications are that it might possibly be a new virus previously unknown to medical science. The virus has been sent to the United States, the United Kingdom and Melbourne for identification and further study. The origin of the epidemic is unknown. It is most likely that it originated in Japan where it prevailed extensively, generally in a mild form, but it appears to have invaded Taiwan, the Philippines, Hongkong and the Federation of Malaya, and I believe that it has already now appeared in Australia.

INFLUENZA EPIDEMIC IN SINGAPORE
(Statement by the Minister of Health)


    The Minister for Health (Mr A. J. Braga): Mr Speaker, Sir, the epidemic of influenza which has visited Singapore has aroused anxiety and I feel it incumbent upon me to make a full statement for the benefit of the House.

    As I have already stated earlier, this disease is believed to have originated in Japan and has invaded Taiwan, the Philippines, Hongkong, Singapore and the Federation of Malaya. A characteristic of this disease is its capacity to attack suddenly, although the actual disease is mild, and to extend very quickly. The extreme conditions of overcrowding, particularly in the town, must certainly have favoured the spread. It must also be realised from the epidemiological point of view that the entire island of Singapore is one composite urban unit with denser aggregations north and south of the Singapore River. In consequence, an island-wide spread was inevitable and indeed the spread to the Federation of Malaya was inevitable. In the view of medical experts, it is likely to prevail in an epidemic form until about the end of this month and from daily reports, there are definite signs of a decline and that it is well on its way out.

Column: 1939



    There is no known preventive or cure for the disease. The only course - which was unthinkable - was to close the port of Singapore and all the ports of the Federation of Malaya and the Siamese border against the entry of any craft whatsoever, and impose martial law to keep people in their homes until the disease had spent itself on a worldwide career. There is no specific cure for influenza as there is none for an ordinary common cold. The number of persons affected by the disease in Singapore and attended to by our medical clinics, and mobile and floating dispensaries totalled approximately 25,000. It is estimated that an equal number have been treated by private practitioners and local medicine men. So much publicity has been given to the epidemic that I have every reason to believe that the number of persons who were actually attacked by the disease and who have not sought treatment would be negligible. Directly it was evident that the disease had attacked Singapore, Government took every possible measure to combat and control it. Briefly, the steps taken by Government were, firstly, my Ministry issued warning and advice through the Press, Rediffusion and the Radio and the personnel of the medical services to the people to keep away from crowds and to rest in bed if the disease was apparent. Secondly, elective surgery was restricted to the barest minimum practicable to make possible the release of medical personnel to augment outpatient services. Further, extra travelling dispensaries were mobilised. Prior to the onset of the epidemic, we had three travelling dispensaries visiting various parts of the island. In addition to this, we had two floating dispensaries doing monthly visits of the islands. On the onset of the epidemic, Government mobilised five travelling dispensaries doing double shifts and the floating dispensaries were put on an emergency basis to visit each of the islands twice a week. The entire staff was stretched to the limit. Simultaneously, the pharmaceutical manufactory immediately increased its production of essential drugs.

    The attack of the disease commences with fever, headache, pains in the limbs

and back, nausea and sore throat and sometimes accompanied with cough and giddiness and a temperature which ranges from 101 to 102. The duration of the fever is about two days and the patient settles down to normal within four to five days and there seem to be very few complications.

    It would appear that four persons might have died of the disease although biological confirmation has yet to be available. Persons diagnosed as having died of influenza depended solely on morbid anatomy. During this period, there were a few other fatal cases in which findings suggest influenza but there were other complications such as broncho-pneumonia, heart disease and nervous manifestations and, in the absence of the virus, it is hardly possible to attribute them to influenza as the causal agent of death. Information from commercial firms is that about 10 per cent of the staff were away at some time or other. The disease seems to have been confined to Asians, Europeans being very little affected. Whether there is an immunity already in the European race is difficult to decide at the moment.
