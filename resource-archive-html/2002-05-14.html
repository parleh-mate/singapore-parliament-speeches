    4.     The Senior Minister of State for Education (Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam) (for the Minister for Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, the approach adopted to nurturing creativity in students is not through teaching specific modules on creativity or teaching it as a separate subject. Our approach to promoting and nurturing creativity is through infusion across the curriculum and through project work, teaching methodologies and the assessment system itself. Lessons in any subject that arouse a lively curiosity in students foster creative thinking and imagination. Our Science and Mathematics programmes, for example, help students to develop this curiosity by encouraging enquiry and getting them to provide their own solutions to problems. Activities with open-ended investigations such as the Maths trail, which is an activity conducted outside the classroom, require students to use varied strategies to solve problems or seek alternative solutions to problems. Teachers are also conducting activities to make science an experiential learning for students, especially at the primary school level. One activity in primary schools, for example, involves having the students learn more about

Project work also encourages students to break away from the compartmentalisation of the different subject disciplines and explore the inter-relationships between different areas of knowledge when applied to real life situations.

    There is no single indicator that can give a reliable measure of the success of our efforts to promote creativity. However, we are changing the assessment system in the individual subjects so that problem solving, inference skills and creative thinking are given greater emphasis in the assessments. For example, in English Literature, the introduction of unseen material - material that the students have not previously seen - in the assessment system develops students' quality of thinking and their application of principles of critical reading to the unseen passages of prose or poetry. In history, questions based on original sources - source-based questions, as they call it - have been introduced to assess students' thinking and analytical skills in the interpretation and evaluation of issues in their original historical context. And in the sciences, we are introducing a new way of assessing practical skills over a period of time instead of a one-off practical examination, to promote a broader understanding of scientific process skills associated with scientific inquiry and problem-solving.
