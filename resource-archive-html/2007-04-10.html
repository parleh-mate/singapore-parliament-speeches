ADMINISTRATIVE SERVICE

(Resignations)

     1.  Mr Siew Kum Hong asked the Prime Minister and Minister for Finance for each of the years from 2004 to 2006 (a) how many officers resigned from the Administrative Service and what resignation rate did that represent; (b) of these resignations, how many Administrative Officers cited higher pay in a new job as the main reason or one of the main reasons; and (c) how many Administrative Officers were asked to resign for reasons related to non-performance.

     The Minister for Defence (Mr Teo Chee Hean) (for the Prime Minister and Minister for Finance):  Mr Speaker, Sir, the number of Administrative Officers who resigned from the Administrative Service in 2004, 2005 and 2006 were four, 10 and seven respectively.  This corresponds to the resignation rates of 2%, 4% and 3% respectively.  If we include Management Associates, who are the officers being tested for suitability for the Administrative Service, the resignation numbers are larger, but, of course, the base is larger as well.  The total number of Administrative Officers and Management Associates who resigned in 2004, 2005 and 2006 were four, 14 and 12 respectively.  This corresponds to the resignation rates of 1%, 4% and 3% respectively.

 

     Administrative Officers leave the Service for various reasons, and we may often not be able to ascertain exactly the real reasons for their departure.  Some indicated that they would like to further their studies while others want to have a change of career.  Some of those who leave for jobs in the private sector enjoy a pay increase in their new jobs.  

 

     The Administrative Service has very stringent retention standards.  Officers are appraised annually and are expected to have the potential to hold at least Deputy Secretary or CEO level jobs.  Those who do not meet the mark are asked to leave the Service but they could remain in other schemes in the Civil Service if they wish and if there are suitable jobs for them.  Over the last three years, a total of 13 Administrative Officers left the Service on such grounds, ie, they were managed out of the Service.  Of these, eight officers left the Public Service. The other five transferred to join other schemes within the Public Service where they continue to contribute to the public service.

     Mr Siew Kum Hong (Nominated Member):  Mr Speaker, Sir, I would just like to ask the Minister how many Administrative Officers are there in grades MR4 and above between SR9 and MR4 and on Timescales. 

     Mr Teo Chee Hean:  Sir, I do not carry the numbers in my head.  If the Member could submit a Question, I would be happy to answer it.

UPGRADING OF ONE-ROOM HDB FLATS

(Temporary units for tenants)

     3.  Mr Siew Kum Hong asked the Minister for National Development in view of the situation of some tenants of one-room flats having to sleep in corridors and common areas when their flats are being upgraded (a) what are the HDB’s reasons for not permitting vacant units to be used on a temporary basis; (b) whether this situation has been encountered in previous upgradings; and (c) what measures will be taken to prevent a recurrence in future upgrading projects.

     The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for National Development (Dr Mohamad Maliki Bin Osman) (for the Minister for National Development):  Mr Speaker, Sir, I would like to thank the Member for this opportunity to clarify media reports in mid-March that the ongoing upgrading of a block of rental flats in Toa Payoh had inconvenienced some residents.

 

     The particular rental block is being upgraded under Project LIFE.  LIFE stands for Lift Improvement and Facilities Enhancement.  This is a programme implemented by HDB for one-room rental blocks where there is a high concentration of elderly residents.  It seeks to improve the physical living conditions of the elderly tenants through lift upgrading and the installation of elder-friendly features, such as support handbars and the Alert Alarm System.  It is fully paid for by the Government.

 

     When these upgrading works are going on in a rental block, HDB implements a host of measures to reduce inconvenience to residents.  The contractors can work inside the flat only from 9.00 am to 5.00 pm during weekdays.   They have to ensure that the flat is cleared up at the end of each day.  There is an air-conditioned area for affected residents to rest during these working hours.  Temporary toilets and storage space are also made available.  And the upgrading is done in batches so that these communal facilities are sufficient for the affected residents’ use.
 
     Sir, since 1993, HDB has upgraded 38 rental blocks under Project LIFE and, in all these projects, no tenant has been forced to sleep in the corridors as a result of the upgrading works.  One of the newspaper articles showed a picture of a man sleeping at the common corridor of his rental block.  HDB investigated and found that he was sleeping outside on his own accord.  The upgrading works at his unit has not even started.  We have informed the newspaper that they had inadvertently misled their readers by using the picture in the wrong context. 

 

     HDB does exercise flexibility in cases of residents with serious medical or mobility problems and will arrange for such households to be shifted to a vacant unit.  However, in the recent case, the resident who complained to the press did not fall into this category.  He had asked for a vacant unit to store his belongings.  He was not happy that he had to store it in the allocated storage space provided.  HDB could not accede to his request, so he complained to the press.

     Although greater financial support for fertility treatment will make ACP more affordable for couples who have difficulty conceiving, unfortunately, the reality is that no amount of financial support is able to increase the success rate of ACP. 

 

     As fertility declines with age, what is needed are upstream efforts to raise awareness on the narrowing fertility window as one ages and the risks associated with delayed childbearing.  I am happy to report that our public education efforts have reaped some results.  From my Ministry’s 2006 survey of 3,000 married respondents, 94% are aware that it gets more difficult for couples to conceive as they get older, up from 89% in 2004.  However, more can and must be done to raise awareness level and to transform awareness into a change in attitude and behaviour.  My Ministry has and will continue to partner medical experts to spread this important message.

     Er Lee Bee Wah:  Mr Speaker, Sir, I think the sentiment among the childless couples is that they have already sacrificed their time in going through ACP.  So they are asking whether it is possible to give them financial aid.  They are aware that the CPF can be used.  But what they are asking for is financial aid, because they have sacrificed their time.  The end result is that what they are doing is beneficial for the country.

ELDER ABUSE

     5.  Assoc. Prof. Kalyani K Mehta asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what are the last three years' statistics on elder abuse in Singapore, with a breakdown according to age, gender, relationship with perpetrator, marital status, ethnicity and citizenship; (b) what is the number of times the Elder Protection Team was activated in the past year; and (c) whether there is an elder abuse protocol put in place by his Ministry.

     Mrs Yu-Foo Yee Shoon (for the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports):  Mr Speaker, Sir, I would like to thank Assoc. Prof. Kalyani Mehta for this Question and for her concern about elder abuse.

 

     The number of reported elder abuse cases in the past three years has been stable.  There were 186 cases in 2006, 181 in 2005 and 194 in 2004.  On average, 64% of the reported cases were elderly women and 66% of the abused elderly persons were 70 years old and above.  In 70% of the cases, the perpetrators were the victims' own children.  The majority of the abused elderly persons were Singaporeans who were married with children.  The ethnic breakdown of the cases is similar to Singapore's racial mix.

 

 


    Any elderly person who is reported to be abused will be attended to.  Depending on the first point of contact, the victim may be attended to by a medical social worker if the person is at the hospital or by a social worker from a Family Service Centre if he or she is in the community.  When MCYS is informed of any abuse, our Ministry staff will also attend to the elderly person. 
 
    The Elder Protection Team is activated only for cases which are more complex. This team, spearheaded by TRANS Centre (a voluntary welfare organisation specialising in family violence work) consists of professionals with knowledge in geriatrics, psychiatry, law and social work.  In the past year, the Elder Protection Team attended to seven new cases and reviewed five cases. 

  

     There is a robust protocol in place to manage elder abuse cases.  A victim of abuse can approach a social worker, the hospital, the police or the court. Each of these agencies and individuals has been trained to provide assistance, make referrals to relevant agencies or activate the Elder Protection Team whenever necessary.  We appreciate that an elderly person may be reluctant to seek help.  Social workers, friends and grassroots leaders therefore play an important part in identifying elderly persons that need help and refer them to Family Service Centres, which can then follow up.

STUDENTS FROM SPECIAL ASSISTANCE PLAN SCHOOLS

(Integration)

     6.  Dr Fatimah Lateef asked the Minister for Education whether there are plans to further integrate students from the Special Assistance Plan schools into the wider, multicultural group of students from other schools in Singapore.

     The Minister of State for Education (Mr Gan Kim Yong) (for the Minister for Education):  Mr Speaker, it is always a pleasure to answer questions in the House.

 

    The Ministry of Education established the Special Assistance Plan, or SAP schools, in 1979 for two purposes.  Firstly, to keep alive the best traditions and ethos of old Chinese schools in a few selected schools, so as to inculcate traditional Chinese values and social discipline in a number of our top students.  Secondly, to encourage a number of our academically able and linguistically talented students to study both Chinese and English at the first language level.

 

     Today, our SAP schools continue to play a key role in nurturing a core group of bilingual Singaporeans who are well versed in the Chinese language and culture, with a deep understanding of their heritage, and who are also well poised to grasp opportunities in the emerging China market.  This is a strength of our system that we will continue to preserve going forward.    

 

     SAP schools are keenly aware that they need to work doubly hard to create opportunities for their students to interact with students from other ethnic groups.  They do so in a variety of forms - from twinning with non-SAP schools with a multi-racial composition to taking part in joint activities with non-SAP schools and community organisations which serve non-Chinese or multi-racial communities.  An example is the twinning arrangement between Ai Tong School, which is a Primary SAP school, and Pierce Secondary School.  Primary 4 pupils from Ai Tong who are weak in Mathematics and English receive tutoring help from non-Chinese students from Pierce Secondary.  Similarly, the Secondary 3 students in Chung Cheng High School (Main) run a Conversational Chinese Programme for Primary 5 non-Chinese students from Geylang Methodist Primary School.

 

      SAP schools also actively celebrate the different ethnic festivals and leverage on these occasions to deepen their students' understanding of other races and cultures.  Non-Chinese students from neighbouring schools are invited to join in for these events.  For instance, Anglican High celebrates Chinese New Year and Racial Harmony Day annually together with Damai Secondary School and Changkat Changi Secondary School. 

 

     Going forward, MOE and the SAP schools will continue to actively explore and create more opportunities for SAP students to interact with students from other ethnic groups.

     Dr Fatimah Lateef:  Thank you very much for the answer.  I would just like to ask one supplementary question.  Will there be a possibility to consider some kind of formal interactive programme or integration programme between uni-cultural schools, basically SAP schools, versus maybe perhaps madrasah schools?  Because a kind of a long-term relationship between two types of schools would probably be good in developing tolerance and understanding of our multi-cultural and multi-racial society.

     Mr Gan Kim Yong:  Mr Speaker, Sir, the Ministry has adopted the approach of top-down support for ground-up initiatives, ie, we encourage the schools to evolve and develop their own initiatives and programmes because by doing so, we give the schools a greater sense of ownership to the programmes that they have developed, and we encourage them to do so by providing them with the necessary support. Therefore, we would like to encourage a diversity of different programmes and activities.  Also, students will learn from different ways. So we encourage them to have different forms of integration programmes, rather than to formalise them and make it a formal programme.  We would prefer to encourage them to have their own ideas so that they will be more effective in inculcating this multi-racial culture within the school.

    Mr Zaqy Mohamad (Hong Kah):   I would like to ask the Minister whether the students from SAP schools could truly benefit from multi-culturalism at the programme level as opposed to a daily interaction basis.

    Mr Gan Kim Yong:  Mr Speaker, in fact, some of the programmes are long-term programmes and they interact quite regularly.  Let me give you an example. Hwa Chong Institution, for example, works very closely with its neighbourhood schools in terms of different cultural programmes.  One example is West Spring Secondary and Bukit Panjang Government High.  For these schools, they actually practise music together.  In Hwa Chong, there is a Chinese orchestra.  They have 24 festive drums as well as wushu. And from March to June, they will practise together with students from both schools. This culminates in a joint performance at the Racial Harmony Day in July. So they actually meet each other quite regularly within these three to four months.  They practise together, get to know each other, learn from each other, and then they jointly perform together at the Racial Harmony Day.

     Mr Hri Kumar Nair: Mr Speaker, Sir, different points of views have been given, I will not try and cover the same ground.  Let me start by talking about what I know best - my own profession, the law.

 

      Our court system is based on the British model. Many years ago, lawyers in England called barristers were regarded like public servants.  They were expected to use their skills and training for the benefit of all whose cause was just and innocent, without fear or favour and without reference to power or wealth.  Consistent with their calling, their fees were regarded as honorarium which they cannot sue to recover.  In other words, you have no recourse if your client decides not to pay you.  Barristers were also not permitted to pick and choose their cases for their clients.  A barrister was expected to represent all to the best of his ability, whether or not he found them loathsome or whether or not they were princes or paupers.  Their overriding duty was to serve the public.  And legend has it that after a barrister finished his work in court, his client will deposit his fee in a little pocket in the barrister's robe or gown. And this pocket was located at the back of the robe so that even the very act of payment could not be seen by the barrister, because such matters of money were beneath him.  So, if you look carefully at the back of a lawyer's robe today, you will see a tiny pouch.  That pouch is all but sewn up, so you cannot put any money in it anymore.  It has no function but merely a vestige of time past. That story may not be entirely accurate, I should say.  But it is dutifully told by one generation of lawyers to the next to emphasise the nobility of the profession.

 

     The legal profession today is still an honourable one.  Lawyers have a higher duty to the court.  They must act in the interest of justice. They are held to a higher standard and respect of their character and conduct compared to any other profession.  They are expected to serve the public good.  But no one, and absolutely no one, expects lawyers today in England, Singapore or anywhere around the world to work with the same financial constraints of days gone by.  Why?  Because those rules are outdated and unrealistic.  Today, having a clean and competent legal system is an important part in any country's economy.  And to achieve high standards, you need bright people willing to take up the law as a career.  We all know that financial rewards play a part in someone making a selection of his career.  And I do not think I will be wrong to say that today in law school in Singapore, the United Kingdom, United States or anywhere around the developed world, very bright students are attracted to the law largely on account of financial opportunities which the legal profession offers, and there is absolutely nothing wrong with that.

 

    Is the legal profession worse for this?  Clearly not!  In fact, by paying well, you are actually raising standards and also levelling the playing field.  Otherwise, you will be left in a situation where only the rich can afford to be judges and lawyers, which was the situation in England many years ago.  You also need good legal minds, both in the private as well as the public sectors.  The system will not work if you have bright judges and government prosecutors but less qualified lawyers in the private sector, and vice versa.  So, the financial rewards in both these sectors must be compatible, otherwise there will be an imbalance of talent and an exodus from one to another whenever that imbalance is not addressed.  This had in fact happened in the past in Singapore.

 

     So, performing a public service is not inconsistent with receiving good pay.  The position of lawyers has evolved. Why should it stand still for Ministers and civil servants?  Yes, it is the duty of Ministers and the civil service to serve the public.  Yesterday, Mr Siew Kum Hong spoke passionately about how Ministers should be cut from a different cloth and how they should be driven by the privilege of leading this country. I agree with him.  A person in that job should not have to be motivated by anything other than the privilege and honour of leading Singapore.  He should be someone who thinks that destiny demands of him to service.  If he does not have that drive or mindset, how will he be able to make the tough decisions which he will inevitably have to make?  But why does this mean that Ministers should be paid less than what they are worth or what their job demands.  And why should their moral authority be questioned or eroded in the minds of right-thinking people simply because we seek to give them a fair wage?  Just because someone should not do this job for money does not mean that you should not pay him fairly.

 

    What are the common arguments we hear?  First, there is a fear that the wrong type of person may be motivated to take office.  I think that is overstated.  One does not get the job simply by applying for it.  You first have to establish a good reputation.  You then have to satisfy the Prime Minister's standards, no doubt highly stringent.  You then have to be scrutinised by the electorate and prove yourself in the office of a junior Minister before you are asked to step up.  It is clearly not in the PM's interest to appoint someone who is not competent and dedicated to Singapore.  In fact, despite the salary increases in 1994 and thereafter, the Prime Minister has said on a number of occasions that it is still difficult to persuade good people to step forward.   Why?  Because asking someone who is probably already successful and comfortable in his career to completely overhaul his life, and more than that the lives of his family, is very difficult.  What value do we place on that? 

 

     Do Singaporeans really believe that keeping salaries low will discourage greedy and dishonest people from trying to get into office?  The many examples around the world tell us differently.  The wrong people will always try to take control because they know that if they succeed, they can rewrite the rules to benefit themselves and they will not bother about having a debate in Parliament or elsewhere about it.  But there is a more fundamental point, I submit. There is nothing wrong with factoring salary in your career choice.  We all do that.  Why is it regarded as an imperfection when our Ministers do it or when the formula allows our Ministers to do it?  If people demand their politicians take an oath of sainthood with their oath of office, then they are just asking to be deceived.

 

     The other common argument is that other countries do not pay their leaders as much and the figures often cited sums which are officially declared.  The additional perks and advantages received during and after office are usually left out. We have heard some examples yesterday.  But, more importantly, again, so what if we pay the highest?  Singaporeans should decide for ourselves what value we place on having an honest, dedicated, competent leadership.  We should not be dictated by considerations and circumstances of other countries which have absolutely no relevance to us at all.  What other leaders earn should not be how we value leadership in Singapore.

 

     Then we have a third argument of Mr Chiam yesterday comparing the Government to a charity.  I am flabbergasted by this.  It is completely different.  A charity's deliverables are limited. It has no obligation to grow its business.  It does not have to look after its donors or give them returns.  The comparison is completely misplaced. 

 

 

So the basic question is: what is a fair wage?  At first blush, it seems a difficult question.  But once we agree on the principle, it is fairly simple to resolve. 

 

     Sir, I fully accept the principle that we should let the market determine the value of our Ministers and senior civil servants.  Benchmarking Ministerial salaries to the private sector is a sensible basis.  Like many who have spoken before me, I believe the formula requires some fine-tuning so that it fairly represents the market and takes into account other important factors.  Why fine-tune it now?  It was simply because the formula was devised in 1994 when salary structures were somewhat different.  It is clear that there has today been a significant widening of the salary ranges and these have occurred even at the very top.  Take the law as an example.  According to the Sunday Times of 1st April this year, in 1998, the difference between the top earner and the eight highest earner in law was $1.2 million.  Today, I believe that figure to be much larger and may even exceed $5 million.  In other words, there is a vast difference in remuneration even among those who are successful.  The amounts earned by those at the very top are therefore no longer representative of what most successful lawyers take home. If the intention of the formula is to be in line with market, then these super earners are not a reflection of what the market pays a successful professional.

 

     Second, the earnings of the top 48, particularly those in the profession, are not really salaries.  Although I do not know who they are, I believe they are probably senior partners or sole proprietors of their own firms.  They are not just lawyers or accountants but entrepreneurs running a business of providing legal or accounting services.  They are very different from salaried persons.  Their earnings represent a return on their enterprise and risk.  If the business fails to generate a profit, they earn nothing and they may even lose money.  So in a real way, their entire earnings are effectively performance based.  It is therefore inappropriate to take their figures and translate them into a regular monthly salary.  In the circumstances, if the intention is to continue using the top eight individuals of each profession, then we should compare like for like.  A substantial portion of our Ministers' and civil servants' pay should be made performance related.  I note that the measures announced yesterday seek to place a greater emphasis on performance but, if you want a proper comparison, a larger portion should be performance based.

 

     In addition, the key performance indicators should be spelt out clearly so that the public is left in no doubt of the standards that are expected.  We are more than willing to pay our public servants a fair wage and, in return, it is reasonable for us to know what we will get in return.  There is nothing that will reassure Singaporeans more than a clear set of concrete deliverables from our public servants and if they do achieve their targets, no one can reasonably begrudge them what they earn.

 

      Sir, my final point is about symbolism.  At the end of the day, whatever formula one chooses, the Government must serve and be accountable to all segments of the society.  The formula should properly reflect that.  Mr Low Thia Khiang had, some time ago, suggested that Ministers' salaries should be a multiple of salaries of the bottom 20%. Politically very convenient to argue but really practically makes little sense. Mr Low obviously agrees because he did not repeat the suggestion at this debate yesterday.

 

     In the same vein, this formula should not just take into account the top 48.  We cannot ignore the perception that if the formula is tied just to the top, Ministers and civil servants will always enjoy the upside regardless of how the rest of Singaporeans are doing.  Indeed, it is well conceivable that the top 24 could continue to earn well and do better even in an economic downturn and increased unemployment.  In that event, the Government may be compelled to impose salary cuts and pay below the benchmark as it had done in the past.  But what is the point of having a formula if you are only going to follow it some of the time? More importantly, if the formula results in figures which the Government is not comfortable paying, then perhaps there is something wrong with the formula.  I believe the formula should contain elements which will tie salaries to the general well-being of Singapore, for example, the GDP, the unemployment rate, the cost of living.  We can work out the details.  I know these matters may be taken into account when deciding performance bonuses, but I think it is important that we spell this out expressly to demonstrate that they will have a direct bearing on salaries as well.  It may be a question of presentation and explanation, but in matters such as these, which are emotional, I think presentation and explanation are just as important.

     Sir, not all highly-paid CEOs of legal firms or banks are top candidate choices to be Ministers or top civil servants.  By the same token, not all top political and civil service leaders may excel in the private sector.  So I do agree that those precious few who are able to thrive in both public and private sectors are Singapore's crown jewels and they are worth millions of dollars.  Even so, I think once that special leader crosses over to serve in a public office, it is futile for him to keep looking over his shoulders to yearn for what he could have made.  Alan Greenspan, economist and former US Federal Reserve Chairman, who spent almost 20 years serving four Presidents, was reported to have accepted an annual pay of only US$180,000.  When he finally retired in 2006, he still had a fruitful career due to his influence and expertise.  He donated free service to the UK Treasury and is now also a highly sought-after speaker with a price tag of US$100,000 per talk.  Alan Greenspan is a good illustration of a respected and skilled leader who is highly capable of working in the private sector but chose to serve his country below his market value.

 

     Finally, Sir, if Government wishes to pay and benchmark public servants against private sector pay instead of against other governments, then it should simulate the work environment as close as that in the private sector.  In addition, start adopting some principles around compensation practices that resemble more closely the private sector.

 

     In this regard, I propose five compensation practices for consideration.

 

     Practice 1.  Set up an independent senior salaries review body comprising independent representatives from various key sectors in Singapore.  The principle of not writing one's own pay cheque is very key in addressing public disquiet on this issue.  The United Kingdom Senior Salaries Review Body, which proposes political and civil service pay, for instance, comprises senior executives from the private sector, HR and finance consultancy, academia and so forth.

 

    Public perception of this body's independence is just as critical as the body's real independence.  In the corporate sector, there is already an uproar over how some CEOs influence their boards to write themselves excessive pay cheques, even to the extent of being paid hefty sums upon exit due to poor performance.  A 2005 study on "CEO Skill and Excessive Pay" by Professors from Stanford, Wharton School and the New York University concluded that particularly in big stable firms, a high salary does not necessarily mean that a CEO is more competent.

 

     Practice 2.  Establish a line of sight that shows clearly the link between pay and performance of political and civil service leaders.  Make key performance indicators (KPIs) transparent so that the people of Singapore know how the leaders have contributed to the Prime Minister's vision of an inclusive and vibrant Singapore in which no one is left behind.  Do not simply use one global helicopter KPI like the size of Singapore's GDP as the basis for performance-based component like bonus.  How does the Health Minister, for instance, see his Ministry's good performance linked directly to the GDP?

 

     Compensation best practice dictates that there be a clear line of sight between one's pay packet and his direct performance.  In the case of public servants, I believe that performance-based pay can tie in to at least three tiers:  (1) country's performance, not just economic; (2) each Ministry's performance; and (3) each individual's performance.

 

     Surely, performance indicators like the customer's or the citizen's feedback to major public services, the number of jobs created for local Singaporeans, percentage of Singaporeans in the lowest-income bracket, the number of Singaporeans who choose to migrate, etc, are all KPIs that can be incorporated in a balanced score card of our top leaders.

 

     Practice 3.  Adopt a more commonly acceptable salary benchmarking formula.  In the private sector, it is rare to see the use of the current salary benchmarking formula which targets only a very small number of top wage earners in the sector that they compete in.  Actually, one key questionable underlying assumption of the current formula is that 100% of all the political office-holders, every Permanent Secretary and every Administrative Officer will surely end up as top wage earners in Singapore.

 

     Most firms I know set their target competitive rates at either the 50th or the 75th percentile of target employee categories in selected companies and industries, but never at such a small number of top wage earners.

 

     Practice 4.  Adopt a broader band pay range for each position that we are talking about, to cater to appropriate salary levels for incumbents ranging from those with entry-level skills to those critical skills experts or long-term outstanding performers.  Hence, hypothetically using $1.2 million as a mid point for a Minister's pay and a minimum-to-maximum spread of 60%, the minimum pay for entry-level Minister can be $840,000, or 30% below mid point; a mature performer gets $1.2 million and a consistent top performer gets close to $1.6 million, or 30% above mid point.  So, adopt a broader band pay range for each position.

 

     Practice 5.  Cultivate more lasting and non-monetary motivators to usher in and retain good people to join the public sector.  Simply throwing money at the problem does not solve it.  There is no quick-fix solution to talent attraction and retention, and there are numerous best practices in this field.  Many employers, though, find it harder to walk the talk. 

 

     In March 2007, a Harvard Business Review article entitled "What it means to work here", the authors lament at their HR equivalent of keeping up with the Joneses.  The authors said that in their quest to find and retain top talent, organisations often try to match competitors' salary offers and training opportunities.  They forget that people will become long-term deeply engaged employees only if their work experience is what they expect it to be, and they are intrigued and excited by a vision that they find inspiring; a leader they want to follow and work roles that match their passion and their talent.

 

     Sir, may I even suggest that for the sake of Singapore, we should release the 32-year-olds who, after serving their bonds, may want to respond to a different drum beat in their lives or simply want to expose themselves to a different work environment whilst they are still young.  If they stay on, because they are handcuffed by a super pay scale or their pension, it is a lose-lose situation for them and the Government.

 

     In conclusion, Sir, the issue of pay is a sensitive one.  Someone earning $1,000 can never fathom another who is earning $10,000.  A person earning $10,000 will never understand how another who is earning $100,000 a month still feels is not sufficient.  For someone like Minister Mentor who will be remembered by all of us as the father of Singapore, how does one even find a price tag big enough for his contribution?

 

     What is enough?  We may never agree.  But let me end with the writing of Charles Handy who said, "The philosophy of "enough" cannot be imposed on a society.  It is a matter of norms, not law.  But norms are set by the elite, whose examples set the fashion.  What the top people do today, the middle level imitates tomorrow, and the bottom aspires to, some day."

 

     The people who follow us are watching us.  Our young people are also watching us.  So it is the people sector, where many are professionals who volunteer their service and money unconditionally all these years.

 

     Sir, I urge our leaders to give some more thought on what is reasonable enough for top leaders' salaries, whilst considering the five compensation practices I quoted.  At the same time, I urge that together, Members of this House will make it a priority to ensure that everyone in Singapore has a real chance of also being able to reach their personal level of "enough".

     Mr Michael Palmer (Pasir Ris-Punggol): Mr Speaker, Sir, thank you for giving me this opportunity to say a few words in this debate.

 

    Let me begin by making my position clear. I am not against paying for results.  In the private sector, you pay for performance. If a person performs to expectation, he is remunerated accordingly.  If a person performs above expectation, then he is rewarded for that exceptional performance.  He can be rewarded with both an increase in basic salary as well as a good bonus.  Depending on the type of industry he is in, this remuneration could also come in other forms, such as stock options.

   (a)  Accountability for performance;

 

   (b)  Revenue centres and cost centres; and

 

   (c)  Timing.

 

     Let me begin with accountability.  We know that in the present system, civil service and Ministerial salaries are benchmarked against the salaries of the top earners in certain sectors of the private sector. In certain top grades, a discount is given off the benchmark. While not everyone understands the intricacies of the formula used, Singaporeans generally know that our civil service salaries are benchmarked against what some perceive to be lofty private sector salaries.

 

     The thing is, if private sector salaries are to be used as the yardstick for measurement, then it is reasonable to expect the same sort of accountability for performance in the civil service that the private sector companies demand.  One cannot just take the rewards without also taking on the obligation of accountability for performance for those rewards.  I am therefore glad to hear that some part of the increases will be paid by way of performance bonuses, especially in respect of the higher grades of the Administrative Service.  However, like my colleagues, Mr Alvin Yeo and Mr Inderjit Singh, before me, I think that a larger part of the total remuneration package should be performance-based throughout the civil service.

 

   Whether it is true or not, public perception is that working in the civil service provides a person with an iron rice bowl.  But that is not the case in the private sector.  In the private sector, if your performance is poor and consistently poor over a year, or even two quarters in some sectors, you could be out of a job. It is not a case of not receiving your bonus or not progressing.  You may not have a job at all if you do not perform.  As Mr Hri Kumar has said, the remuneration of many of the top professionals is almost solely based on performance. 

 

     Do the civil servants face the same guillotine? Are they similarly accountable?  The public perception is that they are not.  If we want to peg our civil service salaries to the top salaries in the private sector, then they must be accountable for their performance.  In a recent article that appeared in the Straits Times, the Public Service Division said that Ministers do not have guaranteed tenures because they face elections every five years. That is a fair point.  But we are increasing the salaries of not just the Ministers and other political appointment holders today.  We are increasing the salaries of the whole of the civil service. They do not face elections every five years. How are they accountable if they do not perform?  The public does not see that accountability for performance.

 

     If we want the remuneration of the civil service to be pegged to that of the private sector, I believe the right thing to do is to set targets and key performance indicators, or KPIs, for our civil servants.  These may not always be revenue-driven, as in the private sector.  For instance, we obviously cannot have revenue-driven KPIs for the departments in MCYS that administer welfare schemes.  But we can set other targets and performance indicators.  It may not be easy to devise, but it can be done.

 

     In order for the civil service remuneration packages to be more closely linked to individual performance, civil servants must have targets and performance indicators set in accordance with their individual roles.  They should be specific, measurable, achievable, realistic and timely. Yes, the percentage increase in our GDP for a particular year can be a factor where it is taken into account, but it should not be the only factor. With a system of KPIs in place, civil servants will have goals to strive for and if they meet or exceed those goals, they will be rewarded accordingly.  And if they do not perform and fail to meet their targets consistently, then they must also face the consequences.

 

    The next issue which we should also consider in the equation is the fact that in the private sector, every company and business has its revenue-generating centres and its cost centres.  Mr Teo Ser Luck has touched on this in his speech.  To put it simply, it is widely acknowledged that workers in the revenue-generating centres of a business will earn more than those in the cost centres, that, at the same time, their remuneration is also far more performance-based.  Again, if the civil service is to be run along the lines of "Singapore Inc.", as Mr Lim Swee Say has referred to, then should we not also look to see which departments in each Ministry are the so-called revenue centres and which are the cost centres?

But the purpose of the segregation is not to create divide within the service. One of the main purposes of such a distinction is to ensure that resources are allocated correctly so that performance and results are rewarded.

    Towards the end of last year, the Prime Minister announced that it was likely that GST would be increased from 5% to 7%.  There were two main reasons for the increase.  The first was because we would need more revenue to invest in research, development and infrastructure to take Singapore forward and make it a top tier First-World country.  The second reason was because there was a widening income gap and we need to redistribute the wealth from the top earners to the lower wage earners who have experienced little or no increase in their salaries over the last few years.  In fact, while top level income earners have experienced up to 4% annual increases in their salaries, even some middle income earners have experienced little increase.

 

    With the fear of the income gap widening, we supported the increase in GST and introduced measures like Workfare and the GST offset package in the Budget to help close the gap.  But in this House today, even before those income gap-closing measures have been implemented, we debate an increase in Ministerial and civil service salaries which can only widen the gap between civil servants and the lower wage earners.

 

     And, ultimately, the question that many people have asked me is  - did we increase GST to pay for this increase in salaries?  While that is clearly not the case, it is most unfortunate that a support package so carefully crafted in good faith to help our lower income families should now be seen in such light.

 

     I suspect there is probably never a good time to introduce the issue of increasing Ministerial and civil service salaries, and there will always be people who feel that the salaries are already too high.  Nevertheless, do we really need to pick a time when the public is least receptive to such increases? Allow me to read just two excerpts from the feedback which I have received.  I quote:

   Sir, I do agree that we do need a more holistic human resource management system in the civil service. Those who remained in the service were demoralised because they had to do more work and they saw their colleagues leaving.  And because the service level fell, they had more complaints from the public, and I am sure, from Members of Parliament, about the quality of service and we were put under more pressure.  So we do not want to find ourselves in that same kind of situation.  We want to make sure that we can continue to remain competitive and we can keep, employ and retain our public servants and have a good working climate in our institutions and organisations.

Economic recovery has been going on for the last three years and we have had good growth for three years, but we have not raised salaries as quickly as we should have.  If we wait too long and we are too far out of line, it makes it even more difficult to move our salaries because each time we do that, there will be consternation with the huge percentage which we are raising salaries by if we have to move once in a very long time to come to market levels.  In the long term, I believe that will be to the detriment of Singapore and Singaporeans.

     Mr K Shanmugam (Sembawang): Madam, after that tour de force by the Minister, I am tempted to say that there is nothing further to debate about.  But regardless of the logic, this issue will remain a difficult topic and I think it is our duty to speak up and slowly convince Singaporeans.  I cannot think of another issue where there is such a disconnect between what is clearly the correct policy and how the public react to that policy.

 

   Should we, as a matter of policy, pay Ministers and senior civil servants salaries pegged to market? The answer is really a no-brainer.  And yet, for over 13 years, this has been a very difficult issue.  Public reaction in Singapore is, however, not unusual.  Many societies in the world keep official pay for public servants slow and have difficulties in increasing their pay.  Paradoxically, many societies also have little difficulty in accepting the institutional hypocrisy of low official pay and large unofficial income.  They are content to let that hypocrisy continue and where it is not possible for them to be unofficial income, such societies are content to accept the loss of talent from the public sector. 

 

 

Everyone is content to be less than honest about the situation, and salaries are held hostage to politics.  This mould is politically very difficult to break.  But difficult as it is, we have to, in Singapore, completely break this mould if we are to prosper and move ahead as a society.

 

     Let us look at whom we want in our public service.  We want the very best.  The people we want have many career choices.  They have to be of a quality to get to the top in any profession or field that they choose.  Put it this way:  if someone is not good enough to get to the top of PricewaterhouseCoopers, would you want that person to be a Minister?  Would we want someone as a Minister if he cannot get to the top in a major law firm, MNC or a bank?   Madam, it is a rhetorical question.  The answer is obvious.

 

     Such a talented person will have to choose, and it is a real, serious choice.  Do I want to go into public service or do I want to be a top lawyer, accountant or banker?  Let us not fool ourselves.  The opportunities in the private sector have always been good.  Now, they have become quite extraordinary.  Many of us here know that for a fact.  Take a bright young man in his 30s.  Assume that he is among the brightest in his generation.  Today, he has an array of career choices.  He can stay in Singapore or go overseas.  He can join any MNC or financial institution, or he can stay in a profession.  If he remains in the private sector, he will have his privacy, he can travel the world, live where he likes, have a second and a third home, and choose to live the life he wants.  In his early 40s, he could be earning $2 million and more, and by the time he is in his late 40s, a real high flyer could be taking home $5 million or more.  If the median for top lawyers and accountants is $4.2 million and $3.7 million respectively, Members can guess what those above the median will be making.  That was in 2005, and I am leaving out the real heavy hitting investment bankers out of these scenarios.  Those who are entrepreneurial and start businesses could earn even more.  We are talking about tens of millions.   Of course, the risks are also higher.  But the trend is clear.  In our globalised economy and in this almost borderless world, the economic opportunities are vast and breathtaking for a bright, talented young person.

 

     Do we think it is fair, just or realistic to expect such a person to come into public service and earn 30% or less of what he could earn outside?  It does not make any sense that Ministers should earn a fraction of top private sector professionals, when we look at the different sets of responsibilities and the consequences of how they perform.  Why do we, as a society, think we can expect the brightest and the best to go into public service, earn much less than what they would earn in the private sector, and get regularly attacked and abused for what they do earn?

  

     Is it all pure fantasy and rhetoric that we look for the best to come into Government?  Well, let us look at the frontbench.  It is not good to personalise an argument, but sometimes an illustration helps.  As an example, let us look at the Minister who kicked off this debate.  Minister Teo, by common consent, is among the very brightest of his generation. His academic and subsequent track records speak for themselves.  He runs the Defence Ministry and oversees the civil service.  The rest of us in the private sector earn our money only because he and his team ensure the safety and security of Singapore.  Only the naive will take that for granted in the case of Singapore.

 

     Minister Teo is not a cog in the wheel.  He is at the very apex of the system, and bears the final and ultimate responsibility for the functioning of the system.  I have yet to hear a single convincing argument as to why someone like him should earn less than the senior partner of a major accounting or a law firm in Singapore.  They are not even in the same ball park when we consider the responsibilities.

 

     Would we be better off as a society if he was now an investment banker earning several million dollars?  There are likely to be several in his cohort in the private sector who are earning much more than him.  It probably does not matter to him, otherwise, he will not be in the public service.  But we have to look at the future.  It may matter to the next Teo Chee Hean in his 30s, and it will matter even more to the future of Singapore.  If we do not keep salaries competitive and we lose some of the potential Ministers and Permanent Secretaries, then we will pay dearly as a society - in reduced opportunities, in reduced competitiveness and reduced national wealth.  We can continue navel-gazing, squabble over the individual salaries and, in the process, we run the risk of losing the plot.

 

     When it is such a no-brainer, why is it that people are so resistant?  Let us consider the arguments that are routinely trotted out in support of keeping official pay low.  In summary, the views against the salary increase are as follows:

 

     A Minister's job cannot be compared with that of a top private sector job.  Motivations in each case are different.  The Minister performs public service and he should not be disgruntled about the extra million or so that he could earn outside.  Ministers are already adequately paid and increasing the salaries may break the compact of trust, respect and regard between the Government and the people.  Ministers are expected to make a sacrifice, and we, as a society, should not be coldly materialistic.  To peg Ministers' salaries to the market will transmit wrong values.  Reference was often made to other countries which do not pay their Ministers as much, and which have no corruption.  These arguments are deeply flawed.  They cannot stand the test of logic or an analysis of the facts.

 

     Let me, first, take the point where our approach to salaries is contrasted with that taken by the US and other western countries.  It is a false point because the comparisons are misleading.  In the US, for example, people are in politics or in the senior civil service for a relatively short period.  When they come out, their market value is enhanced.  It is what the Minister Mentor called the "revolving door" system.  It is not a like-for-like comparison.  If we want a honest comparison with the US, then look at areas where public service is a lifetime career.  One example is the US judicial service.

 

     It is very revealing to look at the Federal Court System in the US.  That system used to attract top lawyers.  But years of policy failure in not paying adequate salaries to Judges has resulted in a serious situation, serious enough in the words of the current Chief Justice, John Roberts, to be called "a constitutional crisis" - a constitutional crisis that has arisen because of the lack of political will to pay Judges adequately.  Chief Justice Roberts said this in January this year in his annual report on the US judiciary.

 

     The Chief Justice did not mince his words.  He considered the issue so important that it was the sole point he made in his report.  He made the following points:

 

     Judicial salaries had fallen far below private sector salaries for lawyers.  As a result, in fact, they have stopped comparing the salaries of private sector lawyers, and the number of lawyers from the private sector who were coming in as judges had fallen sharply.  He said, "It changes the nature of the federal judiciary when Judges are no longer drawn from among the best lawyers in the practising bar."  He emphasised the vital importance of a strong and competent judiciary for the well-being of the entire country and the American people.  He criticised Congress' inaction in failing to raise salaries in the strongest language that a judge could possibly use. He said Congress had failed for over 20 years to do anything.  He called Congress' inaction "grievously unfair".  He quoted the National Commission which said that the inaction was "the most egregious example of the failure of federal compensation policies."  He pointed out that the budgetary cost of the increase would be minuscule in proportion to the benefit.  He accepted that a pay rise would be unpopular, but also pointed out that Congress had a duty, indeed a constitutional responsibility, to make a decision which, even if unpopular, is necessary to promote the long-term good.

 

     What would be the result of inaction?  Again, there is no need to guess.  The US experience shows what will happen and I quote Chief Justice Roberts, "In the face of decades of Congressional inaction, many Judges who must attend to their families and futures have no realistic choice except to retire from judicial service and return to private practice.  The numbers are sobering.  In the past six years, 38 Judges have left the Federal bench, including 17 in the last two years.  If judicial appointment ceases to be the capstone of a distinguished career and instead becomes a stepping stone to lucrative positions in private practice, the Framers' goal of a truly independent judiciary will be placed in serious jeopardy."

 

     When you do not keep salaries competitive, what sort of people do you attract?  Again, I quote the Chief Justice:  "Our judiciary will not properly serve its constitutional role if it is restricted to (1) persons so wealthy that they can afford to be indifferent to the level of judicial compensation; or (2) people for whom the judicial salary represents a pay increase."

 

     If we want a truly class-based system of government, or if we want to attract people who will not make it in the private sector, then, yes, go ahead and let the salaries go below the private sector.  For a lawyer, being appointed a judge is the ultimate recognition, the pinnacle of professional achievement or as the Chief Justice said, "the capstone of a distinguished career."  And, yet, judges resigned in enough numbers for it to be called "a constitutional crisis".  That is what will happen if there is too much of a disconnect between public service and private sector income.

 

     Let us not fool ourselves.  The public service, whether in the US or in Singapore, will fail to attract good people, and it will lose good people in such a situation.  If you want that, then go ahead and quibble about the extra $10.5 million or so that will be paid to the entire Cabinet, an amount which will not even qualify as a single top salary in 2006.  Or put it in another way, that is, marginally more than what the two top lawyers in the median income for 2005 were getting.  It is almost surreal that there is so much angst and debate over this issue.  We need a sense of proportion.

 

     The alarm bells about the low levels of judicial pay have been sounded by others in the US as well.  The Financial Times on 5th April 2007 carried a report about competition between New York and London to be the world's leading financial centre.  The stakes are high  - thousands of jobs, billions of dollars.  One of the disadvantages of New York was stated to be its litigation culture.  In that context, the Chairman of one of the top US law firms, Mr Cohen of Sullivan & Cromwell, said that part of the problem lay in the quality of the US federal judges.  He was quoted as saying, "A very radical idea would be to take the federal judiciary and double the salaries, so you would be able to keep your best judges instead of them being forced to go into private practice."

 

     This is the US which critics of our pay policy frequently cite as a model to be followed.  The truth is that there has been a failure of political will in the US to pay adequately.  Do not compare with the politicians.  That is not a like-for-like comparison.

 

     The politicians in the US will not suffer as a result of such inaction.  They will have opportunities to get good compensation often when they are out of office.  Former President Bill Clinton gets an average of US$250,000 per speech that he delivers.  So does former Secretary of State, Mr Henry Kissinger.  People simply do not understand the US system, and do not realise what it will mean to us if we follow that system.  Look at Senior Minister and Minister Mentor.  If we had the US system, they will be spending their post-Prime Ministerial years giving speeches for money.  Minister Mentor could join Kissinger Associates and Senior Minister could join the Carlyle Group.  Better still, they could form their own lobby group  -  call it Lee-Goh Associates.  This is no laughing matter.  This happens everywhere.  They can then lobby the Government they used to lead or open doors in China, India, the Middle East or elsewhere, not on behalf of Singapore, but on behalf of their own corporate clients and for their own benefit.  Lee-Goh Associates  -  what do you think it could pull in in a year?  $20-$30 million, much more, no problem.

 

     Do we not think about all this when we make facile comparisons?  Is it fantasy?  Well, is that not what happens in a country that we refer to as a model to be followed?  So, why not here?  There is no law against it.  It is ultimately Singaporeans who will pay the price if we do not pay our public officials properly.

 

     These are real issues.  Chief Justice Roberts and Mr Cohen are serious people dealing with serious issues which go to the heart of the American system.  They decry the way American judges are paid.  True, we are here debating the salaries of not only our judges but also a broader group, including politicians.  But the principle, the rationale, is the same because, in both situations, it is a career choice with a substantial element of public service; and it would be normal to think that when you accept appointment of a senior judge, you put the element of public duty over pay.

 

     In this competitive world, there will be a direct impact on all of us if the best people are not in Government.  And in the fierce race to the top, when we are competing with other countries which are bigger and have more resources, it is critical for our future that we have two essential elements: 

 

 

one, a talented, creative population; and two, the very best in leadership positions.  Without either, we will fall short.  We could even seriously falter because, in many ways, we are not a natural state. Too many Singaporeans seem to take our stability and progress for granted without a nuanced understanding of our somewhat precarious geopolitical and strategic situation.

 

     We can be more sanguine if we were Monaco, in the middle of a prosperous and peaceful Europe.  But we are in Southeast Asia, in a region rife with religious, ethnic and racial tensions and afflicted by poverty.  If we leave aside diplomatic niceties, then the truth is that our neighbours do not really love us - and that is putting it mildly.  If it was possible, they would make our lives much more uncomfortable.  And that is also putting it mildly.  It is our task to ensure that it is not possible for them to do that to us.

 

    The natural state of affairs in this region therefore is disorder and tension.  It is not peace, progress and stability.  We have turned this dangerous state of affairs on its head, and used it for our prosperity.  But we have to work with that disorder and work very hard, all the time, to make sure that it does not wash over us.  And we have to keep several steps ahead of it on all fronts.  The arguments about whether we should pay our entire Cabinet S$10 million per annum more, appear almost absurd when compared with the real issues.

 

     So much for comparisons with other countries.  When you deal with policy issues, make sure your arguments are valid.  Look at the US, New Zealand, Finland; look at their history, population, culture, their political systems, the type of people they get into Government and then ask yourselves whether direct comparisons make sense.

 

     Now, let me deal with the second point that is often made - that public office involves substantial public service and therefore, there must be a sacrifice.  It throws up a number of questions.  How do you quantify the sacrifice that is needed?  What is the nature of the sacrifice that is needed?  Or I will make an even more fundamental point.  If you look at how this concept of public service came about, you will see how much it is bound by hypocrisy.  This concept was developed at a time when one had to be an aristrocrat to be in power.  The somewhat patronising approach was that the aristrocrats had to do their bit for society by taking on political office - Noblesse Oblige. 

 

     The truth, however, is more prosaic and less romantic.  A friend sent me an extract from the British House of Commons Information Office on British Ministerial Salaries and that document has this wonderful quote from Lord Macaulay about what office holders who came in for public service, under the so-called concept of Noblesse Oblige, did.  I quote:

 

     "From the nobleman who held the White Staff and the Great Seal, down to the humblest tidewaiter and gauger, what would now be called gross corruption was practised without disguise and without reproach.  Titles, places, commissions, pardons, were daily sold in the market overtly by the greatest dignatories of the realm; in the 17th Century, a statesman who was at the head of affairs might easily and without giving scandal, accumulate in no long time, an estate amply sufficient to support a dukedom." 

 

     Office holding in truth meant power and through power, serious wealth.  Human nature has not changed much over the years.  When you design a system, you do not work on the basis that you will get another generation like our founding generation.  You set up a system for bright people with perfectly understandable and acceptable wishes, aspirations and values.

 

     Ms Denise Phua spoke from her heart.  I admire the conviction with which she spoke and she said there are people who will be prepared to sacrifice.  I agree but that misses the point.  Some may be prepared to sacrifice but we cannot take a chance and rely on people making sacrifices in order to come to public service.  It is too important.  It is the future of Singapore.  You cannot rely on people's charity.  You have to put in place a system that you know will work and not rely on the good faith alone. 

 

     After the 17th Century, the British reformed their system and by the early 20th century, a top Minister like Churchill was earning about 5,000 pounds.   I am told that that would be worth about 350,000 pounds in today's terms.  That is roughly what our MR4 was.  But British Ministerial salaries have not kept pace with inflation.  It is probably not politically feasible in the UK.  But that does not make it right.  As an aside, if you read Roy Jenkin's biography of Churchill, you will see that Churchill also made some money by writing.  For example, he received an advance of 8,000 pounds for a book (about half a million pounds now) in the early 20th century, just before he took office.  In the year in 1960, President Kennedy was getting US$150,000.  The Presidential salaries had kept pace with inflation.  The salary would be higher than the US$450,000 that the US President now gets. 

 

    So we need to know this concept and the context and the history before we start talking about public service and sacrifice.  Historically, there was really no real sacrifice in financial terms and salaries were relatively high and, when they were low, there were other significant advantages.  Through a lack of political will, over time, public service salaries have fallen in many countries.  Why do we think that such an approach is a correct one or that it will work for us? 

 

     When we talk about sacrifice, ask - what is the logic behind the idea of sacrifice?  Why do we demand financial sacrifice from those going into public service?  Is that the most sensible way of showing one's commitment to one's country?  It is surely more logical to make sure that money is a neutral factor, ie, it should neither be an attraction nor a disincentive.  Qualified people should be able to make the choice of public service based solely on whether they have the ability, character and commitment to make a difference to society.

 

     Further, when you refer to idealism and sacrifice, the question also is - how much sacrifice is enough?  Take the young person whom I referred to earlier, who is about to embark on a career.  He can go into public service or he can go and become a lawyer or an accountant with an expectation that he will get S$4 million or more a year; or start a business with the possibility of eventually earning tens of millions.  And we are talking about the very best - the people who know that if they start out on a career, they will reach the top.  If he chooses the public service and if he is paid S$2 million  a year, he will, nevertheless, forgo S$2 million a year over 10 to 15 years.  That is a S$20 million to S$30 million sacrifice.  Is that not enough?  How many successful people in the private sector contribute S$25 million to charity?  I will say, let us get real.

 

     The problem, of course, is for the bulk of the population, it is difficult to comprehend this sort of numbers.  So, it is easy to make political points, as Mr Low sometimes makes, but we have a greater duty to look at the market from which we are sourcing the talent and saying what is it that we need to do in order to get them.  Money should not be an attraction nor should it be a disincentive.

 

     Yes, Ministers are public servants.  Yes, they have to be much more than the equivalent of top private sector professionals.  But taking the arguments made by the critics at face value, a Minister has to have all the brain power and analytical qualities and energy of a top private sector performer; and on top of that, he  has to also have a strong sense of public spiritedness, ie, they should be more capable and more all-rounded than private sector high fliers.  Is that an argument or is that a logic for saying that Ministers then should get less than private sector top performers?

 

     The central thread underlying all these arguments against a salary increase is a breezy assumption: that we are in a steady state, there is a lot of talents available and people will cheerfully give up S$25 million over a 10 to 15-year period for the sake of public service.  And that even if the best do not come into public service, nevertheless, we will somehow carry on prosperously, like various European countries.  I wish such a  "blue sky" scenario is real.  But an hour's plane ride will tell you otherwise.

 

     Then, there are number of other points made: is the formula correct?  Are we looking at too small a sample?  The private sector winners will change every year, whereas the Ministers remain the same, and that means the Ministers will win every year, and so on.

 

     Well, Ministers are at the apex of the system.  You have to compare them with those at the apex of the private sector.  Any other comparison will be meaningless.  If you want the very best - then the comparisons must be with the very best.  In this context, I would like to refer to what my colleagues, Mr Hri Kumar and others, have said - that senior partners, for example, of major professional firms are equity owners and take an enterprise risk and that their income is different from salaries.  In theory, that may be so but, in truth and in reality, the fact is if you are bright and you are very good and you start out in a professional firm, you will reach the top and along the way, you accumulate enough equity.  So, sometimes, the risk is overstated.  The test is that there is really a lot of room at the top.  You just have to be good enough.  And if you are good enough, you will overcome all these risks.  Therefore, the best and most suitable approach is really to compare with the top performers, as long as the Minister is performing.

 

     Madam, I will end with another quote, again from Chief Justice Roberts.  He said: "Reason commands action."  He said that in January 2007.  For us as well, reason commands action.  Inaction, due to lack of political will, will be a failure - a failure to do the right thing.

 

     The critics of this policy are however right on one thing.  Public sentiment is not positive on these changes.  It will strain the social compact.  And I do not believe that the ground sentiment is going to change anytime soon.  So this is one of those policies on which political capital has to be spent.  But spend it we must, for the longer-term good of this country.  And we have to do our best to bring across the nature of our society, the risks we face and how we are different from other more natural countries, to explain and, hopefully, eventually convince the ground.  It will take time. [Applause. ]

6.27 pm

 

     Mr Sin Boon Ann (Tampines): Madam, coming after two very forceful speakers - Minister Teo Chee Hean and Mr Shanmugam - I can only say that they presented very forceful and persuasive arguments as to why the motion standing in the name of the Minister should stand.  However, I can also say that after today's debate, there is still this element of unease that remains with the public and I suspect that the underlying sense of unease is a feeling that the public service and the private sector rewards cannot similarly be compared.  I feel that some comparisons may be made, but I would not think a case can be made that complete comparisons may be made.

 

    If I may repeat, at the heart of the Government's argument lies the belief that people and talent should be adequately rewarded by money. And I agree with Mr Shanmugam that it would be naive and simplistic to assume that anyone, no matter how he loves Singapore, would be motivated to public service by ideals alone. Just how adequate and sufficient the reward should be remains the crux of the issue. So we have identified some professions and used the median income of these professions as a basis for rewarding our Ministers. The question is always: is this a fair comparison?

 

    Some will say that it is not. For them, the main difference lies in the market discipline and the regulatory controls imposed to ensure fair market practices and consumer protection. Take the case of a CEO of a listed company. Many listed company CEOs will tell you soon enough of how the market will punish them, their company stocks particularly, if their periodic numbers are not profitable. Often, CEOs are rewarded on the basis of the financial performance and that is usually benchmarked against the bottom line or the profits of the company. In many cases, where the company is not doing well, or the company continues to bleed red ink, stock prices will more likely dive southwards and in many cases, CEOs will be told to go; which perhaps explains why many CEOs in the US, while they are paid very handsomely, do not last on the average more than five years on the job. In their case, every time the company performs badly quarter on quarter, the CEO's job is at risk. And the market knows it because of the legal requirement, in the case of listed entities, to post the results of the company's performance on a regular basis.

 

     Or, take the case of a professional. Why is a lawyer paid the wages that he earns? Again, market discipline plays a major part in this. Would a lawyer be able to earn the kind of salary that he enjoys if he was no good? I am a lawyer, and I will be the first to tell you that if I am no good, my clients would take their custom elsewhere to other lawyers who will be eagerly standing in line to take my place. Conversely, if a lawyer is good, because he is able to solve complex legal problems, he will be paid a premium and no one would begrudge him of that.

 

 

    In the case of Ministers' salaries, many would argue that it would not be fair to compare Ministers' salaries with the private sector because they are not subject to the same market discipline as the high earners in the private sector are. For goods and services, a client is free to go to another firm if he is not satisfied with what he is getting. The same, however, cannot be said of public services. Outside of general elections, there are no competing services for public policies on health, education, or transportation, for example, that would set the appropriate real time benchmark for determining the quality of the Minister's performance. Equally, in many cases, at least unlike listed companies, there is no established framework of disclosure that would help to determine the actual performance of the Ministries, the quality of the Minister's contribution to the policy process and the outcome of these policies as to its effectiveness. In the context of and the tradition of the British parliamentary democracy which we inherited, the Minister's performance is determined by the ability of the backbencher to raise questions about particular policies and the Minister's continuing stay in Cabinet would depend largely on his ability to give an account of and defend the policies of his Ministry. This is very much a hit and miss approach for which I cannot say is entirely satisfactory. Why so?

 

     Firstly, information flow is more often guarded by secrecy than by open candour; with the consequence that unless a scandal breaks out, whether or not exposed by the media, or the Auditor-General picks up an administrative lapse, it would be difficult for the public to have a full and complete picture of the performance of the Ministry and, hence, the performance of the Minister. I do not deny that our Ministers and Ministries are excellent. However, as it stands, we do not know if they could be better; or if the Ministries are performing badly, it could be worse but for the work of the Minister and his senior civil servants.

 

     Secondly, under the current system, the work of the Ministry is carried out by the professional civil servants who are led by the Permanent Secretary. The Minister’s role is largely political, the main thrust of which is the strategic direction and political defence of the work of the Ministry. In this regard, it is the civil servants who prepare the answers to questions raised in Parliament and often, provide answers to anticipated questions. Political appointees do not often write papers. They do not do the research, neither do they undertake the execution of policies. But they provide policy directions in a similar way that directors of a company provide the strategic thrust for the management. Above all, they take responsibility for the work of the Ministry in the same way that the Companies Act pins responsibility on directors for the failings of the company.

 

     Very often, as in the case of companies, it is the civil servant who may bring up proposals to be endorsed by the political master. How much work, input or value add is provided by the Minister is not clearly defined in the current framework. For this reason, while the public would have no issue with the high salary given to the Permanent Secretaries and senior civil servants, they become a lot more apprehensive when it comes to Ministers. Should the Ministers therefore be remunerated for the responsibility that they take, or should they be paid by policy directions that they provide? I think the Government should be clear about this.

 

     Madam, it has been a while since we initiated the policy of pegging Ministers' salaries to private sector salaries. How have we fared? Underlying the debate is the belief that we would be in a better position in tapping talent from the private sector, after implementing the policy proposed by the White Paper in 1994. In the current Cabinet of full Ministers, one Minister came from a bank and a surgeon came from the private practice. The rest were elevated from the civil service, having spent the most part of their careers in policy-making. With the exception of one, whose profession is not included in the list of professions used as benchmarks, their present salaries generally would have been higher than what they were earning previously.

 

     What does it mean for us? After 14 years since we introduced the policy of pegging the Ministers' salary to the private sector, there is still no clear evidence that pegging the Ministers' salary will necessarily result in more people from the private sector being inducted into public service. The fact remains that the bulk of our Ministerial candidates continue to be drawn from the civil service; and rightly so, as these entrants would have had long years of policy experience to take on Ministerial appointments in the Cabinet. Yet, this does not mean that we do not have to pay our civil servants and Ministers well. I suppose for civil servants, it is settled principle that we should pay them well enough to keep them happy in service and for Ministers, well enough for talented and able civil servants to know that there is career and pay advancement beyond the level of the Permanent Secretary. If we can accept this as a basic principle, even in the way we apply the settled formula, I am sure we will be in a position to decide on what is a reasonable compensation for all.

 

    The question at the end of the day, Madam, is not how much a Minister should be paid. I do not think that people mind generally that Ministers should be paid highly. The question really is whether there are sufficient and clear benchmarks that will properly determine the Minister's work and the quality of his contribution to the work of the Ministry. I believe it will be easier for the people to accept increases in the Ministers' salary if the Government is more open about the process of measuring the performance of the Minister. In past debates in this House on this topic, there have been calls, including today, in the light of Ms Denise Phua's speech, to have a more transparent process of measuring the performance of Ministers; such as perhaps having an independent body, headed by the President, to provide an independent and institutionalised process of assessing the performance of Ministers because this will help to add to the confidence of the process of fixing the wages and rewarding Ministers. I support this proposal and hope the Government will consider this.

 

     Finally, to serve our nation is both a duty and a privilege which all Singaporeans should apply himself or herself to without hesitation. Money is an important consideration. However, it must never be at the forefront of our consideration to enter public service. High salaries as inducement for entering public service will rob the Minister and his office of the moral high ground to serve. Great names in history are often remembered for the sacrifices that were made than by the monies that they earned. Highly capable people who are not prepared to make the sacrifice should not be called to serve. In fact, in some cases, I think our country’s interest will be better served, if they remain where they are. They can be co-opted into service in other ways as this Government has often done.

     Ms Eunice Elizabeth Olsen (Nominated Member): Mdm Deputy Speaker, thank you for allowing me to participate in this debate.

 

     Madam, when I spoke in this House on the Integrated Resorts, I thought that that was one hotly debated topic.  Now, I realise that the debate on public service pay is much more highly charged.  The strange coincidence is on that day, I was also one of the last speakers in the House and I was holding everyone up from dinner, so I asked the remaining Members in this House to please bear with me.

 

     Let me start by listing some basic premises everyone can all agree on.  Firstly, civil servants and public service leaders must be compensated adequately, and wage adequacy depends on the relevant private compensation levels.  I have many friends who are teachers, for example, and I know they toil very hard.  They need to be given their dues.

 

      Next, we need to cultivate a strong public service ethos in our nation so that we can have the right people with the best interests of Singapore, and enough of them to take the nation forward.  For a young nation, it is an imperative to build up dedication to the nation and its institutions, such as the civil service.

 

     Finally, Singaporeans want a good and honest Government.  It is important for us to agree on all these premises, and I think we all do.

 

     This is how I think the issue on the civil service salary revisions should be framed.  However, the way this issue has played out in our national discourse has been unfortunate. The problem is in the messaging to Singaporeans.  Right now, the message from the Government is that the only way to get people to serve the country, especially at the top leadership positions in political office, is to lure them with money. It may not be the message the Government intends to send out.  As the Minister in charge of civil service said yesterday, "financial rewards cannot and should not be the main motivation of those in the public service". But this latter aspect has only been on the periphery of this long-standing and widely debated issue, at least, in respect to service in the top leadership positions. 

 

     As far back as 1989, the Prime Minister, then the Minister for Trade and Industry, said that the Government "will offer whatever salaries are necessary to attract and retain the talent that it needs".  Madam, this has been the consistent message from the Government.

 

     If we have to go extreme financial lengths, I, like my colleague, Mr Siew, question whether we would be getting the right people to lead this country.  Running a country is not the same as running a company.  As a small and young nation, we may be able to ride on the buoyancy of a good economy, but we can only be anchored by firm ideals and values.  Without the anchor, we would lose our bearings.  Surely then, public service motivated by passion and dedication is one of those ideals and values.  Surely then, we have to cultivate and nurture this.  If we focus on the financial rewards, and we design a system which emphasises this aspect, are we acting in our nation's best long-term interest?

 

 

     With the latest revisions, we are told that a $1.2 million salary is not enough to build a good enough Government.  $1.6 million is now needed.  If Singapore's Government waxes and wanes by the dollar quantum, I think we are in trouble. 

 

     Madam, we know we can get a good and honest Government without getting into a race for the highest salaries.  We need not look at Finland, Denmark and Switzerland for examples.  I recently read a research paper by NUS political science lecturer, Jon Quah, that the PAP Government was only able to implement the second prong of its anti-corruption strategy, ie, the reduction of incentives for corruption by improving the salaries and working conditions in the Singapore Civil Service, in the 1980s, long after we had achieved economic growth.  Although they lagged behind what would have been the commensurate market pay at that time, much more than the current gap, Singaporeans still enjoyed good governance.  Although we do not want to return to that situation and we can afford to pay more now, the point is that good governance is not exponentially increased with increasing salaries.  It may be helpful to bear in mind that the principle of diminishing marginal returns will set in.  Beyond paying a certain amount, how much more are we improving good governance?

 

     On the other hand, we should be concerned about the message that we are sending, especially to our youth.  We should be wary about suggesting that we can buy leadership, that we can buy dedication, compassion for people and the willingness to serve.  Administrative culture both reflects and influences the norms of society at large.  In a small country like Singapore, where the State plays a far bigger role than in other countries, it wields greater influence in the transmission of values.  We should moderate the current financial-driven approach to public service.  The Government may put off people who are willing to serve out of passion.  In fact, the suggestion is that the Government is only looking for individuals from the top 438 income-earners to serve, since the emphasis is on the financial sacrifices said to be involved.

 

     Madam, it is not just about idealism, although it must still be the most important point.  Sometimes, the implication of a policy or system cannot be foreseen, and there could be unforeseen dangers in the current system for Ministerial salaries.  We hear continual warnings from this Government about the danger of being led by a rogue government.  Madam, I agree.  Precisely, when political power is coupled with immense financial power, these are the conditions that make it more likely to lead to the creation of such a government, especially if we attract people who are motivated by financial gain.  We can dangle million-dollar salaries in the belief that it is the only way to get good people, but we may instead get people with ulterior motives, which are much harder to detect.  As a small country, we cannot risk that.  Moreover, with a system which pegs salaries at between the 24th and 25th highest earner of only six professions, I fear that it is easy for a government which chooses to do so, to enrich itself in subtle but deceitful ways, such as by providing preferential access to public resources, government contracts or favourable legislation.  In the current system, it is easy for politicians to establish a close nexus of connections with just a few dozen income earners.  While the current set of leaders is honest enough not to exploit this, can this be guaranteed for all future governments?

 

     Minister Teo has pointed out, and I thank him for his explanation, the stringent criteria in selecting good people.  Madam, the reason why I am still concerned is because the question is whether we are willing to take such risks.  At the LKY School of Public Policy event last year, when asked if it is possible to hire foreign talent to run the country, the Minister Mentor said: "No.  You must have the passion, you must have the commitment, you must share the dreams of the people."  So we must always try to nurture this sense of public service and find the right ways and the right balance in attracting our future leaders.  Are we in danger of casting this aspect to the periphery? 

 

     Madam, I am glad the Minister in charge of the civil service welcomes our views and ideas about the methodology for arriving at a suitable salary benchmark for Ministers, senior civil servants and directors in the Administrative Service, otherwise known as the MR4 and SR9 benchmarks.  He said yesterday that there is "no perfect method" for doing these benchmarks.  Madam, I humbly submit that not only is the current method less than perfect, it is flawed.

 

     Besides the possibility of abuse, it also goes against the principle that the Minister said that our public service subscribes to - the principle that "we do not seek to lead the market, but to keep pace with it".  Here is how:  Salaries at the current benchmark at the MR4 grade, would rank our 18 Cabinet Ministers within the top 438 income earners.  The current number of senior Permanent Secretaries in the civil service has not been reviewed, but a Straits Times' article in 2004 reported that there were eight Permanent Secretaries, some of whom were stepping down to be replaced by others.  Let us say that there are 25 Ministers and senior civil servants altogether.  That would mean nearly 6% of the top income earners come from the public service.  But, Madam, the Singapore Civil Service, with a size of 64,000 people, represents less than 2.5% of the Singapore labour force of 2.6 million workers.  If the policy is truly to ensure that public officers are paid commensurate with the private sector, and nothing more, then a better benchmark would be to peg salaries to the point at which the Ministers and senior civil servants are proportionately represented among the top jobs in the private sector.  If there are 25 individuals in the MR4 grade or higher, then the benchmark should be at the 1,000th income-earner.  Likewise, if the figure is 20 individuals, then the benchmark is at the 800th income-earner.  It becomes a self-adjusting formula.  The principle is such:  2.5% of the labour force, 2.5% of the top income earners.  This upholds the principle of keeping pace with the market and not leading it.  The current benchmark does not.  Even the Government has not followed the benchmark more times than it has since 1994.

 

     Madam, this should also apply to the SR9 benchmark.  Depending on how many Administrative Service officers are in this salary grade, the pay package should be pegged to the point at which they are commensurately represented among the top earners aged 32.  Again, the number of officers in this grade has not been revealed.  But whatever the number, pegging it to the 15th income earner is completely arbitrary and definitely leads the market rather than follow it.

 

     Madam, finally, there is one thing that puzzles me.  I understand that the benchmark is meant to be reviewed every five years.  The 1994 White Paper was implemented the following year and the next salary review came in 2000 and was implemented in 2001.  So, given that resignation rates have been going up in the civil service since then, why did we not do the salary review in 2005?  Why did we wait until now when the situation seems to be more dire?  If attracting good people to serve the country is the challenge, why did we not do the salary review two years ago when the PAP Government was looking for people to stand in elections and serve the country?  Perhaps the Minister can help to clarify this.

 

     Madam, we should never downplay the capabilities of Singaporeans in their role in taking Singapore forward, in terms of the potential they have to lead the country with dedication and passion and in their contributions in tandem with the Government.  They, as much as the Government, are the reason we have progressed to where we are now.  Leadership that flies on the wings of passion and dedication can take the country higher than the engine of financial rewards.  We will always reap what we sow.
