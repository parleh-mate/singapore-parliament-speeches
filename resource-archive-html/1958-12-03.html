(Announcement by Mr Speaker)


    Mr Speaker: It is my duty to direct the attention of the Assembly, in its capacity as guardian of the prestige of the Speaker, to a report of a statement purported to have been made by the Honourable Member for Queenstown to the Singapore Standard and reported in that paper of the 27th of November, 1958, under the caption:

"Mr Lee & new Chief Justice's appointment
He protests against Speaker's ruling"

Reference in the article is made to two questions disallowed by me as being inadmissible, one on the appointment of the Chief Justice for Singapore and the other on the proposal to abolish Teochew, Hainanese, Khek and Foochow radio programmes.

    The article, inter alia, states:

    'Mr Lee said that it had been the usual practice for the Speaker to give reasons whenever he ruled questions as "inadmissible".

    "In this case, no reason had been given", he added.'

    At the outset it is my duty to point out that action of the nature reported to have been taken by the Honourable Member in protesting against the ruling of the Speaker through the Press is not only discourteous but also improper, the more so in the light of the implication of a sinister motive contained in the passage quoted by me.

    I am glad to be able to say, however, that the Honourable Member has assured me that he did not intend to be discourteous or improper. He further assures me that he told the reporter concerned that he accepted my rulings but was disappointed that the questions could not be tabled. He would then, of course, have been informed of the reasons for my rulings.

    Honourable Members are, I am sure, aware that if they are dissatisfied with the rulings of the Speaker, the proper course would be to raise the matter with the Speaker direct in an interview or by letter, and if thereafter they are still dissatisfied, their remedy is by way of substantive motions for review.

    I may add that although the relevant Standing Order in regard to questions does not provide that reasons should be given for any ruling, I have for the convenience of Members directed the Clerk to revive the practice of acquainting Honourable Members with the reasons for any adverse ruling to enable them all the better to decide whether they will or will not raise the matter with the Speaker.

SEAMEN
(Discrimination; Signing of agreements)


    7. Mr Seah Peng Chuan asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry whether -

    (a) the Seamen's Registry Board, in supplying registered seamen to employers, discriminates against seamen who are members of unions;

    (b) he will arrange for permission to be given for an official of the union to which a seaman belongs to be present when the seaman signs an "agreement with the crew"; and

    (c) he will arrange for a copy of such agreement to be provided to the seaman concerned.

    Mr J. M. Jumabhoy: The answers are:

    (a) Certainly not.

    (b) Union officials can be and are allowed to be present outside the counter in the Shipping Office when seamen are signing Articles of Agreement. Provided good reasons are furnished to the Shipping Office and upon written application by the seamen themselves, Union officials may be present behind the counter.

    (c) There is no legal provision for such a procedure, but the law does require however that the Agreement be read over and explained by the Port Officer to each seaman. At the commencement of every voyage, the Master is also bound by law to post up a legible copy of the Agreement in some part of the ship which is accessible to the crew. This arrangement is being observed and has been found to work very satisfactorily on all British ships since 1854 and conforms with international shipping practice.

COTTONS FROM CHINA
(Import ban)


    8. Mr Seah Peng Chuan asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry whether he will consider lifting the ban on the import of cottons into Singapore from China.

    Mr J. M. Jumabhoy: It will be recalled that when the Federation requested our co-operation in this matter it was for a period of three months pending the finalisation of the Federation's anti-dumping law to protect its industries. It was on this basis that the Singapore Government agreed to the Federation's request. The Singapore Government will therefore review this suspension at the end of the year in the light of the circumstances then existing and the progress of the Federation's anti-dumping legislation. Meanwhile, import licences which have been issued up to the date of the ban are confirmed without hindrance on proof of commitment before the ban.
