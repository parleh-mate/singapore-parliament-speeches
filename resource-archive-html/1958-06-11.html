The Government agreed with these recommendations in the White Paper on Government policy relating to local government.

    The Local Government Bill, 1957, which has been passed by the Legislative Assembly, makes enabling provision for local authorities to make bylaws now for the control, regulation and supervision by registration, licensing or otherwise of places used for real performances, theatres, wayangs, concerts and other public exhibitions and enter tainments.

    A considerable part of the Theatres Ordinance (Cap. 229) which assigned some of this responsibility to the police consequently becomes obsolete and fit

for repeal. In addition, other provisions of the same Ordinance have become out of date through changes in the social life of the community since it was enacted in 1909. It is timely therefore to repeal the whole of the Theatres Ordinance and to replace it by a new Ordinance directed to the regulation of public entertainments in general. This Bill has accordingly been framed so as to require that the Licensing Authority constituted under it should have regard primarily to the nature of public entertainments for which licences are sought; while also concerned that these are held only in places suitable to their nature, the Licensing Authority is permitted to presume the suitability of buildings and other premises to the extent to which this has been certified by a local government authority.

    This Bill, Sir, which repeals the present Theatres Ordinance and the Billiards Ordinance which is equally out of date and will no longer be necessary if this Bill is enacted, is based on the following considerations and requirements: the interest of the State in controlling entertainments to which the public has access is fourfold - to preserve peace and good order: to prevent moral, social and political degeneracy: to prevent physical injury and suffering whether to performers or to audiences: to secure revenue sufficient at least to offset the expense of control; the definition of "public entertainment" which should be as clear as possible and comprehensive enough to take account of the much greater range of popular amusements available nowadays so that loopholes in the system of control could be prevented as much as possible; the statutory exemptions of some kinds of public entertainment because their sponsorship and circumstances adequately guarantee the State's interest being safeguarded; the exemption from control either wholly or in part of other kinds of "public entertainment" the nature of which cannot readily be perverted to ends contrary to the State's interest; the introduction of a system of licensing of "public entertainment" which provides for the identification of all persons on whom lies the onus of compliance with the
