ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

SINGAPORE ECONOMY
(Volatility)


    1. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) whether the Singapore economy is likely to be subjected to even greater volatility in future as we invest more heavily in the life sciences, pharmaceuticals and bio-medical sectors; and (b) what measures his Ministry has taken or will take to help mitigate the possibility of even greater volatility of the Singapore economy.

Column: 8



    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, the greater variation in Singapore's economic growth recently is in part due to the volatility of the global electronics industry. After the bursting of the Internet bubble, global semiconductor sales fell sharply by 32% in 2001 after a record 37% growth the year before. Given Singapore's small and open economy and the significant size of electronics, we cannot insulate ourselves from the cyclical nature of the global electronics industry.

    To lessen the cyclical swings, we have been diversifying our economy. In manufacturing, we are strengthening the other 3 clusters besides electronics, namely, chemicals, engineering and the Biomedical Sciences. Within electronics, we are developing various segments like data storage, info-communications and key modules to diversify the value chain. As these segments do not necessarily follow the same cycle, the sector as a whole will be less volatile.

    Dr Amy Khor asks if our promotion of life sciences, pharmaceuticals and bio-medical sectors will increase the volatility in our economy. This is not likely, for 3 reasons. First, the cycle for these 3 areas, which we refer to as the Biomedical Sciences cluster, is not synchronised with electronics. Hence, it can help to even out overall growth rates. For example, while electronics fell by 21% in 2001, the Biomedical Sciences cluster grew by 10%, moderating the decline in the manufacturing sector.

    Second, within the Biomedical Sciences cluster itself, there are three different segments - pharmaceuticals, medical technology and biotechnology. Pharmaceuticals account for some 80% of the total Biomedical Sciences manufacturing output. Pharmaceutical producers have diversified their manufacturing activities to include formulation, fill and finishing of final dosage forms and biotech products. Although smaller in size, the medical technology industry is also expanding strongly. Last year, it enjoyed a 14% growth in manufacturing output and

Column: 9

contributed the remaining 20% of Biomedical Sciences output. Over time, the increase in the number of plants and product types will also smoothen output patterns. As the Biomedical Sciences cluster develops greater breadth and depth, the growth pattern will likely be more stable. In this regard, rather than greater volatility, investing in Biomedical Sciences can help to even out the growth of the manufacturing sector in Singapore.

    Third, media reports on monthly figures have characterised the pharmaceutical segment as "volatile". This is not accurate. There are no inherent swings in demand for pharmaceutical products per se. Rather, the variations in output from month to month are due to the way pharmaceutical products are made. Pharmaceutical companies in Singapore manufacture active ingredients for global markets in batches, to take advantage of the high degree of flexibility of our new "multi-product" plants. The plants are designed to be multi-purpose and produce different batches of ingredients at different times of the year. Each batch of ingredients has different value and market requirements. The batch-oriented manufacturing process is a major reason for the seeming volatility in Biomedical Sciences output from one month to the next. It would be more accurate to measure performance on a year-to-year basis, which shows that the Biomedical Sciences cluster has achieved strong and consistent growth in total output, employment and value added for the past 5 years.

    The ERC has also recommended a number of strategies to build a more diversified economy. This includes developing exportable services and encouraging greater entrepreneurship to complement the MNCs. Our national objective is to build a globalised, diversified and entrepreneurial economy.

    Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan (Hong Kah): Sir, may I ask the Minister if, in addition to placing emphasis on sectors such as pharmaceuticals and biomedical, the Ministry would continue to ensure that sectors which may not be identified as

growth areas but which are equally viable, for instance, those proposed by small and medium enterprises, would also receive equal attention or help?

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Absolutely. We are interested in all viable sectors and we are paying particular attention to growth enterprises, including those from small and medium enterprises. In fact, this constitutes a fair proportion of the recommendations in the Economic Review Committee's report.

FREE TRADE AGREEMENT BETWEEN SINGAPORE AND EUROPEAN UNION



    2. Miss Penny Low asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) whether there has been any progress towards a bilateral Free Trade Agreement (FTA) between Singapore and the European Union (EU) or does the EU still prefer to work on a multilateral FTA framework with ASEAN; and (b) what is the probability of a successful conclusion of either FTA in the next three years or so.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Mr Speaker, Sir, the European Commission has not yet replied officially to Singapore's proposal for an EU-Singapore FTA although Trade Commissioner Pascal Lamy commented publicly in February last year that Singapore has made a compelling strategic and economic case for such an FTA. In the past year, the European Commission has been holding consultations with industry on whether there is a strong business case for such an FTA. The Commission has indicated on various occasions that its current priority is to concentrate on the Doha Development Agenda and that it prefers to consider new FTA initiatives only after its completion in 2005.

    As for the EU's economic engagement with ASEAN, Commissioner Lamy has informed me that he is working on a flexible framework which takes into account the different stages of development of the 10 ASEAN countries. This

new framework should be ready by the time he meets the ASEAN Economic Ministers in early April in Laos at our informal retreat. Realistically, an EU-ASEAN FTA can only be a long-term objective.

    Miss Penny Low (Pasir Ris-Punggol): Sir, how important is the EU-Singapore FTA to Singapore?

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: It will be very important. The EU makes up a large part of our total economic account. They have vast investments here. We are by far their most important trade and investment partner in Southeast Asia.

    My own sense talking to EU officials is that they understand the advantages of having such an FTA with Singapore but they are concerned about how they engage ASEAN generally. They are also concerned that they should not send a strong signal to the WTO that it is somehow less interested in the Doha Development Agenda. It is an important initiative for us and we are pursuing it. I believe it will take time but eventually it will come about.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: FTAs mean the removal of tariffs for most products that go to our FTA partner. For example, our FTA with Japan enables some of our petrochemical products to enter Japan without tariff which gives exporters here a major advantage. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for the Environment (a) what are the targeted quantities and proportion of NEWater and desalinated water that will be produced for direct and/or indirect use by households and industries, by (i) 2011 when our first water agreement with Malaysia expires, and (ii) 2061 when our

    The Minister for the Environment (Mr Lim Swee Say): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to answer both Question Nos. 4 and 5 together because they are inter-related?

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    Mr Lim Swee Say: Thank you, Sir. Mr Steve Chia asked whether Singapore is aiming towards 100% self-sufficiency in our water resources and, if so, when will this be achieved. Sir, currently, we have two sources of water supply, namely, water from our local catchments as well as untreated water from Johor under the 1961 and 1962 Water Agreements. As the two agreements will expire only in 2011 and 2061 respectively, the issue of self-sufficiency does not arise until 2061.

    The question before us is therefore not so much one of how soon we can be self-sufficient in water supply. Rather, it is about whether we can become, firstly, more self-sufficient by 2011 when the 1961 agreement expires; and secondly, totally self-sufficient by 2061 when the

Column: 16

1962 agreement expires. And the answers to both these questions are: Yes, we can.

    Dr Amy Khor asked whether the plans to increase the local supply can be speeded up just in case there is a need for us to be totally self-sufficient. Sir, PUB has already taken steps to strengthen our local water supply system. These include expanding the local catchments, launching NEWater for direct non-potable use in the industry and commercial buildings, and indirect potable use in our domestic sector. At the same time, PUB is also introducing desalted water under a water purchase agreement with the private sector by the year 2005.

    Together, these three initiatives will more than make up for the supply of water from Johor under the 1961 agreement. After 2011, we will increase the amount of desalted water in our supply system now that the cost of desalted water has reached an affordable level. We will also produce more NEWater as it is not only cleaner than PUB water, but also cheaper than desalted water.

    As stated by our Prime Minister at the launch of NEWater on 21st February, there is therefore no need to renew the 1961 Water Agreement when it expires in 2011. We can also be totally self-sufficient by 2061 when the 1962 Agreement expires, if there is no new water agreement with Malaysia on mutually beneficial terms.

    Dr Amy Khor asked what are the targeted quantities and proportion of NEWater and desalinated water that will be produced by 2011 and 2061.

    Under the Singapore Green Plan 2012, our projection is that NEWater and desalted water will account for 25% of total demand over the next 10 years. And this works out to about 100 mgd out of a projected total demand of 400 mgd by the year 2012.

    By 2061, the quantities and proportions of NEWater and desalted water in our water supply system will depend on the overall supply and demand situations, as

well as the relative costs of these two sources and that of other potential sources made possible by future advances in water purification technology. As of now, we expect the quantity of NEWater to be higher than desalted water because it is much less energy intensive and cheaper to produce.

Mr Steve Chia is right to point out that indirect potable use of NEWater will cost more than if it is consumed directly. Even though NEWater is cleaner than PUB water and meets all international standards for potable use, we have decided to go along with the recommendation of the international expert panel to consume NEWater indirectly by mixing it with reservoir water. This is to follow the established practices in the United States for more than 25 years. We see no need for Singapore to set a new precedent.

In view of the ultra-pure characteristics of NEWater, more than 80% of NEWater will be used in the industry and commercial buildings directly for non-potable use.     We were also concerned that this exemption would create a loophole, which could seriously erode our tax base. Individuals and companies could avoid tax on their Singapore income by round-tripping and transfer-pricing. This is difficult to detect.

    Notwithstanding the Government's view that this was not a problem, we have continued to receive feedback from the business community, and now from the ERC, that taxation of foreign income indeed poses difficulties to companies.     To help the elderly and disabled meet their transport needs, I have decided to exclude the cost of wheelchair lifts and other costs associated with the installation of such lifts from the OMV of a vehicle when computing the ARF and excise duty payable. This change will take effect immediately, and will complement other efforts by National Council of Social Services and voluntary welfare organisations to provide transport services for this group of needy Singaporeans.

Childcare Benefits

    Many parents find it difficult to juggle both work and childcare, and one parent may have to stop working as a result. Childcare benefits defray the cost of alternative care arrangements for their children and encourage them to stay in the workforce. I have therefore decided to exempt from income tax employer-subsidised childcare benefits paid to licensed childcare centres with effect from YA 2004. This is in line with the Government's efforts to promote pro-family practices in the workplace.

Stamp Duties

Stamp Duty on Leases

    Presently, all property leases are levied with stamp duty. To reduce the tax burden on families living in rental flats, and the inconvenience to businesses and the general public, I have decided to exempt from stamp duty leases with annual rents that do not exceed $1,000. This change will benefit some 3,500 households that start a new lease or renew the lease of a 1- and 2-room HDB rental flats each year. The exemption will also cover the fixed duty levied on 99-year leases that some 32,000 households enter into each year when they purchase flats from HDB. About 30% of all leases will now be exempted. Households and businesses can expect to save about $340,000 a year from 1st April 2003 onwards.

Seller's Stamp Duty

    The seller's stamp duty on the sale of residential properties within three years of purchase was introduced in 1996 to curb property speculation and to stabilise the property market. It was suspended indefinitely in November 1997 when the property market cooled down. Since the

Promoting Philanthropy

    Our tax system recognises taxpayers who support charitable causes.

Donation of Land and Buildings

    From 1st April 2003 onwards, I will be giving income tax deductions for donations of buildings and parcels of land made to Institutions of a Public Character (IPCs). Donors will have the flexibility to decide if they would rather donate the sales proceeds of the asset, or the asset itself, as both options will now allow them an equivalent amount of tax deduction.

Stamp Duty on Donations

    At present, IPCs have to pay stamp duties when they receive donations of immovable properties and shares. To lessen the burden on IPCs receiving such donations and to encourage bequests and donations from estates to IPCs, I have decided to exempt all donations to IPCs from stamp duty. This will take effect immediately.

Donation of Public Sculptures

    As we develop our economy, we should not neglect to preserve our cultural heritage. I am introducing three enhancements to the Public Sculpture Donation Scheme, with effect from 1st July 2003. The enhanced scheme will incentivise donors to adopt existing sculptures, commission and donate sculptures or publicly display sculptures from their private collections. The National Heritage Board will be releasing the details of this scheme by 1st July 2003.

LICENSING FEES OF TAXIS


    17. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for Transport if he will (a) explain the rationale and basis for charging the licensing fee of $25 per month per taxi on taxi operators for monitoring the performance of taxis; (b) explain how the quantum of $25 is arrived at; (c) ensure that this additional cost will not be passed to commuters; and (d) state how the Land Transport Authority plans to monitor the performance of taxis.

    Mr Yeo Cheow Tong:

    The objective of the Taxi Operator Licence regime is to improve the level of taxi service. Under this regime, taxi operators will be made more accountable for the quality of their taxi services, as they will need to meet a set of Quality of Service standards.

    The Taxi Operator Licence regime will require LTA, as the regulator, to closely monitor the performance of taxi operators and their compliance with the Quality of Service standards. LTA will also investigate and enforce against errant taxi drivers under the new Vocational Licence Points System. As these are new activities, additional budget and manpower resources will be required. The licence fee will enable LTA to fund these activities relating to regulating and improving the taxi industry.

    LTA expects to analyse and audit fairly often the performance records of the taxi companies for compliance with the Quality of Service standards in the initial 1-2 years. It has estimated that it needs an annual budget of $5.3 million, which includes the salary cost for 28 staff. The main cost items are:

    To develop and operate a new centralised computer system to administer the Taxi Operator Licence regime and the records of some 85,000 taxi drivers.

    To enforce the Quality of Service standards by analysing the radiophone booking systems of the taxi operators and commissioning external audits of the operators' radiophone booking systems and their operations.

    To conduct investigations and enforcement against errant taxi drivers.

    To monitor taxi services by conducting taxi surveys and auditing indicators submitted by the taxi operators.

    As there are about 19,000 taxis in Singapore, this works out to a licence fee of $25 per taxi per month. This is less than 1% of the gross turnover of the operators. (This is minuscule and comparable to licence fees paid by operators in other regulated industries, e.g. telecommunications sector). It amounts to less than 3 cents per taxi trip, against an average fare of around $7. As you can see, the licence fee is not excessive.

    As LTA will need to set up the audit and enforcement framework, the work will be more intensive in the first couple of years. As the $25 licence fee is based on the budget for this more intensive phase of the regulatory work, LTA will review the quantum of the licence fee after two years, taking into account the actual costs incurred.

    I share Dr Amy Khor's concern that taxi companies should not pass on this cost to commuters. We have liberalised the taxi industry to allow new entrants into the market. Obviously, taxi companies will have to take into consideration the new competitive landscape before considering passing on this cost through any rental adjustment or fare increase.

PRESCHOOL GUIDELINES


    18. Mr Gan Kim Yong asked the Minister for Education how many of the preschools here have complied with the guidelines announced recently by his Ministry and what measures are being taken to monitor the progress of the preschools in complying with these guidelines.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    The Ministry of Education has announced various guidelines and measures to improve the quality of pre-school education in Singapore. The first of these measures concern teacher training in the pre-school sector. In December 2000, MOE announced that all kindergarten principals would be required to obtain the Diploma in Pre-school Education (Leadership) by 2006. In October 2001, MOE further announced that one in four teachers will be required to obtain the Diploma in Pre-school Education (Teaching), and all other teachers to have Certificates in Pre-school Teaching by 2008.

    There has been good progress made in the number of teachers going for training among all the major kindergarten providers. MOE has an open channel of communication with all kindergarten providers and is keeping track of developments.

    The second initiative undertaken by MOE to uplift the quality of pre-school education has been the development of a framework for kindergarten curriculum, based on a set of desired outcomes for pre-school education. The curriculum framework was launched in January this year. The framework is available to all kindergartens, which are encouraged to use it as a guide to design their own programmes that meet the needs of children physically, emotionally, socially and cognitively.

    MOE does not intend to make the pre-school curriculum framework a formal requirement for all kindergartens. This is consistent with the Government's approach of having the provision of pre-school education remain in the hands of the private and community sectors. Such an approach encourages innovation as each kindergarten operator strives to meet the needs of its pupils.

    Finally, MOE is currently reviewing the legislation necessary for regulation of pre-school education. A Kindergarten Education Bill will be introduced in Parliament within a few months.

SINGAPORE PASSPORTS


    1. Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Home Affairs if more pages in the Singapore Passport can be added on, like in Canada and the USA, when it runs out of empty pages before its 10-year validity period expires.

    Mr Wong Kan Seng:

    The Canadian passport contains 24 or 48 pages with allowance for an additional 10 pages to be added on. The US passport holds 24 pages with allowance for another 24 pages to be added on.

    The Singapore passport contains 96 pages. This is more than the number of pages in the Canadian and US passports (even with the additional pages added) as well as the passports of many other countries. Our experience shows that only approximately 10% of the number of applications for passport renewal is due to the pages being used up before the passport's 10-year validity period expires. Hence, for the majority of Singaporeans, the 96 pages are more than sufficient to meet their travel needs.

CENTRAL PROVIDENT FUND


    3. Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Manpower (a) if he will provide a breakdown of how the total funds kept with the Central Provident Fund (CPF) Board are invested and what are their investment returns; (b) what is the amount of interest the CPF

Column: 115

Board is getting from the Housing and Development Board and what is the amount of interest the CPF Board is paying on the members' accounts; and (c) what is the proportion of Singaporeans who have a CPF balance greater than their property loan and vice versa.

    Dr Lee Boon Yang:

    At the end of financial year 2002, the total CPF savings kept with the CPF Board was $96.4 billion. Of these, $94.4 billion (or 97.9%) were invested in Government Bonds and $2 billion (or 2.1%) in Advance Deposits*. * These are unaudited figures.

    The returns on the funds invested in Government Bonds and Advance Deposits are pegged to the same interest rates that the Board pays its CPF members.

    The interest rate that the CPF Board pays on balances in the Ordinary Account (OA) is pegged to the 12-month fixed deposit rates (80% weightage) and the savings rates of banks (20% weightage). The minimum guaranteed rate for OA is 2.5% per annum. For balances in the Special and Medisave Accounts, the CPF Board pays an interest rate that is 1.5% above the prevailing CPF interest rate, i.e. at 4.00% per annum currently.

    The CPF Board does not receive any interest from the Housing and Development Board (HDB), as the CPF Board does not place any funds with the HDB. As highlighted earlier, all CPF savings were invested in only Government Bonds and Advance Deposits. The Government, however, does provide the HDB with mortgage financing loans at an interest rate that is pegged to the CPF interest rate to enable the HDB to finance its concessionary mortgage loans.

    The CPF Board does not track the proportion of Singaporeans with CPF OA balances greater or lower than their property loans. It is unclear why Mr Chia wants to compare a member's CPF balance with his property loan. If the concern is that the member has used too much CPF for housing, there are checks against that, such as the withdrawal limit, which at present is equal to 150% of the lower of the valuation or the purchase price of the property and would be gradually reduced to 120% over the next 5 years. If the concern is about the member's ability to finance his housing loan, his current CPF balance is a poor measure of that, because he could have withdrawn his CPF savings under other schemes, such as for investment under the CPFIS. Also, he could use his monthly OA contributions to finance his loan; that is not reflected in his current CPF balance. Hence, it is not meaningful to compare CPF balances with housing loans.
