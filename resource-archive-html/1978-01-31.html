    In answer to Question 1(c), I would like to explain that the hours of work of doctors vary according to the posting. Normally doctors who are working in Outpatient Departments, for example, work office hours and they have a normal week of 42 hours. Those in the hospitals, besides their normal week of 42 hours, are required to perform night duties. If they have a night duty once a week, it will add 15 1/2 hours to the normal 42 hours quota, and if they do have two night-duty performances in a week, it will work out to about 72 hours in a normal week. In the Accidents and Emergency Department the doctors work on shifts and, therefore, they have a 42-hour week but they are given time off for night duties.

    The problem is a very vexing one because night duties are not necessarily applicable to all doctors working in hospitals. POWER FAILURE ON 17TH JANUARY, 1978


    5. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for National Development and Communications what was the cause of the power failure on 17th January, 1978, and how long it took the authorities to restore the power supply.

     The Minister for National Development and Communications (Mr Lim Kim San): Mr Speaker, Sir, at 9.34 a.m. on 17th January, 1978, a 120 megawatt generator at the Jurong Power Station developed vibration at one of its bearings, and its protective relays tripped it off the system. The resulting system instability caused another 120 megawatt generator, also at the Jurong Power Station, to trip out. The disconnection of these two generating units from the system activated the operation of system automatic under-frequency relays, cutting off power to Toa Payoh, Aljunied, Kallang Basin and Siglap areas. By 10.30 a.m. power had been restored to 50% of the affected areas, and by 11.34 a.m. the situation had returned to normal.

    For the information of Members, under frequency relays are protective devices installed in the system to automatically disconnect loads in pre-selected steps when there is a certain loss of generating capacity

ROADS
(Causes of deterioration)


    7. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for National Development and Communications (a) why so many roads have deteriorated during the recent heavy rains and what are the causes of the deterioration; (b) whether he is satisfied that road surfacing is carried out with proper technique and under adequate supervision; (c) how much money has been spent on the recent road repairs: and (d) whether and what steps he intends to take to minimise damage to the roads during heavy rains in future.

     Dr Tan Eng Liang (for the Minister for National Development and Communications): Mr Speaker, Sir, damage to road pavements occur mainly in bus lanes, bus stops, intersections and tight bends. Observations indicate that oil spillage over a period softens and dissolves the asphalt in road pavements resulting in loss of its

Column: 210

binding quality. With heavy rainfall, water easily penetrates the asphalt film surrounding the stone aggregate, stripping them from the road pavement thus aggravating the damage. In tight bends, the pavement surfaces are highly stressed by turning vehicles and tend to deteriorate faster than straight road sections. In all instances, heavy vehicles and high traffic volumes tend to hasten the deterioration of road pavements.

    The Public Works Department exercises rigid quality control over the materials used for road surfacing and also undertakes strict supervision of the laying of the premixed asphalt concrete. Samples of materials used at every site are taken and sent to the PWD's engineering laboratory for testing. Sub-standard materials are rejected. Financial penalties are imposed for minor departures from the qualities specified. During road resurfacing works, PWD officers maintain standing supervision to ensure that compaction is adequate and the hot mix asphalt concrete has cooled down sufficiently before the road is opened to traffic. Premature opening to traffic will result in some loosening of the surface materials and subsequent deterioration of the road surface.

    For the period April to October 1977, the average monthly expenditure on road patching was approximately $85,000. For November 1977, this rose to $113,000 and could be attributed to the heavy rainfall at that time.

    In 1976, the PWD reviewed the existing asphalt concrete mix design used and experimented with various mix designs with the aim of improving on the asphalt concrete mix that will be used for future road surfacing. Since the beginning of the current fiscal year, an improved mix has been in use. A stiffer grade of bitumen has also been specified. The PWD is in correspondence with various research and highway authorities in other countries, obtaining useful information on how the problem can be solved. The Department has, in addition, initiated systematic scientific studies to determine precisely the factors affecting road surface deterioration.
