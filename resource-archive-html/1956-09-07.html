SUPPLEMENTARY PROVISION
(Sessional Paper No. Cmd. 43 of 1956)


    The Financial Secretary (Mr T. M. Hart): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have His Excellency's authority to move,

    That this Assembly resolves that the sum of $4,308,250 shall be supplied to the Government under the heads of expenditure and for the services specified in the Schedule contained in Sessional Paper No. Cmd. 43 of 1956.

    The Schedule contains the applications for supplementary vote which have accumulated since the last sitting of the Assembly. The applications amount to a total of about $4.3 millions, making a total of supplementary votes for this year of a little over $11 millions compared to the total of $27 millions on approximately the same date last year. I mention these figures because strenuous efforts have, in fact, been made to reduce Government expenditure in general and to limit the number of applications for supplementary vote in particular, and because criticisms have been levelled at the rate of Government expenditure both in and outside the Assembly. The fact of the matter is that applications for supplementary vote are inevitable and inescapable. It is impossible to foresee future needs when the estimates are framed. Needs arise which it would be shortsighted not to meet as soon as possible. The same situation arises in every type of organisation. It is not peculiar to Government alone. All we can do is to endeavour to limit supplementary applications to essential services. I do think we are making some progress in that direction.

    My first task is to report that, contrary to my undertaking at an earlier sitting of the Assembly, I have authorised two applications for funds for posts in anticipation of the approval of the Assembly. The first item is No.202. In this case, which is fully explained in the Schedule, it was represented to me by the Chief Justice through my hon. Friend the Attorney-General (Mr C. H. Butterfield) that the appointment of two additional Reporters was a matter of great urgency. In consequence, I approved the appointments with effect from the 1st of June.

Column: 184



    The second item is No. 238. In this case, it was represented to me by the Registrar of Malayan Statistics that the work of preparation for the field census tests proposed for the autumn of this year would be seriously held up if a stenographer were not appointed immediately to keep the minutes and act as Assistant Secretary to the Census Advisory Committee. After a very careful examination of the case submitted by the Registrar, I agreed to his request and authorised the creation of the post with effect from 10th July, 1956.

    I must also report that I have authorised a start to be made with the construction of hutted camp accommodation for the Singapore Battalion. It is desirable to raise the Battalion as soon as possible. The rate of recruitment depends to a certain extent on the availability of accommodation and it was therefore important to authorise the immediate commencement of work so that recruitment should not be held up.

    I am confident that it was in the interest of efficient administration that these authorities should be given. May I ask hon. Members to give covering approval?

    The various applications are explained in some detail in the Schedule but perhaps attention should be drawn to one or two items.

    A certain amount of publicity has been given to item 206, Cost of Telegrams, which, incidentally, has no direct connection with the Merdeka Mission. As means of communication develop, so one tends to use them. The habit has grown steadily over the years to communicate by telegram and radiogram. It saves much time and, I think, leads to greater efficiency and the speedier despatch of Government business. The practice is common not only in Government but also in business and other circles. Following this growing habit, Government expenditure on telegrams has tended to increase. Expenditure over the past five years has been as follows:1952, $12,000; 1953, $19,000; 1954, $23,000; 1955, $38,000; from 1st January to 9th August, 1956, $32,000. The considerable increase in 1956 over 1955

Column: 185

figures has been due, in the main, to increased communication on constitutional matters in the early part of this year. The original vote has now been expended and a supplement of $30,000 is asked for. This estimate was made in early June on the basis of average monthly expenditure in the first part of the year. There has been a slight falling off in expenditure since that date but the application has been kept to the same level in order to cover all possible contingencies in the future. There has been no extravagance in expenditure. Telegrams are despatched to all quarters of the globe and cover the multifarious business of government.

    Items 209 to 216 inclusive provide staff for the reorganisation of the Office of the Director of Personnel. This office has been seriously understaffed with the result that staff matters, staff negotiations and other similar matters have been considerably delayed. It is hoped that the reorganisation of the Branch now proposed will enable these matters to be speeded up.

    Item 218 seeks funds for ten Supernumerary posts to enable officers of the subordinate services to be tried out for promotion to the Administrative Service.

    Item 233 provides, inter alia, for a small car for a most courageous police officer who is completely paralysed from the waist down as a result of a wound incurred in a fight with a gunman.

    Items 220 to 232 inclusive seek provision for the initial steps required for the raising of the Singapore Battalion which is regarded as a matter of top priority.

    My colleagues and I shall be happy to amplify any of the explanations given in the Schedule. Sir, I beg to move.

    Schedule A lists the projects on which under-expenditure will occur. The total estimated under-expenditure is over $5 millions. The reasons for the under-expenditure are explained in the Schedule. I use the phrase "under-expenditure" advisedly. The word "savings" in this context apparently leads to misunderstanding. In the great majority of cases, the under-expenditure is merely deferred expenditure. There are, of course, a number of cases in which the under-expenditure is true saving. It will also be noted, not only in Schedule A but also in Schedule B, that the original estimate of cost has been revised in a number of cases. It is not easy for the Public Works Department to make an accurate estimate of the cost of a new project in advance of detailed planning and investigation. Many estimates have been made on the basis of a rough guide as to requirements from a department without plans and without knowledge of the site. It is only when plans and working drawings are made and when

quantities are taken and sites obtained and examined, that accurate estimates of cost can be made. Even these sometimes have to be revised. Changes of mind and ideas are not unknown. The surprising thing is not that the original estimates have to be revised but that they have to be revised so seldom and by so little. There are, however, one or two cases where the revised estimate has differed substantially from the original. One case is that of the Polytechnic. The original estimate of cost shown in the Estimates was $5.19 millions. This was based on the estimate made in the Gibson Report. It was taken as a rough guide until a more accurate estimate, based on plans, could be made. Of the $5.19 millions, $1.4 million was for equipment for the workshops, etc. No allowance was made for quarters and playing-fields. Plans were, drawn up by the architects commissioned for the job and it became clear that the sum of $3.8 millions would only go a part of the way towards meeting the cost of essential buildings. Later, these plans had to be revised in the light of advice obtained from the Advisory Council in London about the latest trends in technical college buildings. Only recently has it been possible to arrive at a reliable revised estimate of cost. This totals $11.4 millions and includes $7.5 millions for buildings and site preparation; $2½ millions for equipment and fittings; $1 million for staff quarters; and $500,000 for playing fields. The whole project has been reexamined in the light of this somewhat startling increase in cost. Plans prepared by the architects were examined with great care by the Director of Public Works and his architectural staff. The increase in cost is due in part to a substantial rise in the cost of structural steel and other building materials, and in part to changes in the plan. In the opinion of the Director of Public Works, the maximum economies have been practised in planning the buildings and there is little opportunity for further reduction. It has been decided, however, to refer the plans once more to the Advisory Committee on Building Costs to find out whether economies cannot be achieved on finishes.

    The Minister for Commerce and Industry (Mr J. M. Jumabhoy): Mr Speaker, I rise to move the motion standing in my name,

    That this Assembly, in accordance with subsection (2) of section 490 of the Merchant Shipping Ordinance (Chapter 207), resolves that the Merchant Shipping (Passenger Certificate Partial Survey Fees) (Amendment) Rules, 1956, published as Notification No. S 224 in the Gazette Supplement No. 55 dated 17th August, 1956, as contained in Sessional Paper No. S.L. 24 of 1956, be approved.

    Hon. Members will recall that, at the February sitting of this Assembly, I moved a similar motion to bring into force fees laid down for survey of parts of the machinery and equipment of ships.

    This motion, Sir, now seeks to enforce the Notification in the Gazette made on the 17th of August for fees laid down for partial surveys of the internal and external hulls of ships. It is anticipated that, in the near future, owners of passenger vessels registered in the United Kingdom may call upon us to survey

Column: 217

parts of the internal hull of a ship and we also expect them to call upon us to survey parts of the external hull of ships. This is the first time that such partial surveys will be recognised by the Ministry of Transport and Civil Aviation in the United Kingdom as going towards a full survey for a Passenger Safety Certificate to be issued by the United Kingdom Government. The opportunity has also been taken to see that these fees are fixed as a fair proportion of the full prescribed fees for the complete survey of hulls. Sir, I beg to move.

    Mr Goode: Sir, I second the motion. Question put, and agreed to.

    Resolved,

    That this Assembly, in accordance with subsection (2) of section 490 of the Merchant Shipping Ordinance (Chapter 207), resolves that the Merchant Shipping (Passenger Certificate Partial Survey Fees) (Amendment) Rules, 1956, published as Notification No. S 224 in the Gazette Supplement No. 55 dated 17th August, 1956, as contained in Sessional Paper No. S.L. 24 of 1956, be approved.

REGISTRATION OF DEEDS(AMENDMENT) RULES, 1956


    The Assistant Minister for Local Government, Lands and Housing (Mr Tan Theng Chiang): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move.

    That this Assembly, in accordance with subsection (2) of section 29 of the Registration of Deeds Ordinance (Chapter 255), resolves that the Registration of Deeds (Amendment) Rules, 1956, made by the Minister for Local Government, Lands and Housing on the 15th day of August, 1956, and published as Notification No. S 226 in the Gazette Supplement No. 55 of 17th August, 1956, as contained in Sessional Paper No. S.L. 25 of 1956, be approved.

    Sir, this is a straightforward matter. The Registration of Deeds (Amendment) Ordinance, 1956, which came into operation on the 20th of June, 1956, gave legal sanction to the inspection by the public of microfilm copies of deeds instead of original copies. The amendment of the Ordinance made necessary a few consequential amendments to the Registration of Deeds Rules, 1934, and the approval of this Assembly for these amendments is required under section
