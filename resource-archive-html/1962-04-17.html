TO ZEHNDER ROAD IN PASIR PANJANG


    2. Mr Tee Kim Leng asked the Prime Minister when will electricity supply reach the people of Zehnder Road in Pasir Panjang.

    The Prime Minister: Sir, a scheme for the supply of electricity to the houses at Zehnder Road, Pasir Panjang, was considered by the Urban and Rural Services Consultative Committee in 1961.     This Bill seeks to amend the law relating to the limitation of actions in respect of causes of action which

survive against the estate of a deceased person. Section 7 (3) of the Civil Law Ordinance at present provides as follows:

    `(3) No proceedings shall be maintainable in respect of a cause of action in tort which by virtue of this section has survived against the estate of a deceased person unless either -

    (a) a proceedings against him in respect of that cause of action were pending at the date of his death; or

    (b) the cause of action arose not earlier than six months before his death and proceedings are taken in respect thereof not later than six months after his personal representative took out representation.'.

Where proceedings are pending against the deceased at the date of his death, such proceedings will survive against his estate. In this case the ordinary period of limitation will apply and this, by virtue of section 6 of the Limitations Ordinance, 1959, is six years in the case of an action founded on a tort. Where proceedings are not pending against the deceased at the time of his death, the present law in Singapore is that the proceedings will survive against the estate of the deceased only if -

    (1) the cause of action arose not earlier than six months before the death of the deceased, and

    (2) proceedings in respect thereof are taken not later than six months after his personal representative took out representation.

The first provision, in effect, sets out the special period of limitation and therefore by virtue of section 3 of the Limitations Ordinance, 1959, that Ordinance will not be applicable. The effect of the provision is that if the tortfeasor dies more than six months after committing the tort and proceedings are not pending against him at the date of his death, the person injured will not be able to bring an action against the estate of the deceased tortfeasor because the cause of action arose earlier than six months before .his death. If, however, the tortfeasor dies four months or five months after committing the tort, the person injured can

Column: 732

bring the action against the estate of the deceased tortfeasor at any time, because in this case the cause of action did not arise earlier than six months before the death of the tortfeasor. This provision has been removed in England by the Law Reform (Limitation of Actions etc.) Act of 1954, and in the Federation of Malaya the Civil Law Ordinance, 1956, does not include this provision. The Singapore Bar Committee has considered the matter and they have recommended that the law in Singapore be amended to bring it into line with that in England and the Federation of Malaya. It is not proposed to amend the other requirement-that the proceedings in respect of the cause of action must be taken not later than six months after the personal representative of the deceased has taken out representation.

The reason for the agitation against the use of tariff protection is mainly that of the possible unfavourable effects they have on our entrepot trade. However, our brief experience in protective tariff has shown that the effects could be reduced to a minimum or even be eliminated if tariff duties are imposed with due care on selected com modities only. both committees encountered difficulties in obtaining information, and it was clear that the machinery as provided in the Customs Ordinance has serious weaknesses. One over-riding difficulty was that of obtaining accurate and reliable information. The committee did not have the power to compel interested parties to divulge such information. The delay in submitting their findings has been due, in a large measure, to this, and in particular to the difficulty encountered in ascertaining specific information on prices of imported goods.

    It is essential, if the development of manufacturing industries is not to be delayed, for a proper Tariff Advisory Commission to be set up with the necessary authority to obtain whatever information it needs to assess whether any particular industry requires tariff protection. Without this authority, the Commission will not be able to function quickly and effectively and the Government will not have the necessary data on which to reach a proper evaluation. The purpose of the Tariff Advisory Commission Bill now before the House is to overcome the weaknesses that now exist in operating section 11 of the Customs Ordinance, and to vest the required statutory powers to obtain the necessary information in the Tariff Advisory Commission to be established under this Bill. When such information is available, the Government would be able to make decisions on the basis of properly ascertained facts.
