RESERVISTS' PASSPORTS
(Extension of validity period)


    1. Mr Lew Syn Pau asked the Prime Minister and Minister for Defence whether he will consider extending the validity period of the passports of reservists from two years to ten years, the same as for non-reservists.

     Mr Lew Syn Pau (Tanglin): Question No. 1. Sir, I would like to declare my interest as a reservist.

     The Senior Minister of State for Defence (Dr Lee Boon Yang) (for the Prime Minister and Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, for operational reasons, it is essential for MINDEF to know where our reservists are so that we can recall them at short notice. This is a major consideration when deciding on the validity period of reservists' passports. We must be certain that reservists can be reached when they are needed. Hence, reservists must inform their units when they go overseas. At present, they can leave Singapore for up to six months at a time without applying for an Exit Permit. The computerised immigration system set up last year by the Immigration Department will help in keeping track of reservists who do not come back within six months of being away.

Column: 9



    MINDEF recognises that our operational requirement will inconvenience the small number of reservists who travel regularly. Therefore, in June last year, MINDEF extended the validity of passports of reservists from one to two years. We have also set up a counter at SAFRA where reservists can extend their passports in addition to applying for Exit Permits, ie, if they are going away for more than six months. The counter is open beyond office hours, in the evening on weekdays and the afternoons on Saturdays, to make it convenient for reservists.

     Mr Lew Syn Pau: Sir, is the Senior Minister of State aware that reservists are already required to inform their units when they travel overseas? EU COURT
(Conservation)


    3. Dr Tan Cheng Bock asked the Minister for National Development whether Eu Court along Stamford Road is to be conserved and, if not, why was one million dollars spent to repair and paint a building when it is to be demolished after one year's use.

     Mr Dhanabalan: Mr Speaker, Sir, Eu Court will be demolished to make way for the new two-way Part VIII and Part VII lane carriageway road system starting from Neil Road to Kallang River. This system involves the widening of New Bridge Road, Hill Street, Victoria Street and Kallang Road. The scheme has already been completed from Neil Road to Coleman Street and from Lavender Street to Kallang River. Other sections are presently under construction. In the construction programme, the widening of the Hill Street section is expected to be finished some time in 1992.

    The decision to renovate Eu Court was taken in March 1988 when it was known that the widening of Hill Street section would be delayed by 4-5 years because of certain land acquisition problems. Minor renovations costing about $920,000 were carried out to prevent the building from deteriorating further and becoming a fire hazard. The intention was to rent out the building in the interim period before the building was required to be demolished for road widening. It was estimated, at the time that the decision was taken to renovate the building, that the building could be rented for about 2-3 years which was more than enough to recover the cost of renovation.

    Under the plan to renovate Eu Court, the building was expected to be renovated and leased out by June 1989, thus giving two and a half years of rental income before demolition of the building at the end of 1991. Renovation started according to schedule but was held up because there were still some old tenants in Eu Court who could not be resettled according to the programme. Renovation was only completed in July 1990 and the building was rented out in December 1990.

Column: 12



    Although the present plans on the road widening will require the building to be demolished in early 1992, there is a great likelihood that the building could be rented out until mid-1992 before it is cleared for demolition. The present net rental of the building is $500,000 a year. At this rental, we should be able to recover about 80% of the cost of renovation before the building is demolished. It is regrettable that not all the costs of renovation can be recovered. On hindsight, URA's schedule for renovation and lease did not provide a sufficient margin for slippage in the execution of the programme. If there is any similar interim-use project in the future, more care will be taken to ensure that all contingencies are taken into account.

    In summary, when the decision to renovate was taken in 1988, the study on economic viability clearly showed that the cost of renovation would be recovered well before the building was required to be demolished. But problems in implementation shortened the period that the building could be rented out.
