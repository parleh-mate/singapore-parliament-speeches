ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

HIGHER SCHOOL CERTIFICATE TEACHERS
(Salary increment)


    1. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Education whether teachers with the Higher School Certificate, whether in Government or Government-aided schools and in any of the four language streams, are given one increment more than those with the School Certificate: whether some teachers with the Higher School Certificate have not been given the additional increment, and if he will put the matter right.

    Mr Ong Pang Boon: Mr Speaker, Sir, there is a scheme whereby non-graduate teachers with the Cambridge Overseas Higher School Certificate are given one additional increment if they teach in secondary schools and for so long as they teach in secondary schools. This applies to both Government and Government-aided school teachers. This scheme is confined to the English medium, because when the scheme was promulgated in July 1962, there was a dearth of graduates for the English medium secondary schools, and it was essential to encourage Primary-trained teachers with the School Certificate in this medium to take the higher qualification of the H.S.C. to enable them to teach in secondary schools.

Column: 780



    There are no teachers in the English medium who satisfy the requirement and who have not been paid the additional increment. There are, however, teachers in the English medium with the H.S.C. teaching in Primary schools who have not been paid this increment, because the requirement is that they must teach in secondary schools.

    As this scheme was originated to cope with the shortage of graduate teachers in the English medium, there are, therefore, teachers in the other media who do not qualify for the additional increment.

    Inche Othman Bin Wok: Mr Speaker, Sir, public assistance payments are made by the Social Welfare Department to unemployed persons, including aged persons and widows, at approved rates which have already been increased on two occasions under this Government, i.e., in 1959 and 1962. These rates of unemployment benefits, under which a large family can receive as much as $90 per month, are generally acknowledged to be the highest paid in this part of the world, and it is not considered desirable to increase the rates further. Under this scheme, an aged couple who are on their own and have no one to provide for them, will receive $31 per month and a widow with, say, four dependent children will receive $41 per month.

    I should also mention that the Social Welfare Department runs Old People's Homes to which destitute aged persons with no relatives or friends to look after them can be admitted.
