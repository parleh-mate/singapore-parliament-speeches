ASIA PACIFIC ECONOMIC COOPERATION (APEC) MEETING IN AUCKLAND

(Update)


    1. Mr Leong Horn Kee asked the Prime Minister (a) whether he will give an update on the recent Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) meeting in Auckland, New Zealand, and in particular elaborate on the idea of signing Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) with other countries, and (b) how such FTAs would impact the trade agreements within ASEAN.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo) (for the Prime Minister): Mr Speaker, Sir, from Singapore's perspective, the outcome of the Auckland APEC meeting was positive. There was strong support for the launch of a New Round of multilateral trade negotiations, which would help lower market access barriers for Singapore's goods and services. There was also agreement on the scope and modalities of such negotiations. These are strong messages which would give significant impetus to efforts to launch a New Round at the Third WTO Ministerial Conference later this year at Seattle, as APEC economies account for more than half the global trade.

Column: 279



    New Zealand has ably and skilfully chaired APEC this year towards these strong outcomes. It has managed well the bilateral and sub-regional side issues that had threatened to overshadow the APEC meetings. The general consensus is that New Zealand has put APEC back on track, and restored a good measure of the grouping's relevance and credibility.

    At the fringes of the Auckland APEC meetings, Singapore announced that we would be entering into Free Trade Agreement (FTA) negotiations with New Zealand. We also reached an agreement with Mexico to explore the possibility of a bilateral FTA, and with Chile and New Zealand for a study on a 3-party FTA.

    Let me put these FTA initiatives in perspective. While we pursue FTAs with strategic partners, our primary emphasis is still the multilateral trading system represented by the WTO. The WTO is the only institution that can ensure a fair and predictable environment for global trade. This is particularly important for a small country heavily dependent on trade like Singapore. We would be much poorer off in a world where trade is not governed by international rules and dominated instead by individual economies or regional blocs.

    The objective of our FTA with New Zealand and others is to use them as catalysts to accelerate the pace of trade liberalisation within APEC. In 1994, APEC Leaders at Bogor made a commitment to achieve free and open trade and investment by 2010 for developed economies and by 2020 for developing economies. We think that one way to fast-track the achievement of the Bogor vision is through sub-APEC FTAs. APEC economies which are ready to embrace the Bogor goals of free trade earlier should be encouraged to form FTAs among themselves. Our FTA with New Zealand will be grounded on Bogor goals and will be open to participation by other economies. In this way, we see the Singapore-New Zealand FTA as being a stepping stone towards an eventual APEC-wide FTA, thus realising the Bogor vision.

    At Auckland, we were pleasantly surprised to note that several other APEC leaders shared a similar view. They are discussing FTAs among themselves, for example, Korea and Chile, Japan and Mexico.

    Singapore remains committed to ASEAN and the establishment of a strong ASEAN Free Trade Area (AFTA) that is attractive to investors. This is in our own interest because our long-term fortunes are inextricably tied to ASEAN. Our FTA initiatives will not detract from our commitments and obligations in ASEAN. We will continue to work hard for the implementation of the AFTA Agreement, the ASEAN Agreement on Services, the ASEAN Investment Area and other ASEAN economic initiatives.

    Our decision to enter into FTAs with other trading partners can help to strengthen the region. We see the Singapore-New Zealand FTA as a preliminary step to build up linkages between ASEAN and other regions, for example, the Closer Economic Relations (CER) grouping of Australia and New Zealand. At a recent ASEAN meeting with Australia and New Zealand, the Ministers agreed to set up a high level taskforce to study the feasibility of establishing an FTA between ASEAN and the CER by the year 2010.     The Second Minister for Finance (Mr Lim Hng Kiang): Mr Speaker, Sir, there is no requirement for failed transactions to be reported to IRAS. IRAS therefore does not have information on the number of failed transactions.

    The Stamp Duties Act was recently amended to allow refund in the following circumstances:

    (i) the vendor is unable to prove his title to the property;

    (ii) a purchaser, being a foreign person, is unable to obtain approval under the Residential Property Act (Chapter 274) to acquire or purchase the property;
