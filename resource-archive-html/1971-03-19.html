LEGISLATION AGAINST
JAYWALKING


    1. Mr J. F. Conceicao asked the Minister for Communications the extent to which consideration has been given to legislation against jaywalking.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Yong Nyuk Lin): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Ministry is of the opinion that our present effort in building more and better facilities for pedestrians crossing the roads is a more positive approach than the legislation against jaywalking pedestrians. Overhead bridges, zebra crossings and underpasses, for example, go a long way to help pedestrians cross the road safely and, in most cases, it would appear that they do make use of the facilities provided. However, the Ministry is keeping a close watch on the situation to ensure that overhead bridges, zebra crossings, etc., are not built in vain. If subsequent reports indicate that we are not successful in persuading the pedestrians to make the best use of the facilities provided for crossing the roads. Consideration will be given to introduce legislation to compel the use of such facilities by pedestrians.

PRICE INCREASES OF DAILY
NECESSITIES


    4. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for Finance if he will take steps to curb increases in the prices of daily necessities.

    The Minister of State for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, the question of the increase in prices was thoroughly aired by Members and adequately answered by the Minister for Finance during the budget debate two days ago, on the 17th of this month.

    The Government is keenly interested in stable prices and will do everything within its power to see that there is no unwarranted increase in the price of any goods, especially of daily necessities. But there are limitations to what we can do. This is because we are an open economy and we import most of our goods which are, therefore, subject to fluctuations in world prices.

    I take it that daily necessities mean food, housing and clothing. Let us examine these three broad groups to find out whether there is any rise in their prices and the reasons for such rise.

Column: 872



    On food, we are self-sufficient only in pork, poultry and eggs; the rest of our foodstuffs like rice, sugar, fish, fresh vegetables, are imported. The price of sugar was recently increased by three cents per kati, but this was because the world price of raw sugar went up sharply --- something over which we have no control. There has, however, been a fall in the price of rice recently by some five cents a kati. The statistics gathered by the Statistics Department indicate that 100 per cent Siam rice was, on the average, 41 cents a kati in January 1970 and was only 36 cents a kati in January this year. Because rice forms a large part of Our budget on foodstuffs, the fall in the price of rice should more than outweigh the rise in the price of sugar and some other items like bread.

    As Members know, foodstuffs are also subject to seasonal fluctuations, especially items like fish and fresh vegetables. The statistics seem to indicate that there is always a rise in the price of foodstuffs at the beginning of the year but that the price will go back to normal towards the middle of the year. This may be because of festive seasons, like the Chinese New Year and Han Raya, which normally occur at the beginning of the year.

    On housing, the statistics collected by the Statistics Department show an increase of 1.4 points in 1970 over 1969. This increase is attributed to increases in the Public Utilities Board charges and the tax on P.U.B, bills. There is, of course, a very sharp rise in rentals for middle-class property. But this should not affect the working class who are well looked after by the Housing and Development Board.

    In the clothing group, the increase is really very slight and has occurred in the sectors of clothing material and made-up garments. A large portion of these two items is still imported and as long as the sophisticated consumers here still have a preference for imported materials and made-up garments over the locally-manufactured ones, then they must be ready to pay more for them in view of the customs duty imposed on them.

Column: 873



    It is hoped that if we continue to run an efficient port and the ancillary services and maintain a highly competitive market with the absence of monopolies, we will continue to buy the best goods at the cheapest possible price from any part of the world and thus keep to the very minimum any possible increase in the cost of living.

    So far, we have been able, in spite of our open economy, and in spite of the rising trend in world prices, to keep our cost of living relatively stable. Our Consumer Price Index showed that there was only a 0.4 point increase in 1970 over 1969.

    For comparative purposes, I have handed out a table* containing a cost-of-living index compiled by the United Nations on eight countries. It may be interesting for Members to note that the cost of living in Singapore has risen only 8.5 points in seven years, from 100 points in 1963, as the base year, to 108.5 in July 1970. And of the eight countries shown, Singapore has the lowest rate of increase. The next lowest increase, for Australia, is 23.5 points.
