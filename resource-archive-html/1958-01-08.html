CITY COUNCIL ELECTIONS
(Notices in Vernacular Press)


    1. Mr Lee Choon Eng asked the Chief Secretary whether he will state the reasons why the Notices of Contested Elections in respect of the recent City Council elections, as published in the Government Gazette Extraordinary No. 123 of November 26, 1957, were not published in the vernacular newspapers;

Column: 3406

and whether he will take steps by the introduction of amending legislation, or otherwise, to ensure that Notices of Contested Elections in respect of elections to the Legislative Assembly, City or District Council will in the future be published in the vernacular newspapers.

    The Acting Chief Secretary (Mr S. T. Stewart): Mr Speaker, Sir, section 34 (4) of the Local Government Elections Ordinance requires the Returning Officer to publish these notices in the Gazette and also in such newspapers as he thinks fit. There is therefore no requirement that they should be published in any particular newspaper.

    However, owing to the high cost of advertisement charges, consideration is now being given to the question of amending legislation restricting publication in the Gazette only in future.

    This amending legislation may not be enacted before the District Council elections this year. As these elections will take place in areas which are mainly rural, consideration will be given to publishing these notices in the vernacular newspapers only in addition to the Gazette.

    Sir, the objects of the Bill are clearly set out in the explanatory note. Clause 2 seeks to make an amendment to section 11 of the Singapore Citizenship Ordinance. Under this section of the Ordinance, persons who are recruited in Malaya into the British armed forces have been deprived of the right of becoming citizens of Singapore. I have received representations from this group of people and it has been considered that it is but fair that such persons and their families who have been recruited into the British armed forces in Malaya should be entitled to become citizens of Singapore.

    Clause 3 seeks to make a minor amendment. It makes a correction of a mistake that crept into the Bill when it was considered last time.

    Clause 4 seeks to add a proviso to section 26 of the Ordinance. Under section 26, people who are entitled to become citizens of Singapore are being registered for an initial period of three months ending 31st January, 1958. So far, more than 230,000 citizens have been registered but there have been cases in which the Registrars are not in a position to grant certificates immediately on application and have had to defer the cases for further investigation. This clause seeks to enable those deferred cases, when they have been finalised within one month after the registration period, to be allowed to be included in the electoral register.

Column: 3410

    The Government is doing all it can to finalise consideration of these deferred cases within one month from 31st January, 1958, and it is felt that it is but fair that those who registered during the registration period but whose applications are deferred pending further investigation, should their applications be approved and citizenship granted them, should qualify for inclusion on the electoral register for the purpose of the next general election.

WIDOWS' AND ORPHANS' PENSION
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

10.34 a.m.

    Mr S. T. Stewart: Mr Speaker, Sir, I have His Excellency's consent to proceed with this Bill. I move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The purpose of the amendments proposed in the Bill is to provide additional options not at present allowed by the principal Ordinance to contributors to the Straits Settlements Widows' and Orphans' Pension Scheme who retire under the provisions of the Retirement from the Public Service (Compensation) Ordinance.

Column: 3415



    The present law provides for a compulsory contribution to the scheme of 4 per cent of officers' salaries, and retiring officers are given a choice between paying 4 per cent of their last drawn personal emoluments or 4 per cent of their unreduced pension. Officers who are compulsorily retired under the Compensation Ordinance as a result of the implementation of the policy of Malayanisation are denied the full benefits of the scheme to the extent that their compulsory retirement prevents them from contributing sums which increase progressively as the officers' salaries increase by incremental advancement or by promotion.

    It is therefore desired, Sir, to make the following three further options available to officers retiring under the Compensation Ordinance:

    (a) to cease contributing to the scheme and to have the prospective benefits frozen from the date of cessation; or

    (b) to pay into the scheme an actuarially computed amount which would freeze their prospective benefits at the amounts produced as if they had continued to contribute after retirement at 4 per cent of last drawn salary or 4 per cent of unreduced pension; or

    (c) to allow contributors to withdraw an actuarially calculated proportion of their contributions in return for a complete relinquishment of their rights under the scheme.

    Clauses 2, 3 and 4 of the Bill now before the House, and new rules IX and X are designed to give effect to this intention. Sir, the legislation is, in the nature of things, complicated, but the examples given in clause 8 (b) of the Bill show the effect of the alternatives. Members are assured that the options have been based on actuarial advice and that no loss of Government funds is incurred.

    The Government of the Federation of Malaya has already amended its Widows' and Orphans' Pension legislation

Column: 3416

to provide for these similar additional options, and the Bill will now bring us into line with the Federation in this matter.

    The Bill also makes provision to enable members of the Straits Settlements Widows' and Orphans' Pension Scheme who are compulsorily retired in the Federation of Malaya to avail themselves of the same options.

    Sir, the other clauses in the Bill are consequential amendments to the principal Ordinance. I would like to point out that there is a very minor typographical error in the last line of the explanatory note, where the figure "8" should be "9". I shall, in the Committee stage, move a small amendment of which I have already given notice. Sir, I beg to move.

LOAN TO GOVERNMENT OFFICERS' HOUSING SOCIETY

    Mr Hart: Sir, I have His Excellency the Governor's authority to move,

    That this Assembly approves of the loan by the Government to the Singapore Government Officers' Co-operative Housing Society Limited of the sum of $2,000,000 at an interest rate of 4½ per cent per annum repayable by eleven equal annual instalments to start from the 11th year after the grant of the loan and be completed in the 21st year, to be secured by a legal mortgage of all the immovable properties belonging to the Society.

    Sir, hon. Members are probably aware that following a decision of Government not to provide quarters for locally- domiciled officers except for those who are required to live in institutional quarters, it was decided in 1953 to provide facilities to enable locally-domiciled officers to acquire their own houses. In order to implement this decision, a scheme was formulated in September 1953 whereby Government servants could borrow from Government through the Malaya-Borneo Building Society, Ltd., acting as agents, to enable them to purchase their own houses.
