NEGOTIATIONS WITH MALAYSIA ON OUTSTANDING ISSUES

(Status)

     1.  Mr R Ravindran asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs what is the status of

Column: 7

negotiations with Malaysia on the outstanding issues in the light of Malaysia's decision to proceed with the construction of the bridge on their side of the Causeway.

     The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo):  Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore and Malaysia continue to work at resolving our outstanding bilateral issues.  As the House will recall, Senior Minister Goh Chok Tong visited Malaysia in December 2004 and March 2005 to discuss the outstanding issues with Prime Minister Abdullah Badawi.  SM Goh and PM Abdullah had productive discussions and agreed on the steps to move the process forward.  In particular, they agreed to conduct their talks based on two broad principles:  first, any proposal should be mutually beneficial and not disadvantage either party and, second, both sides should not allow these old issues to hold future cooperation hostage.  SM Goh and PM Abdullah agreed that it would not be helpful to publicise the details of these negotiations while they were going on.  They also agreed that officials from both sides should meet to discuss the outstanding bilateral issues.  Following this, senior officials from both sides have met three times on 6th and 7th September 2005, 19th October 2005 and 24th November 2005 for discussions. 

 

     These discussions have been carried out as part of a process to find a mutually-beneficial solution on the issues, in the context of Singapore agreeing to build a full bridge to replace the Causeway.  These discussions have made progress.

 

     During his visit to Singapore from 4th to 5th February this year for the MFA-Wisma Putra Games, Malaysian Foreign Minister Datuk Seri Syed Hamid and I affirmed that these bilateral negotiations would continue.  We agreed that the

Column: 8

bilateral negotiations should not go on forever.  Syed Hamid and I have asked our officials to narrow the differences and, where this is not possible, crystallise the issues so that they can be surfaced to SM Goh and PM Abdullah for a political decision.  The senior officials will be meeting again later this month.

 

     Mr Speaker, Sir, on 26th January this year, the New Straits Times reported that Malaysia is going ahead with plans to build a scenic bridge across its half of the Johor Strait, and that the Malaysian Government had decided not to wait for Singapore's decision on the matter.  In view of this and other reports, we sent a Third-Person Note (TPN) to Malaysia on 27th January, seeking clarification on Malaysia's position on the bilateral negotiation process and its half-bridge proposal.  We are awaiting an official response to our TPN.  In the meantime, Malaysian leaders, including PM Abdullah and Syed Hamid, have clarified in the press that negotiations between Singapore and Malaysia are still ongoing.     


     Singapore's position on the full bridge remains unchanged.  As I told this House on 17th October last year, Singapore can agree to Malaysia's proposal to replace the Causeway with a new bridge only if there is a balance of benefits on both sides.  Only then can the considerable costs involved in building our side of a new bridge be justified.  It is how this balance of benefits can be achieved that is currently being discussed by the officials from both sides.  As discussions are still ongoing, it would not be helpful to publicise further details at this stage.


     On the half-bridge, Singapore's consistent position has been reiterated on several occasions.  On 25th October 2003, we sent a TPN to Malaysia stating that any unilateral decision to demolish its side of the Causeway to construct a half-bridge has to comply with the principles enunciated in the Order issued by the

International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS).  Our TPN also stated that international facilities like the Causeway could not be demolished without the approval, agreement and involvement of both States. 

 

     When I met Syed Hamid during the MFA-Wisma Putra Games, I said that while Singapore fully respected Malaysia's sovereignty, any major work in the Straits of Johor could affect Singapore and we needed to know the details to assess the environmental and other impacts.

CITIZENSHIP ADVISORY COMMITTEE MEMBERS

(Criteria and selection process)

     2.  Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs (a) what are the criteria and selection process for members of the Immigration and Checkpoints Authority (ICA) Citizenship Advisory Committee (CAC);  (b) what is the exact role of the ICA CAC in the process of granting citizenship to PRs;  (c) what are the safeguards against corruption in the ICA CAC since the members have the power to make recommendations to grant citizenship to foreigners;  and (d) how can citizens and affected foreigners go about blowing the whistle on rogue officials, especially if their citizenship applications are on the line.

     The Senior Minister of State for Home Affairs (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the  Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs):  Mr Speaker, Sir, the Citizenship Advisory Committee (CAC) is constituted under Section 18(2) of the Third Schedule to the Constitution of the Republic of Singapore.  The Committee consists of three persons and is appointed by the President.

 

     Before rejecting a citizenship application, the Constitution requires the

Column: 10

Minister to refer the case to the CAC.  The role of the CAC is to review citizenship applications to ensure that the merits of each case have been duly considered before the Minister rejects the application.  In his decision to reject the application, the Minister must consider any report made to him by the CAC. 

 

     However, while the CAC can recommend granting citizenship to foreigners, the Minister will make the final decision on the application.

 

     To be a member of the CAC, one has to be a Singapore Citizen and a person of integrity, good character and reputation.  These criteria inevitably involve judgment.

 

     The President will appoint the members on the advice of the Minister for Home Affairs after assessing the candidates' background, qualifications and contributions, including recommendations from people and organisations who are familiar with the candidates.

 

     Sir, the Ministry of Home Affairs places a high premium on honesty and integrity for all its officers.  Should citizens and foreigners have any information on corrupt officials, they can lodge reports with the Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau (CPIB).  The Government treats such complaints and adverse feedback seriously.  If the allegations are substantiated, offenders will be prosecuted in court.

     Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong (Non-Constituency Member):  Sir, if the members of the CAC have this power to recommend the granting of citizenship, what kind of checks are in place to ensure that there is no potential abuse of this power or that there is corruption?  What new measures are being instituted since the case involving the professor from the Nanyang Technological University?

     Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee:  Sir, first of all, let me reiterate that whilst the CAC

Column: 11

plays a meaningful role, in fact, its role is limited in the sense that only those applications which the Registrar of Citizens is going to reject will go to the CAC.  If the CAC feels that a case is deserving for approval before it goes to the Minister, it can make the recommendation to the Minister.  The Committee also works as a group of three, not just one person, not even as the Chairman.  So, all, ie, the three members will discuss and then everybody has to sign the recommendation. 

 

     Like I have said, the system has worked well.  In fact, the Ministry of Home Affairs has many such committees, and every person who gets on to these committees is carefully selected.  Many of them, in fact, have very strong credentials.  So, we should not jump the gun.  Yes, there is a case pending in the court.  But let us not overhaul the system.  The system has worked well, and we should continue to work it.

FREE-TO-AIR TV CHANNELS

(Telecasts of old movies and TV programmes)

     3.  Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (a) what are the reasons for repeat telecasts of old movies and other TV programmes on our free-to-air TV channels lately, especially during the recent public holidays;  (b) if there has been a decrease in viewership of TV programmes telecast by MediaCorp since its merger with MediaWorks;  (c) how much television licence fees are collected from households annually and whether the money collected is used to buy new movies;  and (d) why is the penalty fee for late payment for TV licences disproportionately high in relation to the licence fee charged.

     The Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (Dr Lee Boon Yang):  Mr Speaker, Sir, commercial entertainment TV stations regularly carry repeat programmes in their schedules as no station has the resources to run unlimited fresh programming.  MediaCorp, as a commercial broadcaster, is no different.

 

     When MediaCorp acquires its programmes, it usually does so with the licence to broadcast these programmes more than once.  This enables MediaCorp to run repeats off the prime-time, which is usually in the mornings, afternoons or late at night.  Repeats are also broadcasted at a later date or during public holidays and festive periods, such as Christmas and Chinese New Year.  MediaCorp's repeat programmes during public holidays and festive occasions usually include perennial favourites, which is again a common practice.  

 

     Sir, the Media Development Authority does not impose any repeat restrictions on MediaCorp's programming.  However, MediaCorp has to comply with public service broadcast requirements which require them to carry more than 6,500 hours of first-run Mandatory and Additional Public Service Broadcast funded programmes every year.  MediaCorp clearly understands its obligations as a recipient of Public Service Broadcast funds and also the competition coming from cable TV offerings.  It is well aware that, if it cannot offer viewers acceptable programming, it will lose market share.  This is the law of the market.
 
     Sir, the overall viewership of MediaCorp TV channels has remained largely unchanged following the merger of MediaCorp and MediaWorks.  Compared to 2004 pre-merger viewership, Channel 5's average ratings fell minimally by about 0.1%, ie, about 4,000 viewers, in 2005.  Channel NewsAsia, Central and Suria

maintained their viewership.  Channel U's average ratings dipped by 0.5%, ie, about 18,000 viewers, from 2.1% in 2004 to 1.6% in 2005.  Average ratings for Channel 8, on the other hand, grew by 0.4%, ie, about 16,000 viewers, from 4% to 4.4% during the same period.

 

     The decline in Channel U's viewership could be attributed to MediaCorp's re-positioning of the channel from a mass channel to a niche channel.  Under SPH, Channel U competed directly with Channel 8 for the same mass audience.  After the merger, the channel was repositioned to complement Channel 8 by targeting bilingual young adults aged 15 to 35 who are cosmopolitan in outlook.  

 

     Sir, the total collection of TV and radio licence fees was about $111 million in FY04/05.  The licence fees funded more than 3,000 hours of public service programmes on TV and ensured that our local audiences have access to a significant amount of quality programmes in the four official languages.  These include current affairs, information, arts and culture, local sports, local drama, children, and minority language programmes in Malay and Tamil.

 

     The bulk of Public Service Broadcast funding is channelled towards the production of local programmes in various genres.  Local programmes are costly to make but are needed to keep Singaporeans informed about issues of relevance to our society, promote better appreciation of social issues and strengthen community bonding.  Some examples of Public Service Broadcast programmes supported in the past year include:  "40 on 40", a documentary series which traces the significant trends and developments in Singapore over the last 40 years;  "First Mums", a drama series on first-time mothers; and "Manam", a drama on the plight of those suffering from mental illness.

BLOOD TRANSFUSIONS

     1.   Dr Chong Weng Chiew asked the Minister for Health what is (i) the percentage of blood used from our blood bank for elective surgeries in the last 3 years; (ii) the number and percentage of autologous blood transfusions done in the last 3 years; and (iii) the percentage of surgeries utilising autologous blood. 

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:

         The number of units of blood transfused has increased from 64,869 in 2003 to 72,307 in 2004 and 75,919 in 2005.  Elective surgeries accounted for about a third of usage: 31% in 2003, 37% in 2004 and 33% in 2005.

 

     Autologous blood transfusion has helped to reduce the demand for homologous blood transfusion.  But its contribution remains small.  The number and percentage of units of autologous blood transfused in the last 3 years were 1,172 units, or 1.8% in 2003, 1,083 units or 1.5% in 2004 and 866 units or 1.1% in 2005.
