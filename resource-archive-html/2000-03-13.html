ORAL ANSWER TO QUESTION
FIRES IN INDONESIA


    1. Mr Simon S.C. Tay asked the Minister for the Environment what steps have been taken to deal with the fires in Indonesia that have and are again causing haze pollution in the region.

    The Minister for the Environment (Mr Lee Yock Suan): Mr Speaker, Sir, at the last ASEAN Ministerial Meeting on Haze held in Singapore in August last year, ASEAN Environment Ministers agreed that plantation fires are the root cause of the regional haze problem. The Indonesian Government was urged to enforce

Column: 1983

against plantation companies that use fires to carry out land clearing activities, and to put in place the necessary regulations to enforce a zero-burning policy.

    Indonesia has since announced its draft Regulation to implement the zero-burning policy. In January this year, the Indonesian Government held a dialogue with about 60 plantation companies in Sumatra to notify them of this impending Regulation. The plantation companies were also told of Jakarta's determination to enforce against illegal burning activities. Indonesia has also stepped up its awareness-building programmes in fire-prone provinces such as Riau and South Sumatra to alert local populations to the threat of another haze disaster. All these are positive efforts on the part of the Indonesian Government.

    On the ground, unfortunately, political and economic problems have continued to hamper Indonesia's ability and effort to prevent plantation fires. In fact, as we know, there are already large-scale burning activities taking place in Central Sumatra. As a result, the Riau province is affected by severe smoke haze.

    My Ministry has been in constant contact with the Indonesian authorities to track the situation in Riau. To date, Indonesian officials have confirmed with us that most of the recent fires were started by plantation companies to clear land for cultivation purpose. The Indonesian Environmental Impact Management Agency (BAPEDAL) has since despatched teams of officials to investigate the fires in Riau and to take measures to suppress the fires. They also intend to round up the plantation companies and conduct a dialogue to remind them not to use fire to clear land.

    On our part, Singapore has been providing Indonesia with National Oceanic & Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) satellite pictures to alert them to the locations of hot spots since the beginning of the latest spate of fires in Riau. Recently, at the request of the Indonesian authorities,

Column: 1984

we have also started to provide Jakarta with higher resolution pictures acquired from the French SPOT satellites. These are pictures that can pinpoint the locations of fires up to a resolution of 20 metres. We hope that such information would be helpful to the Indonesian authorities in their efforts to act against the errant plantation companies.

    Besides satellite pictures, Singapore has also been providing other forms of technical assistance to Indonesia. To date, we have provided them with communication and computer equipment to facilitate fire detection and enforcement, air monitoring equipment, as well as training for their local officials in the area of satellite imagery and air quality monitoring. We will continue to offer such assistance if requested by Indonesia.

    I have since written to the Indonesian State Minister for the Environment, Dr Sonny Keraf, to express to him Singapore's concern over the fires in Sumatra. I have also sought the co-operation of his office to undertake prompt and firm enforcement actions against plantation companies that use fire to clear land. My counterpart in Malaysia has likewise expressed his concern to Dr Keraf.

    The current La Nina event is expected to weaken in the months ahead. Weather experts have predicted that the El Nino dry conditions may return in the later half of this year or in 2001. For now, because of the prevailing wind directions, Singapore's air quality is not likely to be badly affected by haze originating from fires in Sumatra. However, if the fires and haze there persist in the months ahead, our air quality can be significantly affected when the winds blow in our direction.

    My Ministry will continue to work closely with the Indonesian authorities to urge them to take immediate actions to avert another major haze episode. We will also continue to participate actively in regional and international efforts by working closely with our other ASEAN neighbours as well as international
