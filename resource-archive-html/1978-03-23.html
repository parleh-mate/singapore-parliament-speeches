REVISION OF ATTENDANCE FEES
AND DENTAL SERVICE CHARGES
(Statement by the Minister for Health)

2.37 p.m.

Accident & Emergency Fees

     The Minister for Health (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, during the debate on the budget of the Ministry of Health the Member for Anson mentioned of rumours on my Ministry's intention to increase the attendance fee at Accident & Emergency (A&E) departments in the hospitals. I am using this opportunity to inform the House that the Member for Anson is a good fortune teller and he has made the rumours come true.

    During a press conference last year I mentioned misutilisation of A&E departments. Of the attendances at A&E departments in a 4-month period, 72.8% were self-referrals, 9.4% by general practitioners, 5.1% by the Ministry's Outpatient Dispensaries (OPD's), 3.8% by the Police and 8.9% by others. Only 17.8% of patients were considered serious for admission into hospital and 16.7% were referred to specialist clinics. On the other hand, as high as 61.9% of patients were discharged after examination and the remaining 3.5% were asked to go back to their OPD's.

Column: 1432



    The Member for Kim Seng told the House last week of his own personal experiences with National Service patients referred to the Singapore General Hospital. Most of the referrals according to him had minor ailments which could and should be treated by Army doctors in their camps but are not. This is confirmatory evidence that the hospitals are being misused.

    In A&E departments doctors assume that the patients who turn up are serious or emergency cases. To be fair to such genuine patients, doctors require a longer time to examine them. It is important that doctors in A&E departments should know when a stomach ache is due to acute appendicitis which requires surgery and do not dismiss it as gastritis or a stomach upset from eating contaminated food. But if doctors in A&E departments are faced with large numbers of patients with minor ailments, their mental alertness becomes dulled and mistakes can be made. Patients, who go to A&E departments or visit specialist clinics should now understand why they have long waiting times before being examined or admitted into a ward.

    I have circulated to Members figures (Cols. 1471 - 1472) showing an increasing trend in A&E attendances for the last four years. Something must be done now if the services at A&E departments are not to break down. Having discussed this problem with Medical Superintendents of hospitals and Heads of A&E departments, we have come to the conclusion that because the existing attendance fee of $4 is too low patients with minor complaints or who have a cough and running nose are crowding into A&E departments. In order to protect the efficiency of A&E departments, the Ministry has therefore decided to raise the A&E attendance fee to $10. If patients deserve admission into hospital for treatment, the $10 will be credited to the minimum deposit of $20 which Class C patients have to pay on admission.

    Order for Second Reading read.
2.50 p.m.

     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Defence (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    At present, people who are enlisted for national service are liable to render full-time service in the Singapore Armed Forces for two years. However, those who are selected for officer training or who have been promoted above the rank of Lance Corporal

Column: 1437

are liable to serve 2 1/2 years. For equity purposes, it is necessary to extend the same liability of 2 1/2 years to those full-time national servicemen in the Police Force, the Vigilante Corps, the Singapore Fire Brigade and the Aerodrome Fire Service who are trained to hold a rank equivalent to that of an officer in the Singapore Armed Forces or who are promoted to a rank equal to that of Corporal and above in the SAF.

    We have noted that employers in the past were generally reluctant to give temporary employment to persons who are liable for national service call-up because, under the law now in force, these employers are obliged to reinstate the servicemen after they have completed their national service. So legislation which is intended to assist young men has in practice worked out to their detriment and this is a clear case of counter-productive legislation. To rectify this situation and to encourage employers to give temporary employment to young persons, awaiting national service call-up, the obligation of reinstatement is now removed. Employers are no longer prohibited from terminating the service of temporary employees in their employment by reason of their liability to be called up to perform national service. This will make it easier for school leavers to get paid employment while waiting for their NSF call-up.

    Under the present law, employers are required to pay their employees who are performing reserve, voluntary or mobilised service the difference between the employees' civilian pay and service pay. Employers are then allowed to obtain reimbursement of this amount by deducting from their payroll tax or by claiming reimbursement from the designated authority. This cumbersome procedure is to be replaced by a simple one whereby the Ministry of Defence pays directly to the employees doing reservist or other forms of training the difference between their civilian pay and service pay. This new procedure will also apply to the Ministry of Home Affairs which looks after the Special Constabulary, Vigilante Corps, Singapore Fire Brigade and the Singapore Aerodrome Fire Service.

The make-up pay will then be computed by the paymasters of units and be added to their service pay where civilian pay exceeds service pay.

     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Defence (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    There is always the possibility that ex-servicemen, whether regular or national service, may divulge protected defence information. As such persons have ceased to be subject to military law except during the time they are actually performing reservist training and they cannot be tried by a military court for such offences. It is now intended that a subordinate military court will have jurisdiction to try such persons for the offence of divulging protected information. For this purpose, such persons are to be regarded as continuing to be subject to military law in respect of unauthorised disclosure of protected defence information. The provisions of the Act relating to the arrest, detention in custody, investigation of offences, trial and punishment by the subordinate military court in connection with such offences and execution of any sentence passed shall apply to them.

Column: 1439



    Presently, persons subject to military law who are accused of murder or culpable homicide not amounting to murder or with an offence under section 121 and 121A of the Penal Code may be tried by a subordinate military court, the President of which must be a Judge of the Supreme Court appointed by the Chief Justice. Where an accused is to be found guilty of a charge for which the punishment is death, the decision of the subordinate military court must be unanimous. Other than those presently listed in the Act, there are offences carrying the death penalty; for example, certain offences under the Misuse of Drugs Act, 1973, which may be committed by persons subject to military law and be tried by the subordinate military court. It is necessary that the Act be amended to provide for the President of the court martial trying such other offences carrying the death penalty to be a Supreme Court Judge appointed by the Chief Justice and for a decision of such a court martial to be unanimous.

    Under the Misuse of Drugs Act, 1973, a certificate relating to a controlled drug signed by a Government Chemist is admissible as evidence in proceedings. He need not appear in court to testify to his signature. It will be considered as prima facie evidence of all matters contained in it. A provision is now made to give a Singapore Armed Forces Pharmacist or Chemist the same legal standing. Under the Misuse of Drugs Act, 1973, a person who has been required to provide a specimen of his urine for a urine test has the right to apply for a second test of his urine specimen. This is to ensure that the accused is not convicted due to any error in the handling or analysis of his urine sample. It is intended that the right to apply for a second urine test should also be accorded to SAF personnel and power is given to the Armed Forces Council to set aside the conviction and sentence if, as a result of the second test, it is found that there is no controlled drug in the urine specimen. The power to set aside a conviction and sentence is necessary as drug offences in the SAF are dealt with and concluded very swiftly. This is

in the interest of soldiers who otherwise would have to remain in detention for weeks or months on end.

In addition to or in lieu of such punishment, the court may impose military punishment, for example, discharge with ignominy, reduction in rank or forfeiture of military decoration or military award. Such an additional power of punishment is necessary when dealing with military personnel in the context of the Singapore Armed Forces.

     The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Labour (Mr Fong Sip Chee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Hon. Members will recall that the House in February 1973 passed the Bill which was enacted as the Factories Act 1973. Introduced by the Government then as a result of its concern over the trend of the increasing number of industrial accidents, the Act sought to improve safety, health and welfare provisions as well as to ensure a more stringent observance of safety standards.

    Sir, in the five years since the House dealt with the matter, the industrial safety situation has improved. It is gratifying to note that hon.

Column: 1443

Members have also been as vocal as they are concerned with the well-being of our workers as evidenced by the lively discussions in this House in the Budget debates over the years. Some of their pertinent observations and suggestions which the Minister found to be feasible have also been incorporated in this Amendment Bill.

    Based on the criteria used by the American National Standards Institute, the accident frequency and severity rates in 1973 were 6.8 accidents per million man-hours worked and 1,282 man-days lost per million man-hours worked. In 1977, the accident frequency and severity rates recorded a decline to 5.3 and 501 respectively. In absolute numbers, however, there were in 1973, 2,910 industrial accidents involving 73 fatalities as compared to 4,575 accidents with 57 fatalities in 1977.

    Nevertheless, the number of industrial accidents remains high. It is a matter of continuing concern on the part of the Government. The economic cost of these accidents is large. With manpower as our only dependable resource, we cannot afford to see our workers maimed or killed. More importantly, the social cost is high and cannot be measured by plain statistics. There is the pain, misery and anguish of the worker and his family in every accident. We must avoid this human suffering especially as they are caused by accidents which often need not have happened at all.

    The safety and health situation in industrial establishments should be further improved. The administration of the Factories Act since its enactment in 1973 shows that the law needs to be further strengthened to meet with the increasing sophistication in our technological advancement and it is for this purpose that this Amendment Bill is before the House.     The proposed amendments to the Factories Act are described in the Explanatory Statement to the Bill. I should like, however, to highlight some of the more salient features.

    Sir, our experience shows that the knowledge in safety standards in the use and operation of machinery is found to be wanting. Accident statistics in the past two years have shown that there were more than 2,000 accidents involving the use of machinery. Analysis made on the causes of these accidents revealed a combination of several factors - lack of awareness on the part of the workers on the danger associated with the machinery they handle, poor maintenance of machinery and the absence of adequate training to provide the operators with a thorough knowledge of the machines' peculiarities or potential hazards.

    To meet with this situation, a number of amendments have been proposed. Clause 7 of the Bill which amends section 24 proVides for the proper maintenance of machinery and plant installed in factories. This new provision will minimise the danger of injury to which the workers are exposed or to more nasty consequences as a result of machinery or plant breaking down.

    Clause 8 of the Bill which amends section 28 of the principal Act requires notices to be put up instructing workers in the safe operation of a machine or process which is likely to cause bodily injury. With the introduction of this new provision, neither the worker, the safety personnel or supervisor, nor the employer can plead ignorance, a common tactic frequently employed when accidents occur. It will facilitate more effective and speedier inspection by the enforcement officers.

    Clause 20 of the Bill seeks to amend section 61 of the principal Act by empowering the Minister to make regulations to prescribe the qualifications and training of persons who are employed at any machine or plant. This is an important provision, particularly for construction worksites where accidents involving mobile cranes, excavators and bulldozers are caused

mainly by operators who are not sufficiently trained in the handling of such heavy mobile machinery and plant.

    Another area where similar upgrading of safety measures is most expedient is the shipbuilding and repairing industry, where working in confined spaces and in explosive or inflammable environment is common. Hon. Members are well aware that deficiency of oxygen is a hazard ever-present in confined spaces, particularly where a number of workers are deployed in one confined space, and if the oxygen depletion process is quickened it may result in multiple fatalities. While the existing Act requires a responsible person to test and certify that a confined space is safe before allowing workers to work in it, the tester is not required to keep records of such tests. This has resulted in poor documentation of such records which frustrates safety management and impedes enforcement. To rectify this defect, clause 11 is introduced to amend section 34 requiring records of such tests to be kept. Further, clause 12 of the Bill which amends section 35 of the Act introduces a new requirement for plants, tanks or vessels which have previously contained explosives or inflammable substances to be inspected before hot work is allowed to be carried out. Stringent adherence to this procedure by Safety Personnel in the factories will certainly ensure the elimination of the presence of explosive or inflammable substances which can cause fire and explosions.

    The Bill also includes amendments providing for better observance of safety standards. Under the present Act, the maximum penalty for infringements of safety provisions which are likely to cause death or bodily injury to any person and other offences for which no expressed penalty has been provided for is a $2,000 fine or an imprisonment not exceeding three months, or both. The seriousness of infringements of this nature need not be further emphasised. The present penalties are in our view not sufficiently adequate to deter persons from committing such infringements.     For the domiciled deceased person, estate duty is imposed on his total assets in Singapore as well as on his movable assets elsewhere. In the case of a non-domiciled person, estate duty is levied on his movable and immovable properties situate in Singapore only.

Column: 1462



    The rates of estate duty existing before 1st April 1977 ranged between 5% and 60% for estates exceeding $50,000 in value. Estates below $50,000 in value were exempted from duty. Duty was also remitted at a declining rate of 10% of the duty payable for every $20,000 in excess of $50,000. The remission limit was $23,000.

    In my 1977 annual Budget Statement, I announced that, to encourage home ownership and a more stable society comprising home owners, the rates of estate duty would be revised from 1st April 1977. The exemption limit would be raised from $50,000 to $100,000. The rate of remission would remain the same, i.e. at a declining rate of 10% of the duty payable for every $20,000 in excess of $100,000 in value of the estate. The remission limit would be increased to $280,000.

    The Estate Duty (Amendment) Bill seeks to amend the Estate Duty Act accordingly. Clause 2 of the Amendment Bill levies the new rates of estate duty on the property of any person dying on or after 1st April 1977. Clause 3 sets out the remissions including those provided under the Estate Duty (Remission) Order, 1974. Clause 4 of the Bill exempts bequests to statutory institutions as specified in the Twelfth Schedule to the Act from estate duty.

    In a corresponding Bill, which I am taking through the House today, the Statutes of the Republic of Singapore (Miscellaneous Amendments) Bill, I shall be seeking to delete the estate duty exemption provisions in the various Statutes. These are now more appropriately incorporated in the amendments made by the Bill to the Estate Duty Act.

    Sir, the Board of Governors of the International Monetary Fund has adopted a Resolution on Increases in Quota of Fund members - Sixth General Review, under which there will be an overall increase in the Fund's quotas not exceeding 32.5% of the total quotas as of 19th February 1976.

    Quota reviews are conducted by the International Monetary Fund at intervals of approximately five years so that adjustments can be made in members' quotas to reflect changes in their relative economic strength. An important feature of a member's quota is that it determines the scope of a member's access to the resources of the Fund when in balance of payments difficulties and a member's obligations to lend to the Fund on concessionary terms when its balance of payments position is strong.

    At present Singapore's quota in the International Monetary Fund is 37 million Special Drawing Rights. This is equivalent to US$45.4 million, based on exchange rates at the end of February 1978.

    Parliament is requested to authorise the Government to increase Singapore's quota in the International Monetary Fund by a sum of twelve million Special Drawing Rights, equivalent to US$14.7 million, based on exchange rates at the end of February 1978, to a sum not exceeding forty-nine million Special Drawing Rights (SDR 49 million).
