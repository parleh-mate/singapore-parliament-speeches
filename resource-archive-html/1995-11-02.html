     Mr Kenneth Chen Koon Lap (Hong Kah GRC): Mr Speaker, Sir, in yesterday's debate, it was unanimously agreed that family violence shall never be condoned. Those who engage in acts of beating up family members should be severely punished. This, we are in total agreement. However, it is in the form of legislation between the existing Women's Charter and the Penal Code and the proposed Family Violence Bill that we must decide on which is the best to address this serious social ill. There is a significant difference both in terms of philosophy and procedure between the two. In the Women's Charter and Penal Code, it takes a more humane approach. Every effort is made to mediate and reconciliate the conflicting parties in order to preserve the family unit. The law will only be introduced as a last resort. Whereas in the proposed Bill, the law is to be called upon on the onset of a family conflict. It is confrontational and litigious.

    I cannot reconcile the harsh high-handed and detached way of dealing with family conflicts as I feel that family conflict is a very complex and intimate problem that concerns family members who are bound by blood or marriage. Therefore, I wish to highlight a few provisions in the proposed Bill which I found extremely difficult to reconcile with.

    Firstly, the underlying intention of the Bill is to introduce litigation into family conflict at an early stage. It runs contrary to our strong belief that we should endeavour to resolve family conflict as much as possible through tolerance, compromise and respect. We must recognise that family violence is a very complex matter. It involves deep emotional feelings, relationship which takes years to develop and in the case of husband and wife, affection and a very special type of bondage. It is totally

Column: 170

different from the case where two persons are involved in a fight because if that happens, it is a simple matter of determining who is the guilty one, prosecute him or her, fine and punish the guilty party, and that is the end of the story. The answer is obviously no. Because once a conflict happens, family members are compelled to take sides to give evidence, testimonies or as witness in the court.
