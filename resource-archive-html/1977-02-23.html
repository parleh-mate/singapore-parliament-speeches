Language Policy

    Criticism has been directed at the announcement made by the Ministry of Education on 22nd January regarding first and second language requirements for admission to Junior Colleges and Pre-University classes from 1979. This is no new and ad hoc policy but a continuation and development of the bilingual policy advocated 17 years ago in 1959. It is important for those graduating from universities who will be holding key positions of leadership in our society to be effectively bilingual in their mother tongue and English. The language requirements announced on 22nd January are principally for the purpose of raising the level of the first language of those entering the universities. Employers in both the public and private sectors have complained of the declining standards in language of our university graduates. The universities, on the other hand, blame this on the standard of the students coming to them from the schools.

    The need to raise the level of the first language is shown by the results in English as a first language in the GCE `O' level examinations. In 1973, 35% of those who sat for the examination passed in English as the first language. 37% in 1974 and 44% in 1975. While the intention of the announcement is to raise the level of the first language, we have to ensure that this is not done at the expense of the second language.
