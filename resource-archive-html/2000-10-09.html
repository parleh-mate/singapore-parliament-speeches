UPSURGE IN OIL PRICES


    1. Mrs Lim Hwee Hua asked the Minister for Trade and Industry whether the upsurge in oil prices, if sustained, will (i) affect economic growth for Singapore; (ii) exert inflationary pressure; and (iii) lead Government to introduce measures to curb oil-related consumption.

    2. Mr Leong Horn Kee asked the Minister for Trade and Industry what in his assessment are the various effects of rising oil prices on the economy and its various component sectors, especially

those that are dependent on oil, such as transportation, public utilities and manufacturing.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to take Question Nos 1 and 2 together?

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Oil prices have risen sharply over the last two years, from a low of US$10 per barrel in January 1999 to a high of US$37 in September this year. This has raised global concerns about the impact on inflation and economic growth.

    The oil shocks in 1974 and 1980 triggered global slowdowns1. However, the economic impact of the recent surge in oil prices is likely to be much less severe.

    Firstly, energy conservation and the shift to alternative fuels have made the global economy less dependent on oil. Since 1974, the oil consumption per unit of real GDP has fallen by almost half in the US, EU and Japan, and by about 30% in the rest of Asia.

    Secondly, the US$10 per barrel price in January 1999 was abnormally low. The average price over the last 4 years has been US$18. Compared to this, the present price of US$33 is not very high. Adjusted for inflation, the present price is only two-thirds (62%) of the 1974 level, and one-third (35%) of the 1980 level.

    Studies of the economic impact of high oil prices have shown that it should be quite small. The OECD has estimated that a US$10 increase would only raise inflation by 0.4-1.1 percentage point and lower real GDP by 0.2-0.5 percentage point in the US, EU and Japan. Similarly, the Asian Development Bank (ADB) has projected that a price of US$35 a barrel for a year would reduce real GDP growth in the world by 0.2- 0.25 percentage point,
