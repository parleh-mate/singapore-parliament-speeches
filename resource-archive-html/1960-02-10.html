    2. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Deputy Prime Minister whether he will make a statement on the policy of the Government with regard to the nationalisation of the Singapore Traction Company and/or other bus companies in Singapore.

    Dr Toh Chin Chye: Mr Speaker, on principle, it is the policy of the P.A.P. Government to nationalise public road transport. When and how nationalisation of the bus companies will be brought about must depend on the ability of the Government to meet the financial problem of compensating the bus companies.     We are not committed within the next five years to bring about this nationalisation, as this has not been declared as par of our Five-Year Plan. Nonetheless, it will be our intention to organise and train an administrative and managerial staff to form the nucleus for a future Road Transport Authority which will effectively take over the control of public transport. This is a prerequisite, as the complicated machinery of running public transport is not within the capability of any Government department at the moment, or, for that matter, of any statutory body that can be created in the immediate future.

    Statement by the Minister for Education

2.45 p.m.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, I rise to speak on the subject of the Nanyang University consequent upon the presentation to this Assembly on 6th February, 1960, of the Report of the Nanyang University Review Committee headed by Dr Gwee Ah Leng.

Column: 139



    This Review Committee was appointed by me on 23rd July 1959, to "review the Report submitted by the Prescott Commission on Nanyang University and to determine the extent and sequence of the reorganisation deemed necessary." The appointment of the Review Committee was in keeping with one of the recommendations (except in the manner of the composition of its members) contained in the 1959 Prescott Commission's Report on Nanyang University, which stated:

    "We recommend that an Ad Hoc Committee be formed of not more than seven members, consisting of representatives of the Nanyang University and of the Government, to review our Report and to determine the extent and sequence of the reorganisation deemed necessary."

In view of the findings of the Prescott Commission, it was considered desirable that the members of the Review Committee should be outstanding, in, dependent, local men, each of whom having distinguished himself in varied fields of activity, for example, higher education, medicine, journalism and finance. One member from the staff of Nanyang University and another from the staff of the Ministry of Education acted as Joint Secretaries to the Review Committee. It is worthy to record, at this stage, that when persons appointed to the Review Committee were made known to the public in July last year, they received warm approval from all quarters.

    From the Report now in the hands of Members of this Assembly, you will agree with me, Sir, that the Review Committee had put in considerable work during the four months of its deliberations. On behalf of the Government, I wish to place on record our deep appreciation of their devotion to duty and sacrifice of time and labour in the service of their country.

    The signed Report was handed to me on 20th November 1959, and copies have been forwarded to the Federation Government authorities for their information. The Review Committee's findings are, in a few words, reorganisation of Nanyang University with Government support. The Singapore Government now finds itself in the position of having

Column: 140

to carry the whole financial responsibility of Nanyang University, in addition to its share of subsidising the University of Malaya. The Singapore Government will face this problem to the best of its ability in the interest of the people and of education of our City State.

    It is regretted that the Nanyang University authorities failed to take any positive steps towards the maintenance of academic standards during the recent graduation examination held in December, 1959, for its first batch of graduates, by not implementing the "external examiners system" which was part of the Nanyang University Examination Regulations as approved by its Executive Council on 3rd July, 1956. Examination Regulation 28 (v) states as follows:

    "Graduation Examination: conducted at the end of the course by a Board of Examiners appointed by the University and consisting of internal and external Professors and specialists. The subjects and scope of the Graduation Examination shall be the principal special subjects in each course together with a thesis. The standard shall be set by the Board of Examiners."

To our knowledge, no external examiners were appointed and according to newspaper reports which are the only information we have, there was a Final Examination Board of three; firstly, a Member of the Executive Council, and now its Vice-Chancellor; secondly, the Secretary-General of the Executive Council who is resigning in a few days' time; and thirdly, the Dean of Studies, who has since resigned from Nanyang and returned to Hongkong.

    It would therefore appear that the first batch of graduates from Nanyang University have been allowed to graduate without fulfilling the requirements of its own Examination regulations.

    Furthermore, up till today, there is no official published list of the reported 437 who graduated out of the 471 students who presented themselves for the Final Examination. The public is, therefore, kept in the dark as to who has or has not, in fact, graduated. In the meantime, graduates have to seek employment and employers are kept wondering. Even my

Column: 141

Ministry, which will be the largest employer of Nanyang University graduates, is without an official list of graduates; all we have are stencilled statements of 1959 Final Year students, with blue pencil lines drawn across certain names purporting to be failures!

    It is unknown in any university that such a high percentage (more than 92 per cent) of the students would be allowed to pass in the Final Examination. Again, another unusual feature appears to be no grading amongst those who have passed; good students have been thrown in with poor students in the same unclassified lot and thereby penalised. To the many parents who have saved and stinted for years in order that their children may pursue a course of higher education at Nanyang University, the conduct of the graduation examinations must come as a shock.

    The Review Committee came to the core of the problem when it stated that -

    "The administration of Nanyang University has inherited certain features from the system of management in many local Chinese schools. This is a system where the financiers of the school or their nominees form the Board of Management which is responsible, not only for the appointment and dismissal of staff, but also for the educational policy of the school. With men of vision and good sense on the Board of Management, it is possible for such a school to achieve a higher degree of efficiency. On the other hand, it is not uncommon for a Board of Management with good intention but little knowledge of education to interfere with the staffing and the educational policy of the school to such an extent that progress is seriously hampered."

The Review Committee clearly indicated that something is basically wrong with Nanyang University. The ideals of the founders of the Nanyang University were admirable. They set out to establish a University to meet the demand for higher education for students from the Chinese secondary stream at a time when the colonial government failed to do anything. However, in moving towards their goal, the founders, who have become the Management Committee, were confronted with university problems which their commercial and trading

experience could not provide the answers, to. Had the previous Government been realistic in their approach to problems, they should have immediately warned Nanyang University, soon after its establishment, of the dangers arising out of the control of the University by businessmen, who are without the experience of dealing with matters concerning higher education. This misfortune of Nanyang University would not have reached such proportions today had the previous Government acted in a more responsible manner.

    Before the Nanyang University Ordinance came into being, Nanyang University was organised and run on the basis of a limited company. The major preoccupation of the Management Committee was how to run the University without too big an annual deficit. No doubt some $20 million had been promised to the University, but out of this sum only $13 million is reported to have been subscribed.

    In its four years of existence, Nanyang University suffered many setbacks, but none more crippling than the lack of expert planning and advice. A group of shrewd businessmen started the University with a great deal of initiative, but a university is an organisation for learning and research and not a business enterprise. The attitude which goes to make for a successful business enterprise is not necessarily the attitude required for administering a seat of learning. In a university, a line must be drawn between general policy and academic policy, and the latter should be left in the hands of academic personnel. In Nanyang University, the curious situation had arisen where the Secretary-General, who has no acquaintance of university administration, found himself on all academic committees and also one of the three members of the Final Examinations Board.

    A new university could not possibly achieve both quality and quantity in a short space of time. Perhaps out of consideration for financial stability, quality has been sacrificed in the interest of quantity. Further, there appears to be no proper evaluation of the individual

    What then is the Singapore Government's stand on Nanyang University in view of the findings of the Gwee Ah Leng Review Committee? Despite the critical findings contained in the Review Committee's Report, the Singapore Government has decided to employ Nanyang University graduates and to recognise the Nanyang University degrees as equivalent to Pass Degrees of a recognised university. Selection will, as usual, be on individual merits through interview by the Public Service Commission. But in the public interest, the Singapore Government will have to reconsider the position if the next graduation examinations are conducted in this lackadaisical manner.

    The next important question is, in what manner and under what conditions will the Singapore Government be prepared to support Nanyang University?

    Since the People's Action Party Government's education policy is based on parity of treatment for the four streams of education in Singapore, it follows that the new Government will be prepared to support Nanyang University on the same basis as it does the University of Malaya, provided that there are similar safeguards as to the maintenance of proper academic standards and proper accounting of public monies.
