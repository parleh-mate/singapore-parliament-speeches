    1. Mr Lee Choon Eng asked the Minister for Health whether he will make a statement explaining the reasons for the increases in the Hospital Charges in respect of the 1st and 2nd Class Wards in Government Hospitals.

It is also the policy of Government, in accordance with the Labour Front programme, to provide hospitalisation and specialist treatment free to one and all, and the provision of special wards to be maintained for those who can afford it.     The Chief Secretary (Mr W. A. C. Goode): Sir, I move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This is a Bill, Sir, to assist the police in dealing with secret society gangsters. It is unfortunately notorious that secret society gangsters here in Singapore depend on violence, or the threat of violence, for their existence and for their power over the unfortunate people who get into their clutches. They get their funds by

threats, and to be effective, threats have got to be backed up by deeds. So it is that victims who fail to pay extortion money are brutally assaulted, stabbed and even murdered. Further, these gangs have to fight for their own existence in the jungle war that goes on between rival gangs to secure control of particular places or particular rackets. Before the recent police campaign against secret societies, these gang fights and threats of gang fights were almost daily occurrences. In the three months April, May and June, there were no less than 70 occasions on which there was either a gang fight or the threat of a gang fight, and in these fights, as I think it is well known, all sorts of vicious weapons are used - knives, bottles, knuckledusters, bicycle chains, iron bars and other such things. The victims are very often assaulted in the dark or in a crowded public place where the thugs can easily make their escape. But it does very frequently happen that the police, getting information that a gang fight is about to take place, are able to arrive at the scene and prevent the fight actually developing, even though the strong-armed thugs are there with their weapons ready. But if no breach of the peace has actually occurred, it is not possible to charge these thugs in court, and it is to remedy that difficulty that this Bill is brought to the Assembly.

    This Bill will make it an offence to carry, without lawful authority or reasonable excuse, a knife over 2½ inches long or any other offensive weapon. That means that if the police can arrest these thugs who have gathered for a gang fight and find them in possession of these vicious weapons, they will be able to charge them in court and, on conviction, have them sent to prison. Now, unfortunately in these affrays we do not get much help from the victims or even from bystanders who may witness what is going on. They are, unfortunately, too frightened of the brutal power of the secret society gangs to come and give evidence in court, and in order to help successful prosecution, this Bill therefore puts the burden of proof on an accused person to explain why he was carrying a weapon at that particular time and place.

    I am sure, Sir, that any measure designed to assist the police in dealing with this menace of gangsterism will command the full support of this House and of the public. CRIMINAL JUSTICE (TEMPORARY PROVISIONS) (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Chief Secretary (Mr W. A. C. Goode): Sir, I move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This, Sir, is another Bill to strengthen the law against gangsters. As Members of the House are probably aware, the Criminal Justice (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance was enacted last year for the special purpose of dealing with secret societies and gangsters, and its main provisions were that when a member of a secret society or a person connected with a secret society has been convicted of one of the offences listed in the First Schedule, then the courts may sentence

Column: 235

him either to corrective training or to preventive detention, taking into account the age and possibilities of reforming the person concerned or the need to prevent him from continuing to be a menace to his fellow citizens.

    The Schedule to the Ordinance lists the offences for which those sentences can be imposed by the courts, but it omits from that list the offence of criminal intimidation under section 506 of the Penal Code. This is a patent omission in dealing with secret societies because intimidation is the primary activity and, indeed, the lifeblood of the secret societies. They depend for their existence, for their funds, on getting money by extortion or by protection money exacted from taxi-drivers, stall-holders, hawkers and all sorts of other people. The members of secret societies threaten those persons with injury to themselves or to their property unless the money is paid over, and that, of course, is precisely the meaning of "criminal intimidation". So this Bill is introduced, Sir, to include criminal intimidation in the Schedule to the Criminal Justice (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance.

    The opportunity has also been taken to make a small correction in the Bill to bring it up to date. Paragraph (g) of the First Schedule refers to two Emergency regulations which were in force when the Bill was enacted. The Emergency Regulations have now lapsed and this amending Bill substitutes a reference to section 7 of the Criminal Law (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance which has replaced Emergency Regulation 4, and thereby brings the Ordinance up to date with the law as it now is. Sir, I beg to move.
