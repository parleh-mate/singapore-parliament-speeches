Funds Disbursed from Assisted Reproduction Technology (ART) Treatment Co-funding Scheme

(Effectiveness of the Scheme)

1 Dr Lim Wee Kiak asked the Minister for Health (a) how many couples have benefitted from the Assisted Reproduction Technology (ART) co-funding scheme for in-vitro fertilisation (IVF) since its inception in August 2008; (b) what is the total amount of funds disbursed; and (c) what is the success rate thus far.

The Minister of State for Health (Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan) (for the Minister for Health) : Sir, the Assisted Reproduction Technology (ART) treatment co-funding scheme was introduced to help make ART treatments, such as in-vitro fertilisation (IVF), more affordable for couples who have difficulty conceiving. Since the scheme's inception in August 2008, Ministry of Health (MOH) has subsidised a total of 5,503 ART treatment cycles, benefiting 3,841 couples in the process. Thus far, MOH has disbursed around $13 million in funding for this scheme. We are encouraged by the effectiveness of this scheme. About 26% of the treatment cycles under this scheme successfully resulted in live births, which compares well with the national average of 22%.

Page: 1568

Dr Lim Wee Kiak (Nee Soon) : Mr Speaker, Sir, I would like to thank the Minister of State for the answer. In her reply, the Minister of State said that MOH subsidises about 3,000 couples for this particular scheme. I would like to ask the Minister of State, first, is this a subsidy given, because from what I understand, all patients going through IVF are considered "private patients" and no Government subsidy is entitled? My next supplementary question is that since the success rate is so good, will the Government consider extending this particular programme, because I understand that the grant is only available to couples who are receiving IVF treatment in Government restructured hospitals only? Will that be reviewed and can that be extended to the private hospitals, given that it is a grant and not a subsidy?

Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan : Sir, the Member is right to say that it is not a healthcare subsidy per se. The ART co-funding scheme was introduced as part of the Marriage and Parenthood package to allow couples who have difficulties conceiving to go for ART treatment and make it more affordable for them. ART treatment is not essential to the patient's health and so it is a non-standard form of treatment. Like all non-standard treatment, there is no healthcare subsidy. So, if the patients go to a public hospital for ART treatment, if they are not under this co-funding scheme, they will be treated as private patients and charged private rates. So, the Member is right. But if they qualify under this co-funding scheme, then they will get subsidy under the co-funding scheme. When we implemented this, we did not extend it to the private hospitals because the scheme was new. We will continue to monitor demand and consider this as an option as we review our experience, but currently we are not thinking of extending it beyond the public sector as yet.

Dr Lim Wee Kiak : Thank you, Mr Speaker. My last question. I would like to thank the Minister of State for agreeing to review it with a view that it may be introduced to the private hospitals. I would like to ask the Minister of State when MOH compares the average success rate of 22% at the national level versus 26% for this particular programme, is there any factor that causes the success rate to be higher compared to the national level? From what I understand, patients going through this programme have to be below a certain age limit, and have to meet other governing criteria. In fact, a lot of couples unable to fit the criteria have no choice but to resort to going to the private hospitals to receive treatment. I am not sure if MOH would agree that the age difference may have contributed to the difference in success rate. Will MOH now relook at the data to see if when the patients are equal in terms of criteria, the success rates would be the same between the Government hospitals and private hospitals?

Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan : Sir, the statistics that I quoted, that is, 26% under the co-funding scheme as compared to the national level average of 22% – are based on the same age profile. So, in fact, our 26% is also better compared internationally to, say, Australia and New Zealand for the same age profile, where it is about 22.3%. Yes, the Member is right that there are qualifying criteria for patients to go under this scheme. Age is one criterion. The patient has to be below 40 years old. There are certain clinical considerations too. That would likely have contributed to the higher success rate. Clinical evidence is that there is a higher success rate for patients below 40 years old. We need to impose conditions because we are subsidising using taxpayers' money. So, we need to ensure that it is effectively used.

Mr Low Thia Khiang (Aljunied) : Sir, a supplementary question. I would like to ask the Minister of State this: besides the age criterion, what are the other criteria to qualify for this co-funding scheme?

Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan : The criteria will be that the patient has no more than one living child with her husband; the patient must be below 40 years of age at the start of the cycle; the patient has been assessed by the doctor to meet the clinical requirements for ART; treatment is a fresh cycle; no more than two embryos are transferred during the cycle; the patient has not already received three co-funded cycles in the past. The ART co-funding scheme is up to a maximum of three cycles. And the patient or her spouse must be a Singapore citizen at the start of the cycle.

Ms Tan Su Shan (Nominated Member) : Mr Speaker, Sir, thank you for the opportunity to speak. Allow me to declare my pecuniary interest as a banker. I would like to begin by congratulating the Deputy Prime Minister for delivering a Budget for a more inclusive and stronger Singapore. I am also heartened that the Budget appealed to our younger Singaporeans, many of whom care about our elderly, our poor and our disadvantaged, as we all should.

That said, the Deputy Prime Minister's stated goal is a Budget for the future. A Budget for the future should address the long-term issues faced by Singaporeans. A Budget for the future should perhaps relook or adapt to a different economic model to fuel our future growth. The growth model that propelled us for the past 10 years has worked well but reached its limits. We have been successful in attracting MNCs to Singapore, in building high-end manufacturing, and a vibrant services sector, and tourism.

But these successes have led to a reliance on foreign labour and caused a significant jump in our population. And this in turn has tested the limits of our domestic resources like transport, housing and healthcare. This is not sustainable. For the next 10 years, we need to look for growth that will not add so much to our already highly-stressed domestic resource pool. The kind of growth that will not lead to spiralling inflation, a widening income gap, or an over reliance on foreign labour. This may mean that we look for growth beyond Singapore's shores. This may mean that in the long term, we need to re-sharpen or retool ourselves for a different economic growth model.

So, today, I want to offer three key issues to focus on: (i) inflation and how the high cost of living is impacting our business competiveness and how perhaps a fresh look at our existing monetary policy could address this; (ii) the sustainability of Singapore businesses, especially at the international level; and (iii) the building of our human capital with our education system becoming more innovative to keep us all ahead.

Let us begin with inflation and monetary policy. It is obvious that Singapore is becoming an increasingly expensive city in which to live and to operate in. Last Friday, we saw our Consumer Price Index (CPI) going up 4.8% and core inflation was a three-year high of 3.5%. This reflects a serious build up of underlying cost pressures. Let us not forget that CPI represents a "change", or a delta. So when the previous figure was already high, then our costs get higher each month and each year.

In 2011, the CPI was at a high of 5.2% and that was more than five times the median of the 1.0% in the prior decade. Rising inflation hurts our businesses, especially SMEs who are already operating on low single digit margins. High inflation is passed onto consumers, and reduces our purchasing power, with our retirees one of the worst affected. With the new Budget measures pushing labour costs higher, whilst doing not much to address the key challenges of high rental and housing costs, core inflation will likely remain high this year. Is this the "new normal"? Are we asking our consumers and business community to accept that rising prices are here to stay?

Whilst the MAS has continued to maintain its strong Singapore dollar (SGD) appreciation stance, and this has helped keep imported inflation at bay, it is domestic inflation that is the greater threat. Key drivers of domestic inflation are increases in rentals, wages and COE costs. These are demand-side driven inflation pressures that may require some supply-side measures to counter them. An inclusive Budget should target and hold down domestic inflation. Having a strong SGD policy may not be enough. Perhaps at this point, we may want to think about adapting our monetary policy for a mature economy like ours, focusing on managing our nominal effective exchange rate (NEER) may not be sufficient. We may need to start to focus on other variables, including interest rates and money supply.

Two, business sustainability and internationalisation. It does appear that the Budget seeks to encourage Singapore businesses to venture offshore, be it to tap lower cost facilities and labour or new sources of revenue growth. Whilst this is to be applauded, the facts are not encouraging, especially for SMEs. The National Business Survey for 2011, conducted by the Singapore Business Federation (SBF), showed that businesses are actually looking to scale down their overseas expansion in 2012. The number of companies with an overseas presence has already dropped from 75% to 56%. The main sectors with the biggest drop are: manufacturing -23%; construction and real estate -20%; and trading -10%.

Why? Competition, manpower issues and unclear rules and regulations were cited as key reasons. The volatility of the SGD has not helped either. The SME Development Survey 2011 shows that foreign currency exchange loss is the key challenge for 43% of SMEs. And here is why – there was an 11% spike in the volatility of the SGD, with the standard deviation of the SGD/USD increasing to 0.086 over the last four years, versus 0.077 in the four years before. This spike in volatility has clearly made hedging costs for exporters very high. Furthermore, a continued appreciation of the SGD nominal effective exchange rate, means that overseas profits when repatriated back to Singapore, are worth less in SGD, again compressing margins. Hence, a Budget for the future may need to address some of these challenges faced by local companies as they venture abroad, especially SMEs.

If we are to face a new economic growth model based on innovative thinking and collaborative action, then the old linear way of thinking is no longer sufficient.

At this point, allow me to introduce the House to the Collage Method, a non-linear method of learning that has just been piloted in Singapore. My family actually experienced the Collage model of learning first-hand as my son's school had a team of very brave, forward-thinking teachers, and my son, when he was in Primary 3, was fortunate enough to be picked as a guinea pig. As a mother, I was delighted with the Collage experience and I really hope that more can benefit from it.

So, what is Collage? It is about collaborative teaching and learning. It is about the intertwining of different disciplines, helping children to think for themselves. And it is this connectivity of ideas that make a big difference. Let me share with Members an example where the children were asked to make kites. As they made kites, they learnt Math – angles and triangles. And then they painted the designs on their kites and they learnt art. Several Members have asked about this, including Dr Amy Khor, Mr Seng Han Thong, Mr Lim Biow Chuan and Mr Pritam Singh. There are, in particular, questions on whether this is a subsidy to the transport operators and their shareholders.

The Government considered its investment to expand bus capacity very carefully. This was not a normal budgetary decision. It is an important step, to reduce waiting times and crowdedness that Singaporeans experience daily as they take public buses.

Let me start by explaining what the Public Transport Operators (PTOs) themselves are responsible for. The PTOs are required to expand their fleets, their bus fleets to cater to the growth in passenger numbers, as well as to ensure that they meet the service levels that are mandated under the existing regulatory framework. This is why, in addition to operating the existing bus fleet of about 4,000 buses, they will have to buy 250 additional buses to achieve this; and they will fund this on their own.

The Government is putting $1.1 billion into the bus system in order to step up bus service levels beyond the current service standards that are required of the PTOs.

It will increase bus capacity on existing heavily-utilised routes making them less crowded and giving commuters a more pleasant journey.

Almost all feeder buses will run every 10 minutes or less – for two hours in the morning, and the evening, two-hour peak periods instead of the one-hour peak period under the current service level requirements.

Commuters on existing routes will thus benefit both from shorter waiting times and less crowded bus journeys.

And a number of new bus services, in addition to existing bus services, will be added to improve connectivity, and provide commuters with more public transport choices.

So these are improvements that commuters have been hoping for and waiting for.

As a condition for the Government's investment, the PTOs will have to deliver on these service level improvements, which go beyond existing regulatory requirements.

We cannot simply mandate that the PTOs add these 550 buses to improve service levels. First, because it goes significantly beyond the current service levels in the current regulatory framework. Second, the PTOs' bus operations are already running operating losses, and the 550 additional buses, in particular, are projected to be a loss-making operation. The cost of acquiring and running the 550 buses is beyond what can be recovered from revenues in running the buses.

Take the example of improving the frequency of feeder bus services. Increasing the number of buses to make this possible will shorten waiting times but will add little to revenues, since the total number of passengers taking the service will remain largely the same. So, more buses, more drivers, more fuel but no similar increase in revenues because it is essentially the same pool of passengers. They now have to wait shorter times and enjoy less crowded journeys, but it is essentially the same pool.

Without the Government stepping in, these significant service level improvements would only have been achievable if fares have been raised sharply. The 550 additional buses mean significantly higher costs – not only to purchase the buses, but also because more than 1,000 drivers would need to be hired and paid a good wage, as Mr Seng Han Thong and NMP Mary Liew have pointed out. Fare revenues of the PTOs would have had to go up by about 12% to 13% – which translates into an increase in passenger fares of about 15 cents per journey – if the PTOs had to achieve this on their own. The $1.1 billion package is expected to cover the losses on the 550 buses – in other words, the additional costs, net of revenues. Of the $1.1 billion package, $280 million is budgeted for the purchase of the 550 buses over the next five years, and $820 million to cover the net operating costs over the 10 years. This is based on the best estimates currently. However, we will be monitoring and scrutinising the PTOs' actual costs for both the purchase and running of the buses. Should their losses turn out to be lower than expected, the Government funding will be reduced correspondingly. So, one way or another, there are no profits to be made from the 550 buses.

The $1.1 billion for additional buses complements the $60 billion we are putting into the expansion of the rail system. It will take several years for these new rail lines to come on stream or all of them to come on stream. That is why we are stepping in now to add bus capacity and quickly improve the daily experience of commuters. It is what commuters want, and we have assessed that it is worth the public investment. Mr Chairman, apart from youth crime, we also share Mr Hawazi Daipi and Dr Intan Azura's concerns about drug abuse among youths. Before going into the details, let me begin with the broader drug situation. Firstly, the global and regional trends. Drug use continues to be a serious global problem. On the supply side, drug traffickers and organised criminals form a complex web of transnational networks, fuelling a global criminal enterprise and escalating demand for drugs.

Closer to home, Southeast Asia has seen soaring production, trafficking and consumption of amphetamine-type stimulants, such as Ice and Ecstasy, and a resurgence in opium cultivation and heroin trafficking. In fact, from 2010 to 2011, seizures of heroin and methamphetamine or "Ice" by the Central Narcotics Bureau increased by 48% and 151% respectively.

Secondly, domestic trends. Such developments pose a serious threat to our stability and security. The drug abuse situation is an area of concern. Drug abusers arrested is on a rising trend, increasing from 2,537 abusers arrested in 2008 to 3,265 abusers arrested last year. Malays and Chinese made up the majority of those arrested last year, at 48% and 33% respectively. Of the arrests in 2011, repeat abusers made up two-thirds of those arrested and new abusers the remaining one-third. While the numbers are much lower compared to the peak of the drug problem in the 1970s, we should not be complacent, and instead take decisive action before the situation worsens.

Looking ahead, we are particularly concerned about the large number of about 3,000 repeat abusers expected to be released from Long-Term Imprisonment (LT) from 2012 to 2014. LT abusers are often more likely to relapse into drug abuse. Based on the 2009 cohort, the two-year relapse rate of LT abusers was 36%, compared to 24% for the overall cohort.

Their drug addiction not only harms them, and as Mr Hawazi inferred, inflicts pain on others as well. To feed this expensive habit, many repeat drug abusers turn to crime, including drug trafficking. Indeed, 79% of those in prison have a drug antecedent, and more than 95% of those in long-term imprisonment for drug consumption have some form of criminal antecedents. Thus, the large number of releases over the next few years could result in more arrests in the future as well. To address this, we have allocated additional manpower to CNB and the Prison Service to step up supervision and aftercare of those being released from long-term imprisonment.

On young abusers, we share Mr Hawazi's concern that repeat abusers pose the danger of contaminating others within society. Given repeat drug abusers' close networks with drug suppliers and other drug abusers in Singapore, they can easily influence others, especially young and vulnerable persons, to experiment with drugs.
