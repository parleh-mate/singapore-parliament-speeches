ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

RETRENCHMENT SITUATION AND PROJECTION OF JOB CREATION


    1. Mr Yeo Guat Kwang asked the Minister for Manpower whether he can give an update on the retrenchment situation and projection of job creation for this year.

    The Minister for Manpower (Dr Lee Boon Yang): Mr Speaker, Sir, a total of 3,248 workers were retrenched in the first quarter of this year. In the second quarter, preliminary findings of our survey on private sector establishments with at least 25 workers show that some 5,600 workers were affected by retrenchments. Based on voluntary notifications by companies to the Ministry, the number of workers affected by retrenchments in the third quarter would be higher than the figures registered in the first and second quarters. For the whole of this year, the estimated number of workers who may be retrenched could reach 20,000. This is significantly higher than the figure of 11,624 recorded last year but is lower than the figure of 29,086 retrenched in 1998.

    Total employment in the first quarter of this year rose by 23,210, seasonally lower than the 35,066 in the last quarter of 2000. However, based on changes in employment up to May and forecast of economic conditions ahead, total employment for this year is likely to be flat or slightly negative as against a sharp employment gain of 108,500 in 2000. Unemployment is expected to rise to around 4% by year end. However, the forecast of annual average

SECTORS FACING SHORTAGE OF WORKERS


    2. Mr Yeo Guat Kwang asked the Minister for Manpower (a) whether there are sectors currently facing a shortage of workers and, if so, which are the sectors; and (b) how Singaporean workers can be given assistance to qualify for jobs in these sectors.

    Dr Lee Boon Yang: Mr Speaker, Sir, the employment situation, as I have just mentioned earlier, has deteriorated over the past few months due to the economic downturn. The construction and manufacturing sectors have been badly hit, with net employment declining by 2,160 and 1,075 respectively, in the first quarter of this year. In contrast, the services sector has continued to register positive employment growth of 28,200 over the same period. Job vacancies were highest in the transport, storage and communication industries, which saw nearly 3,000 job openings in the first quarter of this year. Other services industries such as financial services, wholesale and retail trade also had high job vacancies.

    While the situation may have worsened since then, there are still job opportunities. According to industry feedback in recent months, there continue to be job vacancies in the services sector. For instance, there is a shortage of housekeepers, receptionists, waiters/waitresses and cooks in the hotel and restaurant sector; ambulance attendants, healthcare assistants in the healthcare sector; and childcare assistants in the education

There are also vacancies for network analysts and software developers in the infocomm sector; actuaries and underwriters in the finance sector; and engineers and technicians in the pharmaceutical and aerospace industries.

    While manufacturing industries such as the electronics industry are cutting jobs, there are still jobs available in the services sector. However, the skill sets required in the services sector may be different. To help our older and lower skilled retrenched workers make the switch to new jobs, we started a pilot programme called the 'People for Jobs' Traineeship Programme in June this year. This is like an apprentice programme for mid-career workers. Under this programme, my Ministry provides financial support of up to $3.80 per hour or $600 per month for 3 months for companies which employ the older retrenched workers. This will encourage employers to recruit retrenched workers and ease their transition to a new industry and work environment. To date, more than 30 companies from various industries like the hotel, retail, security service sectors have already signed on with this programme. They have made available 1,000 vacancies for room attendants, sales assistants, cashiers, security officers and others.

    In addition, to help Singaporeans better qualify for the available positions, my Ministry works closely with relevant organisations to enhance the short and long term re-employment prospects of retrenched workers through training.     4. Mr Sin Boon Ann asked the Minister for Health whether his Ministry will consider reviewing the guidelines to include the use of Medisave for annual medical check-ups.

    The Minister for Health (Mr Lim Hng Kiang): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Medisave scheme is primarily designed to help individuals meet their personal or immediate family's hospitalisation expenses, especially during old age. However, over the years, we have extended Medisave to certain outpatient treatment, such as chemotherapy.

    My Ministry has considered extending the use of Medisave for health screening, including annual medical check-ups, and decided against it. First, there are many types of health screening, of which many are not cost effective. Second, for certain cost-effective health screenings, such as mammography and pap smear, my Ministry has already made them affordable by providing heavy subsidies at the polyclinics. Also, we have introduced the Community Health Screening Programme for the elderly to detect diabetes, hypertension and high blood cholesterol. Again, the cost is minimal, as the elderly pays up to $5 for the screening which costs around $20. Therefore, Singaporeans need not rely on Medisave to pay for cost-effective health screening.

    The Home Affairs Uniformed Services (HUS) plays a key role in meeting public safety and security needs. The presence of capable Home Affairs Uniformed Services officers will help to maintain a stable environment needed to attract investments.

    To ensure that the HUS is able to execute its roles in meeting public security and safety needs, we need to attract and retain good officers to join the Home Affairs Uniformed Services. It is with this objective in mind that the Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) commissioned a study by an international consultancy firm to review the Home Affairs Uniformed Services career structures and systems in April 2000.

    The Home Affairs Uniformed Services Career Review aimed to restructure the Home Affairs Uniformed Services career to ensure that it is well positioned to meet future public security and safety needs. It studied the career structure, pay and benefits package, career management and performance management of the HUS officers.

    The Government has accepted the recommendation to introduce a super-

annuation scheme for HUS officers. The scheme is known as the INVEST Plan. The Plan will replace the Contract scheme that is currently open to Home Affairs Uniformed Services officers in the Civil Defence, Prisons, Narcotics Service, as well as the Police (Junior) Officers Service. Officers on the Full CPF Scheme in the above services will also participate in the INVEST Plan. They will continue to receive Full CPF contributions and will also receive additional contributions credited into the INVEST Plan to build up a superannuation fund to help them transit out of service at the age of 50 or 55. The Plan will also replace the Pension scheme of the Police (Senior) Officers Service. The Plan will apply to all new Home Affairs Uniformed Services officers from 1st October 2001. Existing Home Affairs Uniformed Services officers are given an option to convert to the INVEST Plan and have the accumulated benefits under their existing schemes transferred to the new Plan. Let me explain the rationale of the INVEST Plan.

    The retirement age for new Home Affairs Uniformed Services officers will be lowered by 5 years, in recognition of the rigorous requirements of the Home Affairs Uniformed Services. Being in a uniformed organisation, HUS officers are required to be physically fit, agile and alert when discharging their day-to-day duties. For instance, Police officers need to be agile and fit to perform patrolling duties, which include physically strenuous tasks like pursuing criminals. Civil Defence officers perform operational duties such as fire-fighting and rescue, emergency ambulance and hazardous materials mitigation, which require considerable physical effort. Similar exacting physical fitness and mental alertness requirements apply to officers in the Prisons and Narcotics schemes of service.

    In addition, the increasing civilianisation and outsourcing of non-core functions will reduce the number of desk-bound administrative jobs. This in turn reduces the traditional channel used to hold older officers who are no longer suited for rigorous, frontline work, till they retire. For example, the number of desk-bound

administrative jobs in the Police force will decrease to about 10 percent in the future.

    Thus from 1st October 2001, all new officers joining the Home Affairs Uniformed Services will retire at age 50 if they retire as Junior Officers, and at age 55 if they retire as Senior Officers. Serving officers can choose to opt into the new scheme. The INVEST Plan is to help these officers transit out of service into their second career. Similar to the SAVER and Premium Plans that have been introduced in the Singapore Armed Forces (SAF), the INVEST Plan offers reasonable superannuation terms to uniformed officers who have to retire earlier than other civil servants.

    The Ministry of Home Affairs has also taken the opportunity to replace the Pension scheme for Police Senior Officers with the INVEST Plan. This is because our review found that a plan which builds up officers' retirement benefits through making monthly contributions into the officers' accounts during the course of their careers, such as the INVEST Plan, is better able to meet the needs of Police Senior Officers, as compared to one where officers receive a fixed quantum on retirement, such as the Pension scheme. This is also to make it consistent with our Senior Officers in the other Home Affairs Uniformed Services Schemes. We will then have uniformity in treatment, instead of differential treatment for the Home Affairs Senior Officers.

    The mechanism of the INVEST Plan is similar to the SAF's SAVER and Premium Plans. Under the INVEST Plan, the Home Affairs Ministry will contribute an amount based on a percentage of the officer's monthly salary into an INVEST Retirement Account kept for the officer. It is 7 3/4% for Junior Officers and 6% for Senior Officers, starting from the 7th year of service. Because of the additional component to replace pension, Police Senior Officers will get a higher contribution rate, that is 11 3/4% from the first year of service.

The payments equal one month of salary for each year of service during the first 6 years. In the 7th to 10th year, the payments rise to 1 1/2 months of salary for each year of service.

    The funds in the officers' Retirement and Retention accounts will be pooled to form an investment fund known as the INVEST Fund. The INVEST Fund will be managed by professional fund managers. Based on returns from investments, dividends will be credited into the individual accounts of each officer. An INVEST Board of Trustees, chaired by the Permanent Secretary, and comprising senior Home Affairs Ministry officials and people with relevant experience from the public and private sectors, will oversee the fund managers.

If the officer resigns prematurely, the Ministry will retain part of the amount in the officer's accounts. The officer can only withdraw the full amount in his accounts if he serves a full career with the Home Affairs Uniformed Services, when he retires at age 50 or 55 for Junior and Senior Officers respectively. The Ministry expects the progressive withdrawal feature of the INVEST Plan to enhance retention, thus helping the Ministry to attract good quality officers to join and remain with the Home Affairs Uniformed Services for a full career.
