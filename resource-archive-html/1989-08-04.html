TITANIUM DIOXIDE PLANT IN TUAS
(Risk of environmental pollution)


    2. Mr Goh Chee Wee asked the Minister for the Environment whether his Ministry carries out a thorough evaluation of the risk of environmental pollution before companies are allowed to set up plants in Singapore; and if he will comment on the setting up of a titanium dioxide plant in Tuas whose production process has generated much controversy in certain countries because of the potential hazard to health and the environment.

     The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for the Environment (Mr Eugene Yap Giau Cheng) (for the Minister for the Environment): On behalf of my Minister, Sir.

    Before companies are allowed to set up plants in Singapore, the Ministry of the Environment thoroughly evaluates the risk of environmental pollution. This was done for the titanium dioxide plant in Tuas.

    Titanium dioxide is a non-toxic, inert material that is used as a high quality white pigment in paints, printing inks, plastics, rubber, synthetic fibres and cosmetics.

    There are two basic types of processes used to manufacture titanium dioxide. The first is the sulphate process which the Tuas plant does not use. The sulphate process uses large quantities of sulphuric acid to produce titanium dioxide from low grade titanium dioxide ores. This process generates about 9(r) tons of acid wastes and iron salts for every tonne of titanium dioxide produced. Such a large amount of waste is very expensive to treat and the plants which use this process usually discharge the waste into the sea. The wastes will acidify the sea water and discolour it red. This process has therefore generated much controversy in countries with plants using this process.

Column: 411



    The titanium dioxide plant in Tuas uses the chloride process which is much cleaner. It also refines high grade, not low grade, titanium dioxide ore. As a result, less waste is generated. Only about 20 to 40 kg of wastes such as unrecovered titanium dioxide, iron oxides, coke and alumina, are generated for every tonne of titanium dioxide produced. These wastes are non-toxic and they are safely disposed of by landfill.

    The chloride process, however, uses chlorine which is a toxic gas. To avoid transporting chlorine on the roads, the Tuas plant generates chlorine on-site. The amount of chlorine stored in the plant at any time has been kept to a minimum. In addition, the plant has installed equipment to collect and reduce chlorine emissions to safe levels in case of a leak from the chlorine tank.

    Besides having a cleaner process and using only high grade ore, the plant at Tuas has also taken all the necessary measures to comply with our standards on air and water pollution.

    As for other industrial premises, regular inspections are being made to ensure all pollution control equipment installed at the plant are correctly operated and adequately maintained. No emissions exceeding our standards have been detected.

     Mr Goh Chee Wee (Boon Lay): Sir, can I ask the Senior Parliamentary Secretary whether the Government has ever turned down any potential investment based on environmental considerations?

     Mr Eugene Yap Giau Cheng: Yes, we have disallowed a few that were known to have pollution hazards. In fact, as a matter of interest, in the early eighties, another investor approached us to produce titanium dioxide using the sulphate process and we had turned it down for the reasons which I have just stated.

WRITTEN ANSWER TO QUESTION
MEDINET SYSTEM


    1. Dr Aline K. Wong asked the Acting Minister for Health what kinds of information will be stored in the MEDINET system; who will be its major users; and what safeguards for confidentiality will be built into the system.

     Mr Yeo Cheow Tong: MediNet is a proposed computer network linking public and the private sector participants in the health care delivery system. The objectives of MediNet are to reduce the cost and turnaround time for the preparation, transmission and processing of health care information, and to facilitate a better understanding of the utilisation of health services, and to enable faster access to health information. This would contribute to higher productivity, better patient care and help slow down cost increases.

    A MediNet Steering Committee, comprising representatives from government and private hospitals, various government agencies, professional and trade associations, has been formed to look into the feasibility, requirement specifications and other issues related to the implementation of a nationwide medical network, including confidentiality and data access rights to sensitive information.

    The kinds of information being considered for storage or processing by MediNet System will include Medisave claims data, health notifications for immunisations and communicable diseases, etc, patients' medical information, drug and surgical supplies data and general medical information databases which will facilitate healthcare planning, patient treatment and research.

    It is envisaged that the major users of the network will include government agencies eg. Ministry of Health, Ministry of the Environment, Ministry of Labour, CPF Board; government and private hospitals, clinics and pharmacies; medical and surgical suppliers; medical associations and research organisations eg. NUS and insurance companies.

    To safeguard confidentiality of data, there will be stringent procedural as well as system controls. Procedural control will include proper and documented work procedures and clear definitions of types of access based on user's job functions and responsibilities. System access control will include use of authorised and predefined terminals, multiple levels of passwords for access and audit trail to enable trace back for subsequent external or internal audit review. Access to patient data will be further safeguarded by an appropriate form of personal authorization.

    The MediNet Steering Committee is expected to complete the studies and submit its recommendations by the end of this year.

TREATMENT AND CHARGES AT THE NATIONAL SKIN CENTRE
