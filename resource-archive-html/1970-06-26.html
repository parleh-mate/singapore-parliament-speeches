ROAD TRAFFIC (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.45 p.m.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Yong Nyuk Lin): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    One of the main objects of this Bill is to provide for more effective action against motor vehicles which emit any smoke or visible vapour so as "to cause

Column: 78

annoyance or danger to the public, or injury or damage to any person or property".

    The present legislation under Rule 104 of the Motor Vehicles (Construction and Use) Amendment Rules, 1963, provides that:

    `No vehicle shall be used which emits any smoke, visible vapour, etc., the emission of which causes or is likely to cause injury or annoyance to any person, or damage to property or which endangers or is likely to endanger the safety of any person'.

    Penalties for the offence of "smoky exhaust" have been dealt with in one of two ways:

    (i) with the Police offering to offending owners of such vehicles a composition fine of $20 under section 79 (1) -of the Road Traffic Ordinance, 1961; or

    (ii) on conviction, to a fine not exceeding $400 and in the case of a second or subsequent conviction to a fine not exceeding $1,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding three months under section 78 (1) of the said Ordinance.

    It is clear, although last year more than 7,000 vehicle owners paid composition fines of $20 each for smoky exhausts, yet the problem has not shown any signs of improvement, that the present legislation will have to be amended so that penalties will be made more deterrent if we are to expect any marked improvement in the situation.

    With the launching of the anti-pollution campaign by the Prime Minister on 31st March, 1970, which, inter alia, has, as a target, the eradication of the smoky exhausts from motor vehicles, this is the opportune time to tighten up on the legislation in this regard.

    It has therefore been proposed, firstly, to raise the limit of the composition fine from $20 to $50, and this is provided for under clause 6 of the Bill.

Column: 79



    Secondly, in introducing the subject of "smoky exhaust" into the body of the Road Traffic Ordinance proper instead of appearing as a minor offence under subsidiary legislation, it emphasizes the importance which the Government attaches to the problem of air pollution and its determination to resolve it, with firm measures wherever possible. Clause 3 of the Bill accordingly introduces a new section 28A and it clearly states that both the owner and the driver of a vehicle are guilty of an offence, if such vehicle emits "any smoke or visible vapour".

    As regards penalties, on first conviction, the maximum fine is to be raised from $400 to $500 to bring it in line with the maximum fine for littering. I am sure Members of this House will agree with me that the offence of air pollution has more serious and damaging consequences to the health of the community than littering and, therefore, the raising of the fine is a legitimate one.

    It will be noted that a separate subsection (2) of section 28A ensures that vehicles of the Singapore Traction Company are brought within the scope of the amending legislation for smoky exhausts, as it is obvious that any strong anti- pollution measures to be taken must be made applicable to all motor vehicles operating on Singapore roads, and that there can be no exceptions to the law.

    Members of this House will, I am sure, be interested to know that consequent on Government's decision to raise the taxi fare for the first mile from the present rate of 40 cents to 60 cents with effect from next month, i.e., 1st July, 1970, all 3,800 licensed taxis have been requested to first pass the smoky exhaust test - namely, not to exceed 40 smoke units as shown by the Hartridge Smokemeter. Up to Wednesday, the 24th of this month, a total of 3,166 licensed taxis or more than 80 per cent of taxis were put through the test, but 664 or more than 20 per cent failed it. It is therefore clear that higher standards of maintenance will be necessary if we are to see the last of smoky exhausts from our midst.

Column: 80



    The next important purpose of this amendment Bill is to be found in clause 4, which introduces another new section 29A, wherein it is clearly stated that "on or after the 1st day of January, 1971, every person who drives or is carried on a motor cycle shall wear securely on his head a protective helmet of a type approved by the Minister".

    Members of this House are probably aware that since February this year, all "L" licence holders of scooters and motor cycles are already required to wear approved-type crash helmets, and the proposed incorporation of a new section in the body of the Road Traffic Ordinance proper once again draws attention to the importance and emphasis of Government on the compulsory use of crash helmets. It is hoped that, in this way, the former wanton waste of young lives of our population will be greatly reduced hereafter.

    The manner in which some motorists are causing obstruction or danger to traffic by leaving or abandoning their stalled vehicles on the road whilst they attend to their personal matters first, before attempting to remove their broken-down vehicles, or by parking their vehicles in prohibited areas, necessitates an amendment to section 71. This is to make it clear that the Minister may make rules to provide for the removal and detention of such vehicles. The amendment is made in clause 5 of the Bill.

    At the same time, the opportunity is also taken to amend the existing definition of "unladen weight" in section 2 of the Ordinance. The new definition will enable the Registrar of Vehicles to prosecute offenders successfully. This amendment is made in clause 2 of the Bill.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, direction of employment is not something new. This Bill is to give effect to the principle I have enunciated and to the undertaking we have given.

    There is already a Job Placement Department in the Central Manpower Base in the Ministry of the Interior and Defence. It has been assisting our discharged national servicemen in finding employment suitable to the skills they have learnt and the training they have received while serving with the Singapore Armed Forces. Wherever possible, discharged national servicemen are placed in jobs relevant to their abilities, skills and experience. This is very important as, upon completion of full- rime national service, they are transferred to the Reserves which form the bulk of our Armed Forces in an emergency and, therefore, a continuity in experience and skill is necessary. I must say we have been most successful in job placement. A total of 98 per cent of those discharged in 1969 have found jobs. In fact, at times we cannot cope with the demand from employers for

Column: 88

discharged national servicemen. I understand that most of the national servicemen who have had some technical training in the Armed Forces are offered jobs by prospective employers even before they have completed their national service. For this happy state of affairs, I must express our gratitude to the first few hundreds of discharged national servicemen who have proved to employers that they are better than average workers and are well disciplined and not afraid of hard work.

    This Bill will now empower the Minister to direct by order published in Government Gazettes that specified categories of employers shall not, without the consent of the Director of Employment appointed under this Bill, engage or re-engage any employee otherwise than from among registered persons. A registered person is a person who has completed full-time national service and whose name is for the time being on the register to be kept by the Director of Employment.

    Under this Bill, a register will be kept wherein particulars relating to the qualifications and skills of such discharged servicemen will be recorded. By this means, there will be made available to employers a reservoir of manpower resources consisting of a representative cross- section of our young population possessed of a variety of skills and experience from which they can draw to meet their needs.

    If an employer is unable to find a suitable person from among the discharged national servicemen to fill any vacancy in his concern, he may, upon the presentation of reasonable grounds, be exempted to fill such vacancy with non-national servicemen.

    Discharged national servicemen should, within seven days upon securing employment, notify the authority of this fact so that correct statistics will be maintained; otherwise, they will disqualify themselves and will not be given priority of employment.

    Any employer who contravenes the provisions of any order made under the Bill will be guilty of an offence punishable, on conviction, by a fine not
