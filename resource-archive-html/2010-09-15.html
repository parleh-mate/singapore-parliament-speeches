ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

YOUTH OLYMPIC GAMES

(Impact, expenditure and future plans)

     The following Question stood in the name of Mr Zaqy Mohamad –

 

     1.  To ask the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what is the final amount spent for the inaugural Youth Olympics Games (YOG); (b) why is the actual amount spent much more than initially budgeted; (c) what are the factors that contributed to this increase; (d) how does this compare with other Games conducted on a similar scale; (e) how does the Ministry rate its performance on the organisation of the YOG; and (f) what returns can Singapore expect from the amount spent on the programmes, talent, infrastructure and volunteers for YOG.

     2.  Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports if he will provide his assessment of the Youth Olympic Games (YOG) and its legacy to Singapore, in particular how much was spent on the YOG and what are the short-term and long-term benefits of this public investment to Singapore and Singaporeans.

     3.  Mr Zaqy Mohamad asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what are the Ministry's plans to further develop Singapore's sports industry following the successful organisation of the inaugural Youth Olympic Games; (b) what are the new infrastructure and programmes that the Ministry has planned to build a sporting culture involving as many Singaporeans as possible; and (c) what measures are in place to better optimise the sporting facilities in schools and other organisations for public use.

     4.  Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports what efforts will be made to develop our young athletes further to the highest levels and to nurture a sporting culture in our schools and in the wider community.

     5.  Mr Zaqy Mohamad asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what are the plans to further develop the talents who have shown potential at the Youth Olympic Games and to ensure that these talents continue to develop and perform as they progress to national teams; and (b) how does the Ministry plan to manage the sporting careers of young male athletes during their period of National Service and to ensure that they are still able to compete at the highest level internationally and not lose time or motivation.

     6.  Ms Sylvia Lim asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) why is the final budget for the Youth Olympic Games more than triple the original estimate; (b) where were the additional funds drawn from; (c) whether any of his Ministry programmes were cut back for this purpose; and (d) what was the amount paid for the tickets purchased by the Ministry of Education and whether this was also part of the YOG budget.

     The Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (Dr Vivian Balakrishnan): Mr Speaker, may I have your permission to take Question Nos. 1 – 6 together?

     Mr Speaker: Yes.

     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan: I thank Members for the many questions on the Youth Olympic Games (YOG). This is the largest international multi-sport event we have

     First and foremost, credit should go to the workforce, the 30,000 volunteers and staff of the Singapore Youth Olympic Games Organising Committee (SYOGOC), and many others from Government and non-Government agencies. Special commendation needs to go to the volunteers stationed at the frontline, running the competitions, greeting the visitors or showing them around, as well as many others working tirelessly in the background. Our volunteers have proven unequivocally that Singaporeans are gracious and hospitable. Our volunteers have shown that Singapore is capable of welcoming the world, with style, grace, cheerfulness and efficiency.

 

     Next, the performance of our Team Singapore athletes at the YOG is something that all Singaporeans can be proud of. It is testament to our Singapore system that our young athletes are capable of competing head-to-head with the best in the world. Their determination and fighting spirit, both in claiming victory as well as in accepting defeat, captured the imagination of Singaporeans. It is this confidence and self belief that was on display that is crucial for our future, not just in sports but indeed for all our endeavours as a nation.

 

     The YOG has also helped to rekindle spectator interest in sports. I would like to thank all the supporters who turned up at

     Last but not least, I would like to pay tribute to our local companies involved in supporting the YOG, either as sponsors or service providers. They did Singapore proud by providing a very high quality level of service and top-notched infrastructure for the Games.      Let me now discuss the budget. The total Government outlay for the YOG would come up to S$387 million. Approval for this expenditure was obtained from Cabinet, and additional funds provided by the Ministry of Finance. Consequently, it was not necessary to cut back on any of the other MCYS programmes in order for us to host these Games.

 

     Our initial budget estimates during the bid phase were clearly inaccurate. We got it wrong. Let me explain how this came about. This was the first ever Youth Olympic Games in the world. Quite frankly, both the International Olympic Committee (IOC) and us were starting from scratch. We, therefore, underestimated the requirements and consequential costs for several major functional areas which were necessary to host these Games. In fact, the finalised specifications and standards for the YOG events were only determined after we won the bid to host the Games. And if I may add, these were in fact, determined very late in the preparations for the Games. Each event was pegged at World

Youth Championship level and all 26 International Federations representing each Olympic sport had to approve the detailed specifications.

 

     To put things in context, we should look at the two most recent Olympic Games, as well as the next. The YOG that we hosted was about one-third the scale of the Beijing Summer Olympic Games in 2008 which featured 11,000 athletes. We had 3,600 athletes. Our projected expenditure for YOG was about 10% of Beijing's reported operating expenditure (S$3.9 billion, which excluded capital expenditure). So, one-third the size, but our spending was only 10% of Beijing's expenditure.

 

     In fact, if we compared against the Vancouver Winter Olympic Games 2010, we had more athletes participating in YOG than in the Winter Olympics.  But our expenditure would likely amount to only about 20% of what Vancouver spent (S$2.26 billion). If we look forward to two years from now, the reported provisional budget for the 2012 London Summer Olympics now stands at S$19.2 billion (9.3 billion pounds), including infrastructure.  The point is, we spent a very small fraction of the expenditure which would be incurred in larger Olympic Games. This is despite the fact that we had one-third the number of athletes and that all the detailed specifications for infrastructure were pitched at world junior championship standard.

 

     In the interest of transparency, let me go down one level in resolution and reveal more details for the budget. The budget consists of several key components. In the area of technology, the projected cost was about S$97 million. The Games were pitched at World Junior Championship standards.  We, therefore, had to provide a highly accurate scoring system as well as a  sophisticated computer system to disseminate and diffuse results instantly and "live" across 18 different competition

The system was also used to manage all the workforce data and the accreditation of all the nearly 370,000 visitors to 44 Games venues. We spent a lot on technology but the point I am making is that this was a state-of-the-art highly sophisticated system.

 

     The next item was the upgrading of our sports venues and equipment needed to host the Games. This amounted to about S$76 million. These were essential to fulfil the requirements specified by the IOC and the International Federations, as well as to allow the Games to be broadcast "live" to the world, and, perhaps even more importantly, for these same venues to subsequently be used by Singaporeans after the Games.

 

     Providing "live" broadcasts through multiple media as well as broadcasting the Opening and Closing Ceremonies that displayed Singapore's finest – both in terms of sports and creativity – accounted for another S$45.5 million. This enabled us to profile Singapore to a potential global audience of over 2.1 billion people. These are not my estimates. The IOC informed me of these estimates. They signed contracts with over 180 international broadcasters who then had rights to feed the sporting events as well as the Opening and Closing Ceremonies to their respective areas.

 

     For the same reason, we also set aside S$7 million for the Journey of the Youth Olympic Flame through the five continents before it arrived in Singapore.  Initially, the IOC was not keen on having an International Torch Relay because of the controversy that surrounded the Torch Relay during the Beijing Olympics.  However because this was the first ever Youth Olympic Games, and I think also because they had confidence in us being able to organise this and to get through

     The YOG was a major logistics operation. Members would really need to go to the back of the house or to go to the warehouse at Changi to understand the mega logistics requirements. Logistics – which also included supply chain management, venue logistics, cleaning, waste management and transport – cost us about S$44 million.

 

     The next critical area was security. Unfortunately, people still remember Munich for the wrong reasons, when athletes were killed.  In our case, we had to do our best to ensure the safety of athletes, dignitaries and visitors.  Security was a priority and people expected that of Singapore.  Auxilary police and screening equipment were available that ensured that detailed security checks could be done, and yet, done without causing a lockjam, and done in a less intrusive way. Security cost us about S$18 million.

 

     S$14.3 million went to the operational requirements such as language translation and interpretation, laundry, catering and other services at the Youth Olympic Village.  Members need to understand that there were 3,600 athletes and another 1,700 officials from all over the world, speaking a wide variety of languages with great individual and customised demands. This was another major operation in its own right.

 

     The Culture and Education Programme (CEP), which was a new and unique feature of our Games, accounted for another S$5.4 million. But through the CEP, the IOC hoped – and they are now convinced that they achieved the

     On the revenue side, our merchandise and ticket sales are expected to bring in about S$7 million. Why so low? Because we charged very low prices.  I felt it was more important to ensure that we had made the Games accessible and affordable to Singaporeans rather than to maximise revenues. We received another S$60 million worth of sponsorships in kind from various companies and another S$7.6 million in cash sponsorship from companies like Samsung, ATOS, Panasonic and DBS.

 

     Overall, the YOG was certainly not cheap. When it became clear during the course of the preparations that our initial estimates were wrong, I was confronted with two pertinent questions. First – if we had known that it would cost S$387 million, would we still have bid for the Games? The second question – if we spent S$387 million, will we get value from that expenditure?

 

     In my mind, the answer to both these questions was "Yes".  I was able to convince my Cabinet colleagues that the answer to both these questions was "Yes". This was why approval was given for us to spend this money to host these Games.

 

     There was another alternative.  I could have just blindly cut the budget and delivered sub-standard Games.  I do not think that would have been the right decision. I do not think it would have been fair to our athletes, to the visiting athletes, to the IOC and, most importantly, I do not think it would have been fair to Singaporeans because at the end of the day, you would want to host something which would be a source of

Now that the Games are over, our main focus has to be to make sure that the full benefits of organising and hosting these Games for Singapore are reaped. I want to thank the many Members in this House as well as many Singaporeans who had emailed me over the course of this journey over the last two-and-half years their suggestions and ideas on both organising the Games as well as how to maximise the legacy from the Games.

 

     Let me spend a few minutes to talk about international branding because this is one of those key intangibles that accrues to an event like this.

 

     Singapore will always be remembered as the country that hosted the world's first ever Youth Olympic Games. This is a rare honour.  It is rare because there can only be one first host.  If I ask Members to name the first host of the modern Summer Olympics, everyone will always remember Greece, Athens in 1896.  If I ask Members to name the first host of the Winter Olympics, some here may remember France – Chamonix, 86 years ago. We are now in that same position; the first ever host.  On all subsequent Olympic flags which are transferred to the host city, at the bottom right corner of that flag is embroidered "Singapore 2010".  This will be a permanent legacy.  It ensures that we will remain on the international community's radar screens.  The next time anyone is making an investment decision, or are trying to decide where to site an international or regional headquarters, or deciding to expand their businesses overseas, they would have heard of or they may have visited us, and they would know that we stand for certain quality and reliability.

 

     The YOG has therefore helped to effectively and positively profile Singapore to an international audience. Before, everyone knew that we are a safe and efficient city, but now everyone knows

that we are a beautiful, vibrant, dynamic, exciting, creative global city where people from all over the world will want to visit, live, work, play or invest in.

 

     So, we have to do it well, and to do it in a way that the world will sit up and take notice of us. The global viewership of the Games and the Opening and Closing Ceremonies has certainly helped to brand and feature Singapore over the 14 days. According to the IOC, the Games were watched by over two billion viewers. The IOC also reported that because of our heavy use of new media, the Youth Olympic Games Channel, the videos that were downloaded from YouTube – people's individual screens – exceeded five million times.  That is double the downloads that the Vancouver Winter Olympics enjoyed.  And at one point, the Youth Olympic Games Channel was the most watched YouTube channel worldwide.  I can guarantee you that these are not achievements which come easily.

 

     Let me now turn to the contributions to the local economy.  The YOG provided considerable benefits for our local businesses. Of the overall budget of S$387 million, S$260 million worth of local contracts went to our local companies, which is about 70% of our projected expenditure.  And another S$46 million went to overseas companies with local subsidiaries in Singapore. We hope that both our local companies as well as local subsidiaries of overseas companies will now leverage on their experience providing the services to further their own business interests and also to compete for other international projects that will arise all over the world.

 

     The Singapore Tourism Board had earlier estimated that the YOG will bring in some S$57 million in additional tourism receipts.      Let me now turn to other benefits, specifically with respect to sports culture.  It has laid  a very strong foundation for building our sports culture, particularly in the areas of spectatorship, community involvement and, just as important, volunteer engagement.  The officials from the Singapore Sports Council (SSC), National Sports Associations (NSAs) and the volunteers were really the key people behind the Games.  Our officials played a critical role in the preparations of our athletes as well. With this experience and the knowledge, and the networks that they have formed, Singaporeans – officials, volunteers and staff – are now better equipped than ever before to host international world class sports events as well as to prepare our athletes to compete at the highest level. SSC and the People's Association will continue to engage the pool of YOG volunteers so that they can offer their experience and expertise for future sporting and community events.

 

     If Members do not mind, I cannot help but again highlight the fantastic performance of our volunteers.  I had never seen people so passionately, so tirelessly, so enthusiastically going through their work, despite the fact that they were not being paid, and even the food was not necessarily the best in the world.  They did so, not for my sake, certainly not for the Government's sake, but because they were proud of Singapore, and in their own quiet and effective ways, they wanted to do their bit to put Singapore on the map to show the world what a wonderful home Singapore is.  That was, to me, a real life example of the Singapore spirit.  And, quite frankly, at the end of this exercise, I ended up more confident about the future because I saw the Singapore spirit.
