WATER SUPPLY IN JURONG
(Salt content)


    1. Mr Ho Kah Leong asked the Minister for National Development and Communications if he will ascertain from the Public Utilities Board whether the Board is aware that the potable water supplied to Jurong residents has an excessive salt content and whether the Board will subject the potable water to a desalination process before supplying it to Jurong residents.

    The Minister for National Development and Communications (Mr Lim Kim San): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Public Utilities Board is aware that potable water supplied to the non-industrial area of Jurong has a higher chloride or salt content than the Board's other supplies. This water comes from the Pandan Reservoir which was completed in February 1975.

    The water stored in the Pandan Reservoir flows through an industrialised and populated area and is therefore subject to pollution. To make it safe for human use, it goes through a more sophisticated and costly process at the new water treatment works at Choa Chu Kang which was completed in October 1975.

    The World Health Organisation Drinking Water Standards allow for a maximum chloride content of 600 parts per million and a Total Dissolved Solids content of 1.500 parts per million. Our record at the Water Treatment Works shows that the chloride content of water from the Pandan Reservoir

Column: 1008

treated at the Choa Chu Kang Water Treatment Works has a chloride content of not more than 200 parts per million and a Total Dissolved Solids content of not more than 400 parts per million.

    The water supplied from the Choa Chu Kang Water Treatment Works is well within the WHO Drinking Water Standards and is perfectly safe for potable use. The Public Utilities Board therefore does not envisage the installation of a desalination plant to further process this water before supplying it to the residents.

    The problem of sea pollution, especially by oil, has been a subject of international concern. Over the last few years more and more efforts have been taken by ports and various authorities in different parts of the world to keep the seas and oceans around them clean and free of pollution.

    The situation in Singapore is no exception. Situated at a focal point of international shipping, the Port of Singapore last year recorded some 40,426 vessel arrivals and departures giving a total of 205.2 million net registered tons of shipping.

    As Singapore is also a major oil refining centre, a large number of oil tankers, some of them Very Large Crude Carriers, discharge crude oil and load refined petroleum products at the various oil terminals in our port. Therefore, the threat of oil pollution in Singapore waters is a constant one.

    The Port of Singapore Authority is charged with the responsibility of keeping our waters clean. Legislations such as the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea Act, 1971 and the Civil Liability (Oil Pollution) Act, 1 973 have been drawn up to prevent vessels from indiscriminately polluting our waters with oil and rubbish.

    However, legislation in itself does not help the Port of Singapore Authority in a real situation arising out of accident in our port waters. Besides improving the navigability of the sea lanes and the addition of navigational aids, the Port of Singapore Authority has to have available sufficient equipment to combat oil spills and to prevent the oil from polluting our beaches in the event of an accident. To this end, the Port of Singapore Authority has worked tirelessly to acquire anti-pollution equipment and to build anti-oil pollution capabilities into its tugs.
