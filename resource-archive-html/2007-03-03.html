Talents in the Creative Sector

 

     Miss Penny Low (Pasir Ris-Punggol): Sir, I beg to move,

 

     That the total sum to be allocated for Head Q of the Estimates be reduced by $100. 

 

     Sir, in the year since the cultural, capital and Renaissance City Plans were introduced, much buzz has been added to the creative sector.  More recently, other initiatives like the Creative Community Singapore, Design Singapore, Media 21, Renaissance City 2.0 and even the iN2015 plans collectively contributed to the making of the creative ecosystem.  MICA deserves praise in this respect.  In fact, the value-add per worker is amongst the highest and its economic pie has been growing exponentially.  To attract, groom and retain creative talents, generate innovation and stimulate economic growth, there must be substantial attention paid to providing a holistic and conducive environment for creative juices. 

 

     I list four essential elements for the Minister's consideration.

 

     One, environment.  Be they artists or technologists, film makers or fashion designers, creative talents need centres of creativity in the places that they work, play and live in.  Diversity adds colour and vigour to the process.  Talent favours open-minded and connected environment where they can fit in easily.  Cities that offer places where talents can share and exchange ideas and even places such as cafes, artists markets, cultural belts will help to integrate newcomers into a community and strengthen the existing community.  Would the Minister consider upgrading current arts housing schemes, especially those at Telok Kurau and the Performing Arts Centre at Telok Ayer, which are rather run down currently.  What about supporting even a dance centre or the likes of a centre like in Paris.  I mean the quality of place counts?

 

     Two, innovation.  We need to support new ideas and put a premium on innovation.  The emphasis on a high trust economy and patents should be extended to the creative and artistic works and not only be focused on traditional hi-tech industries.  This will spell greater growth in original works and entrepreneurial activities. 

 

     Three, recognition.  The need to be recognised.  They need to be recognised by what they do more than who they work for.  Due to the nature of their work and the industry, many may fall out of the traditional definitions of formal employment or full-time job.  They have little or no CPF or employment benefits or protection, even Medisave, and some move out of the creative sector after some years.  I think that is quite a waste.  To retain them, we need to find ways to help them get ahead and retain them effectively.  Our ability to recognise individuals for their unique contribution rather than their company will help create an environment that attracts and retains talents from across the world and give us the competitive edge.

 

     Four, demand and supply.  On the demand side, creative talents need a hand up, not a handout.  Demand spurs supply.  Perhaps the Government, not just MICA but all across the Government bodies, can purchase local creative works, visual performance or design and collectively provide a demand floor to support the creative industry.  On the supply side, each year, MOM projects the manpower needs and NMC translated them into intake quotas which affect the number of tuition grants given.  Last year, for NAFA and LaSalle, the quota was 903 and 102 for visual arts and performing arts respectively, including the arts administrators.  Is this way too conservative?  In addition, perhaps the tuition grant should also be extended to degree level to recognise it as a full profession.  Perhaps the Minister can also explain what are some of the KPIs used in measuring the success of talent cultivation in Singapore and KPIs in measuring the effectiveness of nurturing ground that we have provided. 

 

     I would also suggest considering the criteria used in Richard Florida's book, The Rise of the Creative Class, and I think the four criteria outlined are quite useful.  They are, one, innovation measurements such as the number of businesses created, patent applications, national share of hi-tech output and the concentration of  hi-tech output versus non hi-tech output.  Two, talent measurements such as the number of Bachelors degrees or better, per capita and number of creative sector occupations per capita.  Three, diversity measurements such as number of foreign bond per capita, a Bohemian index of artists, musicians, etc, a percentage of people and colour and integration index because diversity is key.  And, four, workforce measurements such as occupational clusters, industry clusters and creative senses.  Would the Minister please consider the above suggestions?

     Mr Baey Yam Keng (Tanjong Pagar): Sir, talent for the creative sector can be quite different from other sectors.  These are people who may not do well in the mainstream education system but just have a unique creative streak in them.  For example, local song-writer Dick Lee did not even have a full certificate for his 'O' levels, yet he has written many great songs for local and international singers, and for many years, was the winner of the COMPASS Composer of the Year Award for having received the most in music royalties.  On the other hand, creative talent can also be high academic achievers who finally decided to pursue something that they are really passionate about.  For example, theatre director Ong Keng Sen, playwright Eleanor Wong and actress Selena Tan are all law graduates.  It seems like besides becoming politicians, lawyers are also quite versatile and multi-talented.

 

     However, if I may stereotype, creative people do not want to be tied down to a fixed routine or job.  Coupled with the fact that the nature of the work opportunities in the creative industries is more on a project basis, many creative practitioners are freelancers.

 

     I am hence happy to note that the Creative Industries Scholarships offered under MICA and its statutory boards are mostly without a bond.  Therein lies the challenge of how the Ministry is able to be accountable to the public on whether the money is well spent, and how the Ministry is tapping on the returning scholars to further develop the creative industries in Singapore.  As with the high performing Government scholars who are given the exposure to be groomed for leadership positions, is there also a concerted effort to help the scholars scale greater heights in their creative or artistic fields?  Many creative people are waiting for their big break.  The Government could look at appropriate platforms to help showcase the talents of the people whom we have invested the scholarship in.

 

     In the creative sector, not many disciplines can be substantiated solely with a piece of paper.  In most cases, having worked on a great project is far more valuable than having graduated from a top university.  The network is also very important.  Landing a project in the creative sphere is usually dependent on who you know or who knows you.  For example, in my theatre practice, we seldom audition for the cast.  It is usually through our own contacts, referrals or who we have seen acting in other productions.  Our scholarship system may need to be tailored so that they can also help talents gain relevant work experience, track records and networks.

 

     In our pursuit to develop the creative sector as an industry, we need to also develop talent in business, management and marketing.  I find that currently much focus is on the artistic or creative development.  For example, in the performing arts, most arts groups, be it in theatre or dance, the key person is typically the artistic director.  He or she is usually the choreographer, director, actor or playwright, someone usually heavily involved in the creative process. 

 

     In today's Life! section, Mrs Tina Leo-Tan, owner of The Link boutique who has helped local fashion designer Sven Tan break into international market, said, "[Local designers] have to multi-task and play other roles integral to the business, like marketing, overseeing manufacturing, operations and pricing for merchandise."  In the same article, fashion and lifestyle publicist Lionel Lim added that, however, "They stubbornly believe the product will speak for itself."

This could be in the form of scholarships, training grants or operating budget for our major grant recipients.  I believe we could explore having the arts company model of a CEO and a Chief Artistic Officer (CAO).

Creative Intellectual Property

 

     Mr Baey Yam Keng: Sir, the creative sector is not one that depends on high capital investments or a large labour force.  It only takes the talent of one person like JK Rowling to write the Harry Potter series, and now she is a millionaire, or probably a billionaire.  But the application of the copyright in the form of the manuscript into books, movies, computer games and merchandise has created jobs and profits in the publishing, printing, film, gaming and manufacturing industries, not to mention hours of enjoyment for many end consumers.

 

     The creative sector is, hence, one heavy on intellectual property (IP).  Besides stories, these could also include scripts, songs, TV formats, design patents, etc.  We should do more to support the development of new original IP.  In design, the idea can be easily copied and replicated.  A few years ago, I was shopping for a new home and discovered that there are many copycat furniture and lighting designs over Singapore.  Two items very similar in design may look identical, but the original, usually an European design, can easily cost 10-20 times more than a similar piece manufactured somewhere in Asia.  Not only must we have the talent to create the designs, we must have the legal infrastructure to protect the IP.  What are Singapore's plans to increase design IP?

 

     In the performing arts, I have observed that there have been a number of restaging of productions, both local and classic, eg, Forbidden City, Titoudao, Lei Yu. While re-runs could ride on past successful performances, I understand that each re-staged production does bring along with it new elements in direction, acting, design and treatment.  However, we should also give more encouragement for local talents to create new IP.  A piece of new work, as it is untested, is usually more difficult to attract sponsors and audience.  Like in pop music, Singaporeans began to take notice of Stefanie Sun and JJ Lin after they made it big in Taiwan and China.  Our audiences also prefer to pay for tickets to established names, like Phantom of the Opera and Mama Mia.  The new works hence tend to be less commercially popular.

 

     So I would like to call on the Ministry to provide greater incentives for creative practitioners to invest in and to take a greater risk in creating untested new works.

 

 

     The Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (Dr Lee Boon Yang): Sir, I thank the hon. Members for speaking on the creative industries and for raising many relevant comments and suggestions.

 

     Let me begin by sharing with the House a brief overview of what the industry represents.  In our context in Singapore, we describe the creative industries as encompassing the arts, design and media. 

 

     Globally, the creative industries were estimated to be worth over US$1 trillion in 2005, accounting for about 7% of the world's GDP. It has a projected growth of about 10% annually.  It is obviously a big and rapidly growing sector of the global economy. In Singapore, between 1995 and 2004, the creative industries grew by about 7% annually, which is very healthy, better perhaps compared to 4% for the general economy's growth.

 

     The impact of the creative industries goes far beyond their direct economic contributions to the economy.  The indirect contributions are equally, if not more, important.  For example, the creative industries can contribute to even more innovative and desirable products and services in  manufacturing, healthcare and tourism sectors. The strong multiplier effect of the creative industries on other sectors was, in fact, something which the United Kingdom's Creative Economy Programme singled out for mention.  In Singapore's context, we note through anecdotal evidence that the performing arts, for example, have much higher multipliers than many other economic activities. 

 

     So the creative industries also improve our quality of life and make Singapore more vibrant by stimulating awareness and demand for the arts, design and media products.   It opens up new opportunities for Singaporeans to turn their creative ideas into businesses.

 

     By coincidence, The Straits Times today, mentioned by Mr Baey Yam Keng, carried several pages featuring fashion designers and other designers' works as well as pointing out the ups and downs of the design industry.  I think it is natural, like many other industries, they will have their successes and failures.  The article makes for interesting reading about the state of the design sector in Singapore.

 

     Sir, our investment in the creative industries is a long-term effort.  It would be a long journey. The good news is that we are making encouraging progress. The latest economic figures indicated that, in 2004, Singapore’s creative industries contributed about 3.6% to the GDP, total value add was $6.7 billion, which was an increase of 8% over the previous year. Total creative employment increased by more than 1,000 to over 93,000 workers in the creative sector, accounting for about 4.3% of total employment. 

 

     Many countries are aggressively investing in their creative industries. Countries, such as the United Kingdom, the Nordic countries, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, China, including Hong Kong, have all launched national strategies to develop their creative industries.  Singapore cannot lag behind. We must seize the opportunities and be responsive to changes in the global environment. We must update our strategy and effort to promote our creative enterprises.  In response to Mr Baey Yam Keng on what are we planning to do for the creative industries, let me inform the House that MICA will review and refine our creative industries development strategy which we adopt about three years ago and we will update the strategy so that we will be able to develop new initiatives to further support and nurture this very important sector of the economy. 

 

     But not everything has to change when we review and refine. One key component which remains unchanged is developing human capital.  After all, it is the breakthrough ideas by the talented individual that lead to innovative products and services.  We will continue to adopt a holistic approach through education, capability development, awareness-building, showcasing and recognising creative talents and achievements.  If we take this approach, we will be able to make a greater impact to catalyse and stimulate our creative industries.

 

     Miss Penny Low asked whether our artists and arts groups are doing well, and how we measure ourselves in this area.  There is no simple measure of such achievements. Some of it may well be intangible. One rough measure though is the number of international awards and engagements that are secured by our local talents. It is an indication of recognition.  The numbers are not large, but the National Arts Council (NAC) reported a 50% increase in 2005 over the year before for such recognition and international engagements. I would say more about the increasing demand for the arts and internationalisation of our artists and arts groups later on.

 

     On Miss Low's question about the intake quotas for our arts institutions, each year, the National Manpower Council establishes the intakes of subsidised students in the post-secondary education institutions. This is done in consultation with various Ministries, economic agencies and the education institutions themselves in order to be able to channel resources to areas with the highest economic demand.  I am glad to inform this House that to support the growing demand for creative manpower in the creative sector, the local arts institution has overall increased the art intakes of subsidised students by 10% between 2004 and 2006.  We will continue to review and monitor the demand for talent in the creative sector.  As the need manifests itself, we would certainly be working with the post-secondary education institutions to increase their intake so that we will be able to supply the required number of talents to take on these jobs in the creative sector.

     Mr Chairman, Sir, Mr Baey Yam Keng has noted that talent for the creative sector needs to be developed differently.  I agree with him.  He also asked about the scholarships and the informal training opportunities that are equally important in nurturing talent for the creative industry.  Last year, 30 scholarships were awarded by the arts, design and media sector.  Since 2003, MDA has also supported an additional 76 aspiring media talents under the Media Education Scheme at both local and overseas institutions.  I would strongly encourage students who are interested in a career in the creative industry to apply for these scholarships.

 

     Our scholarships and bursaries have enlarged our pool of creative manpower.  Some of them joined Singapore’s creative workforce immediately after graduation, while others opted to work overseas to gain international exposure and establish invaluable networks before returning home.  Our established artists are also helping to mentor and to develop the next generation of artists.  For instance, seven out of 10 recipients of music and dance scholarships given in the early 1990s have remained active musicians or dancers as well as teaching aspiring young musicians and dancers.  A good example is Ms Cheah Mei Sing, a scholar who used to dance with the Singapore Dance Theatre.  She has now joined the School of the Arts to help groom younger talents.
 
     Sir, Mr Baey Yam Keng pointed out that formal education alone is not sufficient.  MICA’s agencies offer various programmes beyond formal training to help creative practitioners to build up their creative skills, arts management and business capabilities, as well as gain international exposure.  I agree with him.  Trying to help and grow these arts companies is not just a matter of the artistic side.  There is also the business side, and we are aware of it.  That is why we help them to develop arts management and business capabilities.  This assistance also includes local and overseas attachments, grants for them to conduct studies, as well as master classes and seminars. 


     We are also partnering the Workforce Development Agency to develop a Creative Industries Workforce Skills Qualifications (WSQ) framework, which includes the development of skills standards, assessment strategies and training curriculum for the creative sector.  We believe that having a creative industry Workforce Skills Qualifications framework will certainly help many workers in this sector to improve their employment prospects.

 

     Miss Penny Low asked about freelancers, workers in the creative sector who are not employees in the usual sense and who will move between projects.  I agree with her that the creative sector has a large proportion of such people, and that freelancers are in fact an essential part of the talent pool for Singapore’s creative industries.  It is also the same in many other creative cities.  They have a pool of freelancers who will move around from project to project and sometimes doing different things.  I agree that it is important for freelancers to also upgrade their skills and capabilities.  MICA’s agencies offer capability development programmes for creative workers, including freelancers. Last year, nearly 5,000 employees and freelancers took advantage of these programmes.  Freelancers can also benefit from our Workforce Skills Qualification framework, which I have just mentioned.
