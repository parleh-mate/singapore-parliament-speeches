SINGAPORE MEDICAL TEAM IN SAUDI ARABIA
(Cost)


    The following Question stood in the name of Dr Lee Siew-Choh -

    To ask the Prime Minister and Minister for Defence what is the cost of keeping the Singapore medical team in Saudi Arabia and whether there was any request from anybody for a medical team to be sent from Singapore.

     Mr S. Chandra Das (Cheng San GRC): Question No. 1, Sir.

     The Second Minister for Defence (Dr Yeo Ning Hong) (for the Prime Minister and Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government sent a team of 30 SAF officers and men to Saudi Arabia in response to a British request, and with the agreement of the Government of Saudi Arabia. It did so in accordance with UN Security Council Resolution 678. The Resolution requires "all States to provide support for the actions" undertaken to further efforts "to uphold and implement Resolution 660 (1990) and all subsequent relevant resolutions and to restore international peace and security in the area."

    Our purpose was humanitarian. We expected many casualties when war broke out. The team's mission was to provide medical care for all who needed it, regardless of nationality. Several other countries, including South Korea, Sweden, Norway and Poland, also sent similar medical teams to Saudi Arabia.

    The team was attached to a British Field Hospital. The British provided all administrative and logistical support.

    As far as cost is concerned, the estimated cost is $630,000 so far. However, the final cost of the entire mission will only be known after the team has returned.

(Erection of shelter)


    2. Dr Koh Lip Lin asked the Minister for National Development (a) what were the reasons for not approving the request to erect a shelter on the overhead pedestrian bridge across Yishun Avenue 2 at MRT Khatib Station and (b) what is the estimated cost of erecting such a shelter.

     The Minister for National Development (Mr S. Dhanabalan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Public Works Department (PWD) has a programme to construct covered bridges where the pedestrian traffic is heavy during peak hours. PWD has adopted the guideline of 1,200 pedestrians per hour (20 per minute) during the peak hours, below which a covered bridge would not be justified. The request by Yishun Town

Column: 276

Council to erect a shelter over the existing pedestrian bridge across Yishun Avenue 2 was carefully considered by PWD. The bridge is not part of a busy walkway linking, say, a bus interchange with a Mass Rapid Transit (MRT) station.
