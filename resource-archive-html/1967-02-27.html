ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

RE-DEMARCATION OF CONSTITUENCIES


    1. Madam Chan Choy Siong asked the Deputy Prime Minister whether it is proposed to re-demarcate the present boundaries of the fifty-one Constituencies as their present population ratios are quite disproportionate.

    The Minister of State for the Prime Minister's Department (Tuan Haji Yaacob Bin Mohamed) (for the Deputy Prime Minister)(In Malay): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have been asked by the Deputy Prime Minister to reply. A complete review of electoral boundaries will be undertaken as soon as compilation of new citizens electoral registers for 1967 based on the new laminated identity cards is completed. Registration of persons which began in May last year has just ended. Preparation of new electoral registers with the correct addresses of citizens eligible to vote shown therein will begin soon, and the review of electoral boundaries will be undertaken in the light of the information contained in the new registers.

    In the past, the names which appeared in the electoral registers included all persons who held Singapore identity cards irrespective of whether they were citizens or not. An amendment to the Elections Ordinance will soon be presented to enable only citizens to vote. It stands to reason that, with such a revision, electoral boundaries must be redrawn so that the average number of electorate in all constituencies will be approximately the same. The creation of housing estates in many areas and resettlement of a section of the population have also made this revision necessary.

TIME OFF FOR MILITARY TRAINING
(Deduction of pay)


    2. Mr Bernard Rodrigues asked the Minister of Defence if he is aware that some employers deduct the pay of their workers who take time off for training

Column: 1096

in one of the military or para-military organisations; and whether and what steps he proposes to take to ensure that no employees suffer any loss merely by reason of their voluntary participation in such organisations.

    The Minister of State of Defence (Mr Wee Toon Boon) (for the Minister of Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, the training undergone by the People's Defence Force volunteers comprises evening parades, weekend camps or training and annual camps. The evening parades are held after office hours. Similarly the weekend camps or training held at weekend camps hardly interferes with the civilian work of such volunteers. In cases where the evening parades interfere with the working hours of the volunteers, the officers in charge of the training depots usually request the employers concerned that the volunteers he allowed to leave their work early. At present, however, there is no law compelling the employers to do so.

    The question raised by the Member for Telok Blangah, therefore, really relates to the annual camp. When they attend such annual camp, the volunteers are given the same rate of military pay as their regular counterparts of corresponding rank. Attention has recently been drawn to the cases of daily-rated employees who are unable to get time off for attending annual camp and whose civilian pay is greater than the military pay that they would draw in attending annual camp. To meet the problem, it has been decided that in such cases a special allowance up to a maximum of $2.50 per day may be paid for the difference between the civilian and military pay. There are 448 daily-rated employees in the People's Defence Force, and the majority of 240 attended annual camp last year.

    Consideration is being given to making legal provision to cover such cases.

    I may add that in respect of Government employees, both the monthly paid and the daily-rated, there are provisions for granting them full pay leave for a

COVENT GARDENS IN DELTA CONSTITUENCY
(Measures to improve health environment)


    5. Madam Chan Choy Siong asked the Minister for Health whether he is aware that the health environment of Covent Gardens in the Delta Constituency has been poor and what measures he proposes to take to improve it.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, Covent Gardens is a very depressed slum area composed largely of attap houses and a few old brick and tile shophouses situated at the junction of Kim Seng Road with Havelock Road and part of the Singapore River. The area is low lying with a tidal creek running through the area, so that it is subject to periodic flooding when heavy rain coincides with the tides.

    In these circumstances, the environmental health of Covent Gardens, as in other kampongs similarly situated elsewhere in Singapore, is bad. It is, however, not of recent origin but has been so for many years. I understand Covent

Column: 1099

Gardens was placed very high on the priority list for immediate re-development by the Housing and Development Board as compared with adjacent Bukit Ho Swee. But when the fire catastrophe occurred at the latter site in May 1961, redevelopment of Bukit Ho Swee was proceeded with apace, and Covent Gardens unfortunately remains what it is today.

    A recent survey of Covent Gardens, which by the way has no Street sign at all to indicate its existence whether access is from Kim Seng Road or Havelock Road, revealed that there are about 2,500 persons living in a little over 100 houses of attap or zinc construction. Of these, 33 houses are built on stilts over the tidal creek or fore-shore of the river. As only half of the houses have latrines, and only 23 use the bucket system, the majority, therefore, have to resort to the primitive method of easing themselves over the tidal creek or the river with consequent pollution. Poultry and pigs are even being reared in about one-half of these houses. As drains are generally earth drains, with refuse being dumped indiscriminately into drains and on to footpaths, the maintenance of public cleansing was almost an impossible task with only one public daily-rated worker being assigned to perform the task of refuse clearance of this area, which includes a small private market as well.

    Mosquito breeding is extensive because of water-collecting junk lying everywhere and with stagnant water in drains and pools. The situation has been prevented from worsening through weekly oiling by the Anti-Mosquito Department.

    Although Covent Gardens can be made to look cleaner soon - as with the recent re-organisation of the public cleansing service which started early this month in spite of the wildcat strike by the Public Daily-Rated Cleansing Workers' Union, it has been possible to post six Emergency Cleansing Corps workers to look after Covent Gardens, where formerly there was only one public daily-rated worker allotted for this task - the long-term solution,

    Although what I have to say further is strictly not within my portfolio hut that of my colleague, the Minister for National Development, I am, however, pleased to inform the Member for Delta that the Chairman of the Housing and Development Board has given me the assurance that every effort will be made to give priority to any family within this Covent Gardens area who would like to be housed in Housing and Development Board flats with a view to enabling the Board to obtain clearance of this area at the earliest possible date. Applications from families staying in the Covent Gardens area will be given special consideration for re-housing in new units that are being constructed in the Urban Renewal Scheme, Precinct S.1, which is just across Havelock Road. SINGAPORE CITIZENSHIP
(Residential qualification for alien husbands and wives)


    10. Mr Bernard Rodrigues asked the Minister for Labour if it is the intention of the Government to maintain the existing minimum two years residential qualification for alien wives of Singapore Citizens for registration as Singapore Citizens and whether similar provision will be extended to alien husbands of Singapore Citizens.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Labour (Mr Sia Kah Hui) (for the Minister for Labour): The Government intends to maintain the existing provision of Article 57 (2) of the Constitution, i.e.:

    '...any woman who is married to a citizen of Singapore may, on making application therefor in the prescribed manner, be registered as a citizen of Singapore if she satisfies the Government -

    (a) that she has resided continuously in Singapore for a period of not less than two years immediately preceding the date of the application:

    (b) that she intends to reside permanently in Singapore: and

    (c) that she is of good character.'

    No similar provision will be extended to alien husbands who are married to Singapore citizens. Singapore citizens who are married to alien husbands are expected to adopt the citizenship of their husbands, as is the normal practice in many countries of the world.

    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Minister for Law and National Development (Mr E. W. Barker): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    As it is the intention to refer the Bill to a Select Committee, I shall only touch briefly upon the more important provisions in the Bill.

    Under existing practice, what the purchaser of a flat usually obtains is a 999-year lease in a building. The freehold title in the land above which the

Column: 1114

flat is built is either retained by the vendor who imposes on each of his lessees a service charge for the maintenance of common parts and property or is conveyed in proportionate shares to the purchasers of all flats built on the land as tenants in common. Experience has shown that it is difficult for tenants to get together and problems arise when lifts break down or pipes burst. This rather unsatisfactory state of affairs has impeded the development and sale of flats in the Republic and the Bill now before the House, which is based on Australian legislation, takes account of the comments of the former Bar Committee and seeks to remove these difficulties.

    The Bill introduces a strata land register under which the proprietor of registered land on which a building stands, who has obtained approval of subdivision of the building into flats from the competent authority, may apply to the Registrar of Titles for the land and the subdivided building thereon to be dealt with in lots under separate subsidiary strata certificates of title.

    The Bill also spells out the rights and obligations of the purchasers of flats in a subdivided building as regards assessments, easements and restrictive covenants. The owner or owners of flats comprised in a subdivided building shown in a strata title plan lodged in the Land Titles Registry will become the management corporation of the building as soon as such strata title plan has been registered. The management corporation, consisting of all the flat owners, is made responsible for the maintenance of common parts like lifts and corridors.
