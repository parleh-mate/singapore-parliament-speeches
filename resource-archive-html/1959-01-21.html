SINGAPORE AND INDONESIA


    5. Mr Lim Cher Kheng asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry whether he will make a statement on the present trade relations between Singapore and Indonesia.

    Mr J. M. Jumabhoy: Mr Speaker, Sir, our trade relations with Indonesia are satisfactory and are best reflected in the fact that, despite an eight-cent drop in the average price of rubber, our trade with Indonesia last year, when the final figures are published, is expected to total $1,300 million in value - only one per cent less than in 1957. To have maintained trade at this level in the face of three successive years of lower average rubber prices, namely from 144.16 cents in 1955 down to 96.76 cents, 88.75 and 80.18 cents in 1956, 1957 and 1958 respectively, is, to say the least, remarkably gratifying.

    If we take the available January/November trade figures as a basis for an estimate, we find that in 1958 imports from Indonesia should total approximately $968 million - a shortfall of 12 per cent compared with 1957. However, the actual volume of rubber imported rose to 311,000 tons from 287,000 tons in 1957. Imports of other important produce items such as copra, coffee, tea and pepper tended to fluctuate and may, when final figures are available, be found to have reached slightly lower levels than in 1957.

    On the export side, I am glad to say that final statistics are likely to show that exports to Indonesia last year rose to over $360 million-an increase of 47 per cent over the 1957 figure. Largely responsible were increased exports of rice (up approximately 61,000 tons to 163,000 tons), cotton textiles (up $7.5 million to $20.3 million) and art silk piecegoods (up $44 million to $54.9 million). A number of other export items made smaller but significant gains.

RICE STOCKPILE
(Government Policy)


    6. Mr William Tan asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry whether he will make a statement on the latest Government policy regarding the rice stockpile.

    7. Mr Lim Cher Kheng asked the Minister for Commerce and Industry whether he will make a statement on the discussions he has had with the rice merchants and the Singapore Chamber of Commerce about the stockpiling of rice, and whether he will revise the present rice-stockpiling policy of the Government.

    Mr J. M. Jumabhoy: Sir, with your permission, I will take Questions 6 and 7 together because both refer to the same subject.

    Representatives of four rice Associations together with representatives of the Chinese Chamber of Commerce saw me on 7th January with a request for unconditional abolition of the stockpile.

    I explained to them that the Government had no desire to carry the burden of the stockpile, but it was necessary in the interest of the general public to have guaranteed supplies in case of any emergency. I asked them for suggestions as

Column: 1636

to any practical alternate machinery that could be devised to meet this difficulty. They saw the necessity for some practical alternate arrangement to meet an emergency but were unable to suggest a solution.

    I then put a suggestion to them that since they claimed that every rice merchant did maintain a certain amount of stock at all times and that since most of their stocks were held in bank go-downs, it could perhaps be a solution if they requested their bankers individually to guarantee to Government to hold on their behalf a minimum specified amount of whole grain rice stocks at all times which will be available to Government on demand at any time. I suggested that such bank guarantees should total to a tonnage of 25,000 tons. This method would not place any additional burden on any of the merchants nor on the public while at the same time ensuring that stocks will be available in an emergency.

    After some discussion, though all were agreed that the suggestion had possibilities in principle, the representatives of the rice Associations desired to discuss further in their own Associations the practical aspects of implementing the suggestion and promised to let me know the results. So far I have not heard from them and therefore I am not able to give any indication at this stage of Government's future policy on the stockpile.

    Hon. Members will of course appreciate that even if my above suggestion is agreed to by the trade, agreement will have to be sought from the Federation Government, as the rice stockpile was set up by joint agreement.
