BOGUS INSURANCE COMPANIES


    3. Mr Ong Chang Sam asked the Minister for Finance what progress has been made in the action taken against bogus insurance companies since 9th April. 1963.

    The Minister for Health and Law (Mr K. M. Byrne) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker. Sir, the Member for Choa Chu Kang will recall that in the last sitting of the Legislative Assembly in April, he was informed that the process of liquidating bogus insurance companies was in the hands of liquidators appointed by the High Court of Singapore in respect of three insurance companies incorporated in the State, and by the High Court of Malaya in respect of the other 13 insurance companies incorporated in the Federation.

    The liquidators of most of these companies are still processing, summarising and sorting out tens of thousands of claims from all over Malaya and Singapore, as well as realising the assets of

Column: 5

companies where available. The liquidators of two insurance companies - one from Singapore and the other from Malaya - however, have already begun paying out claims to policy holders, and payments are now almost completed. These payments are practically in full satisfaction of claims by policy holders. The process to make payments of claims to policy holders for another two companies - again one from Singapore and the other from the Federation - is reaching its final stages and claimants will receive their payments within the next three months.

    The liquidators for the Singapore company reported that all claims from policy holders can be met in full. The remaining one Singapore insurance company under liquidation proved to be insolvent and some legal action is still necessary before any payment can be made.

    As for the other bogus insurance companies incorporated in Malaya, it is not known how long the liquidators will take before any payment to policy holders can be made. However, as stated before, many of these companies were in a poor financial position and it would be optimistic for any policy holder to expect more than a small fraction of his claims to be honoured.

    Sir, the former English law did provide for family provision in giving the wife a right to dower, by virtue of which if the wife survived her husband she became entitled to an estate for life in a third of all her husband's freeholds of which she has been seized at any time during the marriage. As this right to the wife attached even though the husband alienated the land, it gave rise to a great deal of practical inconvenience and was eventually abolished by the Dower Act of 1833. After that there was nothing to stop a man (or a woman with respect to her separate property), from devising and bequeathing his whole estate to charity or to a complete stranger and leaving his widow and children penniless. It was to prevent this evil that the Inheritance (Family Provision) Act of 1938 was enacted. The Act did not cast upon the testator any positive duty to make reasonable provision for his dependants, for it would be impossible to impose any such obligation, but it provides that if he fails to do so the Court may order such reasonable provision as it thinks fit to be made out of the estate for his dependants as defined in the Act. The Act was further amended in 1952 to extend it to cases of intestacy, as it was felt that the general laws of intestacy might leave a child inadequately provided for. The provisions relating to family provisions appear to have worked well in England and it is proposed to introduce them into Singapore.
