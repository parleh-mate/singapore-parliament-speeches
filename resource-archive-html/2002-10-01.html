(Setting up)


    The following Question stood in the name of Mdm Ho Geok Choo -

    1.     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Finance (Mr Lee Hsien Loong): Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore has made significant headway in positioning itself as the regional IT and financial hub. Global corporations and financial institutions are among the early adopters who have consolidated processing along business functions, and also centralised processing either globally or regionally in the American, European or Asian time-zones.

    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Senior Minister of State for Law (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Bill amends the International Arbitration Act to make it clear that parties to an arbitration in Singapore are free to adopt the arbitration rules of their choice to govern their arbitration, and that their choice of arbitration rules will be respected by Singapore law and be given the fullest effect possible. Examples of arbitration rules are the UNCITRAL Rules, SIAC Rules, ICC Rules and LCIA Rules.

    This Bill's clarification will reaffirm Singapore's position as an attractive venue for arbitration in the region. It also reaffirms Government's intention to do whatever is necessary to create conducive conditions for the conduct of international arbitrations in Singapore.

    The International Arbitration Act was passed in 1995 to adopt the UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration as part of Singapore's domestic law. The UNCITRAL Model Law is not a set of arbitration rules. It is intended to be the applicable arbitration law or, in lawyers' parlance - the curial

Column: 1108

law or lex arbitri. Like all arbitration laws, the UNCITRAL Model Law does not contain a comprehensive set of procedural rules, but merely sets out a basic procedural framework. The expectation is that parties will supplement this basic procedural framework with arbitration rules of their choice. Parties are also free to choose their own alternative arrangements in most procedural matters. Indeed, one of the key pillars of the UNCITRAL Model Law is the protection of party autonomy.

    In other words, parties to a Singapore arbitration are free to adopt whatever set of arbitration rules they wish. The Model Law is specifically drafted to ensure that parties' wishes are given the fullest effect possible. Sir, this has been the legal position since this House adopted the Model Law as part of Singapore law in 1995, and has always remained so. And this is how both the international and local arbitration communities have always understood the legal position to be.

    However, some uncertainty has arisen as a result of a Singapore High Court case, Dermajaya Properties v Premium Properties, which was decided earlier this year. The correctness of the actual decision in this case is not in dispute. What has caused concern in some quarters was a comment made by the judge in the course of delivering his judgment. He commented that, if parties adopt a set of arbitration rules which contains procedures which are different from those contemplated by the Model Law, then those arbitration rules would be regarded as incompatible with the Model Law and would therefore be entirely excluded. This comment was strictly not necessary in arriving at the actual decision in the case, and therefore did not have the effect of changing the law on the applicability of arbitration rules.

    Nevertheless, my Ministry has received feedback that lawyers in some international law firms had advised their clients that, as a result of the Dermajaya case, the legal position in Singapore is now uncertain. I understand that some of these

Column: 1109

lawyers have also advised their clients to avoid Singapore as a venue for arbitration.

    The Government has therefore decided to set out the legal position in no uncertain terms. The Bill does this by adding a new section 15A to the International Arbitration Act. The first subsection of the new section 15A declares, for the avoidance of doubt, that arbitration rules adopted by parties shall apply and must be given effect to the extent that they are not inconsistent with a mandatory provision of the Act or the Model Law. A mandatory provision is one whereby the Act or Model Law does not permit the parties to derogate from or vary. This therefore reinforces the principle of party autonomy, and makes it clear beyond doubt that no set of arbitration rules ought ever to be regarded as incompatible with the Act or the Model Law.

    The remaining subsections seek to flesh out the concept of inconsistency, by referring to a number of scenarios where there could be doubts as to whether any inconsistency exists. For example, it is clarified in the Bill that there is no inconsistency if the rules provide for something on which the Act or the Model Law is silent or if the Act or the Model Law provides for something on which the rules are silent. There is also no inconsistency if the rules provide for something which is already covered by a non-mandatory provision of the Act or the Model Law.

    To summarise, the general approach is that arbitration rules adopted by the parties will be given full effect. Where there is a conflict between the arbitration rules and the Act or the Model Law, the rules will prevail unless the conflict is with a mandatory provision of the Act or the Model Law. Hence, in effect, with this narrow exception, parties to a Singapore arbitration will have the full freedom to adopt arbitration rules of their choice.

WRITTEN ANSWER TO QUESTION
CONSTITUTION OF THE REPUBLIC OF SINGAPORE


    1. Mr Charles Chong asked the Minister for Home Affairs whether his Ministry will review Article 122(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of Singapore making the conditions for according Singapore citizenship by descent to foreign born children of a Singapore parent more gender equal.

    Mr Wong Kan Seng:

    Our Constitution does not make any distinction between a child born in Singapore to either a Singaporean father or Singaporean mother. Such children are Singapore citizens by birth. This would apply to most cases, as the majority of children born to a Singaporean parent or parents, are born in Singapore.

    Our Constitution also provides for children born overseas to at least one Singaporean parent to be granted citizenship. Children born overseas to a Singaporean mother (and foreigner father) are not deprived of a chance to be citizens.

    Article 122(1) of the Constitution allows for citizenship by descent to be granted to a child born overseas to a Singaporean father, provided that the child is registered within one year of birth. If the registration is not done within one year of birth, the Constitution allows for citizenship by registration to be granted to such children.

    Article 124 of the Constitution allows for children born overseas of a Singaporean mother (and foreigner father) to be citizens by registration.

    Although the Constitution has made this differentiation for births overseas, the practical effect is not great. The great majority of applications for citizenship of children born overseas to a Singaporean parent are approved.

    The only other discernible difference between citizenship by descent and citizenship by registration lies in Article 129(3)(b) of the Constitution, which states that the Government may deprive the citizenship of a citizen by registration if, within 5 years of the grant of citizenship by registration, he has been sentenced to imprisonment for a term of more than 1 year or to a fine of more than $5,000 or if he has been engaged in activities which are prejudicial to the security of Singapore, or the maintenance of public order or essential services, or in any criminal activities prejudicial to the interests of public safety, peace or good order. For most people, these conditions are practically not applicable. Thus, while the form of the citizenship differs between a child born overseas to a Singaporean father and mother, the substance is practically the same.

    We are not the only country to make such a distinction. We are guided by the reasoning that, in communities like ours, the man is traditionally the main or in many cases, the sole breadwinner of the family. The man's place of employment would therefore usually decide where the family would reside. It would therefore be more practical for the citizenship of the child born overseas to follow that of the father's.

    Since the substance of the citizenship granted to children born overseas in either case is practically the same, my Ministry is of the view that, for now, we should not amend Article 122(1) of the Constitution.
