MERCHANT SHIPPING
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.41 p.m.

    The Deputy Prime Minister (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    There are at present 14 harbour pilots who are self-employed. Arrangements are now being made for pilotage to be taken over by a proposed new Port Authority. The target date for this takeover is 1st January, 1963. In order to maintain an efficient service when the pilots are employed by the Port Authority, it is estimated that it will be necessary to employ 35 pilots. The Singapore

Column: 658

Harbour Board, as the forerunner of the proposed Port Authority, has since October last year recruited and trained 17 pilots, of whom 14 have already been licensed by the Pilot Board. These pilots are directly employed by the Harbour Board, and by the time of the takeover, all pilots, including those pilots at present self-employed and who elect to remain in service, will be in the employ of the Harbour Board or the Port Authority.

    Part X of the Merchant Shipping Ordinance, which deals with pilotage, does not provide for any limitation of liability for loss or damage occasioned by any act or default of a pilot whilst engaged in his duties; neither does the Ordinance provide protection front liability for the pilot's employment. Now that the Singapore Harbour Board is employing pilots, it is necessary to amend the Merchant Shipping Ordinance to protect the Harbour Board and the pilots it employs from claims for damages.

    In other parts of the world, pilotage authorities employing pilots are covered either by being able to limit their liability or by exempting themselves of any liability, in the United Kingdom, this liability is limited to £100 multiplied by the number of pilots in the pilotage district. The Bombay Port Trust is not liable to pay any damages. The pilots in the United Kingdom are enabled by legislation to limit their personal liability to £100 plus the pilotage fee for the ship involved.

    The Bill is therefore designed to enable a harbour pilot to put up a bond of $1,000 and his personal liability shall be limited to that amount and the amount payable to him for piloting the vessel. This Bill also provides that general employers, such as the Harbour Board at present, shall not be liable for any loss or damage caused by a pilot.

    Question put, and agreed to.

SINGAPORE OVERSEAS TELECOMMUNICATION BOARD BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.45 p.m.

    The Deputy Prime Minister (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    It will be recalled that in November 1960, the Government announced its intention to acquire the Singapore assets of Cable & Wireless Ltd, when the Company's licenses expire, on 31st December, 1962, and to merge these assets with those of the Telecommunications Department to form a statutory telecommunications board, at the same time allowing Cable & Wireless Ltd, to retain an interest in the new board.

    The internal and external communications existing in the State of Singapore are held under the control of three separate organisations, namely, the Telecommunications Department, Cable & Wireless Ltd, and the Singapore Telephone Board. The Telecommunications Department provides telecommunication services for the public, including commercial organisations, and for Government or quasi-Government organisations. The Singapore Telephone Board operates and maintains the State's internal telephone system, and Cable & Wireless Ltd, operate and maintain a submarine cable and radio circuits associated with the intra-Commonwealth telegraph service.

    Cable & Wireless Ltd, operate in Singapore under the following three licenses all of which are due to expire on 31st December, 1962:-

    (i) The Landing Licence issued in January 1939 permits the Company "to maintain, renew

Column: 660

and use on and along the shore and bed of the sea specified submarine telegraph cables at Singapore and Penang (and also at Cocos Islands and Labuan).

    (ii) The Cable Licence issued in November 1940 covers the use of underground cables inter-connecting the Company's installations in Singapore, Penang and Labuan.

    (iii) The Wireless Telegraphic Licence issued in December 1953 authorises the Company to establish wireless telegraphy circuits between Singapore and places listed in the Schedule attached to the Licence.

    Before the licences of Cable & Wireless Ltd, expire at the end of this year, we have to reach a decision whether a new lease should be given to the Company or whether the Government should take over the assets and functions of the Company.

    The communications system of any country is one of its most vital assets and this aspect is especially important in respect of external communications. A natural development is for independent or self-governing countries to acquire the control of the nation's communication facilities in order to develop such facilities in accordance with the countries' requirements and also to maintain the prestige value of the external communications. Furthermore, it is not desirable or efficient that the State's telecommunications services should be run by a government department on the one hand and a private company on the other. It was therefore not the Government's intention that Cable & Wireless licences should be renewed on their expiry on 31st December, 1962.

    On the other hand, it was realised that because of the large initial capital outlay involved and the shortage of experienced engineers and managerial staff in Government service, it was not expedient at this moment to acquire

Column: 661

outright the assets of Cable & Wireless Ltd, in Singapore, or to take over the existing functions and organisation of the Company. It was estimated that the assets of the Company in Singapore amounted to approximately $8¼ million. It was therefore decided that the best arrangement would be to merge the assets of the Telecommunications Department, which amounted to approximately the same value, with those of Cable & Wireless Ltd, and to form a statutory board to take over the combined assets and the existing functions of the two organisations. This statutory Board will be known as the "Singapore Overseas Telecommunications Board".

    The proposed integration will not involve the Government in a large capital outlay as would be the case if the assets of Cable & Wireless Ltd, were purchased outright. It is estimated that approximately $300,000 only will be required as Government's capital contribution towards the Board's initial expenses.

    This partnership with Cable & Wireless Ltd, is to last ten years, after which the Government will have the option to terminate or vary the joint ownership arrangement by giving two years' prior notice in writing.

    The Bill provides that Government will own 51 per cent of the undertakings of the Board and the Company will own the remaining 49 per cent. Thus, Government will have control over the activities of the Board. This control by Government is further emphasised in that the Bill provides that the Chairman and two members of the Board will be appointed by the Government and that the Company will appoint the remaining two members of the Board.

    Clauses 9 and 10 relate to the transfer of officers of the Government Telecommunications Department and of Cable & Wireless Company to the new statutory Board. This process of transferring personnel is a complicated operation as the salary and terms of service in two different departments have to be considered. If there are two separate salary scales and terms of service for

Column: 662

the staff of the proposed statutory Board, it can be easily imagined the difficulties which the Board will run into. There will be difficulty in equating status of staff, and at the same time differences in terms of service will engender dissatisfaction among the staff themselves. It is therefore necessary that there should be a unified salary scheme and terms of service for all members of the proposed statutory Board.

    Clause 9 is therefore so worded as to permit discussions between the Board and staff members of the Government Telecommunications Department and the staff members of Cable & Wireless Company. The broad object is that such staff shall not suffer in their overall conditions of service by reason of transfer, but regard to be had with the conditions of service of the Government employees at the time of transfer. In this way we hope a reasonable compromise can be achieved.

    Clause 47 has the intention to train national staff progressively to take over more responsible work with the object of replacing expatriate staff. Suitable nationals will be given the opportunity to undergo technical training in the United Kingdom at the Porthcurno School of Cable and Wireless Ltd, or other suitable technical institutions and to attend administrative courses at the Company's head office in London.
