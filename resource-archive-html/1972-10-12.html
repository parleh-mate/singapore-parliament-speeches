PRESIDENT'S SPEECH


6.46 p.m.

    The President (Dr Benjamin Henry Sheares): Honourable Members, we meet today under circumstances vastly different from those prevailing when the last Parliament was convened on 6th May, 1968. Then, the prospects were grim. We faced the stark question of survival: how to hold up the economy, to generate jobs, of any kind, to mop up massive unemployment.

Column: 9



    The will to succeed was decisive. Sheer determination, hard work and realistic planning enabled Singapore to make the grade. Imaginative policies, combined with tough measures, have minimised the economic and social dislocation. Today's continuing and controlled boom is giving Singapore that glow of success. There is a bloom which will spread from the city to the new towns, and through to the villages and kampongs in the rural areas.

    Now, the challenge is to maintain and continue this rapid pace of growth for continued high growth rates. Then the trend towards a better life for everyone willing to work will become more pronounced, and in more gracious surroundings.

    We cannot seek fame for sheer size. But we can achieve higher standards in everything, with the emphasis on quality, whether in jobs, schools, hospitals, social and recreational amenities or homes. We can become a centre for excellence in all fields important to our economic and cultural life.

    To achieve this, we must fulfil two pre-conditions. First, smaller families-ideally two children per family - so that more resources and care can be given to each child by the family and the State. Second, Singapore must eschew one of the characteristics of the welfare state syndrome, where everyone expects others, not himself, to work harder to carry the subsidies for services everyone wants. For us, a yet to be developed and fully industrialised society, this selfish rejection of the work ethic is fatal. On the contrary, hard work and high performance must be encouraged and rewarded. No one should get away with enjoying services subsidised through the hard work of others, without making his own contribution, to the best of his ability and capacity.

    After the last war, several countries in the West set out to create a more "cushioned" society. The state promised to provide for everyone, from cradle to grave. In the beginning, they

Column: 10

promised free pre-natal and accouchement services, free medical and dental treatment, free spectacles and dentures, free education, free school meals, heavily subsidised public housing, and generous old-age pensions.

    The idealistic social philosophy was to cushion people from the harshness of personal inadequacies, and the sometimes cruel dispensations of fortune. But, in the course of the last 27 years, this ideal has had to be modified, because of escalating economic costs, and worse, because of the debilitating psychological consequences on the working population. Policy makers soon found out that free services led to excessive use, and much abuse. Gradually, but inevitably even under socialist governments, substantial charges have had to he imposed on these initially almost free services.

    But the greater damage to the spirit and morale of a people was harder to correct. A profound change in attitudes to work, and the absence of personal shouldering of responsibility, have permeated considerable portions of their societies. They have reared a younger generation who now believe that the state owes them a living, a good living. They believe the good things in life are easy to come by, by mass agitation, by strikes, and through confrontation with established authority. Quite a few of the younger trade union leaders do not believe that rewards should be equated to hard work or personal performance. They believe that all should be paid equally, the industrious as well as the indolent. Others, who they are not quite clear about, will generate the wealth, the larger part of which the state can cream off to pay for the comfort and welfare of themselves and their followers.

    In some countries, professionally trained men and women, instead of creating wealth, use their knowledge and ingenuity to defeat the tax collector. Many high income professionals and entrepreneurs stop trying, and even stop working, in order not to make profits just before surtax rates. Or they spend lavishly on expense accounts, and other

Column: 11

frills, to defeat the tax collector. Some even commute to tax-free havens in the Caribbean and elsewhere, for more than six months of a year, to escape income tax on dividends from investments they made in foreign countries. Singapore's policies must not cause this kind of distortion, or the workings of the competitive, achievement-oriented society which have brought us to where we are, will fail.

    We must and can keep our population growth small, so that every child can be better fed, clothed, trained and nurtured. We cannot afford to get into this unhappy vicious circle, the something for nothing syndrome. We must pay for what we want. Everyone must work to earn and pay for what he wants in life.

    In two fundamental areas, there must be heavy subsidies. Good health and good education must be available to all. These are costly objectives. But the user must pay a small percentage of the actual cost. It is to remind him of the enormous cost the rest of society is paying for him. This nominal payment will also prevent abuse in the user of these services.

    My Government intends to allow wages to go up, but in a steady and orderly fashion. However, instead of increasing taxes on his increased earnings, we shall leave the worker with most of the increased income. Thus, the responsibility of what he does with this increased income will rest with each individual. He has to decide whether be wants to spend his money on luxury articles or expensive services he does not really need, or to save his money in buying a home, or putting it in the Post Office Savings Bank or investing it in Government bonds and stocks which yield interest or dividends.

    The principles of our taxation policies must encourage people to want to work hard and to cam more, so that they can save more. High savings lead to high capital accumulation, and, as in Japan, will enable more wealth to be generated.

Column: 12

Taxes will fall at least as much on spending as on earnings, to encourage savings. We should not get caught in the consumer-society before we have become fully developed.

    Everyone will try to earn more if he is allowed to retain the major part of his earnings, to invest in sound enterprises which can further increase his income. Many a newly independent state, barely developed, that have promised to carry their voters from cradle to grave, have had their backs broken. And it will take decades to mend the damage. Even highly developed industrial societies have been beset with ever increasing taxation to meet ever higher subsidies in social services. Even the most affluent have not been able to protect the pensioner and the fixed income groups from being impoverished by inflation. We must learn from the lessons which others have paid bitterly for, that nothing in life is for free.

    On constitutional and security matters, the last few years have shown that some safeguards are necessary. Constitutional amendments will be made early in this session of Parliament, so that no surrender of our sovereignty, or any part thereof, by way of incorporation into, or federation with, any other country is possible, unless two-thirds of the total electorate vote positively for it in a referendum.

    It would be criminal negligence to allow foreign interests with large financial resources to manipulate our internal politics, by financing Singapore political parties, professional, academic and other groups, to try to beguile domestic opinion, especially if their efforts are insidiously multiplied by purveying them through mass media set up and supplied generously with funds for this purpose. No foreign interests can be allowed to take over Singapore through financing and manipulating political parties through their naive or vain, self-proclaimed leaders. Whether fools or knaves, they must be saved from their own follies or villainies when powerful groups set out to use them.

Column: 13



    However, this legislation will not prevent my Government, at present or in the future, from joining other countries inside or outside the region, in arrangements for mutual security, or for economic purposes, such as a free trade area, and perhaps, eventually, even a common market.

    Legislation will also be introduced to ensure that all political parties will have their party expenditure and income periodically inspected. The inspection will be done by Supreme Court Judges, with the help of accountants, auditors and other expert examiners. The purpose of inspection will be to discover whether foreign money has been injected directly or indirectly into any of our political parties. Where the Judge is satisfied that donations are genuinely from Singapore donors, their identities and all other information will not be disclosed, not even to the Government.

    These are present safeguards for future contingencies. For the immediate

Column: 14

present, there are five challenging years ahead. They will not be free from the unexpected. But an alert people, in trim, and prepared to meet sudden dangers, and to respond swiftly to sudden changes, have a more than even chance of overcoming their problems. In the next five years, may the deliberations of honourable Members help us all to achieve, in qualitative terms, what, in the last five, we have achieved in quantitative terms.

ADDENDUM TO PRESIDENTIAL ADDRESS AT THE OPENING OF

THE FIRST SESSION OF THE THIRD PARLIAMENT ON

THURSDAY, 12TH OCTOBER, 1972

MINISTRY OF FINANCE


    For the next few years, the principal objective of financial and economic policy in the Republic is to sustain the momentum of economic growth achieved in the last few years. The package of incentives, the build-up of infrastructure, the sustained effort at industrial promotion in overseas countries and the restructuring of our financial institutions to support economic expansion, they have contributed to the present state of Singapore's economic well-being.

    To sustain the momentum that has been achieved, a policy of "more of the same" would not be entirely adequate. This is because Singapore's targets as well as the socio-economic environment itself are not those of the past. Today, it is no longer a matter of inducing more and more industries into Singapore, whatever the cost. It is possible to be somewhat more discriminating; to seek industries that do not ruin the environment, industries which hold good prospects for the continuous upgrading of manpower skills, be they plant operatives, supervisors and technicians, or higher level engineering and scientific staff, and industries that have a catalytic effect on the economy, encouraging a growth of allied and supporting industries.

    These objectives, however, have to be reached under conditions totally different from those of five years ago. There is no large pool of cheap unemployed workers. But, on the other hand, Singapore's vocational institutes, industrial training centres, Polytechnic and universities are turning out each year more and more of the skilled manpower that industry needs. And work permits are issued, liberally, to those industries and enterprises which upgrade the skills of the workers, or where the level of sophistication of the products manufactured is high.

    A more refined economic policy is required in the new situation to ensure that intending investors, both from overseas

Column: 16

as well as our own citizens, will be able to obtain the essential factors of production - capital, labour and land - in such proportions, grades, skills and quantity as they may require.

    If we succeed in this, we should see a continuation of the fast rate of economic growth of previous years, subject to one proviso. This is that no severe disruption of the international monetary system will occur to seriously affect world trade. An economy like Singapore's, highly geared to export markets of the world, is extremely sensitive to changes in conditions in world trade. There is little that Singapore can do to promote the badly needed reforms of the international monetary system. What it can do is to protect itself against the adverse effects of economic troubles created by such problems. Fortunately for us, sufficient reserves have been accumulated in the past which we can use judiciously to cushion off the worst effects of such crises. Also, the further development of an efficient and highly sophisticated banking and financial sector will help in providing solutions to monetary problems.

    Should conditions be more or less as favourable as in the last five years, our economy will continue its robust rate of growth. The Ministry of Finance should be able to meet its other main obligation, to provide sufficient funds to other ministries for their current as well as development needs. While changes in tax rates and additional taxation may from time to time, be necessary or desirable to effect social or economic policies, continued economic growth will generate the increased funds to be made available to expanding ministries as in the past. As long as the economy is buoyant, and labour-management relations sensible and reasonable, there will be no need to impose major or painful increases in tax rates. But it will be necessary to constantly review the tax machinery to ensure a high level of efficiency, particularly to ferret out tax evasion.

Column: 17



    While rapid economic growth has given tax payers the same tax rates for nearly seven years now, it has created a major problem for the government services. As the demand for skills and talent increases, the problem of attracting and retaining high calibre personnel in the government services has become increasingly severe. It is necessary to reexamine once again the structure of pay and service conditions and to make the rewards in certain departments, ministries, grades and professions more comparable to what they can get in the private sector. This is not a new problem. But whereas in the past we had resorted to ad hoc solutions, it is now necessary to look for a more fundamental and lasting solution.

    Concurrently with increased rewards for, and consequently higher costs of, Government personnel, it will be necessary, by greater mechanisation, and computerisation, to streamline the organisation of the Government and the Statutory Boards to increase efficiency and productivity.

    Finally, to achieve quality in jobs, homes, schools, hospitals, social and recreational amenities, much more money will have to be spent on capital equipment, on more intensive and more prolonged training of skilled workers and technicians. We must increase our capital by adding to it what foreign investors can bring in by way of machinery and foreign exchange in manufacturing industry and finance. More expenditure will go into educating the professionals, engineers, scientists, especially in Applied Sciences, computers and systems analysis, accountants, architects, doctors, managers, bankers, lawyers, economists, and other professionals whose expertise is part and parcel of an industrial society. We are producing more and more middle-technology products, whilst developing into an international banking and financial centre.

Column: 18

MINISTRY OF DEFENCE


    The urgency over our defence build-up has lessened as we now have some defence capability. The emphasis must now be to train men to higher levels of competence, and among the regulars, to develop an increasing sense of professionalism. Further, the range, quality and intensity of training of technical and engineering personnel must be given the highest priority as a modern armed force uses weapons of great complexity and sophistication.

    With proper selection of personnel, with improved systems of training, with a clear-cut policy of career development, we shall achieve quality in commanders and staff officers in whom we entrust so large a proportion of our young men. Then the prodigious effort and enormous sums of money we have put into defence would yield returns in security and stability which an efficient combat force of high discipline, morale and professional skill will provide.

Column: 18

MINISTRY OF FOREIGN AFFAIRS


    We live in vastly a changed world. We are more likely to safeguard what we have all built if we face squarely these changed conditions and make the adjustments, however inconvenient or unpleasant.

    The super powers and the major powers have changed their relationships with each other. They were dramatised by President Nixon's visit to Peking in February, and to Moscow in May. The Japanese Prime Minister's visit to Peking in September in which 23 years of dogma that Taiwan was China was thrown overboard showed how swiftly they have changed their policies to meet a changed situation.

    But as conflict between the super powers themselves is reduced, so their competition for power and influence will sharpen in third countries, in the Middle East, and Southeast Asia. Singapore is neutral and non-aligned insofar as we are asked to take sides between competing power blocs or competing ideologies. But, where our

Column: 19

survival is concerned, we cannot afford to be neutral. We will not stand nonaligned if we are threatened by superior force. However there is no need for the same haste as in 1968, in the build-up of our defence capability.

    In conjunction with our neighbours, through ASEAN, we hope Southeast Asia will remain an area of relative peace and stability, so that now we and our neighbours can concentrate our efforts and resources on the reconstruction of our societies and re-development of our economies. Perhaps eventually a neutral Southeast Asia can be agreed upon and guaranteed by the major powers. But let us not forget that the Swiss were neutral but not unarmed in two world wars. It was because they were capable of considerable defence that it was not necessary for them to demonstrate their capacity. Warring parties respected their neutrality because not to do so would have meant large forces being despatched and tied down, entangled with the Swiss military forces.

Column: 19

MINISTRY OF HOME AFFAIRS


Modernisation of the Police

    With the implementation of the new salaries and conditions of service of junior officers, the Police Force will have officers of better quality and educational standards.

Personnel

    (a) By the end of 1974, all junior officers without a Secondary II education will have been retired. Those who wish to may join in the newly established Commercial and Industrial Security Corporation. The consequential vacancies will be filled by recruitment of junior officers with School Certificate education or its equivalent.

    (b) The reorganisation of the Police Force involves a considerable devolution of responsibilities and duties on to sergeants and constables. These duties had formerly been performed by inspectors and sergeants respectively. A flew training programme designed to

Column: 20

develop and increase the capacity of junior officers to fulfil these functions is already underway. It should be completed over the next three years. Once this devolution of responsibilities has been completed, it will be necessary to review the officer structure and strength of the Police Force.

Criminal investigation

    A centralised Criminal Intelligence Unit has been the subject of detailed study by a committee. Their recommendations have been accepted for implementation.

    An adviser on criminal investigation is being appointed from the United Kingdom Police, with the specific objective of upgrading investigative skills in the Criminal Investigation Department as well as improving the standards of detective training.

    The forensic capability available to the Police Force is developing in close co-ordination with the Chemistry Department.

Prevention of Crime

    Beginning from 1973, a system of team policing will be introduced in two Divisions to replace the traditional beat system. Team policing involves much more use of transport and communications. But it can achieve flexibility in crime prevention patrols. If this system is successful under Singapore conditions, team policing will replace traditional beat system in all Divisions.

Drainage

Air Pollution

    We should now be more careful in the types of industries that are allowed. Heavy pollution industries will not be allowed. Existing industries which are causing too much pollution will have to install anti-pollution devices, or they will have to move elsewhere.
