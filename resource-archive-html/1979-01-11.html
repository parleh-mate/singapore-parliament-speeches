     Mr Teo Chong Tee (Changi): Mr Speaker, Sir, when the House rose yesterday I was speaking on the Addendum on the Ministry of National Development. I would like to continue today.

    The administrative procedures of the Housing and Development Board should be revamped to cut down unnecessary queuing time. People who go to the HDB offices either to register for flats or to collect keys to their flats have to queue for as long as half a day, if not more. This is unhealthy as hundreds of man-hours are wasted each day, and many people are subjected to a lot of inconvenience. People who queue up at HDB offices to apply for flats or to collect keys to their flats have to take time-off from their work or business. To these people, time is precious. Some of them, after queuing for a long time, are even turned away and told to return later because they have failed to follow certain administrative procedures due to ignorance. Their anger and frustration are often unpleasantly expressed in heated arguments with HDB officials at the service counters. This, in fact, is happening every day.

    Previously, when large numbers of people called at the HDB office to apply for flats, it was unavoidable that applicants had to queue up for some time. However, now that the number of applicants has reduced significantly, they should not be made to wait too long before being attended to. The HDB should find out what is wrong with the

administrative system and rectify the situation immediately. As for applicants who go to the HDB office to collect the keys to their flats, they should be expeditiously attended to. The HDB should know, through past experience, the number of people it can serve a day. Therefore, it should space out the appointments with applicants so as to minimise queuing time.

    Sir, in recent years the Building Control Division of the Ministry has been spending considerable time and effort in the rural areas demolishing structures which were found to have been constructed not in conformity with the building laws. It is understandable that demolition action has to be taken against newly-built private dwellings and structures which were constructed without prior approval from the BCD. However, where the structure is small, in the form of extension by the kampong folk and it has been in existence for many years, the BCD is requested to be sympathetic and not to be too harsh on them. Their appeals for retention should be kindly considered. Many of the rural folk, in their ignorance of the BCD requirements and needs, have somehow committed minor infractions of the building law, such as putting up corrugated zinc awnings in front of their wooden houses, additional bathrooms, kitchens and other minor extensions. In spite of the fact that these structures are old and in poor condition, the owners are still served with demolition notices, as they are not able to produce documentary proof to justify their claim that the shed or structures have been in existence for many years.

    Such unauthorised structures are bound to appear in the rural areas. It is only a matter of time before the rural folks are resettled in Housing and Development Board flats in view of our fast developing programmes. It is my hope that this matter can be favourably reviewed by the BCD which should divert more of its energies to the bigger projects and problems rather than to small issues and minor projects. I am sure many of my colleagues in this House will fully share my sentiments.

    Developed countries have always expressed their desire to help developing

countries in their economic development. But the economic policies of these countries show that they are more interested in protecting their own economies.     The proposed low air fares between London and Australia is an indication that Australia is taking advantage of her economic strength to cover up her weaknesses. Already our trade balance with Australia is not in our favour. Instead of taking measures to correct the imbalance in trade, Australia imposed tariff and import restrictions on our goods. Besides, travellers should be allowed to break their journey at reasonable cost. Furthermore, if Australia is sincere in offering cheaper travel, the airfares between Australia and the ASEAN countries should also be lowered to encourage people to travel between ASEAN countries and Australia.

It is not formulated with the travellers at heart, but with the aim of selfishly restricting travellers between Australia and London to Qantas and British Airways.     I propose to present my comments in two ways.

    The policies presented to us through the Addenda cover a wide variety of topics. Notwithstanding this, I see two unifying and inter-related themes which underlie them. Firstly, there are policies which reflect the long-term problem that concerns all of us. This is the fundamental question: "How we Singaporeans living on a small island, devoid of natural resources, seek to earn a decent living for ourselves and our children and also our children's children in such a way that will safeguard our national integrity, yet earn respectability, regionally and internationally."

    The new and promotive policies set out by the Minister for Finance in regard to monetary and fiscal policies, industrial development and trade development clearly reflect the attempt to upgrade the economic basis upon which we depend to fulfil our long-term national programmes. Similarly, the proposals to upgrade the use of manpower and development of leadership by the Minister of Defence serve to further refine the organisation of manpower in the SAF which, in turn, will consolidate the build-up of the SAF which the Minister says has been practically completed.

    The Addenda on the Ministries of National Development, Communications and Education also contain various programmes which attempt to make our island republic more amenable to and responsive towards rational development in the various tasks of social and economic development programmes.

    The second category which I see from the various Addenda are policies which reflect short term efforts. They are policies which reflect our effort to improve what we have achieved in the past; to adjust to economic and social imperatives which we are subjected to; or to seek new and better targets as a response to demographic, social, economic and political conditions and trends that have an impact on us.

    Though the Ministerial Addenda are in a sense unified by these two underlying themes they, however, vary to a certain degree in the following ways.

    Firstly, they are policies which clearly seek higher and new targets through consolidation, expansion and restructuring programmes. Secondly, they are policies which, though consolidating in nature, are quite tied up with resolving long-standing problems. Thirdly, there are those which are primarily preoccupied with resolving long-standing problems or are not yet geared towards vigorous consolidation or new programmes.

    Again, the Ministry of Defence is the best example of the first type. The Minister's Addendum strongly reflects the Ministry's confidence and readiness to upgrade programmes. The Minister is satisfied that the primary task of a build-up of the SAF is practically completed. The Ministry is now geared to undertake new tasks in improving the quality of its personnel, introducing computerisation of allocating technically qualified national servicemen and upgrading SAF leaders. Only two programmes in the Minister's Addendum are essentially continuing in nature.

    The Ministerial Addenda on Finance, Communications and Science and Technology also contain exciting policies which project the predisposition and ability of these Ministries to embark upon major expansion and new programmes.

The Ministries of the Environment, National Development, Home Affairs, Health, and Culture seem to fit quite well under this classification.     The tendency is that expanding departments will be given greater priority (as it should be) but the problems will arise when this is done without sufficient regard to service departments where the problems are more intractable and are less quantifiable.
