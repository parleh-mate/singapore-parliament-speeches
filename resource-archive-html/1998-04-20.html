SINGAPORE-INDONESIA
BILATERAL TRADE FINANCE GUARANTEE SCHEME

(Details)


    1. Mr Zainul Abidin Rasheed asked the Prime Minister if he will give more details on the bilateral trade financing guarantee scheme that he has offered to assist the Indonesian Government to overcome the currency and economic crisis.

Column: 1892



    2. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Prime Minister how soon can the proposed US$3 billion bilateral trade financing scheme for Indonesia be implemented and whether the details of the proposed scheme can be given to businessmen early to prepare themselves to resume trade with their counterparts in Indonesia quickly.

    The Prime Minister (Mr Goh Chok Tong): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to take Questions 1 and 2 together as they are both related?

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    Mr Goh Chok Tong: Thank you. The Singapore Government originally proposed a Multilateral Trade Finance Guarantee Scheme to help resolve the trade finance problems faced by Indonesian firms. However, the G7 countries, whose participation is vital since they are the largest trading partners of Indonesia, prefer their own bilateral trade financing schemes.

    On 28th February 1998, then Minister for Finance Mar'ie Muhammad asked our Finance Minister Richard Hu to have Singapore launch the Multilateral Trade Finance Guarantee scheme on our own. On 2nd March 1998, then Minister for Trade and Industry Tunky Ariwibowo repeated the request to our Ambassador in Jakarta, Edward Lee. On 20th March, in Jakarta, the Minister for Trade and Industry Muhammad Hasan made the same request to Ambassador Lee.

    In response to these requests, Singapore decided to launch a Singapore-Indonesia Bilateral Trade Finance Guarantee (BTFG) Scheme. I wrote to President Soeharto on 31st March informing him of Singapore's decision to establish the BTFG after Indonesia and the IMF have reached an agreement on their latest round of negotiations. He called me up in London on 2nd April to thank Singapore for its decision.

    The BTFG is a bilateral scheme to enhance confidence in trade financing transactions undertaken by banks in Indonesia. The aim is to help restore established trade between the two countries.

Column: 1893



    As Singapore has no experience designing or operating a government-sponsored trade finance guarantee scheme, we have to start cautiously and modestly. Initially, the scheme will only provide export credit insurance for our domestic exports to Indonesia. After we have gained experience with the scheme, we will expand it to cover retained imports from Indonesia. Singapore will use US$3 billion out of the US$5 billion that it has earlier pledged to Indonesia for its IMF package to back this facility.

    We will eventually expand the scheme further to cover exports through Singapore by Indonesia's trading partners who do not have their own bilateral export credit schemes. The remaining US$2 billion of the US$5 billion that Singapore has pledged to Indonesia will be used for this purpose.

    Conceptually, we have the following mechanism in mind. ECICS Credit Insurance Ltd will administer the Scheme. ECICS is an unlisted public company that is in the business of insuring Singapore exports against risks of non-payment. The Singapore Government will select several banks operating in Indonesia to be designated as Trade Finance Banks (TFBs). We will also identify a group of Singapore exporters to participate in the scheme.

    Indonesian importers will approach one of the approved TFBs to issue a Letter of Credit for a sales transaction secured with an approved Singapore exporter. The TFB will bear the commercial risk on this transaction. The Letter of Credit will be presented to the Singapore-based bank of the Singapore exporter. Bank Indonesia will guarantee fully the obligations of the TFB to this Singapore-based bank. The Singapore Government, in turn, counter-guarantees part of Bank Indonesia's obligations under the scheme. The Singapore exporters, the Singapore-based bank and ECICS will share in some of this risk. This is necessary to ensure that each party in the scheme does due diligence.

    If the TFB fails to honour its obligation on the Letter of Credit it has issued, Bank Indonesia's unconditional guarantee will apply.

    The Scheme will run for an initial period of two years or until the Indonesian trade finance cycle is restored, whichever is earlier. However, the Government of Singapore reserves the right to discontinue the Scheme in the event of a change in circumstances which renders it no longer relevant or valid.

    Singapore and Indonesian officials will meet to flesh out the scheme soon. I am unable to say now the exact date when the BTFG scheme will come into operation. We will need some time to work out the procedures and rules to create an effective scheme which will achieve its objectives. We are seeking help from international experts. We know its importance for Indonesia and will endeavour to get it going as soon as possible.

Out of the total 468 illegal foreign workers arrested in January and February this year, 117 were immigration offenders.     5. Mr Low Thia Khiang asked the Minister for Trade and Industry whether the Government has made any study on the possibility of using underground water resources in Singapore to supplement our water supply.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Lee Yock Suan): Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore's water supply comes entirely from the collection of surface runoffs from rainfall. The findings reveal that there are no significant ground water sources available, even at relatively great depths.

Introduction

    Sir, the number of foreign maids has increased from around 80,000 in December 1993 to more than 100,000 in December 1997. We also have a few thousand locals working as maids. Many could be working part-time.

    Domestic maids are female, work within the confines of their employers' home for 24 hours of the day, and except during their time-off, are isolated from the rest of society nearly all the time, and depend on their employer for food and lodging. Maids are therefore more vulnerable to

They get upset easily when their maids do not meet their expectations. Some even physically abuse them. Maid abuse could be in the form of simple assault or causing serious hurt. Some employers took advantage of their maids by outraging their modesty.

Of the 192 reported cases in 1997, 157 cases were substantiated with evidence, and appropriate actions had been or are being taken against the offenders. Although the number is still small relative to the maid population in Singapore, we should curb such incidents before they get out of hand.

The recent case of Hartati, the Indonesian maid, for example, was widely reported in the Indonesian media. Jakarta imposed a temporary ban on maids coming to Singapore, following that incident.

Enhanced Penalties

    My Ministry proposes to designate certain offences in the Penal Code to carry 1.5 times the normal maximum penalties, if perpetrated upon a domestic maid (regardless of whether she is a local or a foreigner) by the employer or a member of the employer's household. This will signal clearly to employers of domestic maids that the Government takes a serious view of maid abuse.

    Specifically, these offences are:

    (1) Hurt or Grievous Hurt (section 323, section 324 and section 325) of the Penal Code;

Column: 1925



    (2) Wrongful Confinement (section 342, section 343 and section 344);

    (3) Assault or use of Criminal Force with intent to outrage modesty (section 354); and

    (4) Insult of Modesty (section 509).

    Simple hurt and outraging of modesty are the two most common forms of maid abuse. These two offences together constitute about 92% of all cases in the last four years.

    Clause 2 of the Bill creates a new section 73 in the Penal Code, prescribing that the maximum penalties for the offences I have listed would be 1.5 times what the offender would otherwise have been liable for, when the victim is a domestic maid, and the offender is the employer or a member of the employer's household. For example, the current maximum penalty for voluntarily causing hurt is 1 year's imprisonment, or up to $1,000 fine, or both. The maximum penalty will become 1.5 years' imprisonment, or up to $1,500 fine, or both. Clause 2 will also make any attempt to commit or abet these offences punishable with the enhanced penalties.

Applicability of enhanced penalties

    Sir, the enhanced penalties will apply whether the offences take place inside or outside the employer's place of residence. This is because the abuse need not necessarily take place within the four walls of the employer's home. Employer's household will be defined as family members of the maid's employer who are residing in the same home as the employer and the maid. It will exclude visiting relatives and friends, as they do not exercise any authority over the maid.

    The enhanced penalties will also not apply in situations such as when a maid's employer sends her to work in another household, say, his brother's home on an ad hoc basis, and a member of that household, say, his brother, subsequently abuses her. Of course, the employer should not have sent the maid to work in his brother's home in the first place, as

this is against the Employment of Foreign Workers Act. But should he do that, the enhanced penalties would not apply because like visiting relatives and friends, the brother does not exercise any real authority over the maid.

    By the definition of domestic maid in the Bill, the enhanced penalties will also not apply to part-time maids. This is because part-time maids maintain their network of family and friends, and are consequently less vulnerable to abuse.

    Since I first introduced the Amendment Bill in this House, I have received feedback that some employers are concerned that their maids may exploit the special protection provided to them under the Amendment Bill to get out of their contracts or to spite their employers. Let me assure Members that we will continue to safeguard employers' interests against such maids. Police will warn and, where appropriate, prosecute a maid for giving false information to a public servant, under the Penal Code. Foreign maids who misconduct themselves stand to lose their work permit and can also be debarred from working in Singapore.

Jurisdiction

    The Magistrate's and District Courts are currently empowered, under the Criminal Procedure Code, to impose sentences up to two years' and seven years' imprisonment respectively. To enable these Courts to continue to hear maid abuse cases carrying the enhanced penalties, clause 2 of the Bill provides for these Courts to continue to have jurisdiction over such cases and impose the enhanced penalties.
