(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.36 p.m.

    Dr Toh Chin Chye: Mr Speaker, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This amendment Bill is being introduced partly in the light of experience that has been acquired by the Elections Office over the past years. As the list of claimants after the register of electors and Lists A and B have been exhibited to the public has increased enormously from about 9,000 in 1959 to 22,500 in 1963, more time is now required for the registration of these claimants. This is provided for in clause 2 of the Bill.

    Clause 3 proposes an amendment to reduce the period between the date of issue of the Writ of Election and Nomination Day from nine days to five days. I would like to point out that this is in accord with the practice in the United Kingdom where from the time the Returning Officer receives the Writ up to the Day of Nomination, the period can be calculated to be about five days. In the United Kingdom, however, this period of five days is the maximum and cannot be increased by statute, whereas in Singapore the provision which we are asking now of five days should be the minimum period and this period could be increased to more than five days, if at any time it is desired to do so.

Column: 1051



    The amendment in clause 4 is consequential to the amendment in clause 3 if this is accepted. It seeks to reduce the period between the publication of the Notice of Election by the Returning Officer and Nomination Day from five days to four days. This is in line with the reduction of the period between the date of issue of the Writ of Election and Nomination Day as proposed in clause 3. The number of days between the Notice of Election by the Returning Officer and Nomination Day can be calculated to be the same as in the United Kingdom. It is considered that the publication of the notice by the Returning Officer at least four clear days before Nomination Day is sufficient notice to prospective candidates.

    Clause 5 suggests removing the limitation in the present Ordinance which stipulates that Polling Day shall be a Saturday. There is, in fact, no justification for this limitation at all. There has been some doubt as to whether Polling Day being a holiday should or should not be counted for purposes of determining the minimum number of days required between the publication by the Returning Officer of the notice of contested elections and Polling Day. This amendment is for the purpose of clearing that doubt.

    The amendments proposed in clause 6 follow the provision in the National Referendum Ordinance, which are necessary to ensure that on Polling Day there is no canvassing or undue influence exercised on electors who should be allowed to consider the platform of the various political Parties and candidates and vote according to their best judgment and free will.

    The intention of clause 7 is to prohibit secret society gangsters who are under Police Supervision Order from taking part in any election activity.

    Finally, clause 8 empowers the Returning Officer to use free of charge as a Polling Station any school or portion thereof for the purposes of any election. In the past it has been found that on occasions persons who were in charge of the premises of certain private

Column: 1052

schools were reluctant to allow the Returning Officer to use the schools as polling stations. Although the amendment empowers the Returning Officer to use such schools as polling stations, the power will not be exercised except in places where no other suitable building or place where a temporary building could be erected can be found. This applies mainly to the crowded city area where it is difficult to find suitable alternative buildings or vacant sites for temporary buildings to serve as polling stations.

    The Government is conscious of the importance of the tourist industry as a source of income and employment. Everybody knows the great potential of tourism in Singapore by virtue of its international name, and its strategic location on international air and sea routes. This potential needs to be exploited and this is best carried out by the joint efforts of both private business and Government. With this in view, the Government propose to establish a Tourist Promotion Board on which both business and Government will be represented.

    The main functions of the Board will be to publicise Singapore and its attractions in the principal tourist markets, to co-ordinate the activities of tourist enterprises such as hotels, airlines, shipping lines, travel agencies, tour operators, tourist shops, to regulate standards to be maintained by tourist enterprises so that they compare favourably with standards elsewhere, and generally to improve facilities for visitors to Singapore. We must seek not only to attract visitors to Singapore, but we must also see that they go away satisfied so that when the word gets around more visitors will come to Singapore.

Column: 1087



    As regards the composition of the Board, Members will note that five of these will represent business interests as against four representing official interests. I think it is proper that those in business should be given adequate representation in a Board of this nature.

    The activities of the Board will be financed by funds from the Tourist Promotion Fund (clause 11) to be established under this Ordinance. Both business and the Government have a stake in the tourist industry and accordingly contributions to the Fund will be from both business and the Government. Initially, the Tourist Promotion Fund will be financed from a small cess of 3 per cent on every room or suite occupied each day in every tourist hotel as well as from a subvention from the Government.     Treasury Bills of 91 days' maturity were issued on tap to statutory bodies as and when required, and are offered to the public every month on tender. Tap bills carry 4 per cent interest per year, tender bills average 4.1 per cent interest per year. The amounts outstanding on these issues are: Tap bills $22.8 million, and Tender bills $17 million.

    It is the Government's intention to encourage the growth of a short-term money market in Singapore. There is evidence that one or two discount houses or short-term money market dealers are coming into operation. These dealers should be provided with an ample supply of Treasury Bills which should be the main stock in trade.
