FREE TRADE AGREEMENTS
(Benefits)


    1. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) what kind of programmes does the Government plan to initiate in order to reap the benefits that will be derived from the recent successful Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) reached between Singapore and various countries; and (b) how can the recently retrenched employees from the banking sector be reskilled and trained to take up job opportunities arising from the successful FTAs.

    The Senior Minister of State for Trade and Industry (Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam) (for the Minister for Trade and Industry): Mr Speaker, Sir, our FTAs enhance Singapore's attractiveness to investors and strengthen our hub position in Asia. This is because the market access benefits and other preferential treatment that our FTAs carry will enhance the exports of companies in Singapore. Singapore companies and foreign

Column: 1488

investors will find it more attractive to invest in Singapore, thus generating more employment opportunities for Singaporeans.

    Our efforts to ensure that businesses are able to benefit from the FTAs begin well before negotiations are concluded. In the course of the negotiations, we actively seek feedback and suggestions from the private sector, in order to find out where the problems are. Armed with this information, we are able to negotiate a better agreement for companies. For the FTAs that are still under negotiation, we strongly encourage companies and business organisations like the Singapore Business Federation to play an even more active role in this process.

    Once an FTA is concluded, our focus turns to helping businesses understand the agreement so as to take advantage of them.

    First, we seek to reach out to the general business community through the media, the Internet and seminars. Such large-scale programmes allow us to create a general awareness of FTAs.

    Second, we recognise that a focused approach will be more effective in helping individual companies understand how they can directly benefit from the Agreements. Therefore, our economic agencies have also been in touch with Singapore-based companies to see how they can specifically leverage on FTA provisions to gain a greater competitive edge.

    Companies may not always want to reveal details on how they organise their operations to Government agencies. Instead, they may wish to tap on their legal or accounting advisors or other consultants to advise them on how to take advantage of the FTAs. We would therefore welcome efforts by the legal and accounting professions, as well as management consultants, to develop capabilities to be able to advise their clients on international trade and FTA issues.

    Ultimately, the extent to which Singapore benefits from these FTAs will

depend on our people and businesses. Singapore businesses must seize the opportunities afforded by our FTAs to grow and expand internationally.

    At the same time, Singapore workers must be prepared to retrain and prepare themselves for the new job opportunities that will be created as a result of the FTAs. The FTAs will not help us to hold on to jobs that are no longer competitive, whether in the manufacturing or services sectors.

    Workers can take advantage of MOM's retraining schemes, such as the WorkSkills Training Programme (WSTP), the Self Employment Training Programme (SET), the Strategic Manpower Conversion Programme (SMCP) and the Skills Redevelopment Programme (SRP). Retrenched banking sector employees who are subsequently re-employed in the financial sector, can also take advantage of MAS' Financial Sector Development Fund Financial Training Scheme to upgrade their technical expertise.

    We encourage workers and unions to look to the new opportunities provided by the FTAs, and take advantage of the resources provided by the Government to prepare themselves well for these opportunities.

RECESSION
(Assistance to companies)


    2. Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Trade and Industry in view of his statement that Singapore may slide into another recession, (a) how long and how deep will this coming recession be, and (b) what additional measures are being taken to help small and big Singapore companies to tide over this difficult period.

    Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam (for the Minister for Trade and Industry): Mr Speaker, Sir, MTI's growth forecast for this year has been revised downwards to 2-2.5%. For next year, we are forecasting 2.0 - 5.0%, although there is considerable

Column: 1491

uncertainty because of the state of the global economy and the possibility of war in Iraq.

    Our growth forecast for this year reflects expectations of continued positive growth in the fourth quarter on a year-on-year basis, in other words, compared to the fourth quarter of last year. This is because our key exports markets are still growing, albeit more slowly. However, should GDP contract on a quarter-on-quarter basis in the fourth quarter, as happened in the third quarter, we will technically be in recession. We do not expect this to happen, but we cannot rule out the possibility.

    In an environment as uncertain as today's, there can be little certainty in short term economic forecasts. If US consumption does not fall sharply in the fourth quarter, and the major economies do not stumble badly, we should be able to achieve positive quarter-on-quarter growth in the fourth quarter and avoid a technical recession.

    Singapore's economic performance next year will also depend very much on how various external events pan out. We could see a good recovery in the economy in the second half of 2003 if the US has by then worked through the after-effects of the investment bubble of the late 1990s, and if confidence in the region around us is sustained.

    As mentioned in this House last month, several of the measures the Government implemented in July and October last year to help companies during this difficult period are still in place. These include JTC and HDB rental rebates, the cut in foreign workers' levy and concessions on port dues. We have also made enhancements to the Local Enterprise Technical Assistance Scheme (LETAS) and the Local Enterprise Finance Scheme (LEFS).

(LEFS), and help SMEs get better access to financing. Unlike LEFS, which stipulates a fixed interest rate, LIS gives participating financial institutions the flexibility to decide on interest rates according to the risk profile of the borrower. This increases the chance of success of an SME's loan application. Private credit risk insurers insure 50 per cent of the loan against default. The Government co-shares the cost of the insurance premium with the SME.

    The Micro Loan Programme was also set up in November last year, to provide easier access to loans for very small enterprises to meet their working capital needs. Since its launch in November last year, almost 1,000 firms have benefited from this scheme with a total of $32 million in loans.

    Finally, the Government has also implemented reductions in corporate income taxes, and postponed the restoration of the CPF contribution rates. These will help all businesses during this difficult period.

    The Government is monitoring the economy closely. It is looking at whether some of the earlier economic relief measures need to be extended, and whether additional ways to help businesses and workers tide over the difficult period are necessary.

CODE ON RELIGIOUS HARMONY


    4. Ms Braema Mathiaparanam asked the Prime Minister (a) if he will clarify how the Code on Religious Harmony can be applied by Singaporeans; (b) from whom individuals can seek counsel when the Code is flouted; (c) what kind of remedial action can be taken when it is flouted; and (d) whether he will consider setting up a mediation council comprising different religious leaders to resolve conflicts arising from non-compliance with the Code on Religious Harmony.

    The Minister of State, Prime Minister's Office (Mr Chan Soo Sen) (for the Prime Minister): The proposed Code on Religious Harmony is meant to be a set of guiding principles on how Singaporeans should practise their religion when they interact with one another in our multi-racial, multi-religious, society. As Singapore has already achieved many years of religious harmony, I think the Code, when it is completed, will look rather familiar. However, it is still useful to remind ourselves of all these basic principles which have helped, and will continue to help, achieve and maintain religious harmony in Singapore.

    Therefore, the Code is actually a reflection of moral values and social norms. It is not a piece of legislation. Hence, there will be no penalties if the Code is flouted. If someone acts in such a manner as to violate religious harmony, there are existing mechanisms to deal with them, and it will not be dealt with by the Code.

    For example, under the Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act, the Minister for Home Affairs is empowered to issue restraining orders of up to a maximum of two years against persons inciting religious misunderstanding or causing inter-religious hostility. Any person who breaches the restraining order shall be

Column: 1496

guilty of an offence and be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or to both. In the case of a second or subsequent offence, the person will be liable to a fine not exceeding $20,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding three years or to both. The Act also provides for the establishment of a Presidential Council for Religious Harmony. The Chairman and every member of the Council are appointed by the President on the advice of the Presidential Council for Minority Rights for a period of three years. The Council considers and reports to the Minister for Home Affairs on matters affecting the religious harmony in Singapore which are referred to the Council by the Minister or by Parliament.

I will listen to and, as far as possible, incorporate all relevant views. The Code will be referred to the Presidential Council for Religious Harmony for consideration before it is finalised.

SELF-HELP GROUPS
(Review of model)


    5. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Acting Minister for Community Development and Sports, after September 11 and the recent development of Jemaah Islamiyah (JI) activities, (a) are there plans to (i) review the model of self-help groups and (ii) enhance the concept of cohesiveness by amalgamating them under one umbrella body without disrupting their individuality at the same time; and (b) what more can be done and how can we ensure that there is more collaboration of resources among these self-help groups.

    The Acting Minister for Community Development and Sports (Assoc. Prof. Dr Yaacob Ibrahim): Sir, September 11 and the arrests of the Jemaah Islamiyah members reinforce the need for a concerted effort to build harmony and trust among Singaporeans. But they have nothing to do with the self-help groups' role in Singapore.

    The self-help groups were set up to help the underachievers in their community to level up. While national programmes and CDCs can cater to most Singaporeans, the self-help groups are more effective in reaching out to the last 5% that need help, given their cultural affinity, language and networks. Moreover, different communities have different priorities to address.     The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Finance (Mr Lee Hsien Loong): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Bill will amend the Stamp Duties Act to give legislative effect to the following measures:

    a) To specify the conditions under which stamp duty relief may be granted for gifts of property made as a consequence of marriage;

    b) To charge stamp duty on an arrangement to dispose of shares by cancelling existing shares and re-issuing new shares;

    c) To limit stamp duty exemption only to co-operative societies, instead of granting exemption of stamp duty to all parties transacting with co-operative societies;

    d) To give finality to the assessment by the Commissioner of Stamp Duties if the taxpayer does not object within 30 days from the date of assessment;

    e) To treat an exchange of properties as two distinct transactions and to levy two sets of buyer and seller stamp duty; and

    f) To charge ad valorem duty on transfer of shares and/or properties to a shareholder where he becomes a shareholder only after winding up proceedings have commenced.

    Sir, I shall now explain the main amendments in the Bill.

Transfers made in consideration of marriage

    It is common for parents to transfer property to their children, when they marry, to provide a matrimonial home for them. The current Act already provides stamp duty relief for property that is transferred to a newly-wedded couple by their family as a wedding gift. However, the

Column: 1537

conditions to qualify for this relief are not specified.

    Clause 3 of the Bill amends section 16 of the Act by inserting a new section 16(3A) to specify that the relief is only granted for the transfer of property to a party to the marriage. The property must be transferred from their spouse or a parent, grandparent or sibling of a party to the marriage. In addition, the transfer must take place within one year of the date of solemnisation of the marriage, and the property must be used as the matrimonial home of the couple. These conditions are put in place to ensure that the transfer represents a genuine gift to the couple for their marriage.

Stamp duty on disposal and re-issue of shares

    Currently, no stamp duty is levied on the cancellation and issue of shares. Thus, a company intending to sell existing shares to a buyer may avoid incurring stamp duty by cancelling existing shares followed with an allotment and issue of new shares to the same purchaser for a consideration. Clause 5 will close this loophole by amending section 33 of the Act to impose one set of stamp duty on a document that effects such an arrangement to cancel and re-issue shares.

Limiting stamp duty exemption only to co-operative societies

    Currently, section 36(e) exempts instruments executed by or on behalf of co-operative societies from stamp duty, if the underlying transaction in the instrument relates solely to the business of the society. Both the co-operative society and the party it enters into a contract with are exempted from stamp duty.

    Under the current laws, parties entering into such commercial agreements with co-operatives would stand to benefit from stamp duty exemption. There is no good reason for parties transacting with the co-operatives to enjoy the exemption from stamp duty, as they have entered into the agreement based on purely commercial considerations. Thus, clause 6

Column: 1538

amends section 36(e) of the Act to limit the exemption from stamp duty only to co-operative societies, and not to extend it to the parties transacting with co-operative societies.

Objections to the Commissioner's assessment

    Currently, objections to the Commissioner's assessment under section 37 of the Act must be made within 30 days. Section 16(3) of the Act also empowers the Commissioner to impose an additional dutiable amount when he assesses that the consideration is far lower than the market value. However, there is no finality to the time period within which a taxpayer can object to the Commissioner's assessment under section 37. As such, it is possible for a taxpayer to dispute the assessment many years after the assessment.

    Clause 8 amends section 39A(1) such that any objection to the Commissioner's assessment of an additional dutiable amount must be made within 30 days. The 30-day time period is the same as that currently provided in the Income Tax Act for a taxpayer to raise any objections to the assessment of income tax.

Levying two sets of Stamp Duty on exchanges of properties

    Presently, for an exchange of properties, only one set of stamp duty is payable, based on the property with the higher value. However, a transaction involving the exchange of properties between two parties should be treated as two transactions involving two parties each buying and selling a property at the same time. Hence, an exchange of properties should be levied with two sets of stamp duty. Clause 9 amends the First Schedule of the Act to effect this.

charged ad valorem duty on the transfer of shares or properties instead of a fixed duty of $10. This amendment will prevent opportunistic attempts to acquire the liquidating company's assets with minimal stamp duty.

Proper protection of such systems is critical to the effective functioning of the financial system. The Bill strengthens the legal environment for operating stable and secure payment and settlement systems by empowering the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) to designate payment and settlement systems.     Sir, before I go into the Bill proper, let me first give some background on the importance of protecting payment and settlement systems from disruptions.

Importance of protecting payment and settlement systems from disruptions

    Payment and settlement systems consist of sets of instruments, banking procedures and funds transfer systems that ensure the circulation of money. They are therefore an important part of the market and economic infrastructure. However, payment and settlement systems also provide a channel for shocks to be transmitted across financial markets. This is especially true in large value inter-bank payment and settlement systems that are systemic in nature, where one disruption could trigger or transmit further disruptions to the financial system. Take, for instance, MEPS, the Real-Time Gross Settlement system operated by the MAS for the settlement of funds between banks. In 2001, MEPS processed a total of $11.6 trillion of payments; this was more than 75 times the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of Singapore over the same year. Clearly, any disruption to the sound operation of MEPS would have a significant impact on Singapore's financial system. Therefore, payment and settlement systems with systemic implications like MEPS must have a robust architecture, well-planned contingency arrangements, proper operational procedures and clear rules for participants.

    It is equally important for these payment and settlement systems to have a sound legal basis. The rules, regulations and agreements relevant to such systems must be legally enforceable. In particular, transactions that have already been settled by the systems should be final and irrevocable, and must not be unwound or otherwise adjusted by any legal proceedings. Any netting arrangements in the payment and settlement systems should likewise be enforceable. While the payment and settlement systems should have contractual arrangements and rules in place to set out these provisions, existing laws can operate to override them. For instance, the law of insolvency allows a liquidator of an insolvent corporation to reverse, or "claw-back" payment transactions that have already been settled if these transactions are deemed to be unfair or to have been made at a significant

Column: 1541

undervalue. Such claw-back provisions are based on the general principle that favours the equal distribution of assets to the creditors of an insolvent corporation. This principle must, however, be balanced against the disruption that might be caused to payment and settlement systems and to the participants if it applies to a disposition made through these systems.

    There is therefore a strong case for the law to recognise the importance of the integrity and finality of transactions in payment and settlement systems in maintaining financial stability and for the law to protect these systems from the legal risks that I have just outlined. The legislatures in countries such as Australia, the United States of America, Canada and in many European countries have already taken steps to address these legal risks by enacting similar legislation.

Enhancing Singapore's Legal Framework to Protect Designated System

    In Singapore, section 59A of the Banking Act has provided for the finality and irrevocability of transactions made through MEPS since 1998. However, section 59A is not wide enough to cover payment and settlement systems that are not operated by MAS. We are an important international financial centre. With the continuing advancement of technology, greater competition and increased scale and sophistication of financial players, there will be new payment and settlement systems in Singapore that require the same legal protection. The Continuous Linked Settlement (CLS) system is one such system.

    The Continuous Linked Settlement system is a global payment and settlement system that aims to reduce foreign exchange (FX) settlement risk. Launched in September this year, with the participation of over sixty international banks and seven major currencies, CLS has already reached a daily settlement value of US$300 billion and it is expected eventually to settle a large percentage of the US$1.2 trillion a day global foreign exchange market. The Singapore Dollar

is expected to be included as a CLS currency in the second quarter of next year. The three local banks in Singapore have established the capability to join CLS as participants in December 2002. As one of the largest foreign exchange trading centres in the world, Singapore's participation in CLS will reinforce our position as a key node in the global financial system.

    The Payment and Settlement Systems (Finality and Netting) Bill will provide an omnibus solution to the legal risks that surround the operation of payment and settlement systems by according legal protection to systems that are designated by MAS. It will allow us to provide for the integrity and finality of transactions in systems such as MEPS and CLS, as well as any new payment and settlement systems that may be introduced in the future.

    Sir, I will now highlight the major provisions that are being introduced in this Bill.

Major Provisions in the Bill

    Clause 3 gives MAS the power to designate payment and settlement systems to provide them with the protection offered under this Bill. Clause 4 gives MAS the power to revoke the designation in certain circumstances. In determining whether to designate a system, MAS will take into account a number of factors, in particular the systemic risks associated with that system.

    Part II of the Bill sets out the protection that is accorded to transactions effected through a designated system when a participant in that designated system is bankrupt, in judicial management or winding up.

    Firstly, clause 7 gives statutory backing to any rule of the designated system that provides for the finality of fund transfers into and out of the accounts of participants, as well as the finality of transactions settled in the system.

    Secondly, clause 8 provides that the regime established by the rules of a designated system is to take precedence over the insolvency law principle of equal and pro-rated distribution of assets.

    Clauses 9 and 10 then go on to make clear that the legal provisions providing for the "claw-back" of transactions that are unfair preferences or transactions at an undervalue, and other like provisions, are not applicable to transactions effected through a designated system.

    Clause 11 gives effect to the default arrangements that are provided in the rules of the designated system and provides that only the net sum, following completion of action taken under the default arrangements, shall be provable or payable.

    Clause 12 establishes the end-date for the protection accorded by Part II. Transactions effected after the date the participant goes into insolvency will not have the benefit of the protection under Part II.

    In Part III of the Bill, clause 13 makes netting available to a designated system where a participant becomes insolvent. In such a situation, the operator of the designated system may net all obligations owed to or by the participant incurred before or on the date the participant goes into insolvency, with the result that only the net sum shall be provable or payable.

    Clause 14 fortifies the position by providing that a court in Singapore is not to recognise or give effect to an order from a foreign court in so far as that order would be contrary to the provisions of the Bill.

    Clause 15 clarifies that the protection accorded by the Bill will not affect the rights of persons arising from the underlying transaction or under the general law. For instance, if a person had made a payment to another by mistake, and this mistaken payment was routed through a designated system by banks that were participants in the designated system, the person who received the payment would not be immune from a legal action that might be available to the payer for the recovery of the mistaken payment. The Bill is intended to prevent the unwinding of transactions made through a

designated system. It is not intended to accord additional legal protection or benefit to the recipient at the end of the payment chain.

    As the protection given by the Bill will only apply to transactions entered into a designated system before or on the day a participant becomes insolvent, the system should not accept any transactions from a participant as soon as it has notification that the participant has become insolvent. To facilitate this, clause 16 of the Bill imposes an obligation on the participant that is insolvent to give notice of that fact to the operator of the designated system.

    Clause 17 protects MAS and its officers from liability in connection with any action taken in good faith in MAS' capacity as the designating authority.

    Clause 18 empowers MAS to make regulations for carrying out the purpose and provisions of the Bill.

    With the enactment of the Bill, MEPS will be a designated system and clause 19 accordingly provides for this.

    Clause 20 deals with the migration of the current section 59A of the Banking Act to the Monetary Authority of Singapore Act. It is more appropriate for the statutory provision relating to the operation by MAS of real-time gross settlement systems such as MEPS to be in the MAS Act. This will also make it possible to extend participation in such real-time gross settlement systems to institutions other than banks. Accordingly, section 59A of the Banking Act will be repealed and a new section 29A will be inserted into the MAS Act.

Conclusion

    In conclusion, Sir, the Bill provides the broad legal foundations for the operation of stable payment and settlement systems, thereby reducing the risk of systemic disruptions to Singapore's financial system. This is essential for Singapore's competitiveness and development as a major financial centre in the world.

    17. Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan asked the Minister for Education (a) what is the number and percentage of children entering primary 1 each year who are identified for the Learning Support Programme (LSP) since the Programme was introduced; (b) how are these children assessed to determine if they need to attend the Programme; (c) what is the standard against which the primary 1 children's reading abilities are benchmarked to determine if they need to attend the Programme; (d) what is the duration of the Programme and; (e) how effective has the Programme been.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    The Learning Support Programme (LSP) (English) is an early intervention language and literacy programme. The programme was started in 1992 and implemented in all primary schools in 1998. The need for this programme arises out of the complex home language background of our students, resulting in their entering Primary 1 with very different levels of literacy skills in English. The programme provides additional support to pupils in P1 and P2 who do not have the pre-requisite English Language and literacy skills to access the curriculum. Pupils in the LSP are identified through a screening test administered to all P1 pupils at the beginning of the school year. Each year, about 12% to 14% of P1 pupils, or some 7,000 pupils, are supported in the LSP.

    The effectiveness of the programme is measured through gains in children's reading levels and performance in school-based assessments. P1 LSP pupils start school reading at levels equivalent to children of less than 5 years of age, on average. After about 10 months of intervention in the LSP, the average reading age of LSP pupils is 7 years, reflecting a reading gain of 28 months. They are thus comparable with the P1 cohort in terms of their reading levels.

    Improvements in reading have helped the pupils do better in their English tests. For example, in 2001, less than half (45%) of P1 LSP pupils passed English at the mid-year Semestral Assessment (SA1). By SA2 in November, the pass rate was 64.9%.

    The duration of the Learning Support Programme is 2 years. At the end of P2, a small proportion of LSP pupils (about 15% of those originally identified at P1) continue to have difficulties in the English Language. Schools will determine the most appropriate mode of support for these pupils after the 2 years in the LSP programme. These pupils are put through customised remediation programmes conducted by the school, or the ENABLE (ENcouraging Achievement and Better LEarning), a specialised intervention programme in English and Mathematics for pupils who are underachieving.
