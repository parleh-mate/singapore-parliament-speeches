ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

PRICE INCREASES OF DAILY
FOODSTUFFS
(Committee of inquiry)


    2. Mr Yeo Toon Chia asked the Minister for Finance if he will consider setting up a committee to inquire into the reasons for the increases in the prices of daily foodstuffs in recent months in order to ensure that the public are not cheated by market stall-holders and shopkeepers.

    Mr Tang See Chim (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, it is not proposed to set up a committee to inquire into the reasons for price increases in recent months. As I already explained in some detail on Friday, in Singapore where a substantial proportion of daily foodstuffs are imported, prices are subject to fluctuations in supply. For some items the price increases in December/January are seasonal, due to the festive season.

    Where the main fluctuations in prices are due to such factors as mentioned above, a committee on prices cannot exert any influence on prices. This point was discussed by the then Minister for Finance [Dr Goh Keng Swee] in December 1968 when the same question was raised.

    What is possibly more useful is the formation of a Consumers' Association to keep watch on price movements and to ensure that prices of essential foodstuffs are not manipulated by unscrupulous stallholders and shopkeepers and any such malpractices are detected and publicised. It is understood that there is a move for such an association -- the Consumers' Association of Singapore - to be registered. The Government,

Column: 1052

on its part, will give every assistance to the Consumers' Association and will do everything possible to curb unwarranted price increases.

    There have been some remarks in Parliament regarding the reliability of official prices used in the compilation of the Consumer Price Index. A full list of the average retail prices of essential foodstuffs is published in the Monthly Digest of Statistics regularly and the study of fluctuation in the prices will clearly indicate that official prices do properly reflect price fluctuations experienced by the public.

TRAFFIC POLICE SUMMONS NOTICES
(Delay in issue)


    3. Mr Low Guan Onn asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) if he will give the reason for the delay of over one year in the issue by the Traffic Police of a summons notice in respect of a 13-month old traffic offence; and (b) what is the backlog of such cases, and if he will take steps to remedy the, situation.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Home Affairs (Mr L. P. Rodrigo) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, normally, if the personal particulars of the offending driver are available on the spot and if the offence is compoundable, an offer of composition will be sent to him within two weeks of the offence. If the offer is not accepted by the driver after a period, action by way of summons will be instituted against him.

    However, there are many cases of traffic violations where the particulars of the offenders are not obtainable on the spot by the Traffic Police. In these cases, some time is spent in obtaining the particulars of the owner of the vehicle from the Registrar of Vehicles, and thereafter, the particulars of the driver at the time of the alleged offence from the owner. Often reminders have to be sent to the vehicle owners.

Column: 1053



    When the particulars of the driver are finally obtained, it will take some time before an offer of composition is sent to him. If the offer is not accepted, the case will be further delayed in that some more time will have to be spent in obtaining a summons from the court.

    There are also difficulties in effecting the service of summons. In many cases, accused drivers change addresses without notifying the National Registration Office and the Registrar of Vehicles of these changes, although required by the law to do so. Hence an accused driver may receive his summons for an offence committed one year ago.

    At the moment, there is a backlog of approximately 21,500 traffic cases, all more than a year old, waiting for the issue of summonses.

    However, a ticketing system will soon be implemented to deal with traffic offenders more effectively and expeditiously. Under the new system, a ticket will be issued to the offending driver on the spot stipulating the amount of fine which he has to pay at the Report Section, Traffic Police, within two weeks from the date of the offence. The necessary amendments to the Traffic Ordinance have been made and, as soon as the mechanics of the ticketing system as well as the rules governing it have been finalised, it will be put into operation.

    In the meantime, measures are being taken to process the backlog of traffic cases more rapidly without at the same time causing a drag on the pile of current cases.

    Mr Low Guan Onn (River Valley): Sir, will the Traffic Police give them a target date to finish all the 21,000 cases.

    Mr Rodrigo: Sir, that is hardly possible. All that my Ministry can say is that specific measures are being taken by additional manpower to try to reduce this backlog as rapidly as possible.
