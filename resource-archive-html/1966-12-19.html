ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

STRIKE BY CLEANSING WORKERS


    1. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Health what plans he has to meet the threatened strike by the cleansing workers of the Ministry of Health.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: Mr Speaker, Sir, as the Ministry of Health has been planning for a complete re-organisation of the public cleansing service for some time and has also been gradually building up an Emergency Cleansing Corps towards this end, I can assure the Member for Bras Basah that should the cleansing service go on strike at midnight on 29th December, 1966, we shall be able to maintain not just a skeleton service during the strike but at the start perhaps provide one-half of the present service with a gradual buildup thereafter.

    On the 26th of August this year, I reported to this House that the then strength of our Emergency Cleansing Corps was 624 and it could be stepped up without limit to keep up with the requirements of re-organisation. I am happy to state that the present-day strength of our Emergency Cleansing Corps is 1,325. This is twice the strength

Column: 869

of what it was four months ago and is equivalent to one-half of the strength of the present work force in the Public Cleansing Department of the Ministry should they go on strike.

    I wish to add, Sir, that strike or no strike, I shall personally take charge of the complete re-organisation of the public cleansing service with effect from the 3rd of January, 1967, which is only two weeks ahead. Re-organisation will proceed along the following principles.

    Firstly, our public cleansing service will henceforth be on a two-shift basis and this has twofold advantages. One, productivity will be practically doubled because the same equipment and vehicles could be used to clear twice the present output of refuse daily. Two, we can double our work force if necessary and thus provide excellent employment opportunities for our large pool of unemployed citizens now numbering about 70,000.

    Secondly, it will put a stop to the nonsensical system of cleansing workers turning up for work on Sundays for double pay and public holidays for triple pay as of right and absenting themselves on Mondays and days following public holidays! This racket has unfortunately gone on for too long even since 1960 - for six long years! We must put an end to this historical farce! Hereafter, Sunday and public holiday work, if any, will be at the request of the employer, which is and has been the prevailing situation in Singapore for some time.

    Finally, since the re-organisation is to take effect, strike or no strike, as from 3rd January, 1967, I am now in the position to offer to all members of our Emergency Cleansing Corps prospects of permanent employment in 1967 subject, of course, to completion of three months' satisfactory service in the Emergency Cleansing Corps.

FIRE BRIGADE POLICY ON ERECTION OF TARPAULIN
SHEDS FOR PARTIES


    2. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Culture and Social Affairs what is the policy of the Fire Brigade Authorities in regard to permits for the erection of tarpaulin sheds to shelter the audience at a Chinese "wayang", a dinner party, etc.

    Inche Othman Bin Wok: Mr Speaker, Sir, the Fire Brigade authorities have no objection to the use of tarpaulin sheds for parties and other social functions generally. For the performance of Chinese wayangs on wooden stages in public streets, however, we generally discourage the use of tarpaulin sheds on the ground that such sheds tend to increase the danger to members of the public watching a wayang should a fire break out on the stage or in the vicinity.

    This is a policy which quite obviously must be administered with due regard to the particular circumstances of each case, and cases of refusal brought to the attention of the Ministry are reviewed sympathetically resulting in the Fire Brigade's objections being over-ruled in some cases.

QUARTERS FOR PUBLIC UTILITIES BOARD EMPLOYEES


    3. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Law and National Development if he will consider asking the Public Utilities Board if the Board will consider providing quarters to its employees with rent concession as is done by the Port of Singapore Authority.

    Mr E. W. Barker: The answer to the question is `No'.

    The Public Utilities Ordinance does empower the Minister to issue to the

Column: 871

Board such general directions as appear to him to be required in the public interest. But it is not considered that the question of providing quarters for the Board's employees is one on which any direction should be issued. Rather, it is one for the Board to consider in consultation with its employees' representatives.

    As of now, the Board has provided quarters for about 38 per cent of its daily-rated employees at rents ranging from $6 to $16 a month. Free light and water are also supplied with the quarters. With the construction of more quarters in future, the Board expects to provide housing for a larger proportion of its daily-rated employees. At the moment, about 782 units are under construction.

    In the case of monthly-rated employees, it is the policy of the Board to provide, wherever possible, quarters for officers who are required because of exigencies of service, to live at or near their place of work. About 30 per cent of the Board's senior officers and about 18 per cent of its junior and subordinate officers at present occupy quarters provided by the Board.

    Mr Ho See Beng: Can the Minister inform this House what is the figure represented by 38 per cent of the total number of daily-rated employees who are provided with quarters?

    Mr Barker: Mr Speaker, Sir, there is a total number of 4,489 daily-rated employees. Of these, 1,724 are housed.
