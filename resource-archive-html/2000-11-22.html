STAMP DUTIES (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Second Minister for Finance (Mr Lim Hng Kiang): Mr Deputy Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

Introduction

    The Bill seeks to amend the Stamp Duties Act to give legislative authority to the tax changes announced in Budget 2000.

Rationale

    The current section 15 of the Stamp Duties Act provides stamp duty relief for corporate restructuring and mergers. Relief from ad valorem duty is given based on the principle that there will be no substantial change in ownership of the transferred assets. As section 15 is a relieving section, several conditions have been set for the concession.

    In recognition that companies restructure and merge to obtain greater synergy, achieve cost competitiveness and deliver better products and services to increasingly sophisticated consumers, Dr Richard Hu announced in Budget 2000 that the conditions for section 15 relief will be relaxed so that more companies can enjoy the tax savings.

    Dr Hu also announced that relief would be granted to a transfer of assets between associated companies. This relief was previously not available.

    To effect the above changes, the Bill repeals the current section 15 and introduces a new section 15 to allow ad valorem duty relief for:

    (a) a transfer of the undertaking or shares in respect of a scheme for the reconstruction or amalgamation of companies; and

    (b) the transfer, conveyance or assignment of any beneficial interest in any asset between associated companies.

    In order to prevent abuse and to limit the concession to deserving restructuring and merger schemes, we have imposed qualifying conditions. The conditions to be imposed have been announced via a circular issued by the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore. The expanded relief scheme has been effected administratively since 1st July 2000.

    The response to the expanded relief scheme has been encouraging. We will continue to monitor industry trends and amend the conditions when necessary, to make sure that they remain relevant to the business community.

CUSTOMS (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Minister for Finance (Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau): Mr Deputy Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Bill seeks to amend the Customs Act to give legislative effect to a change of definition of customs duty to exclude excise duty. It also incorporates three other amendments to the Customs Act.

Re-definition of Customs Duty

    Currently, our Customs Act defines customs duty as `any import or excise duty imposed by or under this Act'. The common international practice is that customs duty refers exclusively to tariffs applied on imported goods for protective and other trade policy purposes. Our current definition of customs duty may therefore cause confusion to our trading partners since it also includes excise duty which is applied in a non-discriminatory manner on imported goods and locally manufactured products for fiscal and other domestic policy purposes.

Column: 1216



    To make clear to our trading partners the respective purposes of our customs duty and excise duty, our definition of customs duty should be amended to exclude excise duty. In this connection, a new subsection 10(1A) will also be inserted to state clearly that excise duty shall be imposed in a non-discriminatory basis regardless of the place of origin or manufacture of the goods. For equal treatment of imported goods and locally manufactured goods, section 22 will also be amended so that the same basis of valuation will be applied on imported goods and locally manufactured goods for the purposes of levying excise duty.

Other Changes to the Act

    In addition to the aforementioned amendments, the following amendments will also be made to the Customs Act.

    Currently, all sellers of liquors must apply for a licence. A new subsection 71(3A) would be inserted to empower the Minister to exempt any person from having to apply for a licence for the sale of liquors subject to conditions imposed by the Minister. This amendment will allow individuals who sell small quantities of liquors to be exempted from applying for a licence. An example would be a person who wants to auction a few bottles of his personal collection of wine through the Internet. We have decided to cap the sales limit at 9 litres per calendar year.

The new section 75 will enable the Minister to prescribe by regulations certain duties and requirements of persons licensed to sell liquors. This will give us the flexibility to revise those duties and requirements to keep up with changing circumstances without having to amend the Act.

    First, on the investment of Special Account fund. Since its introduction in July 1977, the CPF Special Account had remained the most protected portion of a CPF member's savings. Over the years, we had gradually liberalised the CPF rules to allow members to use savings in their Ordinary Account to purchase homes, invest in shares, buy insurance policies, unit trusts, or even place in fixed deposits.

Only the savings in the Special Account had remained untouched. Why? Because the Special Account was seen as a member's cash savings for his retirement. The Special Account complements the Minimum Sum Scheme. This is so because, for most of the CPF members, the cash needed to be set aside for the Minimum Sum would come largely from the Special Account savings. Most members by age 55 would have withdrawn a large part of the Ordinary Account savings to finance the purchase of their homes. Hence, the Special Account would provide most of the cash savings for their old-age needs.

    In November 1999, the Inter-Ministerial Committee on Ageing Population recommended that Special Account savings in excess of the Minimum Sum be allowed for investments in retirement-related instruments. The Committee concluded that there are in existence today adequate avenues for low risk long-term investments which can help CPF members to enhance their old-age cash savings. The Government accepted this recommendation to give CPF members the opportunity to earn higher returns on their retirement savings. On closer study, my Ministry concluded that by restricting the class of retirement-related instruments, we did not even require CPF members to lock up the Minimum Sum. In other words, it was possible to allow members to invest all of their Special Account savings in such a retirement-related instrument.

Hence, such investment should not go to high risk high return speculative instruments. We have to make a risk/return trade-off and balance the goal of enabling CPF members to earn higher returns against the fact that the Special Account savings represent the basic safety net for many Singaporeans. The advantage of allowing all Special Account funds, including the Minimum Sum, for approved investments is to encourage our citizens to take greater responsibility for their old age security and to take financial planning more seriously.
