Column No : 1682

APEC MEETING IN SINGAPORE

(Assessment of outcome)

     1.  Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs if he can give his assessment on the outcome of the APEC meeting in Singapore when the meeting concludes.

     2.  Mr Teo Siong Seng asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs what is his observation and assessment on the important outcome of the 17th APEC Economic Leaders Meeting and in what ways Singapore and the business community can benefit from this mega event. 

     The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to take the first two questions together, please?

     Mr Speaker: Yes, please.

     Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo: The 17th APEC Economic Leaders' Meeting (AELM) was the culmination of our 2009 APEC Chairmanship, during which we also celebrated the 20th anniversary of the formation of APEC.  When it was born in 1989, trade barriers were high.  Political and ideological barriers were even higher.  Besides the economic integration of the region, we wanted to make sure that no line was drawn down the middle of the Asia Pacific.  At that time, trans-Pacific trade was already growing at a faster pace than trans-Atlantic trade.  Now, more so than ever, trans-Pacific trade looks set to grow even more, with some likening the Asia Pacific region to the Mediterranean. 


     The APEC Leaders' Week culminated with the two-day Leaders' Retreat, chaired by Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong, centred around the theme of "Sustaining Growth, Connecting the Region".  The series of APEC meetings took place at a critical juncture for the global economy and the region.  Accelerated by the economic crisis, the global landscape is changing.  With the rise of Asia, in particular China and India, the world has become multi-polar but the US will remain the dominant pole for a long time to come. Against this backdrop, APEC Leaders were united in their support of actions to promote global economic recovery, and create conditions for strong, stable and sustainable growth. 


     The overall mood was positive and upbeat, with broad agreement on the fundamentals.  There was a full turnout of APEC Leaders, which underscored the importance that they placed on APEC.  The involvement of the business community is an integral part of the Leaders’ Meeting.  Activities involving the business community have grown through the Annual CEO Summit which provides an occasion for Leaders to interact with business leaders.  We received good feedback on the CEO Summit which was one of the largest, if not the largest, ever.  Fourteen APEC Leaders participated in the CEO Summit. 


     At the various meetings, there was a general hopefulness about the future of the region, many believing that this will be an Asia Pacific century. As the premier regional organisation representing more than half global GDP, APEC is well-placed to articulate the region’s perspective on global economic issues. There was agreement that APEC should work with the G-20 and that the APEC and G-20 agendas should be aligned, so that they can be mutually supportive for the good of the region and the world. 


     APEC has become a platform for Leaders, Ministers and officials to meet regularly and develop personal ties.  In addition to the formal APEC meetings, many bilateral meetings were held at the sidelines. When problems arise, the institutional and interpersonal networks are already in place to help address them.  A former European Commissioner remarked to me that APEC meetings had a different quality from many others he attended. Unlike European meetings which were legalistic, APEC meetings were more exhortative. The non-binding nature of APEC decisions creates different conference dynamics.  


     At the end of the day, APEC's relevance depends on its ability to deliver tangible outcomes.  Because of the non-binding nature of APEC decisions, Leaders and Ministers can afford to be more candid and more open to new ideas.  Decisions nevertheless carry force because of peer pressure.  A recent study by an independent research team on the much-debated question "Does APEC really make a difference to trade?" concluded that the impact of APEC membership on trade is comparable to that of a free trade agreement, even without a binding agreement.


     The Ministerial and Leaders’ meetings covered a lot of ground. We reviewed the global economic situation, launched new initiatives that will help businesses and discussed growth strategies to promote durable recovery. In particular, there was broad agreement on re-orientating our growth strategies to address the challenges of climate change, globalisation and macroeconomic imbalances. There was also a clear stance rejecting all forms of protectionism and we reaffirmed our commitment to conclude the Doha Development Agenda in 2010.  We continue to hold high the Bogor Goals of free trade and investment in the region.


     One common target agreed on is to make it 25% cheaper, faster and easier, on average, to do business in the region by 2015 in five priority areas. This means time and cost savings in areas like starting a business and the processing of trade documents. We also started an initiative among seven economies allowing traders to self-certify a product’s origin in order to claim preferential tariffs made available by Free Trade Agreements (FTAs). They are Australia, Canada, Japan, Korea, New Zealand, Singapore and the United States. This initiative will help companies more fully realise the benefits of FTAs by reducing the cost of complying with multiple Rules of Origin. 
 

     There was consensus among the Leaders that APEC economies should step up their efforts to realise the vision of a Free Trade Area of the Asia Pacific (FTAAP).  An important development was US President Barack Obama’s announcement that the US will engage in the Trans-Pacific Partnership (TPP) and work towards a broad-based and high-quality agreement. There are plans to extend the TTP to an eight-member grouping with Australia, Peru, US and Vietnam joining the original four members which are Brunei, Chile, New Zealand and Singapore. The TPP will set off positive competitive dynamics which can help us achieve the ultimate objective of an FTAAP. 
 

     To answer the second part of Mr Teo Siong Seng’s question, with more than 7,000 foreign delegates in town, the APEC meetings were also a good opportunity to showcase Singapore on the global stage and enhance our attractiveness as a leading global city.  Although there were minor problems, the meetings and collateral events were generally well run.  The feedback that we have received is mostly positive.  As a result of APEC, I can say that Singapore’s reputation as an efficient place to hold conferences and do business has been enhanced.  This is valuable.


     Singapore is proud to have been the host APEC economy in APEC’s 20th year. It has been a long journey, involving not just Government officials but many Singaporeans who played their part to make the event a success. Our goal was not only to deliver a good meeting and be efficient and effective but also to be good hosts, showing the warm and friendly side of Singapore. It was more than a cast of thousands. All Singaporeans participated directly or indirectly, including many who were overseas who cheered us on.

     Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong (Tampines):   Mr Speaker, Sir, I thank the Minister for his reply.  If I may say so, I think the army of volunteers and all the staff involved in organising APEC, including the security agencies, deserve special commendation for hosting such a good meeting in Singapore and showcasing Singapore's ability to host such a major global conference.  As the Minister noted one of the spin-offs of APEC is the meetings that took place on the sidelines.  One of them was the historic meeting between US President Barack Obama and the Leaders from the ASEAN countries, including Myanmar. Can I ask the Minister, first, for his views on the significance of the renewed US policy of engagement in this region, in particular towards Myanmar?  The Minister also mentioned that it is getting to be a multi-polar world with the rise of China and India but with the US as a dominant pole, so can I ask the Minister for his sense of the shifting strategic landscape given that China has overtaken the US in becoming Southeast Asia's third biggest trading partner, after Japan and the EU?

     Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo: Thank you to the Member for her remarks about all those who have contributed to the success of the recent APEC meeting. Prime Minister Lee will be hosting a reception to thank all those who participated directly or indirectly. As for the Member's question about the US engagement of the region, for the first time, we have had a summit between the US President and all 10 Leaders of ASEAN. And for the first time, the US did not make Myanmar an issue in the way, which it was in the past. There were proposals to host such summits before but they always stumbled over this issue of Myanmar. There are things happening now, not all of which are in the public domain. After Senator Webb's visit to Myanmar some months ago, there is now direct contact between the two governments – the US and Myanmar. When Assistant Secretary Kurt Campbell was there recently, he met Daw Aung San Suu Kyi. She, in turn, has asked to meet Senior General Than Shwe, so things are happening. In the meantime, the US and Europe keep sanctions on as a way to pressure the government and I think they are only prepared to relax sanctions if Daw Aung San Suu Kyi were to ask them to. So in this way, their strategy is to strengthen her and not to weaken her. 

 

     But the government, on its own, has undertaken to hold free and fair elections next year. Well, we hope they will be free and fair, and we hope there will be national reconciliation involving not just NLD (National League for Democracy) but also the various ethnic groups, many of which are still armed, and that the country can then move smoothly into a constitutional framework and make progress after that. In a sense, we in ASEAN feel justified that all these years we have advised the Americans and the Europeans to continue engaging Myanmar even though they may be unhappy about its policies, which is indeed our position as well. Members of Parliament, like Mr Charles Chong, have always taken a very principled stand on some of the things that some of us find objectionable. But what is happening with respect to Myanmar has to be seen against the larger backdrop of the US engagement of Asia as a whole.  The key is really US-China relations, which have become very multi-faceted, a very big account which was put on a stable footing by former President Bush, and which President Obama is determined to maintain and keep steady.  As part of it, the US has to strengthen relations with other countries in Asia, including countries in ASEAN. 

     Mr Teo Siong Seng (Nominated Member):  Thank you, Minister for the very comprehensive assessment. No doubt the APEC meeting was a good success. In fact when I was in Manila last week to attend the World Chinese Entrepreneurs Congress, I was congratulated by many of my counterparts for the good success. Still, I cannot help but raise the issue of how the man-in-the-street felt. The week before the APEC meeting, I was in a taxi and I was asking the taxi driver how APEC was affecting him. He said it was terrible because of the road blocks and he said "how can I compete with 350 BMWs?" While we know that $100 million was spent to make the APEC a great success and to show that we can really host such a big event to bring world Leaders together, the man-in-the-street feels that Singapore is famous enough already. Do we have to make ourselves more famous?

     Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo: Mr Speaker, I have also personally received a few of those complaints, including an email from a businessman in Suntec City who said business was down during the week that the APEC meeting was on.  But at the same time, we have learnt from the experience of the World Bank/IMF experience and even though keeping security in place was critical, we have been able to do so, by minimising the inconveniences to Singaporeans. To be a conference city, there are bound to be such inconveniences from time to time, affecting taxi drivers, shops and Singaporeans who live in the city area.  But I believe, on the whole, there is general recognition that all this global limelight on us redounds to a much larger, greater advantage, and that in the end it means more people coming here, more people respecting our ability to organise and do things in an efficient, simple, practical way.  It will, in the end, lead to more investments and jobs.  I think, on the whole, the positive feedback drowns out the few negative comments that we have received.

 

 


     Mr Speaker: Last question, Mdm Halimah.

     Mdm Halimah Yacob (Jurong):  Sir, one of the strategic objectives of APEC is to develop a balanced sustainable and inclusive growth.  This is an important objective, considering that the impact of globalisation, widening income gaps and the perception that there is unbalanced distribution of wealth in the region.  I would like to ask the Minister what are the specific measures that APEC has agreed upon in order to achieve this vision of balanced, sustainable and inclusive growth.  My second question to the Minister is to ask Minister whether he agrees that, while non-binding decisions create a more open environment for discussion, at the same time, when it comes to results in terms of follow-up, there will be problems.  So, how do you balance these? 

     Mr George Yong-Boon Yeo:  The problem of balanced development will become more challenging in the coming years because of globalisation.  It is natural that with competition, some groups will do better than other groups, some countries will do better than other countries.  The solution is not to prevent those who can move faster from moving faster.  The solution must be to help those who have difficulty catching up, to catch up more quickly.  For this reason, one of the critical pillars of APEC is helping countries which are further behind with capacity building. And we do this in many ways, through conferences, through courses, through peer assessments. In fact, what we call ECOTECH – Economic and Technical Cooperation – dominates the practical agenda of APEC.  In other words, we are not tying ourselves to specific measures to help one another, but we are making use of peer pressure and competition to encourage everybody to move along and together.  And it has worked quite well so far.  In Europe, every meeting they vote to take decisions. Because of that, officials enter meetings in a very tense way with negotiating positions all ready for fights and long meetings.  In APEC, because the targets are often soft, people are prepared to discuss things more freely.  This was pointed out to me not just by the European Commissioner I mentioned earlier, but by others as well.  There are pros and cons.  It means that if countries do not measure up to what they said they will do, there is  nothing we can hold against them as these decisions are non-binding. So what has happened within APEC is within that collegial framework, there are specific agreements which are binding being carried out. 

 

     So APEC provides, as it were, general goal-setting for the entire region. Although it is invovles only 21 economies, it accounts for more than half the worlds' economy and it is almost half of global trade.  And it is by far the faster growing half of the world, one which is full of hope, full of optimism about the future, eager to learn from one another, keep the lines of communication and trade open, and then within this framework, if there are specific agreements that we can enter in a binding way, we will do so because they are mutually beneficial. So we talk about how to actualise the Bogor goals, work towards a Free Trade Area of the Asia Pacific, which is binding, even though the Bogor goals themselves are not. The Trans-Pacific Partnership which if it comes about, with eight members, will be binding and serve as a building block towards a future FTAAP. So I think we have somehow in the Pacific, without quite planning for it to be so, achieved a good balance between binding agreements and non-binding efforts to keep everybody moving and encouraged in the same direction.

MEASURES TO COOL THE PROPERTY MARKET

(Update)

     4.  Mr Christopher de Souza asked the Minister for National Development if he will provide an update on the impact and effectiveness of the measures to cool the property market, in particular (i) the effect of the abolition of Interest-Only Loans (IOL) and Interest Absorption Scheme (IAS); and (ii) the re-introduction of the Confirmed List of land sales for the first half of 2010. 

     The Minister for National Development (Mr Mah Bow Tan):  Mr Speaker Sir, on 14th September 2009, I announced in this House a package of property market measures to temper the exuberance in the market and pre-empt a property bubble from forming.  One of the measures was the removal of the Interest Absorption Scheme (IAS) and the Interest-Only Loans (IOL), which was effective immediately.  Another was the resumption of land sales on the Confirmed List for the first half of 2010.

(Measures and community-based programmes)

     10.  Mr Christopher de Souza asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) if he will provide an update on the types of community-based initiatives which have provided platforms for Singaporeans and new immigrants to integrate and gain understanding of one another; and (b) what are the examples of such current initiatives.   

     11.  Mr Teo Siong Seng asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what measures and programmes are put into place to help new immigrants integrate into Singapore society; and (b) what special incentives are being extended to encourage the ethnic Chambers of Commerce and Industry and other trade associations to play a proactive role in helping the new immigrants and foreign workers to adapt and work in harmony alongside our Singapore workers. 

     The Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (Dr Vivian Balakrishnan):  Mr Speaker, may I have your permission to take Question Nos. 10 and 11 together?

     Mr Speaker:  Yes.

     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan:  Mr Christopher de Souza and Mr Teo Siong Seng have both asked about programmes that we have to help integrate new immigrants. Let me start by saying that this is a long-term, multi-faceted and complex challenge that all stakeholders in our society, and not just the Government, will have to take up. First, Singaporeans have to understand the need for immigrants and be comfortable with the social adjustments that are needed in order to maintain an open and welcoming society. We do so with our own enlightened self interests in mind.  Second, the new immigrants must subscribe to our core values of multi-racialism and meritocracy in a fair and just society.  Third, we need to build more social bridges to connect these new Singaporeans to the already existing much larger pool of  Singaporeans in order to build a larger united and cohesive community.  The children of new immigrants must grow up and be indistinguishable from the children of local Singaporeans.  That is the way we build a long-term united society.

 

      We are looking at the existing community bonding initiatives, and all these encourage broad-based participation by both locals and newcomers.  Let me give Members an example.  The Tampines East grassroots organisation organised an International Water Festival in April this year – this was an occasion to celebrate the New Years of both our Indian as well as our Indo-Chinese communities in Singapore.  We encourage locals and newcomers alike to participate in these community activities and to use them as an opportunity to expand their social circles.

 

     The National Integration Council (NIC) was set up in April this year.  It does its work through four working groups which aim to promote integration in, first, the community, second, in schools, third, in the workplaces and, fourth, in the media.  The NIC will consolidate the existing orientation programmes for new citizens, for instance, we are reviewing the National Education Experience Programme (NEEP) and we are also looking at introducing a more former Naturalisation and Integration (N&I) Journey. The Naturalisation and Integration Journey will leverage on interactive online components as well as meaningful site visits so that our new citizens can get a better idea about our history, our social norms and our core values. They will also be introduced to our grassroots communities and be encouraged to take part in their activities.

 

     The NIC is also reviewing the citizenship ceremonies, which are, in a sense, the culmination of a newcomer's Naturalisation and Integration Journey.  We have to make these ceremonies more significant and more meaningful for both the newcomers and equally important the family members of newcomers as well as the larger community that will eventually envelope and surround these newcomers.

 

     Another key ongoing initiative is the People's Association's Integration and Naturalisation Champions (INC) programme. These are active grassroots leaders.  The first batch of them was appointed in 2007.  The Integration and Naturalisation Champions play a crucial role in seeking out and welcoming newcomers into the neighbourhood and encouraging them to join and participate in community activities. We now have about 640 of these Integration and Naturalisation Champions.  And I must say that even amongst these leaders are some new immigrants who themselves are now volunteering their time and energy to help other newcomers settle into the local neighbourhood.

 

     Volunteering is a good way for newcomers to be plugged into the wider community and it is an excellent way to get to know their neighbours and also to demonstrate their commitment at the local neighbourhood level.  We now have newcomers who have volunteered in various grassroots, community and professional committees.  In fact, currently, we have about 4,500 new immigrants who are serving as grassroots leaders.  In addition, the Working Group for Schools is exploring ways for students from both our national as well as our Foreign System Schools to participate in joint community involvement projects. 

 

     We will also be working on increasing awareness among new immigrants of the opportunities to volunteer in areas which they are interested in.  For instance, Singapore Cares will set up a volunteer matching platform which will provide a team-based volunteer-led programme which will allow people to provide their efforts in voluntary work on a part-time basis, on a flexible basis, in areas which they truly are interested in.  We will provide a Kindness Exchange which is an online "meeting place" that will match professional services and the needs of voluntary welfare organisations with the available pool of volunteers and we hope that new Singaporeans will also take advantage of this exchange.

 

     We recently launched the Community Integration Fund (CIF).  This is a $10-million fund which will provide co-funding of up to 80% for social integration projects over the next three years.  This will help promote programmes that will bring newcomers and locals closer together.  These programmes can be put together by a wide variety of organisations, be it community organisation, grassroots organisation, educational organisation, industry association or the Chambers of Commerce.  For example, the Singapore Indian Development Association (SINDA) formed an Indian Integration Committee, and this Committee has hosted various dialogues, and I think some Members of this House have also participated in these dialogue sessions.  The Singapore Federation of Chinese Clan Associations (SFCCA) has also reached out to Chinese newcomers, in a sense continuing their traditional role of helping newcomers sink roots into the local community and to be plugged in while still engaging and preserving their ethnic heritage.

 

     Mr Teo asked about special incentives being extended to encourage ethnic Chambers of Commerce and Industry. Currently, there is already some work in progress.  I am sure the Member is aware that the Singapore Chinese Chamber of Commerce and Industry (SCCCI) has formed a new Immigration Liaison Unit which helps newcomers to integrate within our society and, I suppose, more specifically within the business community.  This is a laudable project and one which we would be most happy to support.  The National Trades Union Congress and the Singapore National Employers Federation have also jointly set up the Migrant Workers Centre, which aims to facilitate a harmonious co-existence between both local and foreign workers.  We also have professional bodies like the Institute of Engineers which is looking to attract new immigrant professionals into their fold so that these members can benefit from the networks and their mutual exchange of both knowledge and contacts that these institutes provide.

 

     All these initiatives form a good start, and I would like to encourage all the Chambers of Commerce, the industry organisations, the unions and the professional bodies to do their part to integrate newcomers into the larger Singaporean pool.  As we embark on this, let us also remember that we must do so in a way which is natural, in a way which celebrates both the contributions and the cultures of both newcomers as well as local Singaporeans.  And the CIF funds will be made available for all these efforts.

 

     Both locals and newcomers must realise that integration is a two-way process and it does not come about by accident.  Much effort and commitment will be required from all of us in order to make integration work, and I encourage newcomers to be proactive in learning about Singapore, widening their social circles, participating and contributing to the larger Singapore family. 

     Mr Christopher de Souza:  Thank you, Minister, for your informative reply.  Moving from the base plate of policies that he has explained to us, could we move further to ensure that further policies are in place to prevent enclaves from happening?  I think integration is one thing but we need to have our new immigrants, our fellow Singaporeans and our new Singaporeans live alongside us.  Hence, is there anything that we can do to ensure that we remain geographically multi-racial?


     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan: I think the Member has touched on an important point.  Actually, it is a long-standing policy of this Government to ensure that there are no ethnic ghettos in Singapore. This is a point which we will study carefully and I will recognise that in a similar sense, we do not want to promote the formation of newcomer ghettos in Singapore. And this is something which we will certainly discuss in greater detail with the Ministry of National Development.

     Mr Calvin Cheng (Nominated Member): Thank you, Minister, I have a supplementary question. Given that male immigrants have not done and do not have to do National Service, how do we assure Singapore citizens who have this vital contribution is recognised, so that there is less resentment on the ground?

     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan: This is another sensitive point which I think most of us can identify with, since all of us have to serve National Service. For a variety of reasons, we currently exempt first generation permanent residents and those who become citizens in the later years.  I do not want to get into the technical, operational and security reasons as to why we do not currently enlist them for National Service, but the point is that their children will do National Service. And their children should be indistinguishable from our children in their accents, hopes, aspirations, obligations and their duties to our nation.

     Mr Speaker: Mdm Halimah, last question.

     Mdm Halimah Yacob: What the Minister said is that new immigrants must also subscribe to our core values.  But would the Minister consider it desirable or possible to also request that new immigrants or those who apply to be citizens to sit for a test?  This is to ascertain whether they really know about our core values and how our society functions, which is something that is already commonplace in many countries.

For the same period, an average of 46,300 applicants were successful in their PR applications each year. An average of 2,200 PRs renounced their PR status over the same period.
 
     Sir, the number of persons applying for PR and the numbers of persons granted PR have been increasing steadily in recent years. This was due to two factors: the strong economy and the need to augment our population. A booming economy with economic growth of 6% to 8% between 2004 and 2007 meant also that we needed to take in more foreigners to work here. Most of these foreigners are transient, here to work and not to stay long term. But a good many who are well-qualified with university degrees or diploma qualifications, or skilled personnel and professionals, decided to stay longer and applied for PR.
 
     The other reason was to augment our population. As Members are aware, we have a low fertility rate of 1.28. If we close our doors to immigrants, our population will age rapidly and start to decline by 2020. This will affect us adversely. We will lose our economic dynamism and competitiveness as a city state. The burden on Singaporeans will increase.
 
     The Government recognises that some Singaporeans feel anxious about the large inflow of foreigners in recent years. We should not think that all foreigners here are PRs. In other words, they are here on a transient basis and will leave Singapore one day when their services are no longer needed by their employers or when they have finished their studies. However, the Government is mindful of the concerns of Singaporeans. We will manage the inflow of immigrants taking into consideration how quickly our society can absorb and integrate these new immigrants without diluting our national identity or weakening our social cohesion. Such a sustained and calibrated inflow of immigrants will ensure that Singaporeans benefit from immigration in the long term.

To this end, various security measures were implemented at the meeting venues. On average, each day, during the APEC Leaders’ Week, we had about 1,300 Police officers deployed on the ground to perform security duties. During the APEC Leaders’ Week, dedicated teams of psychologists and welfare officers continually monitored morale on the ground. Issues raised by officers were surfaced daily to senior commanders so that they could be quickly addressed.

The officers understood the importance of their roles in the challenge of keeping the delegates and Singapore safe and secure.  I highly commend all our officers for sacrificing their time to undertake such an important duty successfully.

The delegates and many visiting business leaders thanked Singapore for handling the arrangements, including the security arrangements, effectively and efficiently. This would not have been possible without the collective efforts of all stakeholders and a cooperative public, who, like the Home Team, recognised the importance of keeping the Singapore flag flying high. Our officers know that their extra efforts were for an important cause and also know that Singaporeans and the Government are grateful to them for the part they played in maintaining security throughout the APEC meetings.

This constitutes slightly under 5%, or 4.9% to be exact, of all HDB households. PR households are found in all HDB towns and estates.

 

     Sir, assimilation of PRs is an important national objective. As the Member suggest, a good mix of PRs in HDB estates is important. My Ministry will keep a close watch on the distribution of PRs living in HDB estates.  Where necessary, we will consider measures to prevent the congregation of PRs and foreigners.

     The Income Tax (Amendment) Bill 2009 comprises two categories of legislative changes. The first category puts into legal effect the income tax changes announced in the 2009 Budget Statement. The second category covers other amendments to the Income Tax Act arising from regular reviews to improve our income tax system.

 

     The Income Tax (Amendment) Bill was released for public consultation from 22nd June to 14th July this year. The Ministry has revised the Bill to incorporate the public feedback received.

 

     Sir, the key tax changes have already been debated upon in this House following the announcement in the 2009 Budget Statement. Let me summarise some of these key changes which are covered by this Bill. I shall begin with the permanent changes:

 

     Firstly, the reduction of the corporate income tax rate from 18% to 17%, with effect from the Year of Assessment 2010, to enhance Singapore’s attractiveness as a business hub and to spur entrepreneurial activity. This change and its consequential amendments are covered by clauses 33(b), (h) and 36 of the Bill;

 

     Secondly, the introduction of a new corporate amalgamation framework to ensure that most of the tax consequences of a continuing business will apply to the amalgamated company, thus making it easier for companies to restructure and rationalise, as effected through clause 23; and

 

     Thirdly, an enhancement of the incentives for promoting fund management to further strengthen Singapore as a hub for fund management. These changes are covered by clauses 8, 9(f), 13(a) and 14.  Sir, let me now cover the key one-off or temporary measures which were part of the Resilience Package announced during Budget 2009.

 

     Firstly, the enhancement of the existing capital allowances regime and loss carry-back relief scheme to ease business cashflow, and the liberalisation of foreign-sourced income exemption to help businesses meet their financing needs in Singapore. Clauses 7(c), 18, 19, 21, 26, 27, 28(b), 29 and 30 of the Bill provide for these changes; and

 

     Finally, the granting of a one-off 20% personal income tax rebate, capped at $2,000 for YA2009, to reduce the tax payable by individuals. This is aimed at providing immediate cashflow relief to taxpayers.  Clause 41 of the Bill provides for this change.

 

     Sir, I shall now deal with the other tax changes which require amendments to the Act arising from our ongoing regular review. Let me highlight three of these changes.

 

     Firstly, the removal of the Commonwealth Tax Relief.  Currently, we grant the Commonwealth Tax Relief (CTR) to our tax residents for income derived from and taxed in Commonwealth countries which do not have an Avoidance of Double Taxation Agreement (DTA) with Singapore. This relief will be removed with effect from Year of Assessment 2010 to rationalise the various tax reliefs for alleviating double taxation for our tax residents. This removal of the now obsolete relief takes into account enhancements to the tax regime over the years, namely the introduction of foreign-sourced income exemption for income remitted on or after 1st June 2003 and the extension of Unilateral Tax Credit (UTC) for all foreign-sourced income from non-DTA countries from YA2009. Clauses 37 and 40 effect this removal of the Commonwealth Tax Relief.

 

     Secondly, we will exclude from withholding tax, management fees paid to related persons outside Singapore for services performed outside Singapore. This is in line with the policy intent not to deem the income from certain services performed outside Singapore, to be derived from Singapore. Clause 6 effects this change.

 

     Thirdly, we will provide for the avoidance of doubt that the arm’s length principle, an internationally accepted standard, is to be adopted for all related party transactions. This amendment provides explicitly that the Comptroller of Income Tax may make adjustments to the profits of a taxpayer in a related party transaction if that transaction is not carried out on an arm’s length basis.  Clause 24 effects this change.

 

     Sir, as for the remaining 11 legislative changes arising from our periodic review, they are either technical in nature or relate to improvements in tax administration.

 

     With the above changes, the Income Tax Act will be enhanced and updated to better achieve our economic and social objectives.

Strategic imperatives

 

     The last major review of the SAF career schemes was conducted over a decade ago. The manpower landscape and the needs of the SAF have shifted significantly since then, in particular, we face two strategic human resource (HR) challenges.

 

     First, the demographic and educational profile of our population is changing.  The number of Singaporeans aged 17 to 25 entering the workforce each year will start to decline from the year 2015 onwards.  Also, young Singaporeans entering the workforce today are better educated and aspire for more challenging and responsible jobs commensurate with their qualifications.  Today, 25% of each cohort graduate with degrees while 42% graduate with diplomas.  In addition, 50% of diploma holders expect to subsequently go on to obtain degrees.  This provides the imperative as well as the opportunity for the SAF to restructure its careers for good diploma level entrants to provide them with better opportunities for self-development,  job growth and advancement.  This will also allow the SAF to make full use of the potential of these servicemen.

 

     Second, the SAF is transforming into a leaner and more potent Third Generation SAF that leverages on advanced technology and sophisticated systems.  We still need people with leadership ability, dynamism and high levels of physical and mental energy.  But, in addition, we need people with deeper specialisation, broader knowledge and more experience to fully exploit these advanced technologies, and to operate our more complex military systems.   Our HR policies and schemes of service must therefore evolve to address these strategic challenges. 

 

Strategic response by MINDEF

 

     Over the last two years, MINDEF and the SAF conducted a fundamental review of our military schemes of service in order to better match the aspirations of our people and potential recruits with the evolving HR needs of the Third Generation SAF.   We will be making significant changes in three key areas.

 

New military domain experts scheme

 

     First, we will introduce a new military uniformed scheme of service in the SAF called the Military Domain Experts Scheme.  This new scheme will complement the existing Officer and Warrant Officer schemes.  The Military Domain Experts Scheme will allow us to build and retain deep professional expertise and experience in critical military domain areas such as technical, engineering and intelligence.  It will also allow us to fully develop our current servicemen who are in these lines of work.  It will also attract the more technologically savvy polytechnic and degree graduates who want to work with cutting-edge systems and advanced technologies but are not so inclined to join the traditional Officer or Warrant Officer schemes.  It will also facilitate mid-career professionals with the relevant expertise to join the SAF. 

 

      Personnel who join the new scheme will be known as Military Experts. Military Experts will enjoy a longer career with the SAF on full CPF up to the age of 60.  The longer career will allow them to develop specialisation and deep expertise as well as the necessary experience on the job required for key military domain areas.  They will advance along an eight-rank structure – Military Expert 1 to Military Expert 8, with the pinnacle rank being Military Expert 8 (ME8).  ME8 will be equivalent to a Brigadier General in terms of job size.  The entry rank for degree holders is ME4.  Servicemen who hold the rank of ME4 or above will be known as Senior Military Experts.

 

     Military Experts will be given opportunities to upgrade themselves academically and professionally.  They will be offered attractive market-based remuneration.  Progression will be based on the individual's level of professional expertise and the ability to take on higher professional responsibilities in their expertise domains.   On the other hand, in the Officer and Warrant Officer Corps, progression depends significantly on command ability.

 

     Our servicemen who are currently in vocations under the military domain areas identified for the Military Domain Experts Scheme will be given the option to convert to the new scheme of service.  They will be offered a fair package to transit to the new scheme of service.

 

Enhanced Warrant Officer scheme

 

     Second, we will enhance the Warrant Officer scheme of service.  Our Warrant Officers form the backbone of the SAF.  They set high standards in tactical war-fighting and training, and are the repositories of the values and knowledge in the SAF.

 

     With the creation of the Military Domain Experts Scheme, the Warrant Officer Corps will be leaner and more focused.  Their jobs will be redesigned and restructured to focus on combat leadership roles, and to anchor the key SAF competencies in leadership, tactical war-fighting expertise and training supervision.  They will continue to work in support of and in partnership with the Officers.

 

     We will introduce a new Third Warrant Officer rank.  This will allow those with the potential to reach the Warrant Officer rank to be promoted to a Warrant Officer rank earlier.  Under the enhanced scheme, the best can become a Warrant Officer as early as the end of their sixth year in service compared to 12 years under the current scheme.  This new Third Warrant Officer rank will be an important milestone to signal to a serviceman that he has the potential to rise up through the Warrant Officer ranks and hold greater responsibilities.  Those who do not attain the Third Warrant Officer (3WO) rank can continue in their jobs until their retirement age, at age 50, so long as they are able to contribute.  The retirement age for the Warrant Officers will remain unchanged at age 55.

 

     To meet the aspirations of our servicemen to upgrade themselves academically early in their career, we will offer early degree sponsorships to promising servicemen with the potential to become Warrant Officers.  Currently, they may be sponsored for degree studies only from their sixth year of service.  Under the enhanced scheme, we will provide opportunities for them to embark on their studies as early as the end of their second or third year of service.

 

     We target diploma holders with strong leadership qualities to serve as Warrant Officers of the Third Generation SAF.  These changes will enhance the career proposition and make it more attractive to join the Warrant Officer Corps. 

 

Enhanced Officers' career scheme

 

     Third, are the changes being made to the Military Officers' scheme.  The current Officer career which is compact, fast-paced and challenging, with the retirement age in the mid-40s, has enabled the SAF to retain a dynamic and forward-looking Officer Corps.  Officers receive a lump sum payment at the end of their career with the SAF.  They then transit to a second career in another path of public service or the private sector.

 

     The Third Generation SAF will continue to require dynamic and vigorous leadership.  We will thus keep the Officer's career compact, fast-paced and challenging.  At the same time, however, Officers in the Third Generation SAF need more time serving ground tours and would also need time for more in-depth professional development in military leadership and advanced operational and technological capabilities. 

 

     Hence, instead of retiring in their mid-40s, SAF Officers will have their career extended till age 50.  This extension is provided for all existing Officers, but they have the choice of retiring at the current retirement age with their current retirement benefits.  In other words, the option to stay on till age 50 is with the Officers, so long as they continue to perform their duties diligently and well.

 

     In addition, the SAF will introduce a new Senior Lieutenant-Colonel rank next year.  This new rank  will recognise the larger responsibilities and supervisory roles of certain Lieutenant-Colonel appointments in the SAF, for example, the Brigade 2IC appointment. 

 

     In summary, we will have three military schemes that have distinct roles and functions.  The Officer Corps will continue to provide the senior military leadership; the Warrant Officer Corps will provide leadership to the rank and file and take on greater responsibilities in tactical training; the Military Experts will anchor deep professional expertise for key military domain areas for the SAF.  Each of these three schemes of service will offer opportunities for personal and professional development, job growth and advancement, so that our servicemen can fulfil their aspirations and the SAF can benefit fully from their potential.  The changes to the SAF's military schemes of service will be implemented on 1st April 2010. 

 

Key provisions of the Bill

 

     Mr Speaker, Sir, the SAF (Amendment) Bill will provide the proper legal framework to effect the key changes to the military schemes of service for the SAF.  Please allow me to highlight some aspects of this Bill.

 

     For the new Military Expert, Third Warrant Officer and Senior Lieutenant-Colonel ranks, the Bill proposes to amend the various provisions within the SAF Act to fit these new ranks into the current military law and discipline structures, and accord them the relevant disciplinary powers.  A new section 10C has been introduced to provide for the appointment and cancellation of appointment of Senior Military Experts by the President or the proper authority.  Amendments are also made to section 118 to reflect the appropriate scale of punishment that such personnel can be awarded by the sentence of a subordinate military court.

 

     A new section 205D has been proposed to allow for the change of ranks when servicemen are transferred from the Military Domain Experts Scheme to the Officer or Warrant Officer schemes.  This is intended to cater to two situations. 

 

 

 

 

     First, a Senior Military Expert might resign or be released from regular service before he completes the prescribed minimum term of service.  This minimum term of service is a period of regular service needed before he is deemed to have fulfilled his full-time National Service liability.  He might then have to be transferred to the Warrant Officer scheme with an appropriate rank to serve out his remaining term of fulltime National Service.

 

     Second, a Military Expert, who has served the minimum term of service and who resigns or is released from the SAF, may have to be assigned to a suitable post to serve his operationally-ready National Service.  The amendments in section 205(d) provide for the transfer of these operationally-ready National Servicemen to the Warrant Officer Corps at the appropriate rank to serve their operationally-ready National Service if there are no suitable MDES appointments.  Those who have been commissioned as officers will return to the Officer Corps in their operationally-ready National Service.  This will be done taking into account their background and experience. 

 

     In view of the changes made to the SAF Act, there will also be a consequential amendment to the Enlistment Act which is required to subject Senior Military Experts to the statutory age of 50 for operationally-ready National Service.  The statutory age is the same as that stipulated for officers in operationally-ready National Service under the same Act.

PROXIMITY OF PETROL STATIONS TO RESIDENTIAL AREAS

     4.   Dr Lam Pin Min asked the Minister for National Development (a) what are the potential health hazards posed by the proximity of petrol/gas stations to residential areas; and (b) whether there is a minimum safety distance for the siting of these stations to inhabited areas.

     Mr Mah Bow Tan:

     URA plans the location of petrol stations in consultation with the relevant technical agencies such as SCDF, NEA and LTA.

 

     NEA imposes requirements to ensure that petrol stations do not cause pollution or nuisance to the surrounding residential premises.  For instance, the stations are required to install vapour recovery systems to minimise smell nuisance from fumes.  

  

     Another key potential safety issue posed by petrol stations is fire. While SCDF has guidelines for petrol stations to be sited at a minimum distance of 50 m and 90 m from residences and places of public assembly respectively in the Fire Code, these distance guidelines cannot be strictly met in all instances given the challenges posed by our urban density and site constraints. However, the SCDF also imposes other safety requirements as part of the planning process. For instance, the SCDF requires all petrol stations to apply for a licence to store or keep petroleum. SCDF requires that all petroleum tanks be installed underground in well-protected enclosed concrete or brick chambers. Access to the tanks is also required to be secured and monitored closely. Co-location of petrol stations together with other commercial or residential buildings is also strictly prohibited.  These fire safety requirements help to prevent fire hazards to nearby residences.

 

     As part of the planning process before a petrol station site is approved, the relevant agencies will carry out the necessary technical assessment to ensure that all health and safety requirements are complied with.  Operating petrol stations are also subject to annual inspections by these agencies to ensure continued compliance.

It is the responsibility of the foreign governments to enforce their laws and take action against the errant employment agencies in their own countries.
