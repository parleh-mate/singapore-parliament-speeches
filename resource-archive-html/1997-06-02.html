     Dr Wang Kai Yuen (Bukit Timah): Mr Speaker, Sir, it is an honour to be given this privilege to move the motion.

    I beg to move,

    That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the Opening of the First Session of this Parliament.".

    Mr Speaker, Sir, 65% of the voters voted for the PAP in the last general election, reversing a trend of the previous three general elections, a trend which the opposition had been counting on to carry them into Parliament in greater numbers. Instead, the election saw opposition parties humbled and decimated, so much so that the SDP, the main opposition party in the last Parliament, is no longer represented in this House.

    The PAP's success is based on its record of good Government. Singaporeans have found their standard of living improved for the past five years. In his 1992, 1993 and 1995 National Day Rally speeches, the Prime Minister had reiterated that Singaporeans' assets would be enhanced during his tenure. A series of asset enhancement programmes such as HDB upgrading and share ownership were introduced, benefiting a large majority of Singaporeans.

    Our booming economy has raised our per capita GDP to US$28,500, placing us amongst the top 10 countries in the world. Real wages have increased by 4-5% per annum over the past five years. Propelled by the increasing income, the appreciation of our homes over the last five years was unprecedented in Singapore's history. As inflation has been low and our currency remains strong, our purchasing power has increased dramatically. The Institute of Management Development, a Swiss business school, has ranked Singapore

Column: 70

equal with Japan in terms of purchasing power parity. With such a strong record, it was no surprise that the voters supported the PAP.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the results reflect the trust and confidence the people of Singapore have in the Prime Minister and his Cabinet. As we are poised at the threshold of the new millennium, our people have given the PAP a renewed mandate to lead them into the 21st century. They have endorsed the vision of the PAP Government for this term of Parliament, namely, Singapore 21, with its five pillars: create wealth, share success, invest in the young, care for the old, and build ties.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore's competitive advantage lies in our ability to figure out how to organise ourselves more efficiently and effectively than others. The process is simple. We identify where we are. We determine where we want to be. Then we implement a gap-closure plan to get there. Nothing fancy, just a step-by-step systematic approach.

    While the recipe is simple, to implement it nation wide requires strong leadership, clear minds, focused and consistent execution. Being simple, it is also a recipe that others could copy. A good example is our Operational Headquarters strategy which has been quite successful in attracting multi-national companies to establish their OHQs here. Malaysia adopted the same strategy and has succeeded in attracting several major MNCs, including the software giant Microsoft, in direct competition with Singapore.

    We welcome other countries to emulate us. We support their effort to improve the standard of living of their people. The world will be a better and safer place if our neighbours are also prosperous. Still, Singaporeans should realise that competition will become even stiffer in future.

    Now that Singapore is amongst the richest economies in the world, the question is how do we continue to enlarge the economic pie and raise the income of our people. We may be rich in terms of per capita GDP but Singapore cannot be

considered a developed country as we lack depth in R&D and other capabilities that will ensure continued demand for our goods and services. Being rich also means that the cost of doing business in Singapore is now comparable to those in the developed countries. In a recent dialogue session of the Feedback Unit, chaired by Dr John Chen, on the President's Address, the high cost of doing business in Singapore was a main concern of the participants. They reflected that high office rental is one of the main components. Secondly, the use of the pricing mechanism by the Government to achieve policy objectives, for example, COEs, is another factor that causes the cost to rise in Singapore.

    Their concerns are also reinforced by the recent slow-down in our economy. The economic growth for the first quarter of 1997 was a slow 3.8%, with manufacturing registering a minus 6.7%, the third quarterly decline in a row. By economic definition, in fact, manufacturing should be in recession. While this is partly due to weak global demand, increasing business cost is definitely a contributing factor.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the message is quite clear. While we must continue to become more efficient, that is no longer enough. In many areas, we are already rated No. 1 in the world and have reached the point of diminishing returns. Consider, for example, the much acclaimed baggage delivery system in Changi Airport. Already we have achieved the target of delivering the first bag on the belt when the first passenger reaches there. There is little strategic value to do this any further.

    The Government's strategy here is to move our economy up the technological curve. The National Science and Technology Board is provided with ample funds. Good progress has been made, and more remains to be done. Let us look at the number of Research Scientists and Engineers (RSEs) per thousand in the workforce, a statistic commonly used to gauge a nation's strength in R&D. In the five-year period from 1990 to 1995, the number of RSEs increased from 27.7 per thousand to 47.7 per thousand, an increase of 72% or an annual growth rate

of 12%. Still, we are 13% behind the UK, 18% behind Germany, 36% behind the US and 41% behind Japan. Even amongst the NIEs in the Asia Pacific region, we are 17% behind Korea and 22% behind Taiwan.

    We are also investing in our IT infrastructure, including Singapore One, a $80 million project involving 31 companies. The spread of the Internet is probably the most important event in the post-industrial revolution. With more and more information accessible on-line, the future belongs to those proficient in surfing the Net. Only those tapping the resources on the Net will discover new business opportunities. Thus, it is most heartening that the Government has announced a $2 billion IT plan for schools. Our citizens' proficiency in the use of computers will ensure that Singapore will remain in the forefront and ahead of the competition.

    Even in R&D, high cost is a deterrent. Several US R&D operations in Singapore have closed down over the last several years and relocated their R&D operation back to the US, citing high cost. The recent shut-down of Apple Design Centre is a good example. As cost increases, we no longer can depend entirely on MNCs to develop our R&D capabilities. We need more home-grown companies, such as Creative and Aztech. We need more young men and women to take up science and engineering to produce the next generation of techno-entrepreneurs.

    Here, Sir, we face an uphill task. Young people are naturally attracted by the financially more rewarding careers. In all modern economies, the closer one's job is to the management of large sums of money the better is the pay package. Old favourites like lawyers and doctors are still very popular, with fund managers and remisiers increasing in favour. Young people are also choosing accountancy, business studies and shying away from the technological areas like computer science, engineering, manufacturing and other technical disciplines.

    Yet they need to be convinced that science and engineering are also rewarding career choices. In terms of pay, this choice should be as good as being a

On the other hand, the Government has been generous in granting Employment Passes to foreign engineers and software professionals to satisfy surging domestic demands. While this enlarges the pool of talents, it will also increase the competition in these careers and moderate the wages. Are we sending mixed signals to our young?

    Though technology is important, we should not ignore the opportunities in other sectors of our economy such as financial services, legal services, life sciences and medical services.
