    The Minister for Finance (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government's main interest in this river project is, of course, from the point of view of trade. In fact, a Committee was formed in 1954 to investigate the condition of the river and to make recommendations for its improvement. That Committee went into some detail in the engineering aspects of river improvement. Perhaps I may be permitted to explain briefly what are the improvements envisaged, and what is the Government's attitude towards these improvements.

    The river is important because it is one of the principal arteries of our entrepot trade. Many people may not be aware of the fact that more cargo is discharged through the river than through the wharves of the Singapore Harbour Board, that is if we exclude bunker oil. The traffic up and down the river is impeded in two ways. First, part of the river has been rather heavily silted up, so that at low tide movement of lighters is not possible. Then again at high tide there is not sufficient headway under the bridges, so that again lighter traffic is held up.

    The Committee proposed certain types of alteration. First, to repair or to reconstruct the bridges so that there is sufficient headway at high tide. Then it would be necessary to dredge a channel along the Singapore River so that traffic can proceed at low tide. Once dredging

operations are commenced, there is every danger that the quays of the river may collapse. Therefore, before the channel of the river is deepened, it will be necessary to strengthen the quays by means of sheet piling. This will be a most costly operation. The original cost is $7ï¿½ million and the latest estimate is that it will probably be nearer the region of $14 million. The estimated original cost of $14 million has now been increased to some $23 million.
