LAND ACQUISITION ACT
(Compensation at current market value)


    1. Mr Chiam See Tong asked the Minister for Law whether he will review the Land Acquisition Act to pay compensation at current market value for land compulsorily acquired as Singapore is now a first-world developed country.

    The Senior Minister of State for Law (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the Minister for Law): Mr Speaker, Sir, MinLaw has an ongoing review of various legislation. The relevant provisions of the Land Acquisition Act referred to by the Member will be included in that review. In fact, Dr Amy Khor and Mdm Cynthia Phua, during the Committee of Supply debate this year, made similar requests for review.

    Sir, the current approach in the Act is to peg compensation to the market value of the property as at the date of gazette notification or a statutory date, which at present is 1st January 1995, whichever is lower.

Column: 2459



    Although the approach in this provision has not been amended, Government, in fact, has regularly reviewed the statutory date to ensure that compensations are generally in line with market levels. Previous statutory dates were 30th November 1973, 1st January 1986, and 1st January 1992. We have not changed the current statutory date of 1st January 1995 because, given the depressed state of the property market, current values are below 1995 values. This means that, today, the statutory compensations paid to affected owners are already at current market value. When circumstances require it, Government will continue to update the statutory date of compensation.

    In addition, the Government introduced an ex gratia payment scheme in 1982 to cushion any financial hardship faced by owners of acquired properties. This scheme was initially introduced to top up statutory compensation to market value, should the statutory compensation be lower than market value. Since 1982, we have considerably expanded the scope and quantum given under the scheme. For example, whilst the scheme originally was available only to owner-occupied residential properties, today, owners of other types of properties, whether owner-occupied or not, are eligible to be considered for ex gratia compensation.

    Whilst, as I have said, we are prepared to review the relevant provisions, let me say that I do not agree with the suggestion inherent in Mr Chiam's question that we can completely jettison the Land Acquisition Act and its provisions because Singapore is better off now than before. Whether or not Singapore is a first-world developed country today, one feature about Singapore will never change, namely, that we are a small country and land is, and will always be, a scarce commodity.

    The Land Acquisition Act has made possible some of the important developments in Singapore, including our public housing programmes and other physical infrastructure, such as our ports, expressways and mass rapid transport system.

Column: 2460



    For future developments in land scarce Singapore, it is obvious that we will continue to need the Land Acquisition Act. Of course, we will need to address and review the question of how we must strike a balance between the interests of the individual landowners and the wider interests of the public good, which is to press on with development projects for public purposes without unduly increasing the taxpayer's burden.

    Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan (Hong Kah): Sir, I would like to ask the Minister to explain the rationale for the $1 compensation for the land acquired from Faith Assembly of God Church at Kim Keat Road.

    Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee: Sir, Dr Amy Khor refers to the recent case, publicised in the media, about the acquisition of part of the land of the Church at Kim Keat Road. Let me explain this case in greater detail.

    Sir, the Chief Valuer's Office accepted the recommendation of a private valuer on its panel and valued the strip of land acquired, which is about 200 square metres, at a nominal $1. In making this award, the Chief Valuer's Office applied existing law, including relevant provisions of the Land Acquisition Act and the Planning Act, which takes into account the zoning of the acquired land. On that basis, the Collector's award of $1 was determined. On a legal basis, Government stands by this award.

    Nevertheless, after further reviewing the case, Government has decided to give an ex gratia payment to the owners. Let me explain why. Before 1998, the portion of land, which has now been acquired, which formed a small part of a larger piece of Church land was affected by a road widening line. The affected portion was at that time not zoned as road. If the affected portion had been acquired before 1998, the statutory compensation would have been computed taking into account the existing use prior to acquisition, ie, carpark use. In that event, more than nominal compensation would have been given. In 1998, after amendments were made to the Land Acquisition Act and the

Planning Act, the portion which was previously affected by a road line was zoned road in the Master Plan. This was a change in planning practice so that the Master Plan would better reflect planning intention. The rezoning led to a valuation of a nominal $1 as there is no market value for a strip of land zoned road. But it was not Government's intention to change the compensation approach when it designated road lines as road zones under the Master Plan. In short, there was an unintended effect when it came to valuing the affected strip of land for compensation purposes upon acquisition. Hence, the $1 nominal compensation was an unintended consequence.

    The Minister for Law and I asked our officials to see how we can help the owners in this case. On review, we determined that this case is a special one relating to a specific situation. Given what I have said earlier, Government has now decided to make an ex gratia payment to put the owners in the position they would have been, with regard to compensation, as if the land affected was acquired before 1998, ie, based on a road line instead of a road zone. On this basis, a payment higher than $1 in statutory compensation will be given.

    We have informed the Church of this and we are working on the final amount with the Chief Valuer.
