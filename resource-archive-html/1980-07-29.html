ENERGY CONSERVATION
(Measures to introduce)


    4. Haji Sha'ari bin Tadin asked the Minister for Trade and Industry whether he plans to introduce energy conservation measures to encourage the people to save energy.

     The Minister for Finance and Acting Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Hon Sui Sen): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am glad the

Column: 1472

Member has raised this question. It gives me the opportunity to inform the House of the Government's energy plans.

    The second oil crisis last year has re-emphasized the fragile position of the world supply of oil and the importance of having a secure source of energy. The recession in the world economy and the current inflation are the direct effects of the disruption in oil supply and the doubling of oil prices. All forecasts indicate that global demand for oil will exceed supply if the world does not become more efficient in the use of energy and reduce its dependence on oil. If the world fails to moderate its oil consumption, the consequences will be increasing shortages and escalating prices. Any disruption of supply will be disastrous.

    Singapore is especially vulnerable because all our energy requirements are met entirely from oil, all of which has to be imported. Energy is therefore one of the most important factors affecting our economic growth. In view of this, my Ministry has set up an Energy Unit which will be responsible for formulating policies to reduce our vulnerability to fluctuations in oil supply and to enable us to be more efficient in our use of energy. As the implementation of energy policies falls under more than one Ministry, we have also established an Energy Coordination Committee to ensure that consistent energy policies are vigorously pursued in all sectors of the economy.

    The Unit and the Committee will study how we can diversify our sources of supply, reduce our dependence on oil, and encourage greater efficiency in energy usage in industry, commerce, transportation and in the homes.

    My answer to the Member's question is therefore in the affirmative. In fact, the Government has already taken a number of measures to promote energy conservation. Last year, the PUB organised a nation-wide "Save Energy Campaign". Amendments to the Building Regulations have been made to incorporate energy conservation requirements for new buildings. Electricity tariffs were also revised to discourage excessive

Column: 1473

consumption. An inter-Ministry committee under the Ministry of National Development is now studying how to ensure the economic use of power and fuel for equipment, plant and vehicles and to encourage energy conservation in buildings within the public sector. The findings of this Committee will provide guidelines that can also be applied in the private sector.

    For the future, the Energy Unit in the Ministry will consider further measures to promote economy in energy usage on a national scale. The Japanese have pursued energy conservation in a very serious and systematic manner. We are considering whether some of their ideas can be adapted for our use, for example, appointing energy managers in factories which are large users of energy, raising the minimum level of temperature by air-conditioning in buildings and systematically distributing information on standards and guidelines for reducing energy consumption in residences, offices, vehicles and factories.

    In essence, we must instil a high level of consciousness of the need to conserve energy in our population. A sense of energy economy, like courtesy, should become our way of life. We must also build up the expertise on how conservation can be achieved in industry, commerce, transportation and households.

CONSTITUTION OF THE REPUBLIC OF SINGAPORE
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister for Law and Science and Technology (Mr E.W. Barker): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Our Constitution, as it now stands, allows a citizen of 21 years of age to stand for election to this House. Insofar as residence is concerned, all he has to show is that at the date of his nomination for election he is resident in Singapore. Accordingly, a citizen who has spent a greater part of his life abroad and who may have resided in Singapore for only a brief period in his youth can stand as a candidate for election to Parliament. This is not in the national interest. A person who has spent a greater

Column: 1476

part of his life outside Singapore is likely to have his interest and sympathies elsewhere. Such a person would not be capable of truly sharing and reflecting the feelings and desires of our people and having their interests at heart. Furthermore, he would not have acquired sufficient experience of local conditions and problems if he has not actually resided here for a considerable period. Accordingly, this Bill introduces a minimum residential qualification of 10 years for candidates for Parliamentary election.

    I would like to point out to hon. Members that the Bill does not require the residential period of 10 years to be continuous. Further, the Bill does not require this residential period to be a period "immediately preceding" the date of nomination for Parliamentary election. All that the Bill requires is that a candidate must have been resident in Singapore for periods amounting, in the aggregate, to not less than 10 years before he can stand for election to Parliament.

    It is hoped that with this requirement we would be more in a position to ensure that only those citizens with a genuine interest for and a sense of commitment to Singapore will stand for election to Parliament.

    Sir, I beg to move.

     Mr Speaker: As this Bill seeks to amend the Constitution of Singapore, in accordance with the provisions of clause (2) of Article 5 of the Constitution, it shall not be passed by Parliament unless it has been supported on Second and Third Readings by votes of not less than two-thirds of the total number of Members of Parliament. The Bill must, therefore, receive the affirmative votes of not less than 46 Members on its Second and Third Readings.

    To ensure that the aforesaid provisions of Article 5 of the Constitution are complied with, Divisions will be taken on the Questions on the Second and Third Readings of the Bill.

    Question put, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Division taken: Ayes, 56; Noes, Nil; Abstentions, Nil: Absent, 12.

NATIONAL UNIVERSITY OF SINGAPORE BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.
3.08 p.m.

     The Minister of Education (Dr Tony Tan Keng Yam): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The future of Nanyang University has been for many years a matter of concern to Government and all those connected with the University. However, it was not until March this year that the discussions on the future of Nanyang University became a subject of public debate. The correspondence between the Prime Minister and members of the Nanyang University Council have already been published in the Press. Little will be achieved by going over the arguments again. It suffices for us to note that the Nanyang University Council, after considering the matter deeply, unanimously agreed that the future for Nanyang University (NU) lay in a merger with the University of Singapore (SU) to form the National University of Singapore (NUS). This proposal has the support of all the Nanyang University Members of Parliament.

    The Bill before the House seeks to give effect to the merger and to formally establish the National University of Singapore by legislation. The long title to the Bill which reads

Column: 1483

"An Act to give effect to the merger of the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University by the establishment and incorporation of the National University of Singapore and for matters connected therewith." emphasizes the fact that the National University of Singapore is in effect a merger of the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University. As suggested by the Nanyang University Council the full name of the NUS, provided in clause 3(2) of the Bill, will be the National University of Singapore (incorporating the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University) and the NUS may use its full name for such purposes as it thinks fit. This will give recognition to the fact that the National University of Singapore is not an entirely new institution but has its origin in the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University.

    The Bill is patterned along the lines of the University of Singapore Act but many drafting changes have been made and amendments and additions have been incorporated to bring the legislation up-to-date, I shall deal briefly with the significant provisions of the Bill.

    Clause 3 of the Bill formally establishes and incorporates the National University of Singapore. The functions of the University are specified in clause 5. These functions are common to most universities.

    Clause 6 provides that the University will be governed by the provisions of the University Constitution set out in the First Schedule. As in the case of the present Constitution of the University of Singapore, the Minister is empowered, on the advice of the Council, to amend the University Constitution by order published in the Gazette. This flexibility is necessary as occasion may well arise in future when the University Constitution has to be amended.

    Clauses 8 and 9 relating to accounts and the audit of accounts of the University are standard provisions. Clauses 12 and 13 which deal with the grant and acquisition of land for the purposes of the University and

Column: 1484

the application of the Societies Act to student bodies of the University are based on the existing provisions of the University of Singapore Act.

    Turning to the transitional provisions, hon. Members will note that clause 18, which repeals the University of Singapore Act and the Nanyang University Act, also contains transitional provisions to enable both these Universities, notwithstanding their dissolution, to confer degrees and diplomas on their graduates later this year as well as on higher degree candidates who have submitted their theses on or before 30th June 1980.

    Clause 15 read in conjunction with the Third Schedule deals with the transfer to the University of property and staff of the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University. All property, assets, rights, liabilities and obligations of the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University will be transferred to the National University of Singapore. All members of the teaching staff of the two existing Universities will become members of the teaching staff of the National University of Singapore. The deployment of all other persons employed by both existing Universities will be determined by the Minister.

    Clause 16 provides for all contracts, agreements, bequests, wills, trusts and other instruments referring to the University of Singapore or the Nanyang University to have effect as if the reference were to the National University of Singapore.

    In addition to these transitional provisions, clause 17 will also empower the Minister to make rules to provide for the removal of any difficulty which may arise by reason of the establishment of the National University of Singapore and the dissolution of the two existing Universities, and generally for the purpose of effecting the transition from the two existing Universities to the National University of Singapore. It is intended that rules will be made to provide for the initial appointments of the ViceChancellor, Deans of Faculties, heads of insti-

    The National University of Singapore will be the apex of the education system in Singapore. It is appropriate that the head of the NUS should be the highest personage in the Republic. The University Constitution therefore provides for the President to be the Chancellor of the University. The University Constitution provides that the Council will consist of 18 members. One-third of the members will be appointed from the staff of the University. These comprise the Vice-Chancellor and five members of the Senate. Two-thirds of the Council members will be appointed from outside the University. These comprise four persons to be appointed by the Chancellor, four persons to be appointed by the Government, and four persons to be appointed by the Council from the Register of Graduates.

    The National University of Singapore Students' Union (NUSSU) will be established. In the interim period, until the NUSSU begins to function, a pro-tem committee comprising officials from the existing University of Singapore Students' Union and the Nanyang University Students' Union has been formed to look after the activities of students. The National University of Singapore will not have a body similar to the Court of the University of Singapore, as such a body does not serve any useful function. The Court of the University of Singapore merely receives copies of statutes as well as the annual reports and audited annual accounts of SU and may pass such resolutions as the Court thinks fit on those documents. It does not play any meaningful role in the functioning of SU.

    The NUS will not have a Guild of Graduates. The experience of both the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University is that their Guilds of Graduates, unlike their alumni or Graduate Associations where membership is voluntary, have not been an effective instrument for the maintenance of links with their graduates. The mandatory meetings of Guild members are poorly attended. The number who turned up

Column: 1486

As for Nanyang University, the number who turned up at the last meeting of the Nanyang University Guild of Graduates was 64 out of a total membership of 11,085. If we exclude staff members who were persuaded by the respective administrations to attend the meetings, it would have been difficult to gather sufficient number of members to constitute a quorum.

    Instead of a guild, the National University of Singapore will have a Register of Graduates incorporating the members of the Nanyang University Guild of Graduates, the members of the University of Singapore Guild of Graduates and the National University of Singapore graduates as three separate parts of the Register.

    The Bill also provides for the National University of Singapore to maintain a register of members of the Nanyang University who are on the register of subscribers of NU.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, University education in Singapore has come a long way since the King Edward VII College of Medicine was founded in 1905. In 1929 Raffles College was established and the two Colleges were merged to form the University of Malaya in 1949, Nanyang University admitted its first students in 1956 and the Singapore Campus of the University of Malaya became the University of Singapore in 1962. Two years ago, the Joint Campus was set up in an attempt to work out a viable future for the Nanyang University. The formation of the National University of Singapore is, therefore, the culmination of 75 years of development of University education in Singapore.

    Operationally, the National University of Singapore has been a fact since July 7th this year when the first students were enroled. Dean-designates and head-designates of the University have been appointed so that they can commence the planning and teaching on the various courses without delay. The staff, both academic and non-academic, of the University of Singapore and the Nanyang University have been integrated. There have,

    The National University of Singapore is a significant institution, with 705 academic staff members and 9,000 students distributed over eight Faculties - Arts and Social Science, Science, Medicine, Dentistry, Law, Accountancy and Business Administration, Architecture and Building and Engineering. Its task will be the education and training of the brightest and most talented of our population.
