      The Senior Minister of State for Health (Dr Balaji Sadasivan) (for the Minister for Health):  Mr Speaker, Sir, my Ministry implements the AIDS Education Programme through the Health Promotion Board (HPB) to educate Singaporeans on how HIV infection is transmitted and its consequences.

 

        The HPB conducts year-long education using both the mass media and face-to-face activities to reach the general public, the high risk groups, foreign workers and the youths. Awareness activities include talks, interactive workshops, mobile exhibitions, video screening and distribution of educational materials.  In addition, a month-long AIDS awareness campaign is held in conjunction with World AIDS Day on 1st December. 

 

       As it is important to educate our youths, the AIDS Education Programme also aims to raise awareness of AIDS and other sexually transmitted infections among students in secondary schools, junior colleges, institutes of technical education, polytechnics and universities.

 

      As community involvement is an important aspect of the Programme, my Ministry and the Health Promotion Board collaborate with various businesses through their workplace health promotion programmes as well as voluntary organisations in our fight against AIDS.

 

       The Communicable Disease Centre (CDC) plays an important role in the education of patients with HIV infection or AIDS and their family members.  Patients seen at the CDC are carefully counselled and given information on the disease, its mode of transmission and how it can be prevented, as well as the risk that they pose to their sexual partners, and how they can care for themselves.  These patients are also informed that it is an offence if they carried out activities that will put others at-risk of the infection.

       The AIDS prevention message is summarised in the ABC Approach: (a) Abstinence from sex with multiple partners and sex workers; (b) Being faithful to one partner; and (c) Condom use.

 

      Singapore's HIV/AIDS rate is much lower than many Asian countries and is within the range found in developed countries.  So, our AIDS prevention programme works as well as that in many developed countries.   However, if we consider the relentless progressive rise in new HIV/AIDS cases in Singapore, with an expected record of more than 300 new cases this year, we clearly need to do more if we are to stem the rise.

 

       We need to get across four public health messages:  First, do not be promiscuous. HIV is a sexually transmitted disease and if all individuals lived an upright and moral life, there will be no HIV/AIDS problem.  HIV spread is related to promiscuity.  The greater the prevalence of promiscuity in a society, the greater the risk of HIV spread.  So we must reduce promiscuity.   Second, if an individual is having casual sex, a condom should be used.  It will provide significant protection against catching HIV/AIDS.  Third, if an individual has casual unprotected sex, that person is at risk of catching HIV and we recommend HIV testing.  If HIV is detected early, treatment can be started early.  Fourth, if a person is HIV positive, it is an offence to have sex without informing the partner of the HIV status.

 

      The first message will be targetted at the general public, and the second, third and fourth messages will be targetted at the high-risk groups, like gays and those having casual sex.
