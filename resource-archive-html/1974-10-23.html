    The Minister of State for Communications (Mr Chai Chong Yii): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    In July, 1973, the Minister for Home Affairs appointed a Road Traffic Management and Enforcement Committee to formulate basic policies of Road Traffic Management as well as to co-ordinate the roles of the various departments involved in traffic enforcement and management and to supervise the activities carried out by these departments with a view to achieving greater efficiency and effectiveness in tackling the immediate, short-term and long-term traffic problems. This Committee, chaired by the Permanent Secretary (Home Affairs), comprised representatives from the Ministries of Communications, Finance, Environment, Public

Column: 1032

Works Department, Registry of Vehicles, Attorney-General's Chambers, Public Utilities Board and the Police Force. This Committee has been superseded by the Road Transport Action Committee which was established in November, 1973, comprising five Permanent Secretaries from the Ministries of National Development, Communications, Home Affairs and the Budget and Development Divisions of Finance Ministry.

    After one year of close study and research, the Road Traffic Management and Enforcement Committee came to the conclusion that our present enforcement system is, amongst other things, noticeably lacking in the following:-

    (i) effective means of detecting persistent offenders;

    (ii) adequate penalties to deter persistent offenders;

    (iii) administrative procedures that allow expedient enforcement action.

    To rectify this, the Committee recommended a number of amendments to the Road Traffic Act (Cap. 92). The recommendations of the Committee which have been accepted are incorporated in the Road Traffic (Amendment) Bill.

    Clause 2 of the Amendment Bill will empower a Police Officer to require any person driving a motor vehicle on a road to produce his driving licence within 24 hours at such police office or station as may be specified by the officer where the person is unable to produce his licence on the spot.

    Clause 4 of the Amendment Bill provides that if a person is convicted of an offence under the Act or is disqualified by a court from holding or obtaining a driving licence, the court shall send a notice of conviction or order of disqualification to the Registrar of Vehicles who will be required to record such particulars of conviction or disqualification and maintain such record for a period not exceeding three years from the date of the conviction or expiry of disqualification as the case may be.

Column: 1033



    Much publicity has already been given to clause 5 of the Amendment Bill. This concerns the Points Demerit System which aims at identifying and removing dangerous drivers from the roads. It also serves as a psychological deterrence against reckless driving. This system entails the giving of points by administrative means for traffic offences likely to cause accidents. A points penalty table under the Rules will specify the points for various traffic violations.

    When a driver has attained a total of 12 or more points within a year, the Registrar of Vehicles will have powers to suspend the driving licence of the driver for a period not exceeding six months. A warning notice, however, will first be sent by the Registrar of Vehicles to the motorist the moment he has accumulated or exceeded six points. On receipt of a notice of suspension, the driver has the right within 14 days to appeal to the Minister against the decision of the Registrar of Vehicles. The Minister's decision on the matter will be final. Any person whose driving licence is suspended will have to surrender it to the Registrar of Vehicles and will be prohibited from driving any motor vehicle on the roads during the period of suspension of his driving licence.

    Clause 8 of the Amendment Bill will impose liability upon a motor vehicle owner for parking offences in cases where the particulars of the offending driver are not available. Unless the vehicle owner can furnish particulars of the driver to the police within seven days from the date of issue of the police notice or satisfy the police or the court that the vehicle was, at the relevant time, stolen or had been illegally taken or used, or establish that he did not know and could not, with reasonable diligence, have ascertained the name and address of the driver, the vehicle owner would be liable for the offence.

    Clause 9 of the Amendment Bill will enable the Minister to make rules with respect to jaywalking. Over the last three

Column: 1034

years, a total of 10,519 pedestrians were killed and injured by motor vehicles. In 1973, a total of 380 persons were killed in road traffic accidents, 185 of whom were pedestrians. While many of these accidents involving pedestrians could be attributed to reckless driving by motorists, a great number of these fatalities were caused by pedestrians crossing the road heedless of traffic conditions. The pedestrians, therefore, need to be disciplined as well.

    Under the Amendment Bill, it will be an offence for pedestrians to cross the road other than at places where proper crossing facilities are provided. This would, however, apply to the area 50 metres from either side of any such crossing. Where the crossings are close to one another, the area of enforcement may be extended to cover the length between and a little beyond such crossings. Traffic signs will be erected to indicate the areas of enforcement. As it is not the intention to punish but to educate pedestrians in the proper use of the facilities provided for them, initially small composition fines would be imposed on jaywalkers.
