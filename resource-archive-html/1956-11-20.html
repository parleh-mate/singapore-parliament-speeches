LUXURY TAX BILL


    Order for second reading read.

10.13 a.m.

    The Financial Secretary (Mr T. M. Hart): Sir, I have His Excellency the Governor's authority to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This, Sir, is a complicated Bill. As hon. Members will see from the Comparative Table which appears on page 25, the Law Officers, in drafting, have referred extensively to United Kingdom Finance Acts, thus taking advantage of the long experience of the United Kingdom in the matter of luxury and purchase taxes. The explanatory note at page 23 of the Bill serves as a useful guide to these provisions. Perhaps I may be allowed to outline some of them, Sir.

    Part I contains definitions which require careful study.

    Part II calls for no comment.

    Part III is most important. It deals with the nature and scope and method of imposition of a luxury tax. Clause 8 (1) prescribes that the tax should be charged on the wholesale value of all chargeable goods bought under chargeable purchases. The Governor in Council is vested with the power of deciding what goods shall be subject to tax and at what rates. Any order in this connection, however, must be ratified by the Assembly within seven days. Clause 8 (4) defines a chargeable purchase as "any purchase made from a wholesale merchant or manufacturer selling by wholesale". Purchases made by a registered wholesale merchant or a registered manufacturer of goods as stock for business or as materials are exempt from tax. Clause 8 (5) specifies

Column: 750

that the tax will become due on delivery of the goods under the purchase and the seller shall be accountable for tax. Clause 8 (6) states that tax will be charged on the wholesale value of all chargeable goods imported into the colony except goods imported by registered wholesale merchants or manufacturers. Clause 8 (7) states the circumstances and conditions under which goods may be imported tax free by registered wholesale merchants and manufacturers. Clause 8 (9) exempts from tax goods imported for re-export or transhipment under certain conditions.

    Clause 9 defines "wholesale value" as the price which, in the opinion of the Comptroller, the goods would fetch on a sale by wholesale in the open market in the Colony to a retail trader carrying on business in the Colony and if it were made in the circumstances specified in the First Schedule to the Bill.

    Clause 10 deals with the registration of wholesale merchants and manufacturers whose business includes the selling of any chargeable goods. Clause 10 (2) provides that a manufacturer who is not required to be registered under the Ordinance shall be so registered if he satisfies the Comptroller that he uses chargeable goods in substantial quantities as materials. Clause 10 (3) provides that persons who should be registered must make application to the Comptroller. Clause 10 (4) provides that any wholesale merchant or manufacturer who finds that the proviso to clause 10 (1) no longer applies to him, shall immediately apply for registration. Clause 10 (6) provides for the issue of a certificate of registration, and the Comptroller may in certain circumstances call for security for the payment of tax.

    Clause 11 contains provisions whereby the Comptroller shall exempt a person from registration when the tax on his purchases shall be not less than the tax on his sales; in other words, where the tax payable on his purchases and importations in respect of stock or materials would be more than the tax he would be accountable for on his sales.

Column: 751



    Clause 12 prescribes when a purchase shall be deemed a purchase by a registered wholesale merchant as stock or by a registered manufacturer as materials, and therefore exempt from tax.

    Clause 13 provides for relief against a double charge of tax.

    Clause 14 states that, where a registered wholesale merchant or manufacturer appropriates or applies any chargeable goods in his ownership to the purposes of any business carried on by him of selling chargeable goods by retail or of letting out chargeable goods on hire or to any other purpose not being a sale of the goods, the appropriation or application shall be treated as if it were a chargeable purchase.

    Clause 15 (1) prescribes that, when any person required to be registered applies a chargeable process, tax shall be chargeable on the wholesale value of the resulting goods and shall become due on the completion of the process. The person who applies the process shall be accountable for the tax. Clause 15 (2) exempts persons who apply a chargeable process from accountability for tax when the goods are his property at the time of the completion of the process, subject, of course, to the provisions of clause 14.

    Clause 16 provides that any person who carries out a chargeable process shall be treated as a manufacturer whose business includes the selling of chargeable goods.

    Clause 17 states that any retail trader purchasing on a wholesale scale shall be treated as a wholesale merchant.

    Clause 18 states that the seller of chargeable goods under a chargeable purchase shall add the amount due in tax to any invoice issued by him.

    Clause 19 provides relief for exported goods. Under subclause (1) the Comptroller may exempt from tax goods which would otherwise be chargeable if they were exported from the colony. Under subclause (2) the Financial Secretary may give directions for the payment of an allowance for tax paid or payable in respect of goods exported when the Comptroller is satisfied that the conditions specified in the subclause exist.

    It will be seen, Sir, that the Bill gives wide powers to the administration. Any goods can be deemed luxury goods and any rate of tax imposed on them by the Governor in Council, subject, of course, to the approval of the Assembly.

    Again the Comptroller of Customs, who is responsible for the care and management of the tax, is given wide powers under a number of clauses. The wide discretion given to the Comptroller has apparently been a cause of anxiety to the Bar Council which has addressed the Attorney-General and other Members of the Assembly on the subject. In particular, the Bar Council takes objection to the Comptroller's powers under clauses 9, 17 and 26. Note has been taken of these objections and they will be given careful consideration in due course, and doubtless when this Bill is sent to Select Committee, the Members of the Committee will consider their objections too.

    Government has also given consideration to the question of local industries and has decided, as a measure of encouragement, to exempt from tax all goods entirely manufactured locally and also goods in respect of which a certain percentage of their wholesale value has been expended locally. The necessary amendments to the text of the Bill will be moved in Committee in due course.

    Justification for this Bill is that Government will have to tap all sources of revenue in the future, and luxury goods appear to be a justifiable source of revenue. As I said earlier, very considerable sums of money are spent each year on goods which cannot, by any stretch of the imagination, be deemed essential or even desirable. If confirmation of this statement is required, one has only to look about one, in the shop windows, in the homes of people in Singapore and at people proceeding about their lawful occasions. That being so, it is only proper that Government should have some small share of the sums spent on luxury goods. A great majority of countries in the world today impose taxes on goods which are outside the "essential" category. In the great majority of cases, the tax is imposed on the wholesale price.

    It would not be possible to give any estimate of the possible yield from this tax until it is decided what commodities should be taxed. In selecting goods to be taxed, careful consideration will be given to the nature and needs of the entrepot trade. This action was taken on the understanding, however, that the manufacturers of the local cigarettes would maintain the price of the product at pre-Budget levels and that any increase in price would immediately result in an increase in duty.
