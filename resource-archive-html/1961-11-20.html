COMMISSION OF INQUIRY
INTO
CONSTRUCTIONAL CAPACITY
OF SINGAPORE
(Employment of technical men to do administrative work)


    1. Mr David Marshall asked the Minister for Finance whether, in the light of the finding of the Commission of Inquiry into the Constructional Capacity of Singapore that "the employment of technical men to do administrative work is a wasteful use of an extremely scarce resource", the Government would consider reorganisation of administrative work in all departments where technical and professional men discharge administrative functions.

    Dr Goh Keng Swee: Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission and with the agreement of the Member for Anson, I propose to table the answer.

    Copy of answer handed in.

    Mr Speaker: Of course, the formula would be that the Minister will publish the answer in the Official Report. He is entitled to do that even without asking for permission of the Member who asked the question.

    Following is the answer:

    (a) The quotation in the Interim Report of the Commission referred to by the Honour-able Member has specific reference to the circumstances existing in the P.W.D. and the City Engineer's Department. The Commission pointed out the adverse effect, from the point of administration, on the D.P.W. and his Deputy who are too involved with the daily routine administrative work of the department which could be equally well discharged by an Administrative Officer. In this connection, it was recommended that the former post of A.O. in the P.W.D. which was abolished in January, 1958, should be resuscitated. This recommendation of the Commission was accepted and the post of A.O. was created in January.

Column: 272



    (b) The Honourable Member has suggested the extension of this finding to the other departments in which technical and professional officers perform administrative functions. This is not a new idea and is an important factor to be considered in any organisation. The fact of the matter is that the Head or his Deputy Head of a technical department would have to discharge a certain amount of administrative work which cannot he delegated to any one. Further, at the present moment, experienced administrative officers are scarce, insufficient to man all the posts in the establishments of Government, it is always a problem of getting on with what you have and not with what you would want to have. In the case of certain Ministries, such as the Health and Education Ministries, which have a preponderant number of professional officers, some officers who show a flair for administrative work can be posted for administrative duties.

    Following is the answer:

    (a) The Commission, in their earls deliberations, realised the importance of the immediate problems relating to the capacity of the Government departments concerned with the Government's construction problem and to those departments whose functions and work affect construction activity and the utilization construction capacity, and decided to submit an Interim Report on the first 2 items of the

Terms of Reference. Briefly these are to enquire into the capacity of the building industry in Singapore and how it can be expanded.

    This Bill seeks to make the Control of Imports and Exports Ordinance a permanent Ordinance. The Ordinance was promulgated on 8th December, 1950, initially for one year. It has since been extended for ten further periods of a year each and will expire on 7th December, 1961.

    Although the Ordinance provides that the Assembly may extend its operation for such period or periods as it may think fit, in practice extensions have been for periods of one year. The Ordinance empowers the Minister to make orders to regulate or prohibit the import and export of goods. The reason why this Ordinance had such a temporary nature at the time it was introduced was due to the opposition from the trading community who feared that controls, if made permanent, would be detrimental to trade and Singapore's free port status.

    During the past ten years, most import and export regulations have been removed and those at present in being are imposed mainly for health, security and exchange control reasons. In present world conditions, the power to exercise controls on imports and exports is one maintained by all trading countries as being essential in a lesser or greater degree to the requirement of their economic policies concerning trade and industry. In the interest of the economy of the State of Singapore. it is necessary that the Government should have the powers to restrict or direct imports and exports as might be required.

    My Trade and Commerce Advisory Council, which is composed of representatives of the four Chambers of Commerce, has been consulted and has no objection to the proposal that the Ordinance be made a permanent one. It is considered that this procedure takes up unduly the time of the House and that the basic reason for its temporary nature, namely, the fear of the trading community over controls, has now been allayed over these last ten years.
