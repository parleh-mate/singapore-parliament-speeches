DENGUE EPIDEMIC

     1.  Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for the Environment and Water Resources what new measures will be adopted by the National Environment Agency in

fighting the present dengue epidemic after receiving the advice from the panel of dengue experts following their two-week study in Singapore.

     The Minister for the Environment and Water Resources (Assoc. Prof. Dr Yaacob Ibrahim):  Mr Speaker, Sir, during their 2-week study, the expert panel looked into the epidemiological features of the current dengue outbreak, conducted field trips to observe dengue control operations and reviewed the national framework for dengue control.  The panel recognised that Singapore has one of the best dengue control programmes in the world and had also supported our outreach efforts to involve the community and the private sector.  However it recommended that more research should be carried out to better understand dengue transmission dynamics and changes in mosquito's behavior, and to enhance the vector surveillance systems.

 

Research on Aedes Mosquitoes and the Dengue Virus

 

     We agree with the expert panel that more research be done to enhance our dengue surveillance and control efforts. Effective prevention and control of dengue requires an active laboratory-based dengue surveillance programme to provide early warning for possible dengue outbreaks. 

 

      In this respect, studies to understand prevailing serotypes and dengue virus strains are already being carried out in various laboratories such as the Environmental Health Institute (EHI), Genome Institute of Singapore (GIS) and some of our hospitals such as Tan Tock Seng Hospital.   To ensure that there is quality control for these lab tests, MOH has designated the Public Health Laboratory currently sited at Singapore General Hospital (SGH) as the national reference laboratory for all infectious diseases.  It will be supported by the Environmental

Column: 1499

Health Institute (EHI) of NEA in the area of vector-borne diseases including dengue. 

 

     As pointed out by the expert panel, we also recognise that transmissions can occur at sites away from home or go unreported.  There are asymptomatic dengue cases, where a person infected with dengue exhibits no symptoms.  We will intensify our research efforts and have identified a number of research projects that will be undertaken by NEA and MOH.

 

Enhanced Mosquito Surveillance System

 

     We will also improve on our operational vector surveillance system.   NEA has been using ovitraps as a surveillance tool to detect mosquito activity to direct its vector control work.  We will now study how to enhance this to provide early warning system to residents on increases in mosquito populations in their estates.  This will allow actions to be taken before any dengue outbreak occurs.  A pilot study will be conducted by NEA at Marsiling using ovitraps for monitoring the Aedes population. 

 

Continuing Medical Education

 

     The experts also affirmed the important role played by GPs in tackling dengue.  MOH will intensify its efforts to educate GPs by continuing to circulate advisories to medical practitioners and convene seminars from time to time.  This is to keep the medical professionals abreast of the latest developments in the epidemiology and clinical diagnosis to prevent and control dengue.  In fact, MOH has worked with the College of Family Physicians to issue a handbook with the detailed guidelines on clinical diagnosis and management of dengue patients to all physicians.

Need to Remain Vigilant

 

     It is encouraging to note that our 7-day average daily number of dengue cases has progressively declined from 106 on 23rd September to 56 on 16th October 2005.    But it is still too early to determine if the downward trend will continue.  Dengue is a resurgent problem globally; and being endemic to the region, Singapore can expect dengue to recur on a regular basis.  Hence, all of us must remain vigilant and diligent in playing our part to prevent mosquito breeding.
