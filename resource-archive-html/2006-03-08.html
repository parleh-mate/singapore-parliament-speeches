CHANGE OF ADDRESS ON NRIC

(Updating)

     The following Question stood in the name of Dr Warren Lee -

 

     1.  To ask the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs if the Government can improve the current method of updating a change of address on the NRIC where the changes are typed onto an adhesive strip and stuck onto the existing NRIC as the sticker can be peeled off and the original address represented as the present address.

     Mr Arthur Fong (West Coast):  Question No. 1, Sir.

     The Senior Minister of State for Home Affairs (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs):  Mr Speaker Sir, when an NRIC holder reports a change of his address, the registration officer at the reporting centre would obliterate the old address on the NRIC by making cut grooves into the address section on the back of the NRIC with a metal inscriber. He then marks over it with permanent ink, before pasting the new address sticker over the old address.  Any attempt to tamper with the change in address will show up. We started this procedure in April 1999 to address the problem of NRIC holders tearing off the sticker, and passing off the original address as the present address.

 

     Furthermore, we have put in place strict laws against those who tamper with their NRIC. Under the National Registration Act, any person who forges, erases, alters or falsifies any entry in or tampers with an identity card, can be fined up to $10,000, imprisoned for up to 10 years, or both. Using another person's address as his own is an act of cheating, which is an offence punishable under the Penal Code.


     Over the last three years, an average of 193,000 NRIC holders reported a change of address each year. We have made it convenient for those who need to report their new address by allowing them to do so at any Neighbourhood Police Centre or Police Post. The current procedure balances cost and convenience with security.

      "That the total sum to be allocated for Head K of the Estimates be reduced by $100." - [Dr Amy Khor Lean Suan].

     The Minister for Education (Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam): Mr Chairman, Sir, let me first thank Dr Amy Khor, Dr Tan Boon Wan, Mdm Ho Geok Choo, Dr Ong Seh Hong, Mr Ong Ah Heng and Mr Zainudin Nordin for raising questions on what more we can do to engage students who are at risk of  dropping out from our schools, what more we can do to get them to stay in school and benefit from the quality education that our schools provide. Dr Lily Neo and Dr Loo Chong Yong had raised similar issues during the Budget debate earlier.


     Let me, first, address the scale of the problem.

     First, students who drop out during their primary or secondary school years. This number has been coming down. As a percentage of each student cohort, the number of students who drops out without completing a secondary education has, in fact, declined from 3.6% of the cohort in 2003 to 2.6% of the cohort in 2005.  But this is still too many - 2.6% of the cohort is about 1,200 students in each cohort - and we want to bring it down.


     Another concern for us is the students who do complete their secondary education, but do not progress to a post-secondary education. We do not have a precise handle on the numbers because many of the students who do not enrol in our polytechnics, ITEs or junior colleges, in fact, go on to study in various private institutions, such as NAFA, LaSalle, SHATEC, and so on.  And some of them go overseas. They have not dropped out, but are continuing their studies.

     Our main focus at the post-secondary level is on encouraging as many of our Normal (Technical) students as possible to go on to the ITE.  We are making progress. The percentage of Normal (Technical) students who go on to ITE has been steadily increasing over the years. Last year, 76% of our Normal (Technical) students went on to ITE, up from 63% in 1998.  The increase reflects the efforts of both the ITE and our secondary school teachers, who bring their students to the ITE campuses to see what they offer and get a taste of the range of courses they can take at the ITE. I will touch more on this later.


     But, again, we want to do more. The number of Normal (Technical) students who choose not to go to the ITE translates to about 3.3% of the overall age cohort, which is about 1,500 students. We will do more to engage this group of students, and encourage them to go on to a post-secondary education.  This is the second group we are focusing on, besides the group of students who drop out during their primary and secondary school years. We cannot completely prevent students from dropping out of school.  The reasons why they drop out are varied, and the fundamental reasons usually lie outside the school. And, even within the school, there are no quick fixes to this problem.

    We are working on several initiatives, beginning from the pre-primary level and extending all the way up to the ITE - initiatives to help students learn better, see the relevance of what they learn, and feel engaged in the school community. It is a challenging and complex task, and we are trying some new approaches. We are also working with MCYS and the various social agencies, so that what we try to do in schools goes hand in hand with efforts to strengthen the family.


     Let me, first, focus on a key initiative that Dr Amy Khor, Mr Zainudin Nordin, Dr Ong Seh Hong and others have referred to, which relates to vocational education. In each cohort, there is a small group of students who are unable to cope with the mainstream curriculum. In 2005, there were 1,100 students who failed the Primary School Leaving Examination (PSLE).  Of these 1,100, about 130 of them failed the PSLE for the third time.

     Currently, many of these students enrol at the two Vocational Training Centres (VTCs) under the Institute of Technical Education (ITE), eg, Geylang Serai VTC and Assumption Vocational Institute.  But attrition is high, sometimes because of the lack of home support.  60% of the VTC students drop out. Of the 40% that do complete the programme, less than half made it to ITE.


     We spoke to teachers, students and parents, to work out a new pathway that can help these students stay in education and get the best chance to succeed. First, we will open the doors to such students earlier, as Dr Amy Khor has suggested. Currently, the VTCs admit the students only after they have reached 14 years of age, and provide them with a two-year programme - which means that the student would first need to have failed his PSLE three times before he can enter the VTC.


     Many of the VTC students and their parents told us that even with the best efforts of their primary schools, it was not helpful for them to keep repeating a PSLE curriculum that they were able to benefit from. There are also limits to what we can do to provide a more customised approach for these students while they remain in primary school, as they are distributed among many schools with no more than five or 10 students per school. So we should try to let them move beyond primary school, instead of waiting for them to take a third try at the PSLE, and move them up to a school where they can be provided with an education that is customised to their needs, and their different learning styles and abilities.


     Second, we will take a more holistic approach to engaging these students. They need a programme that not only focuses on building skills, but on nurturing emotional resilience and giving them a sense of belonging to the school. In fact, the students we spoke to - those who had gone through the VTC and succeeded in going onto the ITE - tell us that they find the teachers of the VTC warm and approachable. But they hope for a fuller environment, with a range of CCAs, for instance, a broader range of courses, and a more holistic and school-like environment.

     MOE will set up a new school to implement this new and enhanced pathway for students who can benefit from a more customised and practice-based curriculum. The new school, which will be called NorthLight School, will build on the current VTC programme and enhance it. Geylang Serai Vocational Training Centre will become a part of the new school.


     NorthLight School will admit students from January 2007. It will allow in students who have attempted the PSLE twice or more (in other words, 13 years old or older). It will thus be able to give them a longer programme - a three-year programme will be offered to students who have failed the PSLE twice and want to enter the school. Parents and students will retain the choice, if they wish, of repeating the PSLE a third time.  The school will also be open to students who leave secondary school prematurely. We expect that it will start smallish, about 600 students, including the current Geylang Serai VTC students, and expand up to 1,400 students eventually, which is about the size of a regular school.

     Getting the right school leaders and teachers is critical. Their conviction is critical.  The MOE Committee that studied how we can enhance the VTC programme visited a number of similar schools abroad. One was the Life Learning Academy in San Francisco. In fact, it is set on an island called Treasure Island.  It chooses students who have done very poorly in nearby schools and puts them together in a 'living and learning' community. The principal and teachers were passionate about what they were doing. The principal herself had a rough start in her youth, but turned her life around. She had a personal mission to help youth in trouble make good. One of the Chemistry teachers was an aerospace engineer who left a high-paying job because he wanted to do the same. And they were achieving results. Some of the school's students even made it to the top universities in California.


     We have put some effort into choosing the right people for NorthLight. We have chosen as NorthLight's first principal, an experienced MOE school leader with a zeal for helping students in difficulty. She is Mrs Chua Yen Ching, whose work in recent years as principal of Shuqun Secondary School has gained the attention of the school community and the public. Yen Ching has developed fresh approaches to re-define success for her students, and help them gain the confidence to pursue their dreams.


     She will be accompanied by many dedicated staff from the present Geylang Serai VTC, and other MOE teachers with a passion for helping this group of students. One of them won the Outstanding Youth in Education Award last year, for the way she has used the arts to reach out to students who feel disengaged from their studies. In fact, she had written a thorough report on how we should make more use of the arts to reach out to students at risk, which she had sent to me before she even  knew that we would start a new school of this nature. The school will have a collection of passionate teachers like her.


     NorthLight School will aim to prepare as many of its students as possible for further education at ITE or apprenticeship with industry. It will also aim to give them skills and strengths that they can take with them through life. Its new three-year curriculum will have three key aspects: first, to place emphasis on developing emotional resilience and instilling essential life-skills, as Dr Amy Khor had also suggested, such as taking responsibility and managing relationships. These will take up a greater share of curriculum time but within a longer programme of three years. The school will also have a full CCA programme, including sports, games and the arts, so as to develop bonds amongst the students and develop character. The school will have more full-time, inhouse counsellors to advise and guide students facing social and emotional challenges.


     Second, the school will adopt a hands-on and experiential approach to learning, suited to students who learn better in non-traditional classroom settings.  As Mrs Chua Yen Ching put it to me, she wants NorthLight to be the school where "School Comes Alive" - where students find lessons interesting, and see their relevance to day-to-day life.


     Third, there will be a wider range of vocational options to stimulate students' interests and open up their career choices. Each of the options will have a 10-week long industrial attachment.


     The school's new approach to learning, especially its attempt to create a living and learning community for students where they apply what they learn in their classes in day-to-day real-world applications, will be of interest to some of our mainstream schools as well. The exchange of experience between NorthLight and the mainstream sector can throw up more ideas on how we can engage all our students and help them stay in school.


     Besides the new vocational pathway that Northlight School will provide, MOE is implementing several other targeted initiatives to assist students who are at risk of dropping out. We have mapped out a comprehensive approach, at various stages of the education system.


     First, at pre-school. We know the early years in kindergarten are important in nurturing children's confidence and interest in learning. Children who are confident and eager to learn are more likely to take school seriously, and want to stay on in school. We have been helping to level up the quality of teachers in our kindergartens, and MOS Chan Soo Sen will be talking more about our approach. We are also working on some more targeted interventions at the pre-school level.


     MOE is working with MCYS and other partners to help encourage every family to put their children in pre-school education. That is the first  priority.

     Next, we are starting a prototype project with some neighbourhood kindergartens, aimed especially at helping children who do not receive adequate support at home. We  are working with 10 neighbourhood kindergartens on what we call Project SEAL (Sustained Early Assistance for Learning). It is a multi-pronged effort and a multi-agency approach that pulls together expertise from several bodies. An important part of this prototype project is to identify young children with learning difficulties early while they are in kindergartens.  For example, the Dyslexia Association of Singapore is developing a simple screening tool to help teachers identify children who need special help in language.  MOE will then work with the kindergartens to customise the programme to support these children.  The National Library Board will loan story books to supplement the kindergartens' reading resources and assist them to set up reading enrichment programmes for the children.

     Next, primary schools, when the children enter Primary 1. We are enhancing the Learning Support Programme.  As Mr Ong Ah Heng has pointed out, the Learning Support Programme is a critical piece in our strategy to help students who are not school-ready when they enter primary schools.  The Learning Support Programme has helped many students.  It is a specialised programme, focusing on basic oral language and reading skills.  Each year, about 12%-14% of our Primary 1 pupils with weak basic language and literacy skills are identified for support in the programme.  And on average, after one year of intervention, about one-third of the pupils supported by the programme will graduate from the programme and rejoin the regular classes.


     We are now piloting an enhanced Learning Support Programme for English in 34 primary schools this year.  It will further differentiate the students, because some of them need much more basic support than others.  There is a group of students each year, with very weak language skills, who needs more targeted attention.  We will put them through a more intensive reading support programme in the first instance.  Over the longer term, if we can reach out to such students when they are in kindergartens, we can hopefully reduce the numbers in this group when they enter our primary schools.

     Another feature of our enhanced Learning Support Programme is a smaller group size.  Pupils are supported in groups of no more than five pupils, as compared to 10 currently.  MOE will monitor and evaluate the effectiveness of the pilot at the end of this year, before fine-tuning and implementing it in all our schools from 2007.


    The enhancement of the Learning Support Programme will also be supported by changes in English Language classes for all students in the early primary years.  This will help all students, but it is particularly impactful for students from lower-income families - whose main language deficit is English, because it is not the language spoken at home.


     We have started a pilot programme, or what we call, SEED - English Language. SEED refers to the whole set of experiments we are taking in the early primary years, so SEED - English Language is an experiment that is taking place for English Language teaching.  So this is a pilot programme.  We have started in 30 schools this year to provide students with an enhanced curriculum for learning grammar and phonics, to help them learn English with confidence. I would not go into the details, but it involves a very careful and gradual building up of the students' language skills.  And it also aims to develop amongst young children a love for reading at a young age, by providing more early exposure to books and engaging them in the conversation on what is being read.


     Second, we are also rolling out Learning Support for Mathematics.  We want children with a weak start in numbers to be able to catch up and follow the class.  This is now being implemented in 50 schools at Primary 1, after it has spent the last four to five years piloting this programme, and it will be in all our schools from 2007. All the schools will be provided with additional teachers to provide support to Primary 1 pupils with a late start in numeracy skills.

    We are also investing more to help students with special learning difficulties like dyslexia and ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder) in our schools.  By 2010, we will have 140 trained Special Needs Officers in our primary schools.  We already have 28 of them in our primary schools currently.  We are also aiming to train close to 100 officers for secondary schools and they will be concentrated in certain schools - some schools that will pull together resources for dyslexia, others for students who are autistic.  These are students who do not have severe difficulties and can be well accommodated with our mainstream system and they can benefit from the mainstream curriculum. These provisions that we are making in the mainstream schools will complement what we are doing to improve quality and facilities in our special schools which cater to children with more significant learning difficulties.


     Next, secondary schools.  We are strengthening counselling resources in all our schools but with priority being given to the secondary schools because that is where the challenges of growing up are  greatest.  MOE is on track to providing all secondary schools with one  full-time inhouse school counsellor by this year.  Primary schools and junior colleges will receive a full-time counsellor by 2008.  Many of the counsellors we are recruiting are former teachers or some current teachers who decided to switch to full-time counselling and some former principals.  They understand students and they have a heart for them.  And we put all of them through a rigorous six-month diploma course at NIE.  It is early days, but so far this programme is working quite well.

     But the schools are not just reacting to problems, or dealing with problems as and when they arise and calling the counsellor in.  They are taking a proactive approach well before the counsellor becomes necessary.  It is a holistic approach, getting students to participate in CCAs, camps and other school activities, so that they develop bonds amongst each other and develop confidence in themselves.  The schools are helping students find the best in themselves, whether in their studies or in their CCAs and other activities.  This proactive approach to developing bonds among students and creating a sense of attachment to the school is being enhanced in all our schools.


     Another initiative is the Normal (Technical) course.  We are implementing a revised Normal (Technical) curriculum with a more practical orientation, to better engage students in their learning.  The curriculum content is being made more meaningful to students by relating what they learn to daily life.  There will be greater emphasis on group work and presentations, creativity, hands-on activities and use of IT, and new ways of assessing the Normal (Technical) students, such as evaluating their coursework, either individually or in groups, instead of the traditional pen-and-paper test. We will start rolling out the revised Normal (Technical) curriculum in 2007.


     As part of the revised approach to Normal (Technical), we also introduced elective modules last year to create opportunities to broaden the experiences of the students.  The students enjoy the elective modules tremendously because of the more practical and hands-on nature of these modules.  But they also develop a deeper understanding of the concepts of their learning quite often.  One of our principals gave me an example of students who attended an elective module on baking at the Baking Industry Training Centre.  They were learning to make swiss rolls.  But the four-hour session on swiss rolls also made Mathematics come alive, as the students have to calculate the correct proportion of water, flour, sugar and the other ingredients, in order to bake delicious swiss rolls.  The students were so engaged that they asked if the session could be extended to a longer time.  And the session also gave ideas to the school teacher who was there, on how to conduct the regular classes for the Normal (Technical) students back in school.


    The elective modules are catching on in our schools. More than 40% of schools which offer the Normal (Technical) course are now offering at least one elective module to their students, in a whole range of areas from digital videography to basic mechatronics.  They are making schools more interesting and more relevant to life for the students.


     The next piece is what we are doing at ITE.  We are making ITE a centre of excellence in technical education.  All across Asia and, indeed, internationally, ITE's reputation is now up there - it is well-known.


     ITE's key improvements are in two areas in recent years: new courses to keep pace with the interest of students and the needs of industry, and closer collaboration with industry to ensure that ITE students find fruitful employment after graduation.


     The changes are making an impact.  ITE has been attracting more school leavers, year after year, with enrolment of full-time students reaching a high of 23,000 in 2005.  As I shared earlier, more and more of our Normal (Technical) students are going on to ITE.


     ITE's attrition rate has also been decreasing.  In 2005, the proportion of students who left ITE without a qualification was about 12% of ITE's full-time enrolment, compared to 17% just five years ago.


     Mr Zainudin Nordin also asked about the relevance of ITE's training to industry.  This is something that is foremost in ITE's mind.  The market is the best test and the best testimony.  According to the ITE graduate employment survey, the last one done in June last year, 89% of ITE graduates found jobs within five months of job search.  After 10 years on the job market, ITE graduates had doubled their starting salaries.  And more ITE graduates are moving up the educational ladder. Within 10 years from graduation, 10%-12% had acquired degree qualifications and a further 24% had diploma qualifications.


     So you can see, we are taking a comprehensive approach in education to address students who are at risk of dropping out at various stages.  We are adopting new approaches to help students at risk, from the early years up. We will learn from the experience of these experiments and improve the programmes as we roll them out.  I have to repeat that there are no short cuts to reducing drop-out rates.  The problems are complex and often rooted in the home.


     What we want is to try harder and do better, and we will.  We will help ensure that students are better prepared for school, help them to get a good start in their early primary years, remain engaged through secondary school, and after leaving school go on to a first-class education at ITE that prepares them for employment and further education.  And we will provide the NorthLight School a new holistic approach to engaging students who need a more active, hands-on and practice-based approach to learning.

     The Minister of State for Education (Mr Chan Soo Sen): Sir, Dr Amy Khor raised a number of questions on the Gifted Education Programme (GEP).  Allow me to put the GEP into context.


     Since 1984, we have established the Gifted Education Programme to cater for the top 1% of our students.  The programme provides an enriched curriculum to nurture these intellectually gifted students.  It develops their skills in critical and creative thinking, as well as problem solving and allows them to work with specialists through mentorship programmes.


     Today, our education system is more diversified. It provides different opportunities for all students to discover and develop their talents. We have schools with different specialisation and niches as well as different programmes to suit different needs.


     But GEP remains relevant in this broader context of talent development.  The core focus of the programme will remain, that is, to meet the educational needs of intellectually gifted students.  Nevertheless, we will build on the strong foundation we have achieved and enhance it further.


     Over the years, we have been moving towards greater decentralisation in the shaping of gifted education in schools. The introduction of Integrated Programme (IP) in schools, that are also GEP centres, has allowed these schools to develop their own school-based gifted programmes to stretch their students.  The introduction of Specialised Independent Schools also opens up other new branches in gifted education.  The NUS High School of Mathematics and Science, for example, offers a whole school approach to developing students with exceptional talents in Mathematics and Science.

     Compared to previous years, this Committee of Supply is held amidst much better economic and employment conditions. If Members of this House will remember, and certainly those who stepped in with me to this august Chamber in 2002 and that was not long ago, where the spectre of a jobless recovery loomed in the aftermath of several difficult years, when Singapore's unemployment peaked at 4.8% in September 2003.  The corresponding resident unemployment rate then was 6.3%.  I remember the prevailing mood then was one of despair and despondence.  But in contrast to that bleak period, the economy grew by 6.4% last year, and the outlook for this year is favourable.  And Members will also remember that there were concerns during that period that even recovery would be a jobless one.  It has not happened. Growth has translated into jobs for Singaporeans. Overall unemployment fell to just 2.5% by the end of last year.  The situation has turned for the better and indeed, there are jobs, jobs, jobs for Singaporeans.

 

    I have asked for a table to be distributed.  If you look at the table, years 2002 and 2203, we have GDP growths of 4% and 2.9% respectively, and they were indeed very difficult years.  Following a negative GDP growth in 2001, if Members cast their minds back, our GDP shrank by -2.3%. And because of that, if you look at the chart, 2002 and 2003 combined, those two years, we actually lost jobs, in other words, the economy, in terms of jobs, shrank about 36,000 in those two years. But in contrast, our economy and employment rebounded vigorously in 2004 and 2005 and we created together something like 181,000-182,000 jobs.  These two periods served as very useful periods of contrasting fortunes and indeed, they provide for us valuable lessons that should guide our efforts and strategies, moving forward in helping all Singaporeans find jobs, earn decent wages and remain employable.  Lest we forget too quickly, it would be useful for us to use this occasion where the economy is up and jobs have returned, to recap broad policies that have worked, many of which were prompted by Members of Parliament in this House. But we should also learn from the ideas that were not taken up or those, if implemented, would have created problems for us today.

 

    In the midst of these difficult times, let me just say that I was very encouraged that Members supported the Government when we did not resort to quick and ineffective fixes.  We did not succumb to calls for protectionist policies to shut out foreign workers and impose minimum wages.  We avoided unemployment insurance.  These policies would have led to economic stagnation and more job losses.  Members in this House would have read recently of press articles about how these very same protectionist  policies in some European countries have increased unemployment rates there.  They do not insulate workers from global competition, but worsen their plight. In contrast, we face challenges head-on, press on with restructuring to remain competitive and relevant in a global marketplace. And because we persevered with sound policies, and unions were able to persuade workers to bear some hardship in the process, with some sacrifices, we now have a more resilient and competitive workforce to deal with challenges that lie ahead. We are in a much better position having gone through the difficult periods.

That made a big difference. We set up the Singapore Workforce Development Agency (WDA) which many of you called for in September 2003.  I would like to remind Members that WDA is a relatively infant organisation, but much needed. This was to help unemployed Singaporeans gain employment and to upgrade their skills.
