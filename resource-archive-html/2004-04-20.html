GENETICALLY-MODIFIED FOOD

     3.  Mr Yeo Guat Kwang asked the Minister for National Development (a) whether genetically-modified (GM) food has adverse effect on human health; and (b) what is Agri-Food and Veterinary Authority of Singapore's  position on such food.

     The Minister of State for National Development (Dr Vivian Balakrishnan) (for the Minister for National Development):  Mr Speaker, Sir, this issue of the safety of genetically-modified (GM) foods has been extensively reviewed by numerous scientific organisations at the international level.  The consensus so far is that GM foods are safe to eat.

 

     Nonetheless, GM foods must be individually assessed on their safety for human consumption.  The GM foods traded commercially on the international market have all passed rigorous pre-market risk assessments before gaining approval and are allowed into the food supply.  The safety record has been excellent.


     No adverse health effects have been reported in the general population of the countries where the GM foods are consumed.

 

     In Singapore, our Genetic Modification Advisory Committee sets the guidelines for the import, release and use of GM products here.   At the international level, Codex Alimentarius Commission - this is an international reference body for food standards - develops the guidelines for assessing the safety of food derived from genetically-modified organisms.  AVA

Column: 2849

follows both sets of guidelines in assessing and approving GM foods for consumption in Singapore.

 

     The GM foods currently available in our market include corn, soyabean and canola oil.  AVA has assessed that they satisfy the guidelines and accepted them as safe for consumption.

 

      AVA is now developing its own laboratory-testing capabilities to independently test new GM foods that may become commercially available in the future.

 


      Mdm Halimah Yacob:   Sir, is there a requirement for manufacturers to label their food if it is genetically-modified so that when consumers purchase it, they know that they are buying genetically-modified products?

     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan:  This is an issue which is currently being considered internationally. Once that decision is made, Singapore's decision will follow in due course, taking into account the developments, both on the technical as well as on the regulatory front internationally.

Integrated Industrial Capital Allowance Incentive

 

     The first tax change is the introduction of the Integrated Industrial Capital Allowance, or the IICA in short.  Currently, capital allowances are granted only on plant or machinery that is used in a company's own trade or business in Singapore.  We recognise that there is a growing trend for corporate groups to base their headquarters, marketing and other high value-added activities in Singapore, and have their regional subsidiaries perform other activities like manufacturing.  To accommodate this business model, the IICA allows Singapore companies to claim capital allowance on equipment such as plant and machinery that is used by their wholly-owned subsidiaries outside Singapore.

     Clause 23 of the Bill inserts a new Part XIIID in the EEI Act, comprising sections 97ZA to 97ZE, to give legislative effect to the IICA.

BUDGET HOTELS

     5.  Mdm Cynthia Phua asked the Minister for National Development what is the Government's policy with regard to budget hotels, which seem to have mushroomed all over the island for the purpose of promoting  tourism but which could also breed prostitution and other undesirable activities.

     Mr Mah Bow Tan:

          The current policy for hotels and boarding houses is to allow them in locations zoned Hotel or Commercial. Within sites with mixed use zoning, such as  Commercial/Residential and Residential with 1st-storey Commercial, hotels and boarding houses are allowed on a case-by-case basis taking into consideration the compatibility of boarding houses with the existing uses in the location. 

 

     The development of backpacker hostels and budget hotels is to meet the needs of the changing profile of our visitors. With the advent of budget travel and growth in value-conscious markets such as China and India, Singapore Tourism Board (STB) anticipates an increase in demand for such accommodation.

 

     The primary objective of Government's policy regarding budget hotel is to cater to the needs of budget travelers, while ensuring that boarding houses would not create adverse impact on the amenity of the area. To minimise the possibility of these boarding houses breeding undesirable activities, URA works closely with the Police (Anti-Vice Branch).  Police would also monitor areas where vice activities are prevalent and, if necessary, conduct surprise checks on these hotels and boarding houses to flush out these undesirable activities within them.

 

      The Hotel Licensing Board and STB also inspect the budget hotels to ensure minimum standards are met. This is taken into consideration during the yearly licence renewal exercise.
