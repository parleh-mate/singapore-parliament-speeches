EMPLOYMENT ACT
(Raising of wage ceiling)


    2. Mr Cheong Yuen Chee asked the Minister for Labour, in view of the substan-

Column: 1503

tial rise in wage level arising out of the accumulated NWC wage increases in the past few years, whether he will consider raising the present wage ceiling of $750 p.m. to a higher ceiling for workers covered by the Employment Act.

     The Minister for Labour (Mr Ong Pang Boon): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am pleased to inform the hon. Member for Toa Payoh that the $750 per month wage ceiling laid down in Sections 33, 35 and 144 of the Employment Act will be raised with effect from 1st December, 1980, to $1,250 p.m. in view of the substantial increase in the level of wages since the ceiling was fixed 12 years ago.

    Hon. Members may wish to know that section 35 of the Employment Act stipulates that Part IV of the Act applies to non-manual employees whose salaries are not more than $750 p.m. However, Part IV applies also to manual employees irrespective of the amount of their monthly salaries. Part IV lays down conditions of service like rest days, holidays, hours of work and payment for overtime work. On the other hand, section 33 provides that where the property of an employer is sold, or any money due to such employer is garnished on the application of a person holding a mortgage, charge or lien or of a person who has obtained a judgment or decree, the court ordering the sale or garnishment shall not distribute the proceeds of such sale or such money unless the salaries due to all employees of the employer have been paid. In the case of section 144, it empowers the Commissioner for Labour to enquire into and decide on any dispute between an employee and his employer relating to matters arising out of any term in the contract of service or any provision of the Act.

    The wage ceiling of $750 p.m. was fixed in 1968. Since then the level of wages has increased substantially. The median monthly income of all employees, according to CPF records, rose by 138%, from $175 in 1968 to $416 in June 1980. In terms of occupational groupings, the median monthly earnings of sales and service workers based on the Singapore Sample Household Survey, 1966, and the Labour Force Survey,

Column: 1504

1979, increased by about 94% and 88% respectively between 1966 and 1979.

    Following consultations with the National Trades Union Congress and the Singapore National Employers' Federation, the Government has decided that with effect from 1st December, 1980, the wage ceiling in sections 33, 35 and 144 of the Employment Act be raised to $1,250 p.m. The wage ceiling will in future be adjusted at regular intervals to take into account rises in the level of wages.

CAPITAL PUNISHMENT FOR DRUG OFFENCES
(Deterrent effects)


    3. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Home Affairs whether capital punishment for certain drug offences has had the desired deterrent effect, and, if not, whether he will consider introducing amending legislation to make the law more effective.

     The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Chua Sian Chin): Mr Speaker, Sir, since the enactment of the death penalty in 1975, 20 major drug traffickers have been arrested and prosecuted. Eighteen have been tried in Court and sentenced to death. Six of them were executed after they failed in their appeals to the Privy Council. With vigorous and sustained enforcement, the organised drug trafficking syndicates have been smashed. The deterrent effect of enforcement, convictions and executions is reflected in the declining number of major drug traffickers arrested for such offences after the introduction of the death penalty. In 1976, 11 major drug traffickers were arrested. The figure dropped to six in 1977, one in 1978 and one in 1979.

    What is even more significant is that the pattern of trafficking has changed to ant trafficking. The ant traffickers smuggle very small amounts of drugs from Johore Bahru into Singapore. With strict enforcement action at our checkpoints, not much could get through. As a result the addicts are finding it difficult to get their supplies from the street pushers and they have to pay a high price for the drugs. This scarcity has

Column: 1505

resulted in an increase in the price of heroin at the street level from $35 per gram in 1977 to $333 per gram in the first half of this year.

    For the foreseeable future, I have no intention of introducing amending legislation. There must, however, be no relaxation in enforcement so that distribution networks which have been smashed are not rebuilt or new ones created. This together with the deterrence of the death penalty will continue to keep the drug problem in Singapore under control.

     Mr Ho See Beng (Khe Bong): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I ask the Minister whether there is any difference in the number of cases of capital punishment between 1979 and 1980.

     Mr Chua Sian Chin: Sir, to date, in 1980, we have not arrested any drug trafficker for an offence that entails the death penalty if he is prosecuted and convicted.

     Mr Ho See Beng: Sir, I would like to know whether there has been any conviction of a drug trafficker that entails capital punishment in 1980.

     Mr Chua Sian Chin: The answer is none.

MASS RAPID TRANSIT SYSTEM
(Decision)


    8. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for Communications and Acting Minister for Culture whether a firm decision has been made by government with regard to the proposed Mass Rapid Transit System.

     The Minister for Communications and Acting Minister for Culture (Mr Ong Teng Cheong): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government has not as yet made a decision on the MRT. I would, however, like to take this opportunity to elaborate on the developments that have taken place and how we intend to proceed from here.

    As hon. Members are aware, the Hansen Review Team which was recently asked to examine all previous MRT-related studies has finished its task. They have disputed the findings of earlier studies. They state that an MRT is not inevitable and that we will be able to manage quite well in the 1990s and beyond with buses - provided we redesign our bus system in the radical manner that they have proposed. But they have not been able to substantiate their views in a con-

Column: 1509

vincing manner. Serious operational implications, such as high-boarding volumes in the Central Area, have not been considered.

    It has therefore been decided to do further quantitative work to settle, conclusively, this issue of whether an all-bus system will be able to cope. This will be done as part of the Comprehensive Traffic Study to be undertaken by the Provisional MRT Authority.

    This Study, which is scheduled to start in January, will be conducted in two phases. Phase A, which will take about 6 to 9 months to complete, will examine in detail the Hansen Team's proposed all-bus system to see if it can be implemented, and if so, with what consequences. Other all-bus systems thought to be comparable or superior to the Hansen Team's system will also be evaluated. In addition to looking at the operational details such as commuter travelling times, bus fleet size and so on, the Study will do an economic benefit-cost analysis that will rank the various all-bus alternatives in their order of merit.

    Phase B which will be done concurrently with Phase A will take about 2 1/2 years. It will do a refined analysis and examine the basic MRT system established in Phase III of the Mass Transit Study to see how it compares with the all-bus system. In addition, at least two other limited but possibly more cost-effective MRT configurations will also be analysed. All these systems will then be subjected to a benefit-cost and financial analysis and ranked in order of merit.

    Notwithstanding all that I have said, we should not forget that the MRT is more than just a transportation issue. Its implementation has wide implications, and a decision can only be taken after all factors have been carefully considered.

SINGAPORE FIRE SERVICE

    24. Mr Yeo Choo Kok asked the Acting Minister for Social Affairs whether the Singapore Fire Service is sufficiently and adequately equipped to fight fires in high-rise buildings.

     Mr Chan Chee Seng (for the Acting Minister for Social Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, the equipment needed to fight fires in high-rise buildings is basically the same as those used to fight other fires. The Singapore Fire Service has the necessary equipment.

    As the tallest mechanical ladders have a maximum height of 30 metres, fires which break out on the upper floors of high-rise buildings cannot be fought through exterior-based operations. They have to be fought from within, using built-in fire-fighting facilities such as internal fire water main systems, fire lifts, pressurised or ventilated staircases and fire-fighting lobbies. The local fire codes require the provision of these facilities in high-rise buildings.

    The Singapore Fire Service practises high-rise fire-fighting procedures every week. Officers and men are sent to high-rise buildings on these occasions to practise fire-fighting and rescue drills and to familiarise themselves with the layout of the buildings. The High-rise Fire-fighting Unit, which was formed in 1976, carries out periodic inspections of fire-fighting, detection and protection systems in high-rise buildings.

    The Singapore Fire Service has a Search and Rescue Squad consisting of 108 men who have undergone intensive training in the rescue of trapped victims of fires in high-rise buildings. They have been sent for specialised training with the Commando Battalion and Helicopter Squadron of the Singapore Armed Forces. In addition, there is a contingency plan for obtaining the assistance of

Three-quarters of the efficacy of these fire practices depend on constant co-ordination and rehearsals of what to do in an emergency. If these practices are not being taken seriously by occupants of high-rise buildings, they will be repeated until the Singapore Fire Service is thoroughly satisfied.

JURONG TOWN CORPORATION
(AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Goh Chok Tong): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    This Bill seeks to make a number of amendments to the Jurong Town Corporation Act, mainly to Part IV of the Act which deals with the sale of flats, houses and other buildings owned by the Corporation. The

Column: 1537

amendments made to the Act are based on similar amendments made to Part IV of the Housing and Development Act which deals with the sale of Housing Board flats and buildings.

    At present, an applicant or owner of a Jurong Town Corporation flat, his spouse or authorised occupier is not allowed to own any other property. The Bill seeks to allow such applicant or owner, his spouse or authorised occupier with the written consent of the Corporation to purchase any commercial property not exceeding $250,000 in value, or such higher value as the Minister may allow, if he uses it to carry on his own business. The amendment is similar to that made to the Housing and Development Act.

    Recently amendments have been made to the Housing and Development Act to enable the Housing and Development Board, through its authorised officers, to act for parties in the resales and in other transactions of Housing Board flats in cases where the parties are not represented by any solicitors. The Bill seeks to empower the Corporation to provide similar services for owners of Jurong Town Corporation flats in the resale or other disposition of their flats. This is to help the flat owners to reduce legal costs.

    The Act allows the Corporation to acquire a flat sold by the Corporation if the owner contravenes certain provisions of the Act. However, for less serious offences, this penalty is considered too harsh and can cause considerable hardship to a family. In line with the Housing and Development Act, the Bill seeks to enable the Corporation to impose penalties on the owners who contravene the Act, instead of acquiring the flats, where the offences are not serious.

    Under the existing rules of the Corporation, a person cannot apply for a Jurong

Column: 1538

Town Corporation flat if he owns a property during the 30-month period before the date of his application for such flat. He is also prohibited from owning another property after the date of the purchase of a flat. The Bill seeks to extend this prohibition to cover the period between the date of application and the date of purchase of a flat.

    The Bill also empowers the Corporation to take enforcement action where the flat is misused by the owner or any other occupier of the flat. Under the existing law, the Corporation can take such action only if the owner has misused the flat.

    The other aspect of the Bill is to make clear that any notice or document sent by the Corporation to the owner of a flat by registered post or affixed to some conspicuous part of the flat shall be deemed to be of good service. This will enable the Corporation to serve notices on owners of flats who try to evade service of notices by locking up their flats and staying away.
