ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

ELECTRICITY POWER FAILURES


    1. Mr Ang Nam Piau asked the Minister for the Environment if he will ascertain from the Public Utilities Board the reasons for the frequent electricity power failures in the six months from September, 1978.

     The Minister for the Environment (Mr Lim Kim San): Mr Speaker, Sir, the main causes of power failure in Singapore are inclement weather, ground subsidence and careless contractors. Falling trees during a stormy weather sometimes snap overhead lines, but more often the tree branches short-circuit uninsulated lines. Ground subsidence caused by heavy rain and poor shoring by contractors damage underground cables and distribution equipment. For example, the very heavy rainfall in December 1978 is more than double that for 1979 so far. Another cause is careless contractors, who, during excavations, damage cables accidentally. This occurs because of intensive construction activities. A secondary cause of power failure is that some consumers overload power supply, leading to damage in the network.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the PUB makes every effort to reduce power failures. Bare overhead lines are being progressively replaced by insulated lines to reduce short-circuiting. Contractors are required to locate all underground cables within worksites by examining plans or digging trial trenches. They also have to pay for damaged cables. The PUB sometimes assists contractors at road sites to avoid damage. However, Sir, given a network of nearly 30,000 km of cables and the nature of the causes of power failures, a fault-free system is impossible.

Generally, this legislation has become outmoded owing to Singapore's rapidly changing socio-economic conditions and the diversification of co-operative activities in recent years. There are now 81 registered co-operative societies in Singapore, engaged in credit, consumer, housing, transport, producer, insurance and dental activities.

Of the existing 81 co-operative societies, 13 are co-operative ventures established by the National Trades Union Congress and other trade unions. For the past several years, trade union co-operatives have successfully carried out their operations and have no doubt made a significant impact on the economic and social activities in Singapore.     Every co-operative society will be required to set aside 20% of its annual surplus towards its reserve fund, instead of 25% as provided in the existing Act. This transfer to its reserve fund may be reduced to 5% when the fund has reached 10% of the society's total paid-up capital.
