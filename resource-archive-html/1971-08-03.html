Most of the present hawkers in Singapore became hawkers originally because of the unemployment situation in the past. Although many new factories have been established in Singapore after the implementation of our industrialisation programme and thus provide many job opportunities, these hawkers cannot be absorbed into the factories because they are not skilled workmen.     The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Education (Inche Mohd. Ghazali Bin Ismail): Mr Speaker, Sir, I rise to join my colleagues in thanking the President for his speech at the opening of the Second Session of the Second Parliament on the 21st of last month.

    Sir, I would like to examine with you the internal situation we are in today. I am not so much concerned with the political and economic aspects, important as they are, as with the composition of our society. We are multi in almost everything. We have a diffusion of cultures, a diffusion of languages, a diffusion of religions and so on, and the very heterogeneous nature of our society, open as it is to the influence of Western

Column: 154

ideas, makes it imperative for all of us to understand fully the implications of an uncritical adoption of certain aspects of Western culture. I am not saying that some of these aspects of Western culture are decadent, but I must say that some of them do not conduce to our efforts to consolidate our people into a viable and rugged nation. The Chinese, the Indians and the Malays, each has a distinct form of culture in which, in its various ways, is prescribed a code of ethical behaviour that has more or less withstood the test of time. Each has a built-in system of dynamic growth, and throughout the centuries, the cumulative effects of succeeding changes have made our respective cultures the floating-buoys to which we have clung, and from which we have derived our basic strength. Our religions preach the brotherhood of man, extol the qualities of the human mind, make the achievement of peace the supreme pursuit of life, and frown upon irrationalism in all its manifestations. In truth we have, each one of us here, a clearly-defined rule of conduct, adherence to which can ensure our survival as a united and strong people. We have nothing to be ashamed of, and yet today we see, among our youths, almost a total disregard for the values which we have accepted without question. We see before us the collapse of traditional values. Non-conformity, anti-collectivism, anti-establishment and divergent thinking - whatever that may mean -would seem to be the order of the day. We see manifestations of these in the outlandish dress of some of our youths, the taking of drugs, the reading of pornographic literature. Existential philosophy from Soren Kierkegaard to Jacques Maritain has been perverted to mean the denial of God and the ridiculing of all existing cultures and the mocking of social values dear to the older generation. Art for the sake of art has been venerated at the expense of the social ends that art is supposed to sub serve. Traditional faith and values no longer serve for some of our youths as the guide-lines of their life and pointers to their conduct.

    It would be pertinent, at this juncture, to examine the root causes of this apparent weakening of our moral fibre and to think of ways and means of overcoming it by providing our youths with the appropriate alternative moral and ethical values, lest we plunge our society into chaos. We must at all costs, prevent the degeneration of our society into one that is effete and is immersed in the pursuit of material and sensual pleasures. We must think of alternatives to a society that thinks of humility, a society that thinks of pity for the weak and incompetent as vices.

    The world that emerged from the Second World War had very little resemblance to the dreams of idealists before the outbreak of the conflict. There was hunger, fear, hopelessness, misery and unprecedented violence. The hopes of people for peace and security were rudely shattered. The revival of power politics, the fear of the spread of nuclear war, and the use of bacteriological means of destruction, the outbreak of conflicts in South-east Asia, the Middle East and South America, all these had their deleterious effect in moulding the thoughts of our thinking youths. The old order is simply not strong enough to ensure peace and understanding among nations. The great religions of the world have been found wanting, and, as established institutions, they had apparently served their purposes. Some idealism was still left behind. The spectacular advances in science, the achievement of independence by various countries which were former colonies of the imperialist powers, the emergence of newly-independent nations in Africa, the United Nations Organisation lent a ray of hope to a sick world, but apparently these did not contribute enough to sustain the idea that peace and plenty was at hand. Reactions of the most extreme kind began to make themselves apparent all over the world. The spread of education through the mass media merely accelerated the development of these deleterious trends and not even the authoritative pronouncements of church and of Government could put a stop to

them. Overnight, certain countries that professed democracy chucked it overboard and imposed upon their peoples the most authoritarian of regimes. In these fluctuations, the youths of the world have been the main casualties. They feel alienated from society, whether authoritarian or otherwise, and this feeling of alienation has led them to adopt the more nihilistic principles of life. The destruction of the old order - economic, political, cultural and religious - became paramount, no matter what the succeeding situation might prove to be.

We see other manifestation of this nihilistic philosophy in our midst today. The propensity to indulge in the pleasures of the senses for the sake of enjoying such pleasures, the ceaseless search for material comforts at the expense of the less tangible modes of life, the quest for quaint methods of escaping from the grim realities of life are all too real, and, given a measure of permissiveness, one never knows how far these trends might develop for the worse.

    I think I have already spoken at some length on the inherent dangers that permissive attitudes have or dangers that can result from being permissive. I have now to go a little further into the matter. By being non-permissive, it must not be construed that I am advocating that our society should be a closed one. There is always the fear that if one is not permissive, then one is logically authoritarian. This does not necessarily follow if we understand that in being disciplined, one proceeds in a manner socially acceptable in going about one's business, and that none of one's actions are outside the framework of the norm. The question of permissiveness does not arise here, as what one is doing is within the accepted framework of social conventions. For instance, all legitimate intellectual endeavours must not only be sanctioned but encouraged and nurtured as furthering our own cause. In matters of cultural pursuits, any that does not conduce to the furtherance of our culture should he frowned upon. A body of ideas, be they philosophical, religious, political and economic, which do not run counter to the general welfare of the masses, that is, they are not inimical to the state, ought to be supported.
