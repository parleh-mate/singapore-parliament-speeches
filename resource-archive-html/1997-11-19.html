    1. Mr Inderjit Singh asked the Minister for Finance if he will elaborate on (i) the nature and form of the US$10 billion rescue package which the Government has pledged to the Indonesian government in an attempt to rescue the latter's economy and (ii) how Singapore or the other ASEAN countries will benefit from this package.

    3. Mr Chiam See Tong asked the Minister for Finance why is there a necessity for Singapore, being such a small country as compared to Japan, to contribute a large and equal amount of US$5 billion to help Indonesia in the present currency turmoil.

    The Minister for Finance (Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau): Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I would like to take Question Nos. 1 to 3 together as they are related?

    Mr Speaker: Yes, you may.

    Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau: Because of our close links with our neighbours in the South East Asia region, Singapore is affected by the economic well-being of their economies. It is therefore in our interests to do what we can to maintain economic and financial stability in the region.

    I will first take up Mr Inderjit Singh's question and explain the nature and form of the rescue package to Indonesia.

    Singapore's contribution to the Indonesian reform package is in two parts. The first is a loan of US$5 billion to supplement the aid package offered jointly by the International Monetary Fund (IMF), the World Bank and the Asian Development Bank (ADB). The second is the purchase of Rupiah to support the Indonesian currency.

    Let me elaborate on the first part of our assistance package. Indonesia has adopted a strong economic reform programme which should help to restore confidence in the economy. This programme, which was formulated with the assistance of the IMF, World Bank and ADB, will be supported with US$23 billion of financing from the three multilateral agencies as well as from Indonesia's own substantial foreign reserves. However, if for some unforeseen reasons, the multilateral financing proves to be inadequate, then Singapore and several other countries with regional interests, including Japan, the United States, Malaysia, Australia and Brunei, have agreed to provide supplementary financing to support the programme. In particular, Singapore has pledged to contribute up to US$5 billion to this "second line of defence". There is every likelihood that there may be no need to draw on this supplementary line of credit.

    The second part of our assistance package involves the purchase of Rupiah to support the Indonesian currency. Singapore and Japan share the view that loss of confidence was the primary cause of the recent depreciation of the Rupiah. The fall in the value of the Rupiah has been excessive and could jeopardize the successful implementation of the

economic reform programme. Therefore, both countries have agreed to collaborate with Bank Indonesia, through joint intervention in the foreign exchange market, to promote the strengthening of the Rupiah to levels more consistent with Indonesia's economic fundamentals. The amount of our intervention is not predetermined and will depend on developments in the foreign exchange market.

    Mr Seng Han Thong has asked whether the US$1 billion loan to Thailand and US$5 billion assistance to Indonesia will affect our reserves and Singaporeans' Central Provident Fund (CPF) savings, in case of default. I would like to assure Mr Seng that our CPF savings are not at risk at all. CPF funds are invested in Singapore Government bonds and the Government's external reserves are well in excess of its CPF obligations.

    Furthermore, the facilities offered to Thailand and Indonesia are sovereign loans and remain part of our foreign assets. The loans are to help maintain foreign reserves of those two countries at a "comfortable" level to instill investor confidence. Neither Thailand nor Indonesia have ever defaulted on their sovereign debts.

    Mr Chiam See Tong has asked why Singapore, being such a small country, needs to contribute an amount equal to Japan's to assist Indonesia. I would like to point out that Singapore's loan was made as a result of a request from the Indonesian Government for assistance, and Japan subsequently matched our offer. CAUSEWAY TRAFFIC JAMS


    4. Mr Hawazi Daipi asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) what were the causes of the recent Causeway jam and what did the Government do to resolve the problem; (b) what more can be done to resolve the problem earlier; (c) what will be done in the future should such jams recur either at the Causeway and/or the second-link bridge; and (d) what plans his Ministry has to ensure that Causeway jams will not cause serious traffic jams in Marsiling Estate and some parts of Woodlands New Town.

    5. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Trade and Industry whether the Government has taken the necessary actions to alleviate the frequent massive traffic jams at the Singapore-Johor Causeway and whether the Singapore and Malaysian governments will hold a meeting to resolve the problem and find a long-term solution to ensure the free flow of goods, services and people between Singapore and Malaysia.

    6. Mr Zulkifli bin Baharudin asked the Minister for Trade and Industry what is the impact the Causeway jam has caused to companies in Singapore and whether he will ask the Malaysian government to take steps to ensure the smooth flow of traffic on the Causeway.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr Lee Yock Suan): Mr Speaker, Sir, as Questions 4, 5 and 6 are related, may I answer them together?

    Mr Speaker: Yes, you may.

Column: 1815



    Mr Lee Yock Suan: Thank you. Mr Speaker, Sir, this problem began in late September when the Johor Customs implemented stringent checks on both outgoing and incoming vehicles. The result was massive congestion at the Causeway with long lorry tailbacks from Johor Customs Tanjong Puteri Complex.

    Normally, checks are conducted on a selective basis in line with the Best Customs Practices' code advocated by the World Customs Organisation, of which both Singapore and Malaysia are members. The sudden change in procedure resulted in long delays of up to seven hours to clear the Johor Customs.

    The jams at the Causeway have affected bilateral trade and especially manufacturers with plants situated in both Singapore and Johor. Parts and components are sent from Singapore to Johor on a daily basis to be assembled, and then transported to their Singapore plants on a Just-in-Time basis (JIT) for final assembly and distribution.

    Any impediment to the free flow of cargo along the Causeway disrupts production schedules, and directly impacts the Just-in-Time concept of modern factory operation. To overcome this, companies on both sides of the Causeway had to increase their inventories and rent more lorries to transport parts and components, resulting in higher operating costs.

    I raised this issue with my Malaysian counterpart Rafidah Aziz at the ASEAN Economic Ministers' Meeting in Kuala Lumpur last month. MINORITY REPRESENTATION IN PUBLIC LISTED COMPANIES


    1. Mr Shriniwas Rai asked the Minister for Finance if he will consider amending the law to allow for minority representation in public listed company.

    Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau:

    Safeguarding the interests of minority shareholders is important. For this reason, there are currently already a number of provisions in the Companies Act and SES rules to do so. The safeguards are as follows :

    a) the Companies Act confers certain fundamental rights on each and every shareholder, regardless of the number of shares he or she owns. These include the right to attend general meetings, to speak thereat and to vote on resolutions put forth at such meetings. Minority shareholders are at liberty to ask questions and voice their opinions at such general meetings;

    b) under the law, each director has a duty to act honestly and in the interest of the company as a whole, ie, in the interest of all shareholders;

    c) both the Companies Act and SES rules require all listed companies to establish an Audit Committee, which must have a majority of independent directors. The Audit Committee helps to review internal controls, financial statements and procedures for related-party dealings;

    d) in cases of oppression or injustice done to any minority shareholder, sections 216 and 216A of the Companies Act provide avenues for redress; and

    e) where the interest of substantial shareholders might be at variance with those of minority shareholders, such as in material related-party transactions, the SES rules require the appointment of independent financial advisers to advise the minority shareholders. Furthermore, substantial shareholders who have vested interests in such transactions have to abstain from voting on the relevant resolutions.

    The current regulations are adequate to safeguard the interests of minority shareholders. Therefore, it is not necessary to amend the law to allow for minority representation in listed companies.
