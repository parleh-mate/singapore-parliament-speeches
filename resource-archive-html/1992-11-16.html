MUSLIM DIVORCES


    The following Question stood in the name of Encik Othman bin Haron Eusofe -

    1. To ask the Minister-in-charge of Muslim Affairs whether he will ask the Majlis Ugama Islam Singapura, together with the Registry of Muslim Marriages, to urgently undertake a study on (i) the measures and programmes which can help to arrest the high number of Muslim divorces and (ii) the welfare and

Column: 306

well-being of the children of the affected families.

     Mr Umar Abdul Hamid (Ang Mo Kio GRC): Question No. 1, Sir.

     The Minister-in-charge of Muslim Affairs (Dr Ahmad Mattar): Mr Speaker, Sir, there are a number of existing programmes in place to help Muslim couples intending to get married. These have been implemented by the Registry of Muslim Marriages (ROMM) and the Syariah Court with the help of MUIS and the Mosque Management Committees. For instance, all couples intending to marry are urged to attend Marriage Guidance Courses conducted at 19 mosques. The majority of them do so.

    The ROMM provides counselling services to all couples when they declare their intention to marry. However, those couples identified by the ROMM as lacking in religious knowledge are referred to the 19 mosques for religious counselling. They subsequently attend the Marriage Guidance Courses at these mosques.

    Sir, MUIS, Syariah Court and the ROMM are working out further measures to help bring down the number of divorces. One good example is the constant review of existing programmes to gauge their effectiveness.

    As far as the welfare and well-being of the children of affected families is concerned, it is being looked after by MUIS. Under its Financial Assistance Scheme, MUIS provides assistance to affected families in need. This takes the form of financial assistance, study grants for the children, and even payment of certain debts incurred by the families. MUIS also refers some of the cases needing counselling to Malay/Muslim organisations where such counselling is available. School-going children are referred to MENDAKI and other Malay/Muslim organisations for tuition.

PURCHASE OF HOUSING AND DEVELOPMENT BOARD FLATS
(Raising of eligibility income ceiling)


    2. Dr Tan Cheng Bock asked the Minister for National Development whether the Housing and Development Board will consider raising the present eligibility income ceiling of $6,000 so that more Singaporeans can purchase HDB flats.

     The Minister of State for National Development (Mr Lim Hng Kiang) (for the Minister for National Development): Mr Speaker, Sir, the income ceilings for purchase of HDB flats were last revised to $6,000 for nuclear families and $9,500 for extended families on 1st October, 1991. The Ministry of National Development has since reviewed these income ceilings taking wage increases over the past year into account. I am happy to inform the Member for Ayer Rajah that with effect from 1st December 1992 the income ceilings for purchase of HDB flats will be raised to $7,000 per month for nuclear families, and $11,000 per month for extended families.

    With this revision, about 91% of households in Singapore will be eligible for public housing. The revision will be of particular benefit to about 12% of recently married couples in the 25-29 age group who have combined monthly incomes of between $6,000 and $7,000. This group is currently ineligible for public housing and could face difficulties buying private properties. With this

POLYTECHNIC DIPLOMA HOLDERS IN SINGAPORE UNIVERSITIES
(Performance)


    7. Mr Loh Meng See asked the Minister for Education how are polytechnic diploma holders performing in the Singapore universities.

     The Senior Minister of State for Education (Dr Tay Eng Soon) (for the Minister for Education): The polytechnic diploma holders admitted to courses in NUS and NTU have generally performed well. One of the reasons for this is that these students have some working experience prior to entering the university. Their work experience has stood them in good stead to better understand and assimilate what they are taught in the undergraduate courses. The attrition rate of polytechic diploma holders is low. Over the last three to four years, no polytechic diploma holder had to leave NUS for failing the examinations, and only three in NTU did not complete their course of study within the minimum period.

     Mr Loh Meng See (Kampong Glam GRC): Sir, will the Minister consider increasing the intake of polytechnic

Column: 315

graduates into our universities because there are a large number of them going overseas for further studies?

     Dr Tay Eng Soon: Sir, in the last three years, 1990-92 admissions, the universities took in a total of 1,100 polytechnic graduates. The universities are planning to take in more over the next few years as the universities themselves expand to provide more places.

     Mr Loh Meng See: The Minister said that the students have prior working experience. Does it mean that he will rule out those without working experience but with Polytechnic diploma qualifications?

     Dr Tay Eng Soon: Sir, the experience of the universities with polytechnic graduates is that they do well when they have practical working experience in addition to the diploma course that they have done. So the intention is that, in admissions, not only will they take into account the grades of these students at the polytechnics, they will also take into account their working experience.

     Dr Low Seow Chay (Chua Chu Kang): Why is it that polytechnic students require working experience before they are admitted to the university, whereas 'A' level students do not need that particular requirement?

BILLS OF EXCHANGE (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister for Finance (Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Bill seeks to amend the Bills of Exchange Act to give legal status to the words "account payee" or "a/c payee" written on the face of a crossed cheque. This proposal arose from a recent Court ruling that an "account payee only" crossing on a cheque does not, in law, prevent transferability of that cheque. The Court also ruled that a "not negotiable" crossing would not preclude such a crossed cheque from being transferred.

    As a result, there was a surge in the number of cheques being crossed "non-transferable", as cheque issuers were concerned that their cheques would otherwise not be paid to the named payee.

    Under the present law, a "non-transferable" cheque requires that the paying bank to ascertain from the collecting bank that the proceeds of the cheque have been credited to the account of the named payee. To minimise the risk to the paying bank for wrongful payment, the collecting bank is required to manually indicate on each non-transferable cheque that the funds have been credited to the named payee's account before the cheque is presented for clearing with the paying bank. As manual clearing is time-consuming and labour-intensive, the Association of Banks in Singapore decided to impose a handling fee for clearing such cheques. The public, however, misunderstood the rationale and expressed dissatisfaction with the surcharge.

    To resolve the issue, amendments are proposed to the Bills of Exchange Act to give legal status to the words "account payee" or "a/c payee" written on the face of a crossed cheque. Such a cheque will be non-transferable and can only be paid into the account of the named payee regardless of any purported endorsement. A collecting bank will be

Column: 336

protected from liability for crediting the proceeds of such a cheque to the wrong account, if it can demonstrate that it has acted in good faith and without negligence.

    These amendments are contained in clauses 4, 5 and 6 of the Bill. They are similar to amendments in the UK Cheques Act 1992 which were first proposed in a UK White Paper on Banking Services issued in March 1990.

    I now move on to the other amendments of the Bill.

    Clauses 2 and 3 seek to amend sections 57(b) and 72(d) of the Act to enable the Court to award judgments in foreign currencies in respect of bills of exchange dishonoured abroad. These amendments are of a law reform nature to bring the Bills of Exchange Act, which has not been amended since 1970, up-to-date with developments in the United Kingdom. They are also consistent with our standing as an international financial centre that our courts be allowed to award, where appropriate, judgments in foreign currencies on dishonour of bills.

    Clauses 7 and 8 of the Bill seek to make related amendments of a similar nature to the Arbitration (International Investment Disputes) Act and the Reciprocal Enforcement of Foreign Judgments Act to enable various awards and judgments given in a foreign currency, and registrable for enforcement in Singapore, to be registered in the foreign currency instead of Singapore dollars.

    Sir, I beg to move.

    Question put, and agreed to.

    The House immediately resolved itself into a Committee on the Bill. - [Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau].
