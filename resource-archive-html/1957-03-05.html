    The Chief Minister (Mr Lim Yew Hock): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have the Governor's consent to move, and I do now hereby move the motion standing in my name, namely,

    That this Assembly approves the recommendations of the All-Party Conference, as set out in Sessional Paper No. Misc. 1 of 1957, and on the principles therein set out instructs the All-Party Mission to secure from Her Majesty's Government for the people of Singapore the status of a self-governing state with all the rights, powers, and privileges thereto appurtenant in all internal affairs and the control of trade, commerce and cultural relations in external affairs.

    Sir, before I proceed any further, I would like to draw the attention of hon. Members to an error on page 3, paragraph 13 (c) , line 3 - the word "three" should read "two".

    Sir, the terms of the motion are clear. We are seeking complete internal self-government and not independence. We maintain and maintain with all vigor that the people of Singapore have the inalienable right to self-determination and to deny them this right will be tantamount to a breach of faith on the part of Her Majesty's Government. We want to remain friends with Britain, but our dignity as equals must be recognized. A new chapter in the relationship between Britain and ourselves is being written and this chapter can be a happy one of healthy and fruitful co-operation if the Mission succeeds.

    The other picture I hesitate to paint. But the hand that holds the brush is not, in Singapore but in London. The All-Party Mission to London is all set

Column: 1458

to leave tomorrow on its quest for a new Constitution for the people of Singapore. It seeks sustenance from the unanimous support of the people's representatives in this Assembly on the principles enunciated in the White Paper, which is its blueprint for a self-governing Singapore. The Members of the All-Party Mission are drawn from delegates to the All-Party Conference, and I take heart from the unity of purpose displayed in the conference that the All-Party Mission will be a united force fully conscious of the task before them and pledged to do their best for the people of Singapore. The unity of our voice in this Assembly on this national issue will provide the fuel with which the All-Party Mission will steer the ship, the "State of Singapore", safely into port. I do not wish to give the impression that all will be plain sailing. We are bound to encounter rough weather, but we will not flounder nor founder, for team-work will pull us through.

    Sir, I do not propose to go into detail on all the items in the White Paper, as most of them are self-explanatory and do not require elucidation. Moreover, the White Paper is the result of the labours of the All-Party Conference and ten Members of this Assembly have been closely associated in its deliberations. On page 2, paragraph 8, of the White Paper is the following sentence:

    "The conference's decision is that the All-Party Mission will not accept anything less than what had already been offered by the United Kingdom Government."

Sir, some uncharitable and uninformed people may think that we are going to accept what the last delegation had rejected. This is far from the truth. We are pressing for a Malayan Governor-General, which proposal had not been accepted by Her Majesty's Government during the last talks, and the matter was still being examined by Her Majesty's Government before the talks broke down. We do not propose to accept the Defense and Security Council offered by Her Majesty's Government, and the line that the Mission will take on this all-important issue is set out in paragraphs 9, 10 and 11 on page 3 of the White Paper. Apart from these two major issues, the

Thus it can be seen that the blueprint of the All-Party Mission is an improvement on the offers of Her Majesty's Government during the last talks.

    Sir, on the Security Council proposed by us, the set-up includes a Minister from the Federation of Malaya with equal representation by the Singapore Government and Her Majesty's Government. This has been made possible by the fine co-operation of the Federation Chief Minister, Tunku Abdul Rahman, and I would like to express to him in this Assembly the thanks of the people of Singapore.

Whether the citizenship rights should come into force before the creation of the State of Singapore or after was a question that confronted the All-Party Conference. Members to express their views to guide us on the line to take with the Colonial Office on our citizenship proposals.     In the matter of independence or self-government, we must not only be able to do the cushy jobs in offices and do just the administrative part of it, but also must be able to rise to the occasion and to sacrifice our property and our lives if necessary, in the interests of the country. Today, the newspapers report that young men of Singapore are conscious of that. The registration of the Singapore Regiment is ample proof that the people of Singapore are ready to shoulder greater responsibility. They are enthusiastic in joining this new Singapore Regiment, which will be the nucleus of a band of people devoted to the country, and will lay down their lives if necessary to defend this country.
