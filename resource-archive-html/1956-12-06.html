    The Chief Minister: - under the Standing Order. Sir, in rising to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time," I want to stress, first of all, that it is not normal government practice to compensate its officers for loss of expectation of career where such officers are prematurely retired. It is common knowledge that all Government officers hold office at pleasure, and so long as the requisite notice is given for termination of service, the person can have no claim on the Crown. This Bill, Sir, to compensate expatriate officers for loss of career as a result of Malayanisation of the public service, is therefore an unusual measure to meet changes brought about by constitutional advancement in Singapore. Up to now, the Secretary of State has been responsible for the expatriate officers who came out here expecting a full career, that is to say, a career in the service until the normal retiring age. Constitutional changes have brought about a frustration of this expectation of a full career, for one of the fundamental rights of a self-governing country is that it must have control of its public service and no outside authority must be in a position to determine appointments, promotions and discipline in respect of the civil service. The Government not only cherishes this right of determining its own civil service matters, but is of the firm conviction that true self-government means that the entire control of the government machinery must vest in the local people. The people of the country must solve their own problems and determine what is best for them. A civil service staffed and dominated by expatriate officers will make self-government a mockery. We are on the threshold of self-government and it is of the greatest urgency that we must prepare to govern ourselves.

    I said yesterday, in moving the adoption of the Government's policy on Malayanisation, that it is the Government's firm view that the principal civil service advisers of Ministers must be local men. The most effective way of

achieving the objective in the determination of our own civil service is to remove from the Secretary of State and the Governor the final control over those officers who were appointed by the Secretary of State, and this is brought about by the establishment of a Public Service Commission with executive powers. Hon. Members will hear from the Hon. the Chief Secretary in due course how the new Public Service Commission will work before our present constitution is amended. To all intents and purposes, the new Public Service Commission will be executive.

    All these measures, Sir, have brought about a change in the conditions of service of the expatriate officers, and it is for these reasons that a compensation scheme has been negotiated and agreed with the Staff Associations representing them. During the negotiations we were in constant touch with the Federation Government to ensure that their scheme and ours were as nearly the same as possible. The only material difference is in the ceiling of the compensation, and this is due to the generally higher emoluments ruling in Singapore.

    There is urgency in bringing this scheme into operation as soon as possible, for without it planned Malayanisation cannot proceed, and we do want early Malayanisation of the public service which, as I have pointed out, is both a political and an administrative necessity. I do urge hon. I fully appreciate that there is always a danger of a mass exodus of expatriate officers and that it might have a deterrent effect if the scheme now before the House provides half compensation instead of full compensation to those officers who wish to leave without the Government's permission. Since June, Sir, we have given further careful consideration to this question of half compensation and have come to the conclusion that although it may have a deterrent effect on officers going without the Government's permission, the result may well be that we have a service staffed with a large number of discontented officers. Discontentment added to frustration of a full career may well be the end of efficiency in the government machinery. It is also clear that any half compensation scheme would have met with firm resistance from the Secretary of State. In view of Her Majesty's Government's declared policy that once an executive Public Service Commission has been introduced and conditions of service have been changed by the removal of ultimate control from the Secretary of State, officers will receive compensation for loss of career. Also, Sir, other colonial governments, seeking to introduce a compensation scheme, have accepted the principle that no clear-cut distinction can be drawn between the actual termination of a career, through compulsory retirement, and an officer's right to terminate his own career once his conditions of service have been materially altered. The career of every officer in the service, even if the Government wishes to retain him for several years, is automatically prejudiced from the moment the Government's intention

Let us show the world that in our attainment of self-determination the people of Singapore can show understanding and appreciation of the services rendered to Singapore by expatriate officers who will suffer a loss of career resultant upon our gain towards nationhood.

    There is, however, one very obvious peculiarity about this Bill, and that is, although as I have said it seeks to establish a Public Service Commission with executive control, in fact, that is the one thing which is missing from the Bill. Clauses 13 and 14 of the Bill, which lay down the functions of the Commission, prescribe that the Commission shall only advise the Governor. The reason for this apparent contradiction is that at the present stage, when we are still governed by the present Singapore Colony Order in Council, it would not be proper by legislation here to over-rule the provisions of section 11 of the Singapore Colony Order in Council; but, as was explained in the White Paper on Malayanisation, the Secretary of State has agreed that, save as regards a few specified appointments, the Governor shall act on the advice of the Public Service Commission subject only to the right to refer a recommendation back to the Commission once, and once only. I do not say that the difficulties which I have mentioned are insuperable and I propose to try and get them overcome.
