POSTAL SERVICE AUTOMATION
(Cost savings)


    1. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Communications and Information Technology (a) what will be the cost savings in postal operations as a result of automated mailing processing and whether such savings will be passed on to the public and business community in the form of lower postage rates as a real incentive to encourage automation; and (b) whether his Ministry will provide a comparison of postage rates in Singapore, Malaysia and Brunei as well as a detailed explanation on the recent increases in international postage rates despite the cost savings through automation.

    The Minister of State for Communications and Information Technology (Mr Lim Swee Say) (for the Minister for Communications and Information Technology): Mr Speaker, Sir, postal rates were revised on 1st November this year, five years after the last revision in 1995. During this period, the cost of mail processing and delivery has continued to rise due to increases in wage costs and administration costs. However, with automation and computerisation, SingPost is able to increase labour productivity and offset some of these cost increases.

    In the recent revision, it has kept the rates for domestic standard mail, which accounts for more than 90% of domestic total volume, largely unchanged. The postage rate was raised only for standard mails in the 40-50 grams weight step, which comprises less than 0.5% of all standard mails.

    Mr Tay asked for comparison for postage rates in Singapore, Malaysia and Brunei. Sir, for the basic 20 grams standard size mail, our rate is 0.22 cents. This is higher than 0.14 cents in Brunei and 0.22 cents in Malaysia, due to higher wage structure in Singapore. If we were to compare our rates with economies of

Column: 1113

similar wage structure, SingPost's rate of 0.22 cents is lower than 0.29 cents in Hong Kong and 0.28 cents in Taiwan. So on balance, IDA's price control framework does ensure that our postal rates are competitive.

    Mr Tay also asked about international mail rates. The two major cost components are operating costs and payment to destination countries. I wish to point out that operating costs account for only 38% of the total cost. The remaining 62% are payments to destination countries. The rates of payment are determined by an International Accounting System established by the Universal Postal Union, and the payments are beyond the control of SingPost. The recent hike in international postal rates is to allow SingPost to recover part of the increase in the rates of payment to destination countries.

    Mr Tay Beng Chuan (Nominated Member): Sir, is the Minister aware that the abolition of second-class postage for printed materials by SingPost has resulted in a substantial increase in the postal cost for business magazines and newsletters alike, which can no longer be posted at a cheaper second-class mail? This is definitely not a pro-business approach and I wonder whether the Minister could explain this.

    Mr Lim Swee Say: Mr Speaker, Sir, as I mentioned earlier, in terms of international mail, more than 60% of the costs are really beyond the control of SingPost. So the increase in international mail rates is really a cost increase that we have no choice but to bear. As far as domestic mail is concerned, the priority is to encourage automation. For example, if companies or individuals, in particular companies, are able to take advantage of the incentive schemes offered by SingPost, such as the domestic mail bulk scheme, ie, they lodge their letters in a machineable bar-coded and pre-sorted manner, they can enjoy a substantial discount from SingPost. Reasons for regulation of TCM practitioners

    Sir, although Western medicine is the main form of healthcare in Singapore, Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) enjoys considerable popularity as a complementary form of healthcare. It has been estimated that about 45% of the population had ever consulted a TCM practitioner in the past. About 12% of the daily outpatient attendances opt to see TCM practitioners. Hence, TCM is a significant factor in the healthcare scene in Singapore.

    There is a similar trend in developed countries. In the West, complementary medicine, including TCM, has a significant following. Some Western countries now allow insurance claims or reimbursement for acupuncture treatment. Acupuncture and therapeutic massage (or tui na) have been popular among patients suffering from painful conditions. There is also the popular belief that TCM is milder and has fewer side effects, thus making it a preferred modality amongst older persons. As the population in Singapore ages, it is likely that there would be an increased demand for TCM treatment.

    The number of TCM practitioners in Singapore is also sizeable and it would probably increase in tandem with the demand for TCM treatment. A list published by the local TCM community in 1997 indicated that there were 1,807 TCM practitioners in Singapore. There is probably more now. The training and experience of TCM practitioners in Singapore vary. Some were self-taught. Some learned from peers or masters, while others graduated from TCM institutes.

    In July 1994, the Ministry of Health appointed a Committee to look into the regulation of TCM. The Committee recommended a phased approach, (ie a step-by-step approach) to the regulation of TCM practitioners in order to safeguard

Column: 1127

patients' interest and safety, as well as to enhance the standard of training and professionalism of TCM practitioners in Singapore.

    A phased approach is necessary, given the complexities of regulating TCM practitioners. Take the case of Hong Kong as an example. Although Article 138 of the Basic Law provided for the development of both Western and Chinese medicine, and there has been a Working Committee appointed since 1989 to promote, develop, and regulate TCM, the Chinese Medicine Ordinance was only enacted after 10 years in July 1999.

    To facilitate the regulatory process in Singapore, the Ministry of Health first encouraged the eight main local TCM organisations to form the Singapore Traditional Chinese Medicine Organisations Coordinating Committee (STCMOCC) in 1996. The STCMOCC serves as the coordinating body amongst the mainstream TCM community. It also acts as a channel of communication between the mainstream TCM community and the Ministry of Health.

    I must say that this arrangement has worked well. The formation of STCMOCC has led to the upgrading of the local TCM training course to a 6-year part-time diploma course in 1996. The STCMOCC has also worked out a listing of TCM practitioners in Singapore. That was how we knew that in 1997, there were 1,807 TCM practitioners in Singapore. In fact, this Bill was prepared with useful inputs from the STCMOCC. The Bill indeed has the support of the mainstream TCM community in Singapore.

Major provisions in the Bill

    Mr Speaker, Sir, allow me now to present the main provisions of the Bill. This Bill is an enabling legislation to allow the Minister to declare any type of practice of TCM for registration if he is of the opinion that it is in the public interest for that type of practice to be regulated. In other words, the Minister will decide which form of TCM practice to be regulated at which point of time. It will not be

Column: 1128

that, with the promulgation of this Act, every form of TCM practice will be regulated. Clause 14(1) of the Bill states that the Minister can regulate by order published in the Gazette. This is in line with the phased approach the Ministry of Health has been adopting.

    The Ministry of Health's intention is to start with regulating the acupuncturists, because acupuncture is an invasive procedure that carries risks of injury and infection. It is estimated that there are about 1,070 acupuncturists in Singapore. This will then be followed by the registration of TCM general practitioners in about 3 to 4 years' time, and the TCM herbal dispensers at a later date.

    This Bill takes reference from the existing medical- and health-related legislation such as the Medical Registration Act, the Dentists Act as well as the Nurses and Midwives Act. All these Acts are essentially based on self-regulation. This Bill provides for the establishment of a Traditional Chinese Medicine Practitioners Board appointed by the Minister to register TCM practitioners, to accredit TCM training courses and institutions, as well as to regulate the professional conduct and ethics of TCM practitioners. The Board will consist of 5 to 9 members, comprising peers from the TCM community, members of the medical profession, as well as representatives from the Ministry of Health.

    The Board can appoint Committees to which specific tasks are delegated. For example, the Board may appoint an Examinations Committee to set standards and to determine the content of the qualifying examinations for registration, as well as to oversee the organisation and conduct of the examinations. Also, the Board may appoint Investigation Committees to investigate complaints which may lead the Board to take disciplinary actions against practitioners.

    The TCM Practitioners Board will have disciplinary powers to deal with any registered TCM practitioner found guilty of professional misconduct, professional

negligence, or improper conduct, which renders him or her unfit to practise. The Board may -

    (a) caution or censure the practitioner;

    (b) impose a fine of not more than $10,000;

    (c) impose conditions on the practitioner's registration for a period of no more than three years;

    (d) suspend his or her registration for no more than three years; or

    (e) cancel his or her registration.

    Any practitioner who is aggrieved by the decision of the Board may appeal to the High Court within 30 days. A person whose registration has been cancelled can apply to the Board for restoration three years after the date of cancellation and not more than once in any period of 12 months. This is similar to the provisions made under the Medical Registration Act as well as the Dentists Act.

    To deter unlawful practice of TCM, the Bill provides for the imposition of penalties similar to the provisions of the Dentists Act. The Courts may fine the offender not more than $25,000 or imprison him for a term not exceeding sixmonths or both. In the case of a second or subsequent offence, the penalty is increased to a fine of not more than $50,000 or imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or both.

    Any person who procures or attempts to procure registration by fraud shall be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $10,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or to both. This is similar to the provisions of the Medical Registration Act.
