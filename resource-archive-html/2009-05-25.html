     Sir, when the President lasy addressed this House in November 2006, the sentiment was a very different one.  I was a newly-elected Member of this House and I recall that both the mood and economic climate were very upbeat.  The world economy had taken off and we were riding the wave of globalisation.  Unemployment in Singapore was moving towards an all-time low and our GDP growth averaged about 8%.  Some of our greatest concerns at the time were the widening income gap, our ageing population and the impact that globalisation was having on the retention of our local talent.

 

     In just two and a half years, the sentiment has changed drastically. The world is in the midst of one of the worst recessions that it has seen since the Great Depression.  Singapore, being a country that is almost totally dependent on the global economy, is now predicting a -6% to -9% decline. in our GDP. Unemployment and uncertainty is the order of the day.  Preliminary figures for March 2009 showed our unemployment rising above three per cent, the highest since the third quarter of 2005 and it is clear that there will b e further job losses.  Yet in the last few weeks, we have seen a sudden spike in the stock markets across the world.  Some say that these are the green shoots of recovery, while others say that it is a false positive.  I remain cautiously optimistic.  I do not think that we have seen the last of it yet but I have no doubt that we will pull through this crisis and emerge the better for it.  I am happy that that same sentiment reasonated throughout the President's speech to this House a week ago.  I thank the President for his positive and reassuring speech.  The President has laid the foundation and set the tone for what this Government will do to steer Singapore through this crisis.  In particular, I was happy to hear his call for the following:

     One of the first things that struck me in the President's Address to this House was his call for fresh re-thinking and creative answers to the question of how to enhance our economic competitiveness.  The question that particularly stood out for me was how to build new capabilities and to anchor value in Singapore.  To me, that is a more challenging task than attracting foreign investment.  Our basic formula to date has been to equip our people with the necessary skills to provide and advanced and competitive workforce.  We then attract foreign direct investment into Singapore by offering various fiscal incentives and a pro-business environment, a stable Government, and a highly-skilled workforce.  As the President said, this is how Singapore has developed year after year.  Over a period of time, we transformed this tiny island into an economic force to be reckon with in the region, if not, the world.  I believe that as we move ahead in this globalised economy, we need to do more to build new capabilities for ourselves and make sure that those capabilities remain anchored to Singapore.

As part of the SRI, the Government has undertaken a Bridging Loan Programme where 80% of the risk of up to S$5 million is borne by the Government.  According to SPRING, a total of S$2.5 billion worth of loans have been approved, with over S$1 billion made up of Government-backed loans.  Apparently, more than 90% of these loans have gone to our SMEs.
