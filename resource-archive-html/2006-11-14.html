Prof. Dr Yaacob Ibrahim: Mr Speaker, Sir, I appreciate the frustration of Singaporeans with this perennial haze occurrence.  The smoke haze in the region is from land and forest fires in Indonesia.  The fires are a result of land clearing practices by farmers and plantation companies.  The smoke haze situation is aggravated during El Nino years such as in 1994, 1997 and this year.

 

     In Singapore, the three-hour PSI hit a high of 150 on 7th October 2006, the highest level recorded since 1997.  The 24-hour PSI levels also hit "unhealthy" range on three days in October 2006.  The 24-hour PSI level was 128 on 7th October, 109 on 8th October, and 102 on 20th October.

 

     In this regard, we have been working closely with the Indonesian Government to help them mitigate the fires and smoke haze.  Allow me to elaborate.

 

Singapore’s assistance

 

     Since 1997, Singapore has been providing satellite pictures to alert the

Indonesian authorities at the central and local government levels on the locations of hot spots in Sumatra and Kalimantan.  We will continue to provide satellite pictures to assist Indonesia to locate the fires on the ground in order that they could take immediate action to put out the fires. 

 

     We have provided other technical assistance to Indonesia, such as communication and computer equipment, to facilitate fire detection and enforcement and air monitoring equipment.  We have also conducted training courses for Indonesian officials, including those at the district level, on interpretation of satellite imagery, geographical information and health effects of haze and air quality monitoring. 

 

     In August 2005, Singapore provided one C-130 aircraft to assist Indonesia in its cloud-seeding operations and a contingent of 54 SCDF fire-fighters to put out the fires in Sumatra.  This year, Singapore also extended the same offer, which Indonesia did not take up.  Our offer still stands.

 

Regional mechanisms and assistance

 

     In 1997, Singapore helped to shape the regional mechanism to tackle the haze problem.  In December 1997, Singapore hosted the First ASEAN Ministerial Meeting on Haze (AMMH), which developed the Regional Haze Action Plan (RHAP).  The Plan sets out measures to prevent and suppress land and forest fires as well as early warning and monitoring to detect smoke haze in the region.

 

     In 1998, the Sub-Regional Fire-Fighting Arrangements for Borneo and Sumatra (SRFA) were set up.  The SRFA comprises Brunei Darussalam, Indonesia, Malaysia and Singapore.  The aims of the SRFA are to focus on fire prevention and suppression measures on the ground in Sumatra and Borneo.  Thailand joined the

     Singapore also hosts the ASEAN Specialised Meteorological Centre (ASMC) since 1993.  The ASMC provides weather forecast and monitoring of smoke haze and land and forest fires in the region.

 

     Despite all the efforts over the past 10 years, the measures taken have not been adequate and effective.  The region is still badly affected by the haze this year.  Clearly, more needs to be done.

 

Recent developments

 

     After the three-hour PSI reached its peak of 150 last month, Singapore registered our concerns over the haze issue with Indonesia at the highest level.  Our Prime Minister wrote to Indonesian President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono on 10th October 2006 to express our concern and disappointment over the recurring haze problem.  At Singapore’s suggestion, Indonesia hosted the Sub-Regional Ministerial Meeting (SRMM) on Transboundary Haze Pollution in Pekanbaru on 13th October 2006. 

 

Sub-Regional Ministerial Meeting (SRMM)

 

     The Environment Ministers from Brunei Darussalam, Indonesia, Malaysia and Singapore attended this meeting.  Thailand was represented by its Deputy Permanent Secretary.  The SRMM acknowledged that the measures taken to tackle the land and forest fires in Indonesia over the past 10 years had not been effective.  They agreed to formulate a comprehensive Plan of Action (PoA) to deal with the smoke haze issues in the short and long term.  In this regard, assistance and support from international and regional organisations are needed to help prevent and suppress land and forest fires in Indonesia.

FERTILITY RATES AND MATERNITY LEAVE

     16.   Dr Lam Pin Min asked the Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) what is the total fertility rate (TFR) in Singapore since the implementation of the enhanced baby bonus and extended maternity leave schemes; (b) whether there are any significant changes in the fertility rates amongst the different demographic groups according to age, race, income and educational levels; (c) what is the rationale for capping the government-paid maternity leave reimbursement at $10,000 per month (inclusive of CPF contributions); and (d) whether this policy discriminates against the top 5-10% of the female workforce.

     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan:

          After four consecutive years of declining Total Fertility Rate (TFR), the TFR for 2005 remained steady at 1.24, same as that for 2004.  It is premature to judge whether the Marriage and Parenthood package of 2004 has been successful.  Nonetheless, it is encouraging to know that, compared to the same period, January to August last year, there has been an increase of some 451 babies in 2006.

 

     The TFR across all ethnic groups has been declining.  For the Chinese, the TFR decreased from 1.51 in 1995 to 1.08 in 2005.  In the same period, the TFR for Malays dropped from 2.50 to 2.07, and for Indians, it fell from 1.71 to 1.24.  Across all ethnic groups, the more educated Singaporeans are consistently having fewer children.

 

     An MCYS Marriage and Parenthood Survey conducted this year revealed that 83% of 3,000 married respondents agreed that the Marriage and Parenthood Package had created a friendlier environment for having and raising children. 

Maternity leave has been extended from 8 weeks to 12 weeks.  For the first and second child, while employers continue to pay for 8 weeks of maternity leave, the Government pays for the additional 4 weeks of maternity leave, up to a cap of $10,000.  And for the third and fourth child, Government pays for the entire 12 weeks of leave, subject to a cap of $30,000.  The cap of $10,000 per month is reasonable because only a small minority of women, about 3%, earn above $10,000 per month, in 2005.

 

     For these high income women, financial concerns are generally not the main constraints influencing their fertility.
