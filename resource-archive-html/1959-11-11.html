DEPUTY PRIME MINISTER
(Cabinet Member vis-a-vis member of University of Malaya academic staff)


    1. Mr A. P. Rajah asked the Prime Minister whether he is aware that the Deputy Prime Minister is still a member

Column: 722

of the University of Malaya academic staff, that he occupies University staff quarters at a subsidised rent, that he is still a contributor to the University of Malaya Academic Staff Provident Scheme at the rate of 5 per cent of the salary last drawn by him and that the University is still contributing for and on his behalf at the rate of 15 per cent on his said salary to the said Provident Scheme; and whether, in view of the position the Deputy Prime Minister holds in the Cabinet, he will make a statement and say what action he will take in the matter.

    The Prime Minister: Mr Speaker, the Deputy Prime Minister has been granted unpaid leave by the University of Malaya for an indefinite period not exceeding five years. The University has agreed that he should be allowed to continue his work, in particular his research work as Reader in the University. He is not paid for this.

    He continues to occupy his old quarters and the rent is $115 a month with maintenance charges of $25 a month. Similar quarters are being let out to members of the public at the same rent. It is incorrect to imply that the Deputy Prime Minister is being subsidised in his rent. Moreover, the Deputy Prime Minister, if he so wished, could have occupied official quarters whose rent would have been subsidised, but he has chosen to continue to stay in his old University quarters.

    The Deputy Prime Minister is not a contributor to the University Academic Staff Provident Fund, but the University has on its own account continued its contributions as part of the terms of the unpaid leave granted him.

    I am satisfied that the work of the Deputy Prime Minister in the University does not cause any conflict between his public duty and his private interest. I am also satisfied that such work can be carried out without prejudice to his duties as Deputy Prime Minister.

    I am advised by the State Advocate-General that the Deputy Prime Minister's position is perfectly constitutional and proper and I propose to take no action in the matter.

VALUATION LIST FOR 1960
(Statement by the Minister for National Development)


2.41 p.m.

    The Minister for National Development (Mr Ong Eng Guan): Mr Speaker, I wish to make a ministerial statement on the valuation list for 1960.

    The Government has decided not to proceed with the proposed revision of annual values as published in the City Council Valuation List for 1960. It has been decided that for assessment purposes in 1960 the Valuation Lists of both the City Council and the Rural Board should be based on the 1959 annual values.

    Assessment is considered a tax on property-owners to benefit the poorer people in order to provide more amenities like roads, standpipes, bus shelters and other such amenities. However, it has now been decided by Government that for 1960 we shall use the 1959 assessment list and the rectification of

Column: 726

anomalies in the assessment of properties will be deferred.

    The recent revision of properties in the City area was expected to bring about an additional revenue of $6 million. It has been estimated that 99 per cent of this additional revenue comes from these property-owners and commercial enterprises. The money thus collected will be used to provide amenities for the benefit of all other inhabitants of the island. However, it is not practicable to bring in this additional revenue of $6 million for 1960 because of certain misunderstandings which have arisen as a result of the rectification of anomalies in the assessments. Although the Government knows that most of these properties are very much under-assessed, it is not possible at this juncture to proceed with the original scheme due to some unscrupulous landlords of rent-controlled houses who, although not affected by the recent revision, thought it fit to exploit the situation and try to raise the rents of their tenants.

    Mr Speaker, the Government believes that until such time as sufficient information has been effectively disseminated amongst the tenants, action should be deferred. For equitable reasons and administrative convenience, it has also at the same time decided that in the City area as well as the Rural Board area, the Valuation List for 1960 should be based on the 1959 annual values.

SINGAPORE STATE ARMS AND FLAG AND NATIONAL ANTHEM BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.53 p.m.

    The Minister for Culture (Mr S. Rajaratnam): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    As there is a Government motion coming up for debate later on on the State Flag and National Anthem, I will confine my remarks to matters relating to the Bill.

    The objects of the Bill are quite simple and straightforward. It provides for the Yang di-Pertuan Negara to make regulations for the control of the use of the State Arms, Flag and Anthem. These are the new symbols of an authority and loyalty and it is right that there should be rules to ensure that they are accorded the respect they deserve. These rules will cover matters like the places and times at which the Arms and Flag of the State may be publicly displayed or the Anthem played. Some of the rules will prescribe the correct way

Column: 730

of flying the Flag and how the State Arms should be displayed, or prescribe that the State Flag should be flown in Government buildings such as Courts, Secretariats, and so on. There will also have to be rules about the display of the State Flag by ships in harbour; but more important, there will have to be rules to prevent afront to or abuse of our new symbols of loyalty. For example, some bright free enterpriser might take it into his head to adopt the State Flag or the State Arms as a trade mark. Then you might find the market flooded with under-clothing, toothpaste, hair lotion and goods of that sort using the State Flag or Arms as a trade mark. Mr Speaker, Sir, you will agree that such misdirected patriotism must be discouraged.

    There will also have to be rules to prohibit the indiscriminate playing of the National Anthem in public places. It will not do for every bar in town to open and close for the day to the strains of the National Anthem; nor would it add to the dignity of the State National Anthem if it were jazzed up into a rhumba or a samba or a conga.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, the Bill, in other words, seeks to ensure that the new State symbols are not wilfully brought into contempt, ridicule, and hatred. These are symbols of national pride, and there should be rules to see that there is no affront to it.

    Sir, I beg to move.

    Question put, and agreed to.

    Bill accordingly read a Second time and committed to a Committee of the whole Assembly.

    Assembly immediately resolved itself into a Committee on the Bill. -[Mr Rajaratnam].

    Bill considered in Committee; reported without amendment; read the Third time and passed.

PAWNBROKERS (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.00 p.m.

    The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Ong Pang Boon): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

Column: 731



    The Explanatory Statement to the Bill mentions briefly all the amendments which are being proposed. The main intention of the Bill is to give members of the public, especially the poorer classes who patronise the pawnshops, the benefit of cheaper pawnshop facilities.

    The opportunity is also taken to alter the constitution of the Pawnbrokers Licensing Board.

    Clause 2 of the Bill amends the membership of the Board. At present, of the six members who comprise the Board, the Chairman and one other person are appointed by the Minister. One member is the Commissioner of Police or his representative, and three persons are appointed by the Justices of the Peace from among their own number.

    The new Government considers, however, that in view of the constitutional changes, it would be more appropriate that all appointments other than those of the Commissioner of Police or his representative should be made by the Minister.

    Clause 3 extends the redemption period of the pledge from three months to six months. This will benefit pawners, especially the poorer ones, who would have a better chance of not losing their pledges to the pawnbrokers.

    Clause 4 now makes it necessary for pawnbrokers to take out insurance cover, not only against fire as at present, but also for loss by theft, robbery, etc.

    The police have drawn attention to a case early this year where a pawnshop was robbed of $50,000 worth of pledges. Pawners would suffer if the pledges were not insured against such loss.

    Clause 5 reduces the interest rate from the present maximum rate of 2 per cent per mensem or 24 per cent per annum to 1 per cent per mensem or 12 per cent per annum on the amount of the loan. This reduction will bring the interest rate into line with the new Moneylenders Ordinance in that 12 per cent per annum has been fixed for secured loans. This reduction would afford financial relief especially to the poorer classes of pawners, who pawn their belongings, often belongings they

Column: 732

cannot afford to do without, in order to get money quickly for one urgent reason or another.

    Despite the fact that they have pledged their belongings, they still have to pay 24 per cent interest for the money they get as against 12 per cent under the Moneylenders Ordinance for seizure of loans.

    In their business, pawnshop owners do not depend entirely on the interest payable by pawners. Pawnbrokers also get the benefit from the sale of unredeemable pledges.

    Representations have been made by the Pawnbrokers Association that the proposed reduction of the present interest rate by half would seriously affect the pawnshop business to the extent of virtually having to run them at a loss, leading possibly to the closing down of the business. They pointed out that the interest rate had just been reduced from 3 or 4 per cent to 2 per cent in September 1958.

    It should be pointed out, however, that the purpose of abolishing the previous tender system and instituting the present licensing system was to encourage more pawnbrokers into the field. However, this seemed to have failed, and the pawnbroker business is very much of a monopoly in the hands of four or five families.

    In view of this and the representations made, I shall, at the end of the Second Reading, move that the Bill be committed to a Select Committee.

At present, any pledge pawned for $100 or under, if not redeemed within the time allowed by the Ordinance, shall, at the end of the redemption period, become the pawnbroker's absolute property. Any article pawned for an amount exceeding $50 must be something of value, and disposal by public auction would at least give the pawner a last opportunity

1959/1960
