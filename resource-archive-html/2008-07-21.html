OBITUARY ON THE LATE DR ONG CHIT CHUNG

 

 

     The Minister for National Development and Leader of the House (Mr Mah Bow Tan):

Mr Speaker, Sir, at certain moments in time, we are reminded starkly that each of us has but one life here, one lone opportunity to leave a mark on our family, our friends, our fellow men, our country. The true measure of a man is seen not just in the height of his accomplishments here and now, but also in the depth of the gap he leaves behind when he passes on, and in the loss that we all feel.

    

     This past week, Singapore has lost one of its finest sons.  Dr Ong Chit Chung* was a distinguished parliamentarian, a devoted representative of his constituency, a learned intellectual, a leading business figure, and much more.

    

     We salute his achievements in these fields. But we will always remember him as a fellow comrade and colleague, a generous mentor, an inspiring individual. He was, for many of us as well, a close and dear friend.

    

     As with fellow Members of this House, I was stunned when I first heard the sad news of Dr Ong’s sudden passing.  I have known Dr Ong for many years. We both entered Parliament together in 1988, worked closely in many areas, and enjoyed many hours of fellowship.

   

     Only recently, we, the 1988 batch of MPs, past and present, had dinner with him and his wife Elizabeth, a gathering which Chit Chung  had initiated and organised.  We had a good time talking about many things – politics, family, life.  He was his usual meticulous self, making sure all arrangements were perfect, right down to the bottle of 1988 red that Elizabeth told me he had personally selected.

    

Column: 2810

     Many have paid tribute this past week to Dr Ong’s many notable achievements, such as his dedicated grassroots work, his outstanding contributions to defence and security issues and his active engagement with unions. He was an exemplary union advisor who helped to strengthen labour-management relations on the ground, for which he was recognised by the NTUC as a  Friend of Labour.

 

     Indeed, it would be difficult to list fully all of Dr Ong’s accomplishments. I cannot but echo the Prime Minister’s words, when he called Dr Ong a man of deep convictions, a party stalwart, and an indispensable member of the PAP election team. Those of us who have fought many tough election battles will recall the invaluable help rendered by his Bukit Batok branch, whose members churned out the flyers and pamphlets for our campaign, sometimes at very short notice.

 

     Dr Ong was a veteran member of this House. He first entered Parliament in 1988, and served two terms as Member for Bukit Batok from 1988 to 1996. After the January 1997 General Election, he was returned to Parliament as a Member for Bukit Timah GRC. At the November 2001 General Election, Dr Ong was once again returned to Parliament, serving as MP for Jurong GRC, a position which he held into this current term.

 

     He was a dedicated and hardworking MP, who served his residents well and represented them fearlessly and faithfully in Parliament. He reflected their hopes and aspirations,  as well as their frustrations and fears.  He in turn was well-loved by his residents, from all walks of life, all ages and races. This was all too evident in the stream of people who came to pay their last respects to him at the funeral service on Friday.

    

     Dr Ong served this House and this Government tirelessly and with

distinction. He was Parliamentary Secretary for the Ministry of Home Affairs and the then Ministry of Labour from 1991 to 1993. He was Deputy Government Whip from 1994 to 2002.  Apart from various other committee appointments, Dr Ong was also the former GPC Chairman for Education, and for Defence and Foreign Affairs.

    

     We all recall the many passionate speeches which Dr Ong made in this very Chamber, on various issues close to his heart, from defence to education to social welfare. We remember the many sterling contributions he made, both within and outside Parliament. We treasure those too few moments each of us had with him. We will never forget our comrade in arms, this stalwart of a man, this fine son of Singapore.

    

     Sir, on behalf of this House, may I express our deepest condolences to the family of the late Dr Ong Chit Chung.  The family is with us in the House today. To his wife Elizabeth, and his two sons, Christopher and James, their spouses Magdalene and Sharon and his sisters, Chai Ming, Chai Peng and Tan Leong, we mourn with you the loss of your husband, father, brother, and our friend and comrade, Ong Chit Chung.

      2.  Dr Lam Pin Min asked the Minister for Health in light of the recent revelation of illegal organ trading in Singapore (a) what additional measures will be put in place to ensure that similar illegal transactions do not occur; and (b) whether the Ministry will consider legalising the ethical sale of organs in the near future.

 

     The Minister for Health (Mr Khaw Boon Wan):  Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to take Question Nos. 1 and 2 together?

 

     Mr Speaker:  Yes.

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:   Sir, both questions pertain to alleged illegal organ trading transactions in Singapore.  As the case is before the courts and further investigations are still on-going, I should not comment on them.  After the case has been fully wrapped up, my Ministry will review existing measures and fix any inadequacy.

 

     Let me instead discuss this subject generally to outline the extent of the problem, our current approach and our strategy going forward.

 

     End stage renal disease (ESRD) or kidney failure is a dreadful disease.  Every year, about 1,000 new cases are diagnosed here.  38% of the patients will not survive the first year given the severity of their illness.  For the remaining 600 patients, the treatment options are limited: either a kidney transplant or life-long kidney dialysis. 

 

Column: 2813

     Kidney transplant is the preferred option as patients’ life expectancy is longer and their quality of life is better as the transplanted patients do not have to be hooked on to a dialysis machine for a significant portion of their life.  But kidney transplant is a major operation with its attendant risk.  Old age and other co-morbidity of the patient can significantly increase the surgical risk to the patient and reduce the chance of a successful transplant.  That is why only about a third of the 600 new patients, ie, 200, are suitable for kidney transplant and can potentially benefit from the transplant option.  The other 400 new patients will have to depend on kidney dialysis. 

 

     Donated kidneys come from two sources: deceased donors and live donors.  Like the rest of the world, we have worked to increase the kidney transplant rate.  While we are not yet getting 200 kidney transplants a year, we have raised our transplant rate quite significantly.  We are now about 50% sufficient, which is not bad as compared to many other countries.  This is a result of our adopting the Human Organ Transplant Act (HOTA) based on presumed consent in 1987, 21 years ago, and our more recent efforts to promote living donor organ transplants.  HOTA now contributes about 50 kidneys a year and living-related kidneys another 30, for a total of 80 kidneys a year. Another 20 Singaporeans go overseas for kidney transplant annually. 

 

    We can expect our kidney transplant rate to continue to grow.  First, from next month, HOTA will be extended to cover Muslims. Second, the living-related kidney transplant rate has been rising and with greater awareness and understanding, should go up further. 

 

    There is scope to raise both cadaveric and living donor transplant rates, and this is what we will do.  Currently, HOTA sets an age limit of 60 years on cadaveric donors.  This is an arbitrary limit and has

unnecessarily put many organs to waste.  Many countries, including Spain, do not set such an age limit.  The suitability of the organ depends on its condition, rather than the age of the donor.  The condition of the kidney can be determined by the transplant doctor and that should be the criterion for determining if an organ is suitable.  We shall remove the HOTA age limit to help save many more lives.  We will need to amend HOTA and when I bring the Bill to this House, I hope to get the full support of Members for this initiative. 

 

      For living-related kidney donation, there are often family members who wish to donate to their loved ones but their tissues do not match well.  Some hospitals in the US and Europe have shown that with proactive coordination, "pair-matched donations" can be done.  This means matching a donor whose tissue is incompatible with the intended patient with another pair or even pairs in a similar situation.  This has not yet been done in Singapore.  My Ministry is working on this idea so that we can meet the altruistic wishes of such living-related donors.  We will set up a live-donor registry to facilitate this.  This may require the legal backing of HOTA and, if so, we shall amend HOTA accordingly. 

 

    With these two additional initiatives, we can aim to raise our sufficiency level from 50% to 70% in the medium term.

Both of these countries have successful altruistic organ donation systems built up through years of sustained public education.  In Singapore, we have not yet maximised the yield through altruistic organ donations.  Let us emulate them and push altruistic organ donations to their maximum potential. 

 

     With or without legalising kidney trading, HOTA and living-related kidney donations must be the predominant sources of kidney transplantation.  But if altruistic kidney donations cannot fully meet demand, we should continue to search for good complementary solutions.  We should not reject any idea just because it is radical or controversial.  By forcing ourselves to think about unconventional approaches, we may be able to find an acceptable way to allow a meaningful compensation for some living-unrelated kidney donors, without breaching ethical principles or hurting the sensitivities of others.
