SINGAPORE COLONY ORDER IN COUNCIL, 1955, (INTERPRETATION)
(Message from the Governor)


    Mr Speaker: Hon. Members, I have a message from His Excellency the Governor dated the 18th August, 1955. This is the message:

    "In my message to the Legislative Assembly of the 2nd August, 1955, I said that I hoped to be able on the 18th August, 1955, to send a substantive reply to the Resolution passed by the Legislative Assembly on 25th July, 1955.

    I am happy to be able to inform the Assembly that, since the return to Singapore of the Secretary of State last Monday further discussions have been held between him, the Chief Minister, the Ministers and myself, as

Column: 537

a result of which the constitutional issue arising over the exercise of the Governor's discretionary powers has been resolved.

    Her Majesty's Government, while upholding the interpretation placed by me on those sections of the Singapore Order in Council, 1955, which empower the Governor to act after consultation with the Chief Minister, has considered representations made by the Chief Minister and has also taken into account my own view that it is preferable, at this stage of Singapore's constitutional progress, if the present legal requirements connected with the exercise of the Governor's discretionary powers after consultation with the Chief Minister are modified in certain respects.

    Accordingly, Her Majesty's Government has undertaken to give me forthwith, through the Secretary of State, as provided in Section 7 of the Singapore Order in Council, 1955, Instructions that wherever in the Order in Council and in the Royal Instructions, with the exception noted later in this message, the Governor is empowered to act after consultation with the Chief Minister. I shall act in accordance with the Chief Minister's advice.

    These Instructions do not apply to Section 67 (1) of the Order in Council, which provides for the prorogation and dissolution of the Assembly.

    Finally, in regard to constitutional matters, including the fourth point in the Resolution of the 25th July, I am authorised to say that Her Majesty's Government will be glad to welcome to London at a suitable date a representative delegation from Singapore to consider the situation in the light of a year's working of the constitution."

    That is the message. I have copies made, and they will be distributed now.

    Copies of the Governor's message distributed to hon. Members.

    Sir, to me, as I see it, this is indeed a happy day for the people of Singapore. It marks the end of the first phase of our struggle for freedom. It marks the beginning of a new era. The present constitution infused by the spirit of this message, which you have read, marks, if I may use the Royal phrase, "a significant advance" in our constitutional development.

    Recently I read in a Nanyang Siang Pao editorial, which advised patience in this constitutional crisis, a delightful phrase. It said, "He who soweth abundantly shall also reap abundantly." Sir, today's harvest is a goodly one. In so far as this message deals with the constitutional issues, the victory is the people's - a complete, a clean and an exhilarating victory!

    The most understanding summary of these issues that I have read I saw in the Times of India published in Delhi on 1st August, 1955, and with your permission, Sir, I should like to read just two paragraphs from it:

    "The constitutional crisis is not, as has been made out, merely a somewhat exaggerated reaction of the Governor's insistence on his discretion to accept or reject the Chief Minister's advice and recommendations. It is not merely an unseemly squabble over the question of whether there should or should not be four Under-Secretaries. More fundamental principles are involved, and an obsession with constitutional legalities has tended

to obscure the force and extent of nationalist sentiment. As Mr Marshall has pointed out, in the present condition of rapid development in every part of Asia towards self-government, the British Government is under a moral, if not a constitutional, obligation to adopt 'the most liberal construction legally permissible in favour of elected representatives of the people'. Where the Governor enjoys discretionary powers, these powers should obviously be exercised, wherever possible, in accordance with the wishes of the elected representatives. It is on this basic issue that considerable resentment has been aroused, and the reality of this resentment cannot be evaded by insisting on legal and constitutional rights.

    The Governor's recent intimation that in the view of the British Government the Singapore constitution provides for slow rather than rapid progress towards self-government was completely out of joint with the sentiment of the times, and confounded those who had hoped that the present constitution was a framework within which encouraging strides could be made in the direction of early independence."

    Sir, in the message that we have before us lies that assurance, in the words of this editorial writer, 'that the present constitution is a framework within which encouraging strides can be made in the direction of early independence'.

    We based our request, as you will remember, on two grounds when approaching His Excellency.

    Firstly, that the interpretation proposed by us is the legal constitutional interpretation; and secondly, that in any event it was an untenable position that we, who had to shoulder the responsibilities, should have inadequate powers to meet those responsibilities.

    The answer we have today, Sir, is in effect this: we are wrong in our legal interpretation, but in the light of the people's needs, and in view of the sense of responsibility already shown by the people's representatives, Her Majesty's Government cedes the point and accepts our interpretation, even though the legal justification is missing. This is significant. We have asked for a spirit of goodwill. We have asked for a spirit of co-operation in interpreting the constitution so as to make it workable. Since Her Majesty's Government denies the legal justification for our request, the concession granted in this message gives concrete expression of a spirit of
