MRT TRAIN SERVICE DISRUPTIONS

     1.  Dr Lam Pin Min asked the Minister for Transport (a) whether he can provide the number of incidences of MRT service interruptions over the past three years and the underlying reasons; (b) what is the acceptable rate; and (c) what measures are put in place to ensure the reliability of the train services so as to minimise inconveniences caused to commuters.

 

     2.  Mr Lim Biow Chuan asked the Minister for Transport in view of the recent disruption of train services, whether the Ministry will consider regulations to require a public transport operator (PTO) to pay compensation or damages to its passengers if there is a breakdown in the services provided by the PTOs which is in excess of one hour.

 

     The Minister of State for Transport (Mrs Lim Hwee Hua) (for the Minister for Transport):  Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I would like to take Question Nos. 1 and 2 together?

     Mr Speaker: Yes, please.

Column:520

     Mrs Lim Hwee Hua:  Sir, Dr Lam asked about the number of train service disruptions over the past three years. From 2005 to 2007, the North-South-East-West Line and North-East Line had a total of 66 incidents with train disruption of more than 10 minutes, of which 19 lasted for more than 30 minutes. Out of these 19 incidents, 12 were due to track intrusions and two were due to other incidents caused by passengers, such as the activation of emergency buttons.  Five were however due to systems fault, such as problems with signaling and trains.

 

     Under the licensing framework, the train operators are required to adhere to the Operating Performance Standards (OPS) imposed by LTA. LTA monitors and measures the operators’ performance in the areas of service quality, safety assurance and equipment performance through indicators such as Train Service Availability, Schedule Adherence and Severity of Service Disruption. If the operators fail to meet the performance standards without acceptable reasons, LTA will take the necessary punitive action against the operators.

 

     The operators also have in place a Safety Management System, which requires them to continually enhance safety by identifying potential hazards and mitigating them to an acceptable safe level.  Besides carrying out internal safety audits, LTA also requires the operators to engage third party assessors to audit their system regularly.

 

     In spite of these measures, train service disruptions will occur from time to time.  The train operators have Rail Incident Management Plans (RIMP) and Standing Operating Procedures (SOP) in place to handle such situations.  The plans and procedures cover areas such as service recovery, provision of alternative transport such as bus bridging, crowd control and public communications.  These plans were activated during the recent incident.  LTA will work with the operators

to refine the RIMP and SOPs if needed, taking into account lessons from this incident.

 

     Affected commuters may take the free shuttle buses provided or seek a refund from SMRT within three working days of the incident. During examination periods, the operators will provide alternate transport, such as taxis, to affected students so that they will not be late for their examinations.

 

     Mr Lim asked about legislation to require train operators to pay compensation and damages arising from train service disruptions.  Under the Rapid Transit Systems Act, LTA can impose a financial penalty of up to $1 million for each incident if the operator is found to be negligent.  This acts as a deterrent to ensure that the operators upkeep the system well.  It is also in the interest of the operator to minimise any train disruption, and if it does occur, to expedite the recovery process, as prolonged disruptions would have a detrimental impact on their business.

     Mr Speaker: Keep your question short, Mr Lim.  We only have half an hour for Questions.

     Mr Lim Biow Chuan:  Yes.  That is all.

     Mrs Lim Hwee Hua:  Sir, it is not appropriate for LTA to prescribe the kind of compensation or damages that the operators should be giving to commuters who are inconvenienced by disruptions.  Nonetheless, we note a few points.  Firstly, there is a refund mechanism; secondly, there is the financial penalty of up to $1 million for each incident, which is no small matter and which is something the operator would take account of if it persists in not meeting standards.  Lastly, and most importantly, there are Service Availability Operating Performance Standards for the transport operators.  For the Member's interest, for the second half of 2007, SMRT's Train Service Availability was 99.94%, which is above the 98% stipulated by the standards.

SPREAD OF CHIKUNGUNYA DISEASE

     3.  Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Minister for the Environment and Water Resources in light of the spread of the chikungunya disease for the first time in Singapore, what efforts have been made by the authorities to work with the businesses in the affected area to curb the spread of the disease.

     The Minister for the Environment and Water Resources (Assoc. Prof. Dr Yaacob Ibrahim):  Mr Speaker, Sir, the chikungunya disease is transmitted by the bite of infective Aedes mosquitoes.  The key to stopping the disease transmission is therefore to deprive the mosquitoes of a place to breed.  To this end, the NEA has, since the notification of the first local case, carried out more than 4,800 inspections in the affected area to remove all possible mosquito breeding habitats.  These include discarded receptacles, plastic pails and poorly-maintained roof gutters.  NEA has also been working with PUB, URA, LTA and SLA to improve the overall sanitation and housekeeping of premises in the affected area, so that they do not become conducive to mosquito breeding.
 

Column:523

     We recognise that the business operators in the affected area have an equally important role to play in preventing the spread of the disease. NEA has therefore partnered the Little India Shopkeepers and Heritage Association (LISHA) to educate the operators and premises owners on the importance of good housekeeping and regular checking for potential mosquito breeding habitats.  As roof gutters are known to be a potentially productive breeding habitat, premises owners have also been asked to remove the gutters if they cannot be properly maintained. 

 

     Apart from the business operators, NEA has also been advising people visiting and working in the affected area on disease prevention tips through pamphlets that are printed in various languages, including Bengali and Sinhala. 

 

     It is through denying the mosquitoes a place to breed that we can prevent mosquito-borne diseases, such as chikungunya fever, from taking their root here. 

 

     We were able to break the transmission of the disease because of the close coordination among the agencies and the community.  Although the chikungunya outbreak is over, we should all remain vigilant to prevent any recurrence, as we are still susceptible to the importation of the disease.

 

     Mdm Ho Geok Choo (West Coast):  Sir, I would like to ask the Minister how many cases were hospitalised and at what duration were they being discharged.  Although it has been reported that it is under control, are there any indications of any new outbreaks?

MATRIMONIAL SALE OF HDB FLATS

(Single Singapore Citizen Scheme)

     1.   Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong asked the Minister for National Development (a) for each of the last five years, how many of the divorced women who have been affected by the matrimonial sale of HDB flats are below the age of 35; and (b) whether they are affected by the Single Singapore Citizen Scheme.

     Mr Mah Bow Tan:

          For each of the past five years, there were on average 630 women below the age of 35 who sold their matrimonial HDB homes as a result of divorce. 

 

     A divorced person with legal custody of at least one child constitutes a family nucleus.  Upon sale of the matrimonial flat, he or she has the option of buying a new or resale HDB flat according to his/her eligibility.

 

     Divorced persons without custody of children are treated no differently from other singles.  If they are 35 or older, they can purchase a resale HDB flat under the Single Singapore Citizen Scheme if they meet the eligibility criteria.  They can also purchase a resale flat jointly with another single under the Joint Singles Scheme.
