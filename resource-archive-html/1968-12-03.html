ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

DIPLOMATIC RELATIONS WITH
INDEPENDENT ASIAN COUNTRIES


    1. Mr Tay Boon Too asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour if he will take steps to establish diplomatic relations with all inde pendent Asian countries irrespective of their political ideologies provided that he is satisfied that these countries will not interfere in the internal affairs of Singapore.

    The Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour (Mr S. Rajaratnam): Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore's policy - it has been repeatedly said -is to be friends with all countries regardless of their political ideologies or their system of government so long as they respect our sovereignty and territorial integrity and refrain from interfering in our internal affairs. Singapore is therefore prepared to establish diplomatic relations with all countries who want to be friends in Asia and elsewhere too. However, the establishment of Singapore diplomatic missions abroad is a different matter. This will be governed by financial considerations, personnel re sources and the degree of our interest in the various countries. So friendly contacts with countries in Asia will be maintained and consolidated, in some cases, by the establishment of our diplomatic missions and, in other cases, by official visits and by working together with them in the United Nations and other organisations.

    However, in speaking about the establishment of diplomatic ties, one feature which is somewhat unusual in the world today is the existence of divided countries. In the case of divided countries, diplomatic relations with one part of a divided country to the exclusion of the other can only mean lending support to one against the other. For this reason, we have since independence maintained, in the case of divided countries, that we would be most happy to have trade and consular

Column: 8

relations with either of them on an equal basis and that we would do nothing that would give an advantage to one side over the other.

    Mr Ang Nam Piau (Thomson): Mr Speaker, Sir, can the Minister for Foreign Affairs inform the House how many independent Asian countries have already established diplomatic relations with Singapore?

    Mr Rajaratnam: I cannot recollect the precise number. It is neither too big nor too small. But if the Member is interested, he can ask the Ministry of Culture which has a consular list of the countries which have diplomatic relations here.

ASEAN
(Benefits to Singapore and future plans)


    2. Mr Teong Eng Siong asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs and Minister for Labour (a) what economic or other benefits has ASEAN brought to Singapore since its formation; and (b) whether he will inform the House briefly about the future plans of ASEAN.

    Mr Rajaratnam: Mr Speaker, Sir, ASEAN has as yet brought no direct economic benefits to its members. However, this should occasion no special disappointment because ASEAN was formed only a little over a year ago. However, during this year, it has been able to clarify for itself the possible approaches to greater economic cooperation among member states. At the ministerial meeting held in Jakarta last August, reports by ASEAN ad hoc committees on food production and supply, including fisheries, shipping, transport and telecommunications, civil aviation and tourism were approved and adopted. The Ministers further decided that these recommendations should be examined in greater detail by the ASEAN National Secretariat to decide which could be implemented immediately and which would require further study.

Column: 9



    Singapore has already given effect to some of the recommendations made at the last ministerial meeting, though all these related to tourism. Firstly, we have introduced seven-day visa free facilities to persons arriving in Singapore provided they possess valid passports, firm bookings and entry facilities to their destinations and continue their journey within seven days of arrival in Singapore. Secondly, we have agreed to accept collective passports and travel documents issued to parties of between five and 25 persons, all of whom should be nationals of a country and travelling on package or chartered tours to countries within the ASEAN region.

    Singapore, together with Indonesia, has also been pursuing the possibility of joint development of Bali as an international tourist resort. Last April, a ten- man technical fact-finding mission from Singapore visited Indonesia to make recommendations on the project to the two Governments. The mission's report has been presented and accepted in principle by the Government of Indonesia pending a more detailed assessment to be undertaken by the Stamford Research Institute.

    As to the future plans of ASEAN, the hon. Member's guess will be as good as mine. Like many other efforts at regional co-operation, ASEAN too is running into a spot of bother at the moment. I do not want to minimise these difficulties, but all I can say is that the present difficulties would appear trivial and irrelevant to what ASEAN countries would face were they to abandon altogether their belief in the need for regional co-operation. As far as Singapore is concerned, its attitude towards ASEAN has never been one of starry-eyed optimism. Progress towards regional co-operation will be, particularly in the early stages, slow, painful and interrupted by setbacks. But we are equally convinced that hard economic and political facts will push ASEAN countries in the direction of regional co-operation. However much we the members may drag our feet, each passing year will make clearer to ASEAN countries that the

Column: 10

choice for them ultimately is one of hanging together or hanging separately.

    So, Mr Speaker, Sir, to put it succinctly, at the moment as of today, I will describe ASEAN as a state of mind rather than a fulfilment.

    Mr Rajaratnam: I think the hon. Member should read the newspapers because I cannot read them for him!

ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT
BOARD (TRANSFER OF ASSETS)
BILL
First Reading


2.57 p.m.

    Mr Tang See Chim: Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Economic Development Board (E.D.B.) which was established in 1961 has done valuable work in promoting industrial growth, developing industrial estates, financing industrial enterprises and providing technical training and consultant service to manufacturing industries. The scale of operations of the E.D.B, has expanded greatly since 1961, and this has magnified the basic conflict of the various functions which the E.D.B, was originally called upon to perform. Investment promotion, industrial financing and development and management of the industrial estates are functions each incompatible with the other and are best performed by separate organisations. Accordingly, on 1st June, 1968, the Jurong Town Corporation was Set up under an Act

Column: 19

of Parliament and commenced operations. The Corporation has taken over the ownership of the industrial lands and development functions of the E.D.B.

    A further separation of the functions of the E.D.B. took place on 1st September, 1968, when the Development Bank of Singapore (the Bank, for short), a private limited company, commenced operations. The Bank has taken over the industrial financing functions of the E.D.B.

    Since the E.D.B, is not now concerned with industrial financing but is concentrating on its main function of industrial promotion, clauses 2 and 3 of the Bill provide respectively for the transfer back to the Government of loans and debentures made, and stocks and shares subscribed, by the E.D.B, in industrial enterprises. Clause 4 provides for moneys invested in securities or deposited by E.D.B, to be transferred back to the Government according to the directions of the Minister for Finance.

    The E.D.B, loan portfolio, when returned to the Government, will be assigned by the Government to the Bank. The equity investments and all other assets will be retained by the Government. The transfer to the Bank of the E.D.B, loan portfolio is made via the Government, so that the total value of this loan portfolio can be treated as a loan from the Government to the Bank on which a reasonable rate of interest will be charged.

    The transfer of the assets of the E.D.B, to the Government is made in the name of the Minister for Finance, who is a corporation sole, a legal entity established by law to hold investments of the Government.

    It will be noted from clause I of the Bill that the operation of the Bill is with retrospective effect as from 1st September. 1968, which is the date of commencement of operations of the Bank.

    With the establishment of the Jurong Town Corporation and the Development Bank of Singapore, the rationalisation of the structure of the E.D.B, is now complete. The E.D.B, will henceforth concentrate on its major functions of industrial promotion as well as of assistance in overcoming technical and managerial problems of individual firms. Funds to run the E.D.B, will be provided annually as a subvention from the Government.

As there are now approximately 320,000 driving licence holders and 254,000 motor vehicles, the congestion at the Registry, especially during the first ten days of each month, is very bad indeed, thus causing great inconvenience to members of the public and to the over-worked staff, as well.     Towards the end of last year, the problem was studied jointly by a team of officials from the Organisation and Methods Branch, the Electronic Data Processing Unit and the Registry of Vehicles. They came quickly to the conclusion that the congestion at the Registry of Vehicles can only be relieved if renewal of licences is decentralised, In other words, licences should not be renewed at one central place only, but should instead be renewed at post offices throughout the island, as in the case of television and radio licences. However, at the start, only 15 selected post offices will be used out of a possible 42. This team also recommended that the scheme be implemented in two stages. For the first stage, driving licences are to be renewed at post offices, leaving the renewal of vehicle licences to be implemented at the second stage. The scheme recom mended by them also includes the use of computers to record and check the particulars on driving licences to enable

    May I now. Sir, elaborate further on the Bill.

    The Ordinance at present requires applicants for driving licences to furnish photographs and, in the case of paid drivers, to permit fingerprints to be taken. Since photographs and fingerprints cannot be recorded by the computer, the existing requirements are to be dispensed with and this is effected by amending section 13 of the Ordinance in clause 3 of the Bill. However, the necessary identification can still be maintained by the fact that the new driving licence will bear the holder's identity card number and, in the case of foreigners, a special number will be allotted. Since the computer is programmed to work in monthly cycles, it will be necessary to provide that licences expire on the last day of each month. For instance, under the new system, a licence taken out on 12th August, 1968, for one year will expire on 31st August, 1969, (instead of 12-8-69). Eventually, therefore, all licences will expire on the last day of the month, and will then become renewable on the first day of the following month.
