FLU INFECTIONS

     1. Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong asked the Minister for Health (a) whether there has been an increase in flu infections over the last three months and if this is normal for this period of time; and (b) if so, whether this rise in flu infections is caused by any virus closely or remotely related to the H5N1 strain.

     The Minister for Health (Mr Khaw Boon Wan):  Mr Speaker, Sir, in Singapore, influenza viruses circulate all year round.  But over two periods every year, there would normally be an increase in influenza incidence: April to July and November to January.  They roughly correspond to the southern winter and northern winter respectively.

 

     Influenza A is the predominant circulating influenza virus here. In the last three months, its incidence peaked in the first week of January 2006, when 5.7% of all patients who presented with Acute Respiratory Infection (ARI) tested positive for Influenza A. This rate of 5.7% is, in fact, lower than the corresponding figure of 17.9% in 2004 or 7.0% in 2005.

 

     So far, H3N2 - this is the Hong Kong flu that caused an epidemic in 1968 - and H1N1, the infamous Spanish flu which caused a major problem for the world in 1918, have been the influenza virus subtypes in circulation.  To date, H5N1 has not been identified in Singapore, whether among humans or birds.

     Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong (Non-Constituency Member):  Sir, anecdotal evidence from many people I talked to tells me that this is a very strong virus, and people are suffering very badly from the cough.  It seems that this is something different.  Is there a special medicine for this?  Or is it something that normal medicine is able to cure the flu?

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:  Sir, I wonder where did Mr Chia get his anecdotes from because, fortunately, there is no H5N1 yet in Singapore.  He may have friends in Indonesia or Vietnam, but most of them who got it have died.  The symptoms are the usual flu and severe pneumonia with many complications.  The fatality rate is high.  I think the recorded cases in about seven countries is more than 100.  The fatality rate is more than half.

 

     So this is a serious problem.  Fortunately, for human beings, the problem remains one that largely affects the animals.  There is now a world pandemic.  We see it in Germany, Turkey, Africa, among the birds.  It remains among the birds.  The risk is that one day, the virus will mutate and become much more infectious - spreading from human to human.  And that is why all of us have to quickly gear up and we have been doing so, like any other country.

    Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong:  Sir, today's Straits Times has an article advising the shut-down of businesses and Government in the event of a pandemic when the virus is able to transfer to humans.  Is the Government prepared for such a contingency to shut-down the whole Government, all systems and all companies in the event of such a pandemic?  Are we prepared for this?  What are the likely consequences of such a shut-down?

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:  Sir, preparations take the form of the Flu Pandemic Preparedness Plan which, by now, practically all countries would have it.  We have been refining our Plan for a couple of years now, and it is fully published in the website.  Like all plans, you can never be fully prepared, because while the risk now is H5N1, when it happens, will it actually be H5N1 or some other virus?  Nature has a way to surprise.  But there are certain generic principles which we can follow to gear up as much as we can.  And we will continue to refine our plan.  We have, in fact, discussed it in this House not too many months ago in response to some Members' query. 

 

     My own assessment is this.  I think our own domestic preparation plan is as good as one can get.  But in the last few months, we have devoted our energy to work upstream, on the preventive aspects.  And it is possible to prevent it.  The world has a window to prevent this disaster from happening, but it requires cooperation from many countries, especially those with large agricultural sectors.  That is why we work with our neighbours.  In fact, Members would have read and I have spoken about it in this House that we are doing a major project helping Indonesia, working not just between our two countries but involving America and many other countries.  In fact, they are right now in town to discuss this pilot project, and I am meeting them tonight.

     Mr Speaker: Last supplementary question, Mr Chia.

     Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong:  Sir, now that a lot of poultry are affected in many other countries, should citizens make a conscious effort to avoid chickens and ducks in the meantime?

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:  Sir, although I eat very little meat, I continue to occasionally eat Kentucky fried chickens.  This H5N1 reminds me of the SARS virus.  While it is fearsome when it spreads, it is easily killed outside of a host.  At a temperature of 60 degrees, I think, the virus is killed.  Therefore, if the chicken is properly cooked, eggs are properly boiled, the risk is almost zero.  Therefore, when Singaporeans travel overseas, just stay away from chicken farms and wet markets where slaughtering continues.  When we stopped the slaughtering of poultry in wet markets 15 years ago, there was a lot of unhappiness coming from housewives.  But what an important contribution it has made to our public health.  It has reduced significantly this risk.

     Mdm Ho Geok Choo (West Coast):  Sir, the Minister indicated that at a temperature of 60 degrees or so, it will be very safe.  Many eateries are still serving half-boiled eggs.  I wonder whether it is good for us to issue an advisory for Singaporeans to avoid half-boiled eggs.

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan:  Sir, I personally do not think that is necessary because our AVA is very vigilant.  Last fortnight when a case cropped up in Malaysia - kampong chickens were infected with H5N1 - they moved at a fast speed to reduce the risk.  For the past two years, they have been inspecting farms in Malaysia and making sure that the chicken farms which export to us are properly bio-secured.  The risk therefore is very low, as we only import chickens from these areas.  In Singapore, a large proportion of our eggs are locally supplied, and AVA has made sure that our farms in Singapore are properly bio-secured.  Unless you start smuggling eggs from I do not know where, I think it is quite safe to continue eating your boiled eggs.

INDIVIDUAL EMAIL ACCOUNTS FOR SINGAPOREANS

     The following Question stood in the name of Mr Leong Horn Kee -

 

     2.  To ask the Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts in view of the announcement made by the Government to provide ultra high-speed broadband and wireless Internet connections for the whole of  Singapore, whether his Ministry will consider issuing in future an individual email account for every student, working adult and active Singaporean interested in Internet usage so as to facilitate the conveyance of school materials, business communications, Government information and notices among people.

     Ms Indranee Rajah (Tanjong Pagar):  Question No. 2, Sir.

     The Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts (Dr Lee Boon Yang):  Mr Speaker, Sir, Singapore already has one of the highest Internet access and infocomm savvy population in the world.  The Infocomm Development Authority's latest Annual Survey of Infocomm Usage in Households and by Individuals  shows that 66% of households enjoy Internet access.  Over three-quarters of companies  made use of the Internet.  Singaporeans are Internet-savvy and sophisticated in our Internet usage with 61% of Singapore's resident population aged 15 years and above using the Internet regularly.  Email is the top Internet application used.  About 94% surveyed said that they used email.  This means that a high percentage of our people already have an existing email account.  Of these accounts, some may be personal paid accounts offered by access service providers such as SingNet or Pacific Internet, personal free accounts obtained from providers such as Google and Yahoo, or accounts issued by MINDEF, schools and their employers.  In fact, many Singaporeans have more than one email account.

 

     Does the Government need to compete against existing service providers in issuing and maintaining email accounts for every Singaporean?   To do so, the Government will need to expend additional resources in hardware, software and manpower. 

 

    The case supporting the potential benefit from such free email accounts is not strong.  From focus group discussions conducted by IDA, individuals have generally expressed their preference to use their existing personal email accounts rather than a new Government-issued email account.  This could be because many people already have a problem managing multiple accounts for both personal use and their work.

 

    Process-wise, we cannot assume that every Singaporean will diligently access the Government issued email accounts to maintain direct communications with the Government agencies, such as the IRAS or CPF.  This means that the Government will still need to continue with its current efforts to communicate with Singaporeans through a variety of media and also private communication channels.  Hence, while email communication may be instantaneous and technically efficient, in practice, taking such an approach may not significantly affect public communications between the Government and the people.

 

    Nevertheless, Government will continue to evaluate the various ways through which we can communicate more effectively with Singaporeans, taking into consideration practicality, cost effectiveness and customers' usage patterns and preferences.

     Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong:  Sir, a supplementary question for the Minister regarding the ultra high-speed broadband service.  What is the estimated speed that we are looking at and at what cost?  Also, will consumers be tied down with two to three years' contract with the service providers once the system is up, like what we are experiencing now?  Consumers are all tied down?  Are we expected to be tied down?

     Dr Lee Boon Yang: Mr Speaker, Sir, with regard to the next generation infocomm infrastructure, which the Prime Minister announced in his Budget Statement, I seek the Member's patience.  As the Prime Minister has said, the details will be announced during the Committee of Supply.

 

     On his other point about consumers being tied down to the various packages, I do not think that is an accurate reflection.  Today, there are many different packages being offered by Internet service providers.  The consumers have a wide range of choices.  It is really up to them to choose a package that they feel is appropriate or suitable for their needs.  Today, we have dial-up packages that go for as low as only $5.20 per month.  And for broadband, we have packages that are as low as $19.90 per month, with limits to the data that can be accessed or downloaded.  There are also daily plans going for as low as below $3 a day.  In fact, the consumers do have a range of choices.  Each comes with its own terms and conditions.  So the Member cannot say that consumers are tied down.  Having decided


to adopt a particular plan, you go along with the conditions that you have agreed to.  But it is not as if all service providers (or ISPs) are providing only fixed plans, minimum three or five years.  There is a whole range of options available to Internet users and it is really up to them to choose the plan that is most appropriate to their requirements and needs.

     Mr Speaker:  Last question for you, Mr Chia.

     Mr Steve Chia Kiah Hong:  Thank you, Sir.  My experience and research tell me that that is not the case.  We are all tied down, depending on the terms of contract for the services.  If you do not accept the terms of contract by the service provider, you have to pay a very high price.  That is why I terminated my Singnet account because they refused to give me a monthly contract but a minimum contract of one year, which I disagreed.  So we are being tied down.  That is something that I want to clarify with the Minister.

     Dr Lee Boon Yang:  Mr Speaker, Sir, the Member's personal experience does not nullify my point, which is that there is a whole range of service packages available. It is up to you to choose.  If you want a daily package, some companies will offer you a daily package.  If you want a monthly package, they will do so.  If you want a monthly package for one year, two or three years, they will offer it to you.  So it is up to you to decide which one you want.

     Mr Speaker:  End of Question time.

Overall, the number of service points has not changed significantly under the two systems.  There were 98 fixed service points under the previous system compared to 97 fixed service points today, comprising NPCs and NPPs.

      Members would like to note too that many of MFA's visiting delegations were also hosted to exhibitions, performances and exposed to our local culture.  During AMED last June, we took the opportunity to introduce our Middle East and other Asian guests to our prized local dishes like laksa, satay and chicken rice, and also embraced them in song and dance at the Malay Heritage Centre, complete with Yemeni-Arab music, which they greatly appreciated.  Recently, a Chinese delegation was hosted to the "Nutcracker" put up by the Singapore Dance Theatre at the Esplanade.  And during my visit to Indonesia last December, I was also able to establish links with South Sulawesi for cooperation between the Malay Heritage Centre and the Bugis connections in Makassar.  Where appropriate, we also showcase exhibitions by our local artists at our Singapore Art Museum, and/or the exhibitions at the Asian Civilisation Museum and our cultural heritage centres.  Active cultural diplomacy efforts by MFA are indeed on-going.

 

       According to MICA, which leads our international cultural cooperation efforts, seven G-G Cultural Agreements and six Agency-Agency Cultural Agreements have also been signed with countries such as China, India, South Korea, Jordan, Germany and Ireland over the past five years.  Our agencies, and through them our arts groups and companies, are encouraged to pursue whatever arrangement necessary to achieve our goals of internationalising our arts.  MFA will continue to assist our agencies in pursuing international cultural exchanges and in bringing Singapore culture to a wider international audience.

 

     In a wider context, I would also like to raise this question of inter-faith dialogue.  On the Prophet caricature issue, I think we all realise that religion plays an important part of living culture, so inter-faith dialogue is also important to our role.  The Prophet caricature issue has reminded us how sensitive religious divisions can be.  The Internet had quickly propagated what started as an issue in Europe to Southeast Asia as well.  Both President Yudhoyono and Prime Minister Badawi had to talk about it because their Muslim grounds had been aroused.  In ASEAN, we must not allow similar misunderstandings between Muslims and non-Muslims to surface, worse, to divide us.

 

      It is therefore in everyone's interest to have frequent, open and constructive dialogues involving key stakeholders, to address concerns and misperceptions.  This will help to defuse potential conflict amongst the different religions.  Singapore can play its part in promoting such inter-faith dialogue.  Earlier this year, the first Arab-Muslim Israeli Ambassador visited Singapore.  We would receive Cardinal Renato Raffaele Martino for the 25th Anniversary of Singapore-Holy See relations in June this year.  These are occasions where inter-faith dialogues could be organised.  It is educational for us, and we could benefit from the experience and wisdom of these personalities.  At the same time, we can contribute and share with them our own experiences.  These could be small but first steps towards eventually having a wider-based inter-faith dialogue forum in Singapore, a venue where different faiths could come together in open and constructive dialogue.

 

      I would also like to add that this concept of an inter-faith dialogue venue relates back to the fundamentals of Singapore's existence.  As a small country with no economic hinterland, our survival depends on us being able to remain relevant to the world.  Our foreign policy is designed to support this approach.  Singapore is now a key transport and logistics hub with efficient road networks, seaport and airport, as well as extensive air and sea links.  We are a political hub by virtue of being part of Southeast Asia that forms the core of key regional architectures

comprising APEC, ASEAN+3, ARF and EAS, and which is linked to other regions through ASEM, FEALAC and AMED.  You have earlier heard Minister George Yeo say that the region is at a political crossroads between two great civilisations, ie, China and India.  We are developing links with these countries because that is the way we want to encourage their presence in the region, just as with the US.  Economically, Singapore and the region are also actively developing ties with other countries and regions through FTAs and OSAs.  Singapore's role as a multi-dimensional global venue, or hub, will further contribute towards the region's engagement with major global players.
