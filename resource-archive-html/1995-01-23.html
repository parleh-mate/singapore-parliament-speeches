PUBLIC UTILITIES BOARD
(Termination of gas supply to older private housing estates)


    2. Mr Goh Choon Kang asked the Minister for Trade and Industry why the Public Utilities Board has decided to terminate the gas supply to older private housing estates and how many estates are affected.

     Mr Goh Chee Wee (for the Minister for Trade and Industry): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Public Utilities Board (PUB) will be terminating piped gas supply to 40 low-rise estates affecting 15,970 households over the next two to three years.

    Gas pipes in these estates were laid between 22 and 40 years ago. Over the years, house owners have built extensions in their backyards over the run of these gas pipes, thereby rendering maintenance of gas pipes difficult, if not impossible. These gas pipes could be corroded and the risk of gas leak would increase as the pipes get older. The leakage of gas could accumulate in the voids beneath the house extension and accumulate into an explosive mixture. Hence, for safety reasons, these pipes have to be replaced.

    However, the cost of re-laying new pipes is very high and is not economically viable in view of the small number of households which use PUB piped gas in these estates. Although all housing units in the 40 affected estates are served by the PUB gas network, only 46% of them use PUB gas. It would not be justifiable for PUB to expend large sums of public fund when the majority of residents are not using PUB gas but bottled LPG.

    Those households using PUB gas now would not be unduly inconvenienced as they can switch to LPG which is readily available and competitively priced. However, if the affected households wish to continue with PUB gas supply, they could collectively, within each estate, make a capital contribution towards part of the cost of laying the new gas pipes in

     Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee: Sir, speed detection cameras are a means to curb and deter speeding. Their mere presence does not render a road suitable for its speed limit to be raised.     Recently, separate from the question of speed detection cameras, there have been suggestions in the press that existing speed limits be raised. Others, however, have argued that the present speed limits are appropriate and should remain.

    The departments concerned, ie, Traffic Police, PWD and ROV, take great care in recommending speed limits for our roads. The maximum speed we can allow on a particular road would depend on all the factors that affect traffic safety, ie, the physical nature of the road itself such as its width and existence of bends, presence of merging lanes, slip roads and centre dividers, the general traffic volume of different classes of road users and vehicles and also, in general, the behaviour of road users including the level of road courtesy.

    In the past, Singapore had only two speed limits - 50 km per hour for most roads and 70 km per hour for the larger and wider roads. 80 km per hour was introduced when expressways were constructed. At some portions of expressways, the speed limit is lower (70 km per hour) for safety reasons, eg, in the Central Expressway tunnel. These speed limits are reviewed periodically. Based on the above factors, setting appropriate speed limits is a matter of judgment. The

Column: 1003

professionals have, all things considered, advised retaining the present speed limits. We should abide by their judgment.

    While some motorists, eg, those who drive higher capacity cars, may feel safe driving at speeds above the present speed limits, we must not forget that there are many other road users who drive smaller cars or who are relatively inexperienced. At higher speeds, they would have less margin for avoiding accidents. The Commander of Traffic Police informs me that high speed compounds the effect of other causes of accidents, such as skidding, failure to have proper control of vehicle, overtaking dangerously, changing lanes without consideration for other road users and tail-gating. At high speed, a driver is less able to have complete control of his vehicle; he also has less time to react to changing traffic conditions and potentially hazardous situations. An accident which occurs at high speed causes greater damage and injury. Death is also more likely in high speed accidents.

Their figures show that when some 38 states raised their speed limits from 55 miles per hour to 65 miles per hour, deaths rocketed by 34% compared with the previous four-year period.
