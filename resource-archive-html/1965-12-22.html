TEACHING SERVICE SALARY STRUCTURE
RE-INTRODUCTION OF ONE PRINCIPAL ONE SESSION


    1. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Education whether and when his Ministry will commence negotiations with the Singapore Teachers' Union on claims submitted during 1961 relating to (a) anomalies in the salary structure in the Teaching Service between the normal-trained,the old certificated-trained and the new certificated-trained teachers and the graduates; and (b) the re-introduction of the one Principal one session school system as was in practice before.

    The Minister of State for Education (Inche A. Rahim Ishak) (for the Minister for Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, the existing salary structure for the Education Service is based on the scheme of service for the teaching profession which was incorporated in the Scheme of Higher Services, 1953, and implemented with effect from 1.1.50. In the course of development of education in Singapore, several of the salary scales were modified and new salary scales introduced from time to time to meet changing circumstances.

The consolidation of salaries in 1952, the Ritsonisation in 1953, the general revision of salaries for teachers and other groups of public servants undertaken in 1958, have all had their effects on the salary structure. And the Ministry of Education is aware of certain anomalies therein. So with a view to regularising the salary structure which might have become administratively cumbersome, the Ministry of Education has proposed a revised salary structure for the consideration of the Salaries Commission. It is understood that the Teachers' Unions have similarly made recommendations to the Commission on the scales and salary structure. The Ministry of Finance has advised the Ministry of Education that matters relating to salary scales should be referred to the Salaries Commission to be resolved by them. In the circumstances, any negotiations on the salary structure of the teaching service must await the outcome of the deliberations of the Salaries Commission.

    Regarding the second part of that question, the re-introduction of one Principal for one session, the Ministry of Education is aware that in postwar years, there arose the need to build more schools and later to use schools as two-session schools to cope with the increase of the student population of Singapore. At that time, the accent was on primary education. From about 1953 to 1959, if primary schools were used as two-session schools, there were two Principals. In 1960, to cope with the new policy of providing education for even larger numbers of children of school-going age, it was decided that more schools would be used as two-session schools. The move to put one Principal in charge of a school of two sessions was part of the effort of providing the largest number of children of school-going age with an education on an economic budget, coupled with the fact that it was considered then that there were not enough teachers with the requisite experience to discharge adequately the duties and responsibilities of Principals. There are certain administrative disadvantages in having one Principal per session as questioned by the Member for Bras Basah. Morning

Column: 425

and afternoon schools will be separate entities, each with its own Principal, staffed with its own teachers, accounts, sports equipment, teaching aids, school functions, and so on. But the building, playing field, classrooms, furniture, storage space, school servants and watchmen, will have to be shared by both schools, morning and afternoon. Now this leads often to misunderstanding and petty bickering. In particular, it is difficult for school servants and watchmen to work under two independent Principals. The annual interchange of morning and afternoon staff will also present some difficulty if the two Principals are at loggerheads. All in all, administration with two Principals will be more cumbersome than having one overall head.

    To assist the one Principal who is responsible for the two sessions, there are senior assistants provided at the rate of one senior assistant for every 15 classes, taking both sessions into account. The senior assistants do an average of 10 hours 31 minutes of actual teaching a week and assist Principals with the administrative work on an average of 8 hours 17 minutes per week, which assistance is confined mostly to paper work and not to supervision of the staff, the standard and the discipline of the school. This shows that the senior assistant is only effective as a teacher for 10 hours and 31 minutes per week compared with the average for teachers, other than senior assistants, of 18 hours and 2 minutes per week. Senior assistants do not receive any extra remuneration, but they are afforded time off from teaching duties, and the administrative experience which they gain as senior assistants invariably stands them in good stead for promotion purposes.

    The idea of one Principal for one session has been carefully explored and consideration has also been given to the idea of paying some form of remuneration to senior assistants vis-a-vis Vice-Principals. Certain proposals have been put up for the consideration of the Salaries Commission. MEDICAL FACILITIES FOR
AIDED SCHOOL TEACHERS


    2. Mr Ho See Beng asked the Minister for Education whether teachers in the aided schools are not provided with similar medical facilities as their counterparts in the Government Service, although they perform identical duties, and why.

    Inche Rahim Ishak: Mr Speaker, Sir, the Singapore Teachers' Union, the Singapore Chinese Teachers' Union and the Malayan Christian Schools Council have repeatedly asked that the same medical benefits and facilities that are accorded to Government teachers should be extended to teachers of aided schools. Such facilities, if granted, will, of course, apply to the other employees of aided schools, such as clerks, servants, watchmen and so on. The Unions make much of Government's policy of equal pay for equal work, and point out that in most cases of negotiations, disputes and arbitration outside the civil service, medical facilities and privileges are always enforced. While free medical treatment is accorded to all members of public and aided school teachers at all Government hospitals and clinics, the Unions are concerned mainly with the hospitalisation of members and the consequent ward charges, X-ray charges, specialist fees and so on if they go to a paying ward, i.e., First Class or Second Class. The main difficulty in according the same medical privileges and benefits enjoyed by Government teachers, school servants and so on of Government-aided schools is due to the fact that aided school teachers and servants are strictly not Government servants, but employees of aided school management committees. As such, privileges and benefits, if accorded to employees of aided schools, may have to be extended to other bodies of employees.

    The suggestion put by circular to the management committees of aided schools that they should pay the full charges for medical treatment and hospitalisation at public rates in respect of their staff and seek reimbursement from such staff

Column: 429

at rates charged for Government service, is not acceptable to the management committees, and this they have informed us. We are now examining alternative possibilities like the introduction of a medical benefit insurance scheme operated commercially with regard to the provision of medical facilities. Such a scheme would probably involve a certain amount of contributions either from the aided school employees or the management committees.

    In most statutes, there is a section which sets out the meanings which are to be attached to the particular words used in that statute. Such a section, the Interpretation Section, supplements for each particular statute the functions performed for all statutes by an Interpretation Ordinance or Act. The existing statute relating to the interpretation of certain expressions used in our laws is the Interpretation and General Clauses Ordinance and Chapter 2 of the 1955 Edition of the Laws of Singapore.

    The Interpretation Bill now under consideration by the House re-enacts the provisions of the existing Interpretation and General Clauses Ordinance making such amendments as are necessitated by Singapore's status as an Independent Republic together with certain other amendments considered desirable.

    In general, it is the object of this Bill, like all enactments relating to interpretation, to shorten the language of the statutes and to provide in our legislation uniformity of expression, thus avoiding superfluity of language. Within the compass of a single Act are

Column: 457
