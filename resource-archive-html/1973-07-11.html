AUSTRALIAN MILITARY FORCES IN SINGAPORE
(Withdrawal)


    1. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs whether the Singapore Government has been consulted on the Australian Government's intention to withdraw its military forces stationed in this region under the ANZUK arrangement; what is the view of the Government in relation to the statements by the Australian Government over this question; and whether by the withdrawal of the Australian military forces the security and stability of this region will be affected in any way.

    The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Mr S. Rajaratnam): Mr Speaker, Sir, there were discussions between the Singapore and the Australian Governments prior to the recent announcement by the Australian Government of its decision to withdraw by the end of February next year all but 600 Australian troops based in Singapore. However, by April 1975 all but 150 of these 600 men would be withdrawn. The decision by the Australian Government has been as expected. The Australian Government has said that after April 1975 the remaining 150 men would help to facilitate programmes of combined exercises and to support those engaged in such projects.

    The Australian decision will affect the present Five-Power Defence Arrangements, hut the usefulness of the defence arrangements remains because of the contributions which the other partners will continue to make. The Prime Minister of New Zealand, in a statement on 4th July, has reiterated that there is no question of New Zealand forces being withdrawn as long as Singapore wants them. The United Kingdom similarly will continue to maintain forces at their present level. This being the case, the Singapore Government is of the view that the

Column: 1118

Five-Power Defence Arrangements can make useful contributions towards peace and stability in the area.

AUSTRALIAN MILITARY FORCES IN SINGAPORE
(Setting up of espionage unit)


    2. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs whether the Government is aware of the fact that a so-called military electronic espionage unit had been set up by the Australian military forces in Singapore; and what steps the Singapore Government proposes to take to ensure that no such unit may be allowed to be set up here and furthermore that our non-aligned policy is not prejudiced.

    Mr Rajaratnam: Mr Speaker, Sir, the Singapore Government does not regard the Australian Radio Intelligence Unit in Singapore as an espionage unit set up by the military services to undermine and/or subvert the security of Singapore and its neighbours. It is a normal practice of military establishments of all countries to maintain radio intelligence units to serve their defence requirements. In view of this, I see no grounds for concern that our non-aligned policy has been prejudiced in any way.

SPECIAL BUS SERVICES FOR SCHOOL CHILDREN


    3. Mr P. Govindaswamy asked the Minister for Communications if he will look into the feasibility of the bus operators providing some buses for school children only on their bus routes, particularly at the end of the afternoon school sessions.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Yong Nyuk Lin): Mr Speaker, Sir, as suggested by the Member for Anson, I shall ask the Bus Services Licensing Authority and the Singapore Bus Service Ltd, to look into the feasibility of providing special bus services for school children, at the end of the afternoon school sessions.

    It is necessary, however, for me to explain that the public bus service is not the only available means of transport

Column: 1119

for school children. There is in existence a separate school bus service, the expansion of which was one of the recommendations contained in the White Paper on Reorganisation of the Motor Transport Service of Singapore (Paper Cmd 21 of 1970).

    Out of an original fleet of 149 buses, it was hoped that by the end of the three-year Plan, i.e. June 1973, there would be in existence a total fleet of 600 buses. LAND TITLES (AMENDMENT) BILL

    The Minister for Law and National Development (Mr E. W. Barker): Mr. Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The amendments proposed in the Bill are designed to make the legislation more effective and to meet present day requirements in the light of rapid building development in the Republic.

    Under the Bill, owners of lands with outstanding mortgages may surrender their lands for the re-issue of new titles without having to discharge the mortgages at the time of surrender, and new titles when re-issued may be endorsed with any outstanding mortgages. The Bill also enables the Registrar to direct by various methods that lands approved for erection of buildings (intended to be dealt with in parts) may be brought under the Torrens system prior to the granting of subdivisional approval of the buildings.

    The Bill also gives additional powers to the Registrar to enter cautions on certificates of title issued for lands which have not yet been surveyed up to the standard of government requirements and to call a party to an instrument to appear before him for purposes of verifying the party's identity where the party is not represented by a lawyer.

    The Bill also provides that any proprietor of land who has altered his name may execute instruments under his new name different from that in the certificate of title and that an amended caveat will be given priority as soon as it is received in the Registry, and not on the date of its notification on the relevant certificate of title.

    Sir, the Department of Statistics has been collecting passenger statistics by sea under the Migration Statistics Rules. Passenger statistics by air, however, are compiled from the passenger manifests submitted by airlines to the Department of Civil Aviation.

    The Migration Statistics Rules were enacted in 1930 and over the passage of time returns drawn up under the

Column: 1140

Rules are outdated in a number of aspects, such as in the terminology used and the scope and coverage defined. There is, therefore, a need to revise the returns and the notes contained therein from time to time. This in turn would need prior amendments to be made to the Rules. In order, therefore, to avoid the necessity of having to amend the Rules each time, the Schedules under which the statistics are collected are revised, it is proposed to collect migration statistics under the powers contained in the Statistics Act itself. This is possible as migration is included in the Schedule of subject matters covered by the Act. The Migration Statistics Rules can, therefore, be revoked. Collection of statistics under the Statistics Act would enable administrative flexibility to vary the format, scope and coverage of returns as and when circumstances arise, without having to resort to changes in subsidiary legislation.
