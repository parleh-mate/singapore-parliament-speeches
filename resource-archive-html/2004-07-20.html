     Dr Vivian Balakrishnan:  Many MPs have filed questions on the recent power failures. The power failure on 29th June, in particular, caused great inconvenience to many Singaporeans and harmed our international reputation. The Economic Development Board (EDB) has reported serious negative comments from our existing and potential investors. We take a very serious view of such power failures, and will do everything possible to minimise the possibility of their recurrence.


     Mr Deputy Speaker, power failures can occur because of two major factors: first, due to a disruption of the generating facilities; and second, due to a disruption at the transmission or distribution grid level.  Let me deal with each of these two scenarios in turn.


     The power failure on 29th June 2004 was caused by a disruption in the gas supply from the Natuna gas pipeline.  This led to a sudden loss of about 30% of online generation capacity and a blackout that lasted up to one hour and 50 minutes. 300,000 customer accounts were affected. The supply disruption was traced to a defective pressure regulating valve in the onshore gas receiving facility (or ORF) operated by ConocoPhillips at Jurong Island. The valve malfunctioned because the solenoid, which is an electrical device that controls its movements, was water-logged and short circuited.

 

     The pressure regulating valve first malfunctioned at 9.56 pm that night, and this was followed eight minutes later by a total closure of the emergency shutdown valve at 10.04 pm. The generation companies were alerted at their premises that

the gas pressure at the ORF was dropping. When the emergency shutdown valve closes, the gas plants are supposed to "hotswitch" to their backup fuel (in this case, diesel), without affecting the continuity of electricity supply. Unfortunately, on that night, only one of the six gas plants in operation successfully made that  "hotswitch".  Two gas plants failed because of a problem with their diesel filter.  And the other three ran out of gas before completing the "hotswitch" process. This resulted in the widespread power blackout that occurred at about 10.10 pm that night.


     Let me just go through some of the findings and the immediate follow-up actions that the Energy Market Authority (EMA) has been engaged in since.  As the emergency shutdown valve was not initially triggered, it was not immediately obvious at that point in time that "hotswitching" should have been commenced straightaway since, in such circumstances, some mild pressure fluctuations are part and parcel of a normal system.  However, the unanticipated abnormal behaviour of the valve system at the ORF misled the operators that night. In retrospect, we now know that critical time was lost arising from this. EMA has instructed the generation companies to change their standard operating procedures so that they "hotswitch" earlier in the event of any pressure drop. The generation companies are also exploring ways to shorten the "hotswitching" process further.  In addition, they have also stepped up maintenance of the diesel pump system, and will do "hotswitch" drills more frequently.  I want to emphasise that we are talking about a response that takes a few minutes.  They have to make this "hotswitch" between four and 12 minutes.  And in the case of the failure that night, events occurred

     Let me move on now to the valve system at the ConocoPhillips facility.  This system, unfortunately, has failed on five separate occasions for five different reasons since August 2002. I think Members of this House will agree that this is clearly not acceptable. EMA has instructed ConocoPhillips to do a comprehensive technical re-evaluation of its system design and operations, including maintenance and operational procedures. It must submit its report to EMA within three months. Furthermore, EMA will bring ConocoPhillips under licensing control by the end of the year.

 

     I would like to address concerns expressed by many that we are apparently relying too much on a single source of fuel. Today, we have a good balance of steam plants using fuel oil and gas plants generating electricity. About 60% of our installed capacity consists of steam plants and 40% comes from gas plants. Fuel oil for the steam plants is available from multiple sources. For gas plants, the backup is diesel, which is also widely available. Every power station is required to stockpile at least 90 days of fuel reserves, with at least 45 days worth of these reserves being kept on site. Overall, our total supply capacity is almost twice our peak demand. In short, we can actually operate without gas indefinitely if we have to, albeit at a slightly higher price.

 

     EMA is exploring with the generation companies how to back-up gas from dual sources for operational continuity. These dual pipeline feeds will give power stations those precious extra minutes to make a successful "hotswitch".  In addition, EMA is also studying the feasibility of having compressed gas storage. This would, in effect, provide another additional source of redundancy.  Over the longer term, we are also

     Let me now touch on the second group of power failures due to grid infrastructure and accidents at worksites.  Other power failures may be caused by faults within the grid infrastructure or damage to electricity cables.  For instance, on 13th April 2004, a cable fault developed at the Choa Chu Kang substation, which caused a blackout that lasted about 60 minutes and affected 80,000 customer accounts. On 2nd July 2004, electricity supply to 28 HDB blocks at Pasir Ris was interrupted for about 30 minutes, and this was due to a tripping of substation equipment during a testing of the remote control equipment.

 

     Our power infrastructure must have top class standards of reliability. This requires continual investment and maintenance of equipment. EMA will be imposing higher standards on all the companies involved in this entire system.  These companies will have to meet these stringent standards or face financial penalties of up to $2 million for each performance failure. These changes will take effect from 1st August 2004. EMA will release details of these shortly.

 

     Dr Warren Lee has asked how often construction accidents caused damage to major electrical cables. In 2003, there were 15 such incidents. Since 2001, 21 contractors have been brought to court, of which 12 have been fined and nine cases are pending. Errant contractors found guilty by the court can be fined up to $1 million.

     I will answer MP Mr Low Thia Khiang and NMP Mr Steve Chia's questions on electricity tariffs together.

 

     Approximately 60% of our electricity supply is generated from gas plants, with the remaining 40% from oil-fired steam plants*. The power plants do not usually run on diesel fuel. Diesel is only used as an emergency back-up. Fuel costs account for about half of the cost of generating electricity. Consequently, changes in fuel costs will affect electricity tariffs because we do not subsidise electricity.

     The price of our natural gas is pegged to fuel oil prices under long term contracts. This follows international practice in this part of the world. Hence, when oil prices increase, gas prices also increase and electricity tariffs will have to increase.

 

     Over the last one year, households have experienced both increases and decreases in tariff rates. Rates today are 1.3% lower compared to the same quarter (ie, July-September 2003) last year, even though oil prices have gone up by 5% in July 2004 compared with July 2003. Overall, tariffs will move in line with changes in fuel costs.

     The gas plants have the capability to switch to diesel, without turning off the turbines. This allows them to "hotswitch" seamlessly to an alternative fuel in the event of a gas disruption, without cutting electricity supply. The switching takes time to execute and has to be done before the gas in the pipelines runs out. The residual gas in the pipeline typically runs out in about 10 minutes, depending on the physical location of the shutdown and the rate at which gas is being drawn.

 

     If the gas runs out before the plants successfully hotswitch, the gas turbines would trip and would take about one and a half hours to restart with diesel. This will lead to a temporary loss of power and cause a blackout. Power restoration takes time as supply and demand have to be kept in balance at all times. The power stations are also required to stockpile at least 90 days worth of fuel reserves in the event of supply disruption, with 45 days storage on site.
