ESTATE DUTY (INCREASE OF RATES) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

    The Financial Secretary (Mr T. M. Hart): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The object of this Bill is to substitute a new scale of estate duty for the scale contained in the Second Schedule to the Estate Duty Ordinance, Chapter 227, as amended by the Estate Duties (Increase of Rates) Ordinance, 1946.

    I have already explained the reasons why it is considered desirable to introduce this new scale, and I do not think that I need say anything further in this connection.

    There are three main points about the new scale to which I invite attention. It exempts estates of under $10,000 from duty. It makes use of a new method of calculating duty, and of course, it increases the rate of duty on estates of over $10,000. Hitherto, only estates of under $1,000 have been exempted from payment of duty. This level of exemption is low. A considerable number of estates fall within the limits of $1,000 to $10,000, perhaps rather more than a third of all estates assessed for duty in a normal year. On the other hand, the amount of duty collected from these estates is small, and in no way commensurate with the time and labour involved in collecting it. For example, the 400 estates within these limits, which were assessed in 1954 yielded no more than $23,000. The staff of the Estate Duty Office is not large, and clearly the time and labour spent on assessing these small estates could, with much greater advantage, be devoted to larger estates. In the circumstances, it has been decided to raise the level of exemption to $10,000.

Column: 1080



    The same form of graduated scale as is used in income tax has been adopted. This gives a smooth curve in the progression in the graduations of duty, avoids steps and obviates the need for special provisions for marginal relief. The first two characteristics will be seen from an examination of the comparative table attached to the Bill. Examples will illustrate the third characteristic. The existing rate of duty on an estate of exactly $1 million is 23 per cent. The rate of duty on an estate exceeding $1 million but less than $1.2 million is 25 per cent. Duty on an estate of exactly $1 million is $230,000. Duty on an estate of $1,000,001 is $250,000.25. In other words, one dollar makes a difference of $20,000 in duty. Therefore, in this particular section of the existing scale, it is well worthwhile to surrender estate up to about $26,000 in order to retain the lower rate of duty. For example, the duty on an estate of $1,025,000 at 25 per cent is $256,250. If the executor decides to surrender estate, he can pay 23 per cent on $1 million, which is $230,000, surrender $25,000 and still be better off by about $1,000 than he would have been if he had accepted taxation at 25 per cent. On the other hand, if the estate is $1,030,000, it pays to pay tax at 25 per cent. The new form of graduated scale avoids all these anomalies. The rate of tax rises smoothly without sudden jumps. For example, the duty on an estate of $1 million is $328,250. The duty on an estate of $1,000,001 is $328,250.50 and so on.

    The rates of duty proposed are generally higher than the existing rates. In this connection, attention is invited not to the Schedule but to the comparative table attached for information to the Bill. This table shows the effective rates under the new scale by comparison with the existing rates. The effective rate is the rate obtained after allowing for exemption on the first $10,000. For example, on an estate of $25,000 the first $10,000 would be exempt from tax and the next $15,000 would be taxed at 5 per cent. This would yield $750. The effect in rate of tax on this estate is therefore $750 over

$25,000 multiplied by 100 equals 3 per cent. It will be seen that the effective rate of the new scale diverges increasingly from the existing scale until, on estates of $5 millions, there is a difference of about 10 per cent.
