ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

NEWATER
(Education on acceptance)


    1. Mdm Ho Geok Choo asked the Minister for the Environment as the Prime Minister has exhorted us to be less reliant on Malaysia for our future water supply, one alternative source is NEWater where there is some public concern over its quality and hygiene standards of "real" cleanliness, what measures have his Ministry in mind to educate Singaporeans to accept NEWater.

    The Minister for the Environment (Mr Lim Swee Say): Mr Speaker, Sir, after two years of comprehensive physical, chemical and microbiological analyses of NEWater, the Panel of Experts formed

Column: 424

to study the quality of NEWater has submitted its report to the PUB on 2nd July 2002. The Panel has made public its findings on NEWater at a press conference held last week. Based on some 20,000 test results, the Panel of Experts has concluded that NEWater is safe for drinking. It has met or surpassed all the standards for drinking water set by the World Health Organisation and the US Environmental Protection Agency. In fact, it is even cleaner than PUB water. In line with current practice in the USA on potable use of reclaimed water, the Panel has recommended that NEWater be used for indirect potable use, that is, mixing NEWater with raw water in the reservoirs for further treatment to produce potable water.

    PUB is now studying the Experts Panel's report and findings and will make its recommendation to the Government in about two months' time.

    A summary of the Experts Panel's report will be made available on PUB's NEWater website to be launched on 1st August 2002.

    At the same time, to enhance public understanding of NEWater, PUB has stepped up its public education efforts and will continue to do so in the coming months. PUB has started briefing the grassroots and community leaders a few weeks ago. Our public education programme will cover various areas. For example, to assure the public that NEWater is safe to drink, PUB explains the technology employed in the production of NEWater as well as the quality it is able to attain with NEWater.

    At the same time, to help overcome any psychological barrier, PUB explains the experience of the USA in the use of reclaimed water over the last 20-30 years for indirect potable purpose. At two of the briefing sessions I attended personally, 98% of the grassroots and community leaders, having understood the process involved and the quality achieved in NEWater, indicated that they have no problem in drinking NEWater and accepting

Column: 425

the idea of indirect potable use of NEWater. The other 2% expressed that they still have some psychological barrier and that they would like to understand the process more before they are ready to fully embrace the idea.

    The PUB is now in the process of producing a documentary on NEWater. It will be telecast over the TV channels in August. PUB will make available VCD copies of the NEWater documentary to educational institutions and grassroots organisations.

    By early next year, the public can also come to PUB's new Visitors' Centre at Bedok Water Reclamation Plant to see the actual operations of the NEWater plant. They will be able to see at first hand the use of leading edge membrane technology and ultra-violet process in the production of NEWater.

    Through these efforts in public education, we are confident that Singaporeans will come to recognise that NEWater is indeed a safe, affordable and sustainable source of water supply for Singapore as we strive for greater self-sufficiency in the future.

    Mdm Ho Geok Choo (West Coast): Sir, in the US where water reclamation has been carried out for more than 20 years, only one or two of the three methods that Singapore is using had been used. Why is there a need for us to go through the three processes in Singapore's case?

    Mr Lim Swee Say: Sir, the USA started the indirect potable use of reclaimed water in the early 70s. In those days, some of these plants were using traditional filtration method. Over the years, with advances in membrane technology, we are now able to achieve better quality at a lower cost. Technology has now made the production of reclaimed water more efficient and cost-effective. In the USA today, indirect potable use of reclaimed water is a growing trend as more and more cities face the challenge of water shortage. Our understanding is that new water reclamation plants in the USA are increasingly using membrane technology.

    The three processes referred by Mdm Ho are what we call the dual-membrane and ultra-violet processes. This combination of processes is gaining popularity. New water reclamation plants in the USA are moving in this direction of adopting dual-membrane and ultra-violet processes. Earlier this year, our NEWater plant was awarded the excellent award by the National Water Research Institution in the USA because we have established a benchmark that is as best as anywhere you can find in the world in terms of our ability to make cost-effective use of technology to produce water quality of the highest standard.

    Mdm Ho Geok Choo: Sir, since one of the three methods was assessed to be sufficient to process the water, as in the US case, to what extent would the incremental value be in having all three processes done for Singapore's case?

    The second question is: would the additional two or three processes not be adding to the cost of processing the water and who would then be bearing this additional cost?

    Mr Lim Swee Say: Sir, firstly, let me explain that even though dual-membrane is a two-step process, it does not necessarily lead to higher cost. Let me explain. With reverse osmosis, we are going to achieve the highest quality of water. What this means is that before the water goes through reverse osmosis, it has to go through a stage of preparation first. The whole purpose of micro-filtration or ultra-filtration used in the first step of membrane application is to filter out bigger size impurities, for example, bacteria. By the time it comes to reverse osmosis, the process will be filtering out, for example, viruses which are much smaller than bacteria, as well as estrogenic compounds which are again much smaller than viruses and bacteria.

    Basically, the whole process is to remove impurities in a two-stage approach. In this way, not only will we be able to achieve higher quality water but, at the same time, we will be able to optimise the cost performance of dual membrane.

On the whole, it will lead to better cost optimisation.

    Secondly, Mdm Ho asked who will bear the additional cost. Sir, I want to emphasise that the introduction of NEWater does not add to higher cost of future water supply in Singapore. In fact, the whole purpose of introducing NEWater is to reduce the increased cost of future supply of water in Singapore. Members of the House will remember that until the concept of water reclamation came about, the next alternative for Singapore was really to go for desalination. I have mentioned in this House on 22nd May 2002 that when you compare the cost of water reclamation - NEWater versus desalination - in terms of capital cost, the cost for NEWater is about half that of desalination. In terms of energy consumption, it is about one-quarter to one-fifth that of desalination. As a result, the total cost of NEWater production is about half that of desalination. In other words, the introduction of NEWater actually helps us to prevent the need to increase the prices of water in the future because with this lower cost alternative, we are now able to better contain the cost of water in the future.

Based on PUB's recommendation, the ENV will then work out the introduction plan. At this moment, I would not want to jump the gun because the next step is for PUB to study the recommendation from the Experts Panel carefully so that by the time it comes to a decision, we will be able to move forward with complete confidence.

    4. Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for Manpower (a) what are the average monthly earnings of resident females from 1997 - 2001; and (b) whether the wage gap between men and women is getting narrower.

    The Minister for Manpower (Dr Lee Boon Yang): Mr Speaker, Sir, based on the Central Provident Fund Board's records, the average monthly earnings of females increased from $2,256 in 1998 to $2,588 in 2001, or at a rate of 4.7% per annum during the period. After discounting for

inflation, the real earnings rose from $2,263 to $2,535 at an annualised rate of 3.9% during the same period. Earnings data prior to 1998 are not comparable due to a change in the collection methodology. The hon. Member asked for data from 1997. Because of the change in collection methodology, I gave the figures for 1998 onwards.

    Sir, females in Singapore generally earn less than males at the aggregate level. This is not unlike the experience in many other countries. In 2001, the average monthly earnings of females was 72% that of males. This reflects the higher concentration of females in lower paying occupations due to the poorer educational attainment of the older cohorts of female workers. The tendency for females to withdraw from the workforce because of childcare and household responsibilities also reduced the average years of service and experience of working females. With our seniority-based wage system, females, as a group, will therefore earn less than males. For the younger age group, the wage gap differential is generally smaller. In fact, in the age group of 25 to 29, women working as managers, professionals, associate professionals and technicians, and service and sales workers actually earn more than their male counterparts. Other factors contributing to the gender wage disparity include differences in skills, qualifications, age, job nature and position.

    The wage gap was largely unchanged over the last few years. It was also 72% in 1998.

    Sir, the underlying concern in Dr Neo's question on wage gap is whether women in Singapore are discriminated against in employment. Average monthly earnings of women have significantly increased over the years. While differences in wages still exist, I would like to emphasise that it is not the result of discrimination at work. Rather, it is due to legitimate work-related reasons such as working experience, years of service, types of jobs held and skills, as I mentioned earlier.

In collective agreements signed between unions and employers, the principle of equal pay for work of equal value is the norm. In addition, the National Wages Council, which is the national tripartite advisory body on wages, formulates wage increase guidelines each year based on key economic indicators, such as GDP growth, productivity, inflation and international competitiveness. This has enabled Singapore workers, both men and women, to enjoy a fair share of the fruits of economic growth.

    Next, the CPF second charge on bank loans. Currently, for mortgage loans made by HDB, HDB has first charge, followed

    There will be no change to this arrangement when banks start selling mortgages for HDB flats, as far as the mortgagors are concerned. The banks will merely step into the shoes of HDB, and have first charge on the mortgage loans.

    For mortgages on private properties, however, where the banks have been the lenders, the arrangement has thus far been the opposite. In the event of a loan default, the CPF Board assumes priority charge over the mortgaged property, ahead of the banks. This means that, in the event of borrower default, the banks are unable to make effective use of the mortgaged property to mitigate their losses. This is because proceeds from the liquidation of the property will first have to be used to repay the CPF Board, ie, to repay the borrower's own CPF account. And as the loan matures and more of it is repaid through CPF withdrawals, the Board's claims increase correspondingly. The banks are entitled to a diminishing residual amount after the money has been returned to the buyers' CPF account. Hence, the more loan is repaid with CPF, the less satisfactory the bank's collateral position becomes. This is not sound for the banking system.

    So far this has not posed any major problems, because over the past decades, the property market has risen steadily and strongly, especially in the 80s and early 90s when the economy was booming. So long as property values continued to appreciate and few borrowers defaulted on their mortgages, there was less concern about the value of the collateral, and whether banks or the CPF ranked first in a default.

    Looking ahead, the property market is unlikely to be as buoyant as in the 80s

MCDS Delegated Schemes

Public Assistance (PA) Scheme

    Provides a monthly grant to financially distressed Singaporeans who by reason of age, illness, disability or unfavourable family circumstances, are unable to work and have no means of subsistence as well as no one to depend upon. Special Grant

    Similar to PA Scheme except that the Special Grant is meant to assist non-Singapore citizens.

Interim (Short-term) Financial Assistance Scheme [IFAS]

    Monthly grant is given to help applicants to tide over a difficult period of time. The rates are based on the quantum for Public Assistance but may vary depending on the needs of each case.

Rent & Utilities Assistance Scheme (RUAS)

    Provides short-term relief to Singapore Citizens and Permanent Residents staying in one, two- or three-room HDB rental flats who are genuinely unable to pay their rent/utilities/conservancy charges.
