Gradually in the late '60s and '70s, Government began to participate directly in industrial, commercial and financial activities. Through equity participation, the Government is now involved in numerous economic activities, such as banking, insurance, transportation, telecommunications, manufacturing, trading,

shipbuilding and repairing, printing and others. The main reason for the State taking on an entrepreneurial role was because entrepreneurs were slow to respond to Government's stimuli to participate in new ventures where the risks were high and a large capital was required.

    Government's omnipresence in business, however, has its drawbacks. It pre-empts the private sector from taking a more active role in our economic development. It deprives them of valuable opportunities to develop or expand a whole range of supportive services. It creates an impression in the private sector mind that the Government is a potential competitor to his business. This is unhealthy and could negate the efforts of the EDB. Let me relate a personal experience I encountered some time ago. I met a businessman who is a professional engineer and through his years of research and experiment has developed a product which I told him could qualify for Product Development Assistance. I asked him to see the EDB. His reply was, "No." He said the Government would steal away his idea. That would be the end of his business. This is the sort of impression created in the minds of some private sector businessmen about Government's involvement in business. I may be exaggerating but the Government must change this impression and change it fast.

    We are now entering a new economic era where most of our economic sectors have matured. It is perhaps timely now for Government to review its total involvement in business and to withdraw from those areas where it is no longer needed, and to let the private sector take the lead and play a more active role in our economic development. This will create opportunities for potential local entrepreneurs wanting to venture into business. We cannot continue to depend substantially on foreign investment for our growth. We have to rely more and more on our own resources. We have to develop a core of able local entrepreneurs who will help to create the necessary wealth. I propose that the Government looks into ways and means to encourage more people to come forward to become entrepreneurs.
