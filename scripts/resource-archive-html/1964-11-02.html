examples are Pakistan, Indonesia, Ghana, and one of the latest to submit an application for membership in this exclusive club is Melawi, There is no possibility, however, of an absolute autocracy in Singapore, because the crucial powers especially of defence and security rest on the Central Government which has no reason yet to abandon the parliamentary creed. But it is obvious that the trend in Singapore is towards autocracy as far as its limited power permits it. So for the moment we are at least in theory following a democratic parliamentary system.

    In such a system the Judiciary and the Legislature are expected to provide some measures of check against the tendency to absolutism by the Government. What will happen to the citizens of the land if they cannot challenge the excesses of Government in an independent Judiciary? What will happen to our democracy if the representatives of the people cannot voice their opinions against the repressive laws passed in the Legislature? In order, Sir, for the Judiciary to discharge its role as a pillar in the democratic framework, it must be filled by fearless men, by people who are prepared to throw in their resignation papers so that justice and the rule of law may be upheld. Politicians come and go, but the Judiciary, being a permanent institution, should be like Raffles Lighthouse, no matter what the tide.

    In order for the Legislature to be able to voice public opinion - and public opinion is the cornerstone of democracy - it should hold meetings regularly with fixed dates and not convene only when the Government is required to do so under the Constitution because certain acts require the assent of the Legislature. The Speaker, who is the guardian of the Legislature, has therefore an important function to play, more so as ours is not a normal Legislature. The Speaker has no voting rights. He possesses no right to participate in the debates but he acts as the guardian of the Legislature. On vital issues his decisions can be of great consequence.

The Speaker's responsibilities are, to my knowledge, twofold. The public sees him only in his manifest duties when he chairs the Assembly debates in open Chamber. Here, Sir, the public gallery is the final judge of the Speaker's ade quacies and his fair-mindedness. What deliberations are made behind those velvet curtains could sometimes have more consequence on the survival of the democratic system than what happens in open Chamber. For the system to survive, not only should we have an independent Judiciary but we should also have an effective Legislature including a Speaker who is prepared to uphold the parliamentary system and allow parliamentary practice to guide his decisions, rather than political expediency of the majority Party which selects him for the post.

The Estimates before the House and the review I am proposing to make in presenting these Estimates may be of some interest. We have completed a full year of confrontation which has brought about strains and stresses in our economy. Last year I presented a forecast of possible economic consequences of confrontation, and this year, it is possible to compare the outcome with that prediction.

    Indonesian economic confrontation is intended to deny Malaysia and in particular Singapore supplies of raw

materials, such as rubber, copra, timber and other tropical produce which Indonesia used to market through our Malaysian ports. Singapore handled more than 80 per cent of the Indonesian entrepot trade marketed through Malaysia. Indonesia also closed her markets to our exports and re-exports of food, manufacturers and consumer goods. How have we fared during this period?

    The first observation that I want to make is that the trade embargo which Indonesia intended to mount against us had never been at any time 100 per cent effective. In the case of trade carried on through Letters of Credit, the Indonesian authorities were compelled to acknowledge the realities of her economic situation as tens of thousands of tons of low-grade rubber piled up in the ports along the coasts of Sumatra and South Kalimantan. Eventually they had to authorise the exports of these goods to certain ports in Asia with the full knowledge that these products will be transhipped to Singapore. I understand that the export licences for these goods were personally authorised by no less than President Sukarno himself. So, as a result of this leakage, Singapore was able to recover some proportion of trade which she lost after last September. I also understand that the Indonesian authorities have again clamped down on exports through third countries and we shall wait and see what they will do when their ports get cluttered up with unsaleable rubber.

    Then there is the barter trade which was carried on in small craft manned mainly by Indonesian traders and crews. After confrontation the volume of barter trade increased substantially. The Indo nesian authorities, the Navy, the Customs, the Exchange Control, and other departments proved either unwilling or unable to prevent the movement of tropical produce from Rhio and the Sumatra ports to Singapore. The level of efficiency in their economic operations is no better than the military efficiency which they have mounted small-scale invasions on the Malayan coast. With the sales of such produce the Indonesian traders were able to buy food, provisions, textiles and house-hold

goods which they cannot get from other sources.

This trade had been used for the purposes of smuggling explosives and arms as well as for the infiltration of agents. The ban carried out by our own navy, police and customs was completely effective, unlike the futility of controls mounted by the Indonesian authorities. So today no barter trade is going on in Singapore.

    Published figures of our external trade and shipping do not include either third country imports or transactions in the barter trade.
