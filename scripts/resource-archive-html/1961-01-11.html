ANGKATAN REVOLUSI TENTERA ISLAM SINGAPURA
(Plot to precipitate clashes between Malays and Chinese)


Statement by the Minister for Culture


2.32 p.m.

    The Minister for Culture (Mr S. Rajaratnam): Mr Speaker, Sir, a few days ago a handful of desperadoes, from the basest of motives, managed to create among the Malay public an atmosphere of fear and tension with a view to precipitating clashes between Malays and Chinese on 6th January. But for the speedy action taken by the Government with the active assistance of members of the Muslim Advisory Board, Malay P.A.P. Assemblymen, U.M.N.O. leaders and Malay religious leaders, 6th January might have turned out to be a day of terror and mourning for many of us.

    If the vast majority of the non-Malay public was unaware of the mood of tension and fear that had been built up among the Malays, it was because the Government, with the help of Malay leaders, succeeded in preventing these anxieties from spreading among the non-Malays as well.

    Now that the danger has passed, we feel it our duty to tell the people of Singapore about how a handful of desperadoes plotted death for Malays and Chinese alike.

    The plot began on 14th December, 1960, with a meeting in Changi of a body calling itself the Angkatan Revolusi Tentera Islam Singapura or A.R.T.I.S. for short. The leader of this group was a man called Abdul Ghani Bin Hassan. It is worthwhile noting that he had been sentenced in 1949 to six years' imprisonment for robbery (though he only served three years). He was sentenced again in 1951 for two years for theft.

    The meeting was ostensibly called to discuss Malay rights, but a pamphlet distributed at the meeting made it clear that A.R.T.I.S. had more sinister motives - to provoke shedding of blood.

    Let me, Sir, quote a few passages from this inflammatory pamphlet:

    `This pamphlet is directed to my own race, the Malays, who are now fighting to realise their aspirations and rights - Singapore's independence that is untamed.

    Like the uncontrollable elements of the weather in the high seas, to be followed later by a calm, so will our fighting spirit finally emerge triumphant.'

It goes on to say:

    `History teaches us that the aims and aspirations for the race and country are achieved through the sacrifice of lives and property.'

    What is reprehensible about this pamphlet is not that it protests against the real or imagined grievances of the Malays. There is room in a democratic country for protest over grievances, whether real or imagined.

    But what is dangerous and unforgivable is the incitement to violence and hatred. Anyone, whether Malay or Chinese or Indian, who, in a multi-racial society, glorifies violence and preaches hatred and uses them as political fuel inevitably prepares the ground for racial carnage.

    `Fortunately these exhortations to violence alarmed many of the Malays who were present at the meeting and they did not therefore respond to the appeals of leaders of A.R.T.I.S. So nothing happened for a few days after this meeting.

    Then on Christmas Day, rumours began circulating to the effect that Malays would assemble on 6th January to protest

against the P.A.P. Government's alleged neglect of Malay interests and alleged favouring of Chinese.

    These and other rumours gained circulation very rapidly among the Malays. One of the rumours was that the Malays would march to the Istana Negara and that any attempt by Government to stop the procession would be answered by rioting. The Malays were also told to prepare for an alleged attack on them by Chinese on 6th January, which was a Friday.

    By 28th December, a new rumour was added - that a Malay member of the Singapore Work Brigade had been murdered. This was a complete lie but the fabricators of this and other rumours were deliberately out to create an atmosphere of panic among the Malays.

    So on 31st December, the Police raided a house at Fernhill Road and seized a quantity of stencilled posters. The posters were inflammatory in language and talked of violence and revolution if Singapore were not returned to the Malays. These anti-Government posters were scheduled to appear on 3rd January, as a prelude to racial disturbances.

    The posters were issued under the name of A.R.T.I.S. and so on the morning of 1st January, the Police arrested its president, Abdul Ghani. Under interrogation, he made a number of useful disclosures and admitted that he had a hand in preparing the posters. He also revealed plans by A.R.T.I.S. for engineering armed violence.

    By 3rd January, there was widespread fear, largely induced by false rumours spread by the plotters, of racial disturbances in Malay areas.

    In between spreading these vicious and dangerous rumours calculated to bring about the loss of Malay as well as Chinese lives, the plotters decided to make some money out of the anxiety they had caused to their own people. So one of the plotters contacted a bomoh. While spreading rumours about impending racial clash, the plotter also informed his victims that "invulnerability" charms and shirts could be bought for

a price from the bomoh. The bomoh is said to have done well selling invulnerability charms and shirts for as much as $15 apiece. Three Malays wearing these shirts were found in the house and detained by the Police but the bomoh unfortunately vanished before the Police appeared.

    A number of these plotters toured various kampongs spreading false rumours that fighting had broken out between Chinese and Malays and that the Malays should stand in readiness for 6th January when widespread racial clashes were to be expected.

    On the morning of 5th January, the Police seized copies of a Malay magazine just before they were to be distributed. This magazine carried an editorial unjustly accusing the Government of provoking and hurting the feelings of Singapore Muslims. It was also learnt that the proprietor of the magazine had intentions of making a statement on the subject at Sultan Mosque after mid-day prayers on 6th January.

    Sir, it is not suggested that the proprietor was in any way connected with the A.R.T.I.S. group. The appearance of this editorial may have been sheer coincidence. But in view of the prevailing air of tension among the Malays and in view of the fact that according to rumours Sultan Mosque had been selected by the plotters as the spot to spark off clashes, it became necessary for the Government to prevent circulation of the magazine and request the proprietor not to make a statement at the mosque.

    Also on the morning of 6th January, the Police discovered in an unoccupied hut, seven jungle green uniforms with shoulder badges bearing a white crescent and star on a green background.

    The Police also discovered four flags with Arabic inscriptions.

    By 4th and 5th January, rumours about an impending racial clash on 6th January had gained wide currency among the Malay public. Fortunately, the non-Malay public knew little or nothing about these rumours or else the air of tension and. anxiety might have spread to the non-Malays as well and thus created an extremely ugly situation.

    Sir, it may be asked, why then did 6th January pass off peacefully?

    For one thing, the Police acted swiftly. They arrested a number of the plotters. To date, 15 persons have been detained in connection with A.R.T.I.S. This organisation was fortunately not large or influential and these timely arrests put it out of action.

    Another important factor was the wholehearted assistance given by Malay religious, civic and political leaders in helping to combat the vicious rumours that the plotters had deliberately spread. Representatives of the Muslim Advisory Board, Malay P.A.P. Assemblymen and leaders of U.M.N.O. issued warnings telling their brother - Malays not to heed the mischievous lies of the plotters. These warnings were broadcast over the Malay section of Radio Singapore throughout the night of 5th January and 6th January morning.

    The Government also sent out field units equipped with loudspeakers to broadcast the messages of Malay leaders in Malay areas.

    Malay leaders also approached leaders of mosques requesting them to be on the look-out for mischief makers and to counter dangerous rumours. This assistance by Malay religious and political leaders did a great deal to neutralise the mischief created by the plotters.

    Another reason was that these rumours, though they created anxiety among the Malays, roused no Malay animosity against the non-Malays, in particular, against the Chinese. The fear was not that the Chinese would attack the Malays, as the plotters had said they would, but that the plotters would stage a small incident which might panic people into racial clash.

    It was to prevent such an accidental sparking off of racial clashes that the Government thought it prudent not to draw the attention of the non-Malay public to what was happening. A sudden announcement about it a few days before 6th January might have alarmed the non-Malay public who, by and large, were unaware of the rumours and fears circulating among large numbers of Malays.

It is economically impossible, in a complex society like ours, to solve poverty in terms of this or that race. However, the unsophisticated Malay, like the unsophisticated Chinese or Indian, does not understand the complex nature of the problem of poverty.
