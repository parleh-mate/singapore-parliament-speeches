SUBORDINATE COURTS (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister for Law (Prof. S. Jayakumar): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, this Bill before the House seeks to amend the Subordinate Courts Act as part of a general plan to restructure the jurisdiction and powers of the courts in Singapore. Two other Bills have been approved by this House yesterday. This is the third Bill as part of the restructuring. Since certain provisions in this Bill are similar to the Bill approved yesterday to amend the Supreme Court of Judicature Act, I will not be dealing with such provisions.

Column: 131



    The civil jurisdiction of the District Court is at present limited to the following matters: First, monetary claims not exceeding $50,000 founded on contract or tort or under any written law; second, action for the recovery of immovable property where title is not in dispute; third, proceedings for the grant of probate and administration acts; fourth, proceedings for distress of rent; and fifth, interpleader proceedings.

    Sir, the law applicable to these proceedings constitutes actually a very small part of the civil law in Singapore. Most of the disputes in the proceedings turn on the facts and hardly involve any points of law, leave alone complicated points of law. As a result, the civil work of a District Judge does not really prepare him for hearing civil disputes in the High Court if eventually he should be appointed a Judge of the High Court.

    Clauses 5 and 9 increase the civil jurisdiction of a District Court to include the following matters: First, non-monetary claims founded on contract or tort; second, actions for the recovery of immovable property even where title is in dispute; and third, certain equity proceedings.

    Clause 2 increases the monetary limit of a District Court from $50,000 to $100,000 generally, and from $250,000 to $3 million for grant of probate and administration actions. Clause 14 provides that these limits may be amended from time to time, up or down, by the President after consulting the Chief Justice. The President has to act on the advice of the Cabinet before making such order. Under the existing provisions, the Subordinate Courts Act has to be amended in order to vary the monetary limits.

    The enlarged civil jurisdiction will enable District Judges to acquire a broader range of experience and a more extensive knowledge of the law.

    Clause 21 increases the jurisdiction of a Magistrate's Court from $10,000 to $30,000. But there is no increase in the subject jurisdiction of a Magistrate's Court.

NATIONAL HERITAGE BOARD BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

     The Minister for Information and the Arts (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The National Heritage Board will enable the Government to better preserve and promote Singapore's heritage. The Board will be a one-stop resource centre bringing together the National Museum, the National Archives and the Oral History Department, the current heritage departments of the Ministry of the Information and the Arts.

    The National Heritage Board will explore and present the heritage and nationhood of the people of Singapore in the context of their ancestral cultures, their links with Southeast Asia, with Asia and with the world through the collection, preservation, interpretation and display of objects and records. It will also promote public awareness, appreciation and understanding of our past.

    As a statutory board, the National Heritage Board will enjoy flexibility in its operation and in recruiting, deploying and developing staff. There are also cost savings in integrating the three departments into a single organisation.

    Although the Government will fund the greater part of the development and recurrent costs of the National Heritage Board, it is very important that we also encourage members of the public, as corporations and as individuals, to give their full support. The Government has announced plans to expand the present National Museum and organise the exhibits into separate museums. The

Column: 138

Singapore Art Museum and the Asian Civilisations Museum and the History of Singapore Museum will be ready from 1995 onwards. We will provide ample scope for public participation. Collectors and well-wishers from Singapore and abroad will be encouraged to lend and donate their art collections.

    As a young nation, it will take us many generations to build up our national collection. Unlike countries like the UK and France, we have no imperial booty to draw upon. Happily, travelling exhibitions are easier to organise in the age of the jumbo jet. Thus, we have been able to put up world-class exhibitions on Imperial China at Empress Place. We recently signed an agreement to bring in exhibits from India, and I hope to sign similar agreements with the Turkish and the Egyptian Governments later this year. Next month, the National Museum will display 63 exquisite pieces from the Rockefeller Collection of the Asia Society. In this way, we supplement our own collection with loans from others.

    The various communities in Singapore should also participate in the effort to preserve our heritage. They are in a good position to collect, preserve and promote an awareness of their own heritage in a living way. As I announced last year, the Federation of Chinese Clans Association is interested to set up a Chinese Heritage Centre at the old Nantah Administration Building to research and present the heritage of the Chinese outside China, their migratory patterns and way of life and the way they have sunk roots in different countries and made contributions to local communities.

    The Malay community is now exploring the concept and feasibility of setting up a Malay Heritage Centre. The Government will make available the old Istana at Kampong Glam for this purpose at nominal rental. Likewise, the Government will provide similar assistance to the Indian and Eurasian communities.

    As I informed the House during the Committee of Supply, the Government

will help the four communities set up endowment funds to run these centres subject to a ceiling of $5 million for the Chinese, $2 million for the Malays, $1 million for the Indians and $1/4 million for the Eurasians on a dollar-for-dollar basis.     The National Heritage Board will also work closely with the Singapore Heritage Society and other heritage organisations.

    The Government will give maximum autonomy to the National Heritage Board. The Board will formulate its own personnel policies so that the right people can be engaged, retained and developed.
