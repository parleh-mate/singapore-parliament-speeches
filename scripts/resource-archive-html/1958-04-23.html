10.04 a.m.

    The Chief Secretary (Mr E. B. David): Mr Speaker, Sir, I lay upon the Table a Certificate of Urgency signed by His Excellency the Governor in respect of the Criminal Justice (Temporary Provisions) (Amendment) Bill.

    Certificate of Urgency handed in.

    Mr Speaker: The Certificate is in order. Are typed copies of the Bill available for the use of Members?

    Typed copies of the Bill handed in to the Clerk and distributed to hon. Members.

    Mr David: Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, hon. Members will be aware of the growing public concern at the increase in gangsterism and secret society activities of recent months, and as indicated in the Governor's speech on 9th April, the Government considers it imperative to strengthen the laws by which

Column: 118

these activities can be more effectively countered. The majority of these crimes are committed by individual gangsters or by gangs of thugs, who expect to retain all the proceeds or nearly all the proceeds of their crimes and look only to their parent secret societies - if I may put it that way - for protection from prosecution and for protection against rival gangs. These gangs hold sway in their own particular areas, where they organise gambling, smuggling, vice and extortion rackets of different kinds. They provoke gang fights and they intimidate the law-abiding public into the payment of protection money. Their hold, Sir, is such as to frighten the public from giving evidence against them, and their efforts to control each other's activities very often lead to violent clashes between different gangs.

    It is regrettably true, Sir, that in 1957, the number of gang fights, which was 150, was more than in all the three previous years put together, and that figure has not - regrettably - decreased this year since there have been 68 such fights in the first three months of this year.

    The majority of the members of these gangs are under 30 years of age and mostly, as far as we know, are born in Singapore. It directly reflects the serious social and economic problems presented by the great increase in Singapore's population since the War, problems which are receiving the earnest attention of the Government but which are not easy or susceptible to an early solution. The causes of this delinquency can be eradicated over a period of years only by improvement of economic and social conditions and by active youth welfare and educational work.

    The Government is doing all in its power to further these aims but in the meantime the immediate need is to preserve the peace of the Colony and to protect the law-abiding public from the criminal activities of these gangsters. In this, Sir, the police, the public and the Courts all have a part to play. The police require the support and the confidence of the public not only in providing information of actual crimes and projected crimes, but also in willingness

Column: 119

to come forward and to give evidence against those responsible. The Courts, for their part, can only administer the laws enacted by the Legislature and it is for this Assembly to ensure that they are armed with the laws and the sanctions which the present situation requires.

    Sir, the existing provisions in the Criminal Justice (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance whereby a sentence of corrective training or preventive detention can only be imposed on proof of membership of, or connection with, an unlawful society, has not proved as effective as had been hoped, even though special provision is made in section 6 to facilitate evidence of such membership. In a number of such cases in which charges are brought of offences particularly associated with the activities of secret societies, there has not been the proof required in relation to membership of the secret society and it has not therefore proved possible to invoke the provisions of this Ordinance. The purpose of clause 2 of this amending Bill is to remedy that position and to provide the Courts with a wider discretion than they at present have in applying the provisions of this Ordinance, by removing the condition precedent that membership of, or connection with, an unlawful society must be proved in addition to the conviction.

    Another important amendment, Sir, to the principal Ordinance is in clause 3 of the Bill which empowers the arrest of any person found in the immediate vicinity of, or escaping from, any place in which a disturbance is occurring or has recently occurred, or any place where a disturbance, according to information received by the police, is likely to take place, and any person who is reasonably suspected of being connected with a secret society. Such a person can be required, under this provision, by a Court to show cause why he should not be ordered to sign a bond to keep the peace.

    Power is also given to the Courts to impose a curfew order on such a person. For breach of the bond or the order, such a person will be liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding twelve

Column: 120

months, or to a fine not exceeding $1,000, or to both such imprisonment and fine.

    At present, Sir, it often happens that the police, arriving at the scene of a planned clash, in time to prevent it, or arriving shortly after one has occurred, find persons present whom they have good reason to know are the fighter elements of the secret societies concerned, but short of catching them in actual possession of offensive weapons, the police are powerless since witnesses from the neighbourhood are not prepared to come forward to give evidence.

    I should like, Sir, to emphasise on this particular clause that it is not intended that this power should be used in any arbitrary manner. Moreover, the clause provides that the police must take the individual before the Court and it is provided in the following clause that the provisions of section 80 of the Criminal Procedure Code shall apply to an enquiry by the Court under this clause. And section 80 of the Criminal Procedure Code provides that where such an order has been made and a person appears or is brought before a Court in compliance with or in execution of a summons or warrant, the Court shall proceed to enquire into the truth of the information upon which it is acting, and to take such further evidence as appears necessary. Such enquiry shall be made as nearly as is practicable in the manner hereof prescribed for conducting trials in summary cases, etc.

    Sir, I suggest to hon. Members that although this is an exceptional power of arrest, a safeguard against injustice to innocent people is amply provided for by the provisions which ensure that there shall be a proper enquiry before a Court before anyone is required to execute a bond as provided in this clause.

    Sir, the final amendment to the principal Ordinance is in clause 4 of the Bill which adds the offences of carrying knives or offensive weapons in public, loitering with intent to commit a seizable offence and offences under the Corrosive Substances Ordinance, 1956, to the list of scheduled offences under the principal

Column: 121

Ordinance. The reason for this, Sir, is that these are the types of offences which are frequently committed by persons associated with gang warfare. Hon. Members will be aware of the ingenuity shown by these gangsters in devising and concealing weapons which are capable of killing their victims or maiming them for life, and of the increasing use in recent months of the particularly vicious weapon of acid bombs.

    Sir, I believe it will be the wish of all hon. Members of this Assembly to see the scourge of gang warfare wiped out from our midst. The legislation now proposed is designed to further that purpose and no more. I have every hope that, with the co-operation of the public, which I am glad to say is increasing daily, this legislation will strengthen the organs of law and order in removing this menace from our midst.

    Sir, I beg to move.
