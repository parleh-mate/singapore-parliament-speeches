SINGAPORE TECHNOLOGY COMPANIES


    1. Mr Philip Tan Tee Yong asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence, to maximise the productivity of our limited pool of manpower, whether he will consider directing companies belonging to the Ministry of Defence to concentrate on security-related or very high-tech businesses instead of manufacturing and selling personal computers.

     Mr Speaker: Question No. 1, Mr Philip Tan.

     Mr Philip Tan Tee Yong (Paya Lebar): Mr Speaker, Sir, before asking the Question, I would like to declare that I am in the computer software business though I am not in the business of manufacturing personal computers for sale. Secondly, I also want to declare that I am a council member of the Enterprise Development Council. This Council was formed by the Economic Development Board to promote and develop local enterprises to play a bigger role in Singapore's economic growth in the 1990s.

     The Second Minister for Defence (Services) (BG Lee Hsien Loong) (for the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am answering on behalf of my Minister.

    The principal purpose of setting up the Singapore Technology (ST) companies is to provide technical and industrial support to the Singapore Armed Forces (SAF), to enhance its operational effectiveness and strengthen our economic defence.

    However, ST cannot do this solely by undertaking work for the SAF. No defence group in the world does this unless self-sufficiency is to be achieved at all costs. Strengths and capabilities which the SAF will need only in a contingency will almost by definition be surplus to requirements in peacetime. The same technology used by the SAF can often also be employed for civilian purposes. For example, both military data terminals and civilian personal computers require similar integrated circuits and printed circuit boards.

Column: 270

To develop a capability for one and not to use it for the other would be wasteful.

    MINDEF's policy is therefore that ST companies should, besides SAF work, also undertake industrial and commercial activities which have synergies with, support or extend the core defence-related activities. This may mean "crowding out" the private sector from the business. But from the point of view of the nation as a whole, it is better for the defence industries to do this and channel the private sector into other areas of business, rather than for the defence industries to keep their surplus capacity idle in order to give the business to the private sector.

    ST companies are required to maintain a clear business focus. They only undertake those non-military activities which make sense for the core mission of their companies, where there is synergy with the defence business. They are not allowed to practise predatory pricing, or take advantage of their Government-owned status to hide costs and win contracts. They must compete for business on equal terms with private sector companies.

    Electronics and computer systems are pervasive and important components of modern weapon systems. While we cannot become totally self-sufficient in these areas, the ST companies can develop a comprehensive capability in these systems, to give the SAF the extra technological edge. This is why ST companies manufacture and sell personal computers.

HAJ PILGRIMAGE


    5. Mr Chiam See Tong asked the Minister for the Environment and Minister-in-Charge of Muslim Affairs whether he will make a statement concerning 242 Singaporean Muslims who recently nearly missed their Haj pilgrimage although all of them had paid fully for their trips.

Column: 280



    The Minister for the Environment and the Minister-in-Charge of Muslim Affairs (Dr Ahmad Mattar): Mr Speaker, Sir, every year, before the start of the haj season, MUIS appoints pilgrim brokers after carefully screening their credentials. Pilgrim brokers help intending pilgrims to register with MUIS for the haj, provide training on haj rituals and look after their welfare in Saudi Arabia.

    MUIS also selects General Sales Agents (GSAs) who provide haj packages at competitive prices. The haj packages include board, lodging and internal travel in Saudi Arabia. Only those GSAs who are able to satisfy the conditions imposed by MUIS are selected.

    For this year's haj, MUIS selected 13 pilgrim brokers and seven GSAs. When their appointments were announced, MUIS advised intending pilgrims to deal only with these authorized pilgrim brokers and GSAs.

    The pilgrims referred to by Mr Chiam were those who went against MUIS' advice and paid money to a travel agent who was not selected by MUIS as a GSA.
