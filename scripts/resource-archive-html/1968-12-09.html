METRIC SYSTEM
(Introduction in Singapore)


    2. Mr Yeoh Ghim Seng asked the Minister for Finance if the Govern ment will consider introducing the Metric System at this stage of the Republic's development so as to conform with the practice obtaining in most developed countries.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Finance (Mr Tang See Chim) (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, the conversion of the British system of measurement the so-called pound- foot-gallon system to the metric system has been engaging the interests of many countries of the world in recent years, especially those in the Commonwealth and the United States. There is no doubt about the many advantages of the metric system which is based on scientific conception and has a direct parallelism with the universally-used decimal system of numbering.

    Since the signing of the Metre Convention in 1875 in Paris by 18 pioneering countries, more than 85 nations have adopted the metric system officially. They represent roughly more than 60 per cent of the world's aggregate gross national product. More than 50 per cent of the world's trade is based on the metric system. The percentages will be greater when the United States adopts the metric system.

    While both the United Kingdom and the United States of America are signatories to the Metre Convention, it was only in May 1965 that the United Kingdom declared its intention of using the metric system, and it was only until this year, i.e. 1968, that the U.S.A. decided to initiate a three-year

Column: 94

study on the transformation to the metric system. Australia, Canada and South Africa are believed to be seriously considering its adoption.

    However, the change-over to the metric system involves not only a basic change in the system of measurement of the country, e.g., the replacement of old measuring devices and machines by new equipment and the resultant costs, it would also require a national re- orientation towards the use and conception of the new system of measurement. This indeed was the reason why the United Kingdom and the United States, though among the first signatories of the Metre Convention, are only now considering its adoption.

    It is generally true to say that the earlier the change-over is brought about, the less expensive the operation will be and the less difficult will be the problems associated with the conversion. Transformation to the metric system, however, is a long process. The traditional habits of thinking and conceiving of quantities and comparing them with each other grow up with one from childhood. It is not just a simple matter of changing the physical equipment required for weights and measures, but a change in thinking and conception in the new units of quantity is also required.

    A successful change-over could only be brought about with the agreement and co-operation of every sector of the economy industry, trade, education and so on, and not by Government enforcement alone, although Government initiative is required. This is a problem of great complexity affecting the whole society. School text-books, for example, would need to be re-written when we adopt the metric system.

    Despite the many problems associated with the change-over as well as the large financial costs that would be involved, it would be desirable to adopt the metric system in the end. In fact, a study has already been initiated at our Industrial Research Unit to look into the many problems connected with the change-over. An ECAFE adviser

Column: 95

on Industrial Standardization, who had earlier assisted in the conversion to the metric system in India, has estimated that a period of five to six years would be required to bring about the desired change in Singapore.

CIVIL SERVICE
(Courtesy campaign)


    4. Inche Rahmat Bin Kenap asked the Minister for Finance whether he will consider conducting a further campaign to remind civil servants that courtesy and willing assistance are expected of them at all times when dealing with members of the public.

    Mr Tang See Chim (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, in 1959, after the P.A.P. Government assumed office, steps were taken to ensure that civil servants deal with members of the public with courtesy.

    Rudeness and discourtesy to the public were made disciplinary offences, and disciplinary regulations were amended to enable a Permanent Secretary to appoint a Division I officer to make enquiries and deal with such complaints speedily.

    The statistics of the Central Complaints Bureau, which was set up in 1962, show a steady decline in the number of complaints, dropping from 1,064 cases in 1963 to 604 cases in1967. Not all of these complaints were justified. Today, it cannot be said that discourtesy among civil servants remains a major problem. In view of this, a further campaign is not deemed necessary.

    Of course, isolated instances of discourtesy may still occur. To eradicate these, members of the public can do their part by giving full details of

such cases to the Heads of Departments concerned or to the Central Complaints Bureau or to their respective Members of Parliament.

CIVIL SERVANTS RESIGNING AFTER SPECIALISED
TRAINING ABROAD
(Wastage of public funds)


    6. Mr L. P. Rodrigo asked the Minister for Finance what measures are in force to prevent a waste of public funds by the resignation of a civil servant who has been sent abroad for further or specialised training at considerable public expense.

    Mr Tang See Chim (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, when an officer in the civil service is selected for a training course or scholarship award, he is required to sign a bond with the Government in which he undertakes to serve the Government for a specified period, normally five years, after completing his course. He is also required to provide two sureties guaranteeing his performance. In the event of any breach, the officer will be required to compensate the Government by payment of liquidated damages, the amount of which is calculated according to the amount the Government has expended on him and the period (or the remaining period) which he is obliged to serve the Government.

    So far, the incidence of officers resigning before completing the period of their bond is low - 17 out of 479 who returned from overseas during the period 1964 to 1968. And in every case,

Column: 99

there is no loss of public funds, as the Government is able to recover the amount of liquidated damages stipulated in the bond.

CIVIL SERVANTS
(Dismissal for indebtedness)


    7. Mr L. P. Rodrigo asked the Minister for Finance if civil servants are dismissed from the Service for indebtedness as a rule; and whether he will consider applying a rule to give a civil servant, who is indebted, a second chance to prove his worth in the Service if his case deserves it.

    Mr Tang See Chim (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, under civil service regulations, an officer may be dismissed from the service on account of indebtedness. This extreme punishment, however, is meted out only in bad cases. Thus, out of 123 cases of indebtedness which came before the Public Service Commission for the period 1st January, 1966, to 30th June, 1968, 53 cases resulted in dismissal and nine in compulsory retirement.

    A first offender is normally given a warning only, if the amount of debt is small and his work and conduct are satisfactory. Cases of dismissal usually involve officers who are habitual borrowers with a long history of unsettled debts. Such officers are generally found to be of questionable integrity and are unfit for retention in the service.

    The Government is anxious to retain the better and more efficient officers in the service and would be prepared to give an officer who is indebted a second chance in deserving cases.

CIVIL SERVANTS
(Numbers in acting and "officiating" capacities)


    8. Mr L. P. Rodrigo asked the Minister for Finance (a) how many public officers were serving in an acting capacity on 1st October, 1968, and how many such officers have acted for more

Column: 100

than two months; and (b) whether there are officers "officiating" in unfilled posts in the Service, the number of such "officiating" officers in the various Ministries and departments, and why such "officiating" capacity is necessary.

    Mr Tang See Chim (for the Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, there was a total of 946 public officers serving in an acting capacity as on 1st October, 1968, of whom 785 have acted for more than two months.

    Most of the acting posts are in Division I 363 out of 946 as at 1st October, 1968. The reasons for this large number of acting posts have been the expansion of the service and the establishment of new Ministries and Departments (for example, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the Ministry of the Interior and Defence, the Ministry of Science and Technology and the Technical Department in the Ministry of Education) and the difficulty in recruitment. This situation should improve with the promotion exercise to be conducted soon.

    The answer to the second part of the question is "Yes". The number of officers "officiating" in unfilled posts in the service as on 1st October, 1968, was 47. Such "officiating" appointments are necessary as, in some cases, there was no suitable officer in the next lower grade available to act. Under present regulations, where a post is more than two grades higher than that of the officer's substantive appointment, he may only "officiate" in the higher grade.

    "Officiating" appointments may also be necessary in the professional services if, there is a shortage of qualified officers. Here, capable but unqualified officers in lower grades in the same department are asked to "officiate" pending the recruitment of qualified officers.

    Lastly, where there is a shortage of officers in certain grades of appointment and where officers in other classes of appointment are suitable, they are asked to "officiate".

VEHICLES EMITTING EXCESSIVE
EXHAUST SMOKE
(Number and results of prosecutions against offenders)


    12. Mr J. F. Conceicao asked the Minister for Defence (a) what action is taken on information received from members of the public of vehicles which emit excessive exhaust smoke; (b) how many cases of prosecutions there were against owners or operators of vehicles emitting excessive exhaust smoke between 1st January and 30th September, 1968; and (c) what were the results of such prosecutions.

Column: 103



    Mr Lim Kim San: Mr Speaker, Sir, usually on receipt of information from members of the public regarding vehicles emitting excessive exhaust smoke, action is taken under section 37 (1) of the Road Traffic Ordinance whereby the Deputy Commissioner of Police or the Registrar of Vehicles may require the owner of any licensed vehicle to produce his vehicle for inspection. At the time of inspection, a policeman is present and if the car inspected emits excessive exhaust smoke, a report is made by the police officer present and appropriate action is taken, whether or not an informant is prepared to give evidence in court. The police send an offer of composition of $20 to the owner of the vehicle. If the owner of the vehicle refuses to accept the offer of composition, a summons is taken out against him.

    For the period 1st January, 1968, to 30th September, 1968, a total of 2,938 reports were made against vehicles emitting excessive exhaust smoke. In every case, the owners of the vehicles were offered a composition of $20. A total of 502 summonses have been applied for and are due to be issued by the Traffic Courts.
