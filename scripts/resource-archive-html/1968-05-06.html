    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Mr Speaker, Sir, hon. Members join me in extending you our congratulations and our co-operation on your

Column: 7

re-election to the Chair. It is nearly two years since you were first elected to the office. Your experience as Speaker must be unique. In two successive Parliaments you sat over a House without an Opposition. First, the Opposition boycotted the House. Next, they boycotted the general elections. But in spite of the extraordinary circumstances, a legislative chamber without the stimulus of provocative criticism, those of us who were in the last Parliament are confident that under your guidance, this House will keep the parliamentary machine in good shape, and also sustain and cultivate the attitudes of tolerance and calculated deference to opposing views.

    Those of us who are natural optimists, or not long acquainted with the social science of psephology, may believe that the return of all 58 Members from the P.A.P, reflects the unanimity of views amongst people outside. My colleagues and I, who have gone through more harrowing times, cannot afford to be so naive, or so ingenuous.

    In 1963, the pro-Communist groups, under the banner of the Barisan Sosialis collected over 30 per cent of the votes and 13 out of 51 seats. True, their fortunes have declined. This is partly the result of our social and economic policies, partly the result of higher political sophistication amongst the voters. But it is mainly because of their own irrational rigid orthodoxy, their obsequious subservience to directives from outside. They have mechanically chanted ridiculous slogans and asserted incredible untruths - that Malaysia does not exist, that "Malaya" includes Singapore, and that the Republic is a sham.

    Even so, had the Communists decided to contest the elections through the Barisan Sosialis, or other front organisations, like the Communist-led trade unions, or the old boys' associations and the cultural groups, I would not have been surprised if they had collected over 15 per cent of the votes, which would have ensured them some

six to eight seats in this House. If they persist in orthodoxy, and their orders continue to be as unrelated to the realities of the situation in and around Singapore as they have been in the last five years, and more important, if our economic and social policies make the progress they have made over the last five years, then by 1973 their electoral support may further sink down perhaps to below 10 per cent. But I doubt if I can give as cheerful an estimate in the corresponding decline of their cadre strength. One of the subsidiary reasons why their electoral support has declined is that they have withdrawn their trained and experienced cadres from open front activity to conserve their strength for future deployment.

    Sir, of the 58 Members in this House, 26 Members have never known what it is to be in a Parliament with an Opposition. Indeed, 16, having been returned unopposed, have never known the rough and tumble of a contested election. In 1955, the Governors stopped presiding over sessions of the Legislative Council. A mainly elected Legislative Assembly of 32 Members of whom 25 were elected was allowed to meet under an independent Speaker. Three Members were from the P.A.P. I am the sole survivor of that first Assembly.

    Some of the clerks of Parliament and I have personal knowledge of the boisterous and rumbustious past of this House. There was no dearth or differences of opinions, or independence of views, sometimes sincerely, and often vehemently, held. Hansard records the clash of minds, the exchange of retorts courteous and not so courteous, the conflict of ideas and ideologies, and the face-to-face confrontation of the exponents of different ideologies.

But the Opposition will not be individuals claiming to act as the conscience of the people, or offering themselves as the necessary accoutrements of parliamentary democracy on the spurious argument that, because there is no Opposition, therefore there is no parliamentary democracy. Singapore is not that gullible and credulous.

    The Opposition, when it returns, will comprise a group offering an alternative policy, and offering themselves as a credible alternative government to implement the policies they expound. This could come about in one of two ways, one less desirable than the other. First, it could come as a result of severe economic setbacks, the failure to meet and overcome the problems coming upon us in the l970s. Then the Opposition that emerges will be extreme, either orthodox Communist or revisionist, or Trotskyite. Then divisions will be sharp and angry, and the country the worse for it.

    Second, the more desirable alternative, economic success and enhanced social stability, and further reduction in electoral support for Communist or extremist policies. With stability and prosperity, the sense of national urgency and unity which binds together a people faced with common danger, will slacken and a more relaxed mood will set in. A new restlessness will bestir the people, a desire for improvement and change, or just change for change's sake, will appeal to a younger generation. The emergence of a party or parties either to the left or right of the P.A.P, must result. PRESIDENT'S SPEECH


    The President (Inche Yusof bin Ishak)(In Malay): Honourable Members, we are met today after a general election in which 51 Honourable Members were returned unopposed and the rest, seven from the same party, returned with clear majorities. This is a Parliament which is faced with problems of more than usual magnitude. It is not only because Britain will be withdrawing her forces by the end of 1971. The whole security and political situation in South and Southeast Asia is in the process of change. The balance of forces, which have more or less held the position since 1954 when the French relinquished power over the whole of Vietnam, and the Americans stepped into the breach in the south, is now poised for a possible major shift.

    Momentous changes in the political and security pattern of the region have already been set in motion. By the end of the term of this Parliament in 1973, it is probable that new security and political patterns would have begun to emerge. Peace and security will have to be ensured under different circumstances.

    These historic events will have a profound influence on our future. Perhaps it was the awareness of this that made Singapore vote almost unanimously for the P.A.P. In the next five to ten years, issues of life and death will be determined more by factors around, rather than within, Singapore.

    (In English): My Government will seek to achieve three objectives:

    First, optimum use of the time available to build up an effective defence capability which, in conjunction with Malaysia and our other Commonwealth partners, can ensure continuing security. This must be done at minimum cost, so that most of our resources will go into economic development. It means that, although we have small numbers on active service, we shall have large numbers in the reserve service. These

Column: 13

reserves will be well and regularly trained, ready for combat, but otherwise they will be in productive employment, contributing to the economic growth and progress of the country.

    Second, greater economic growth, higher productivity with the elimination of restrictive practices, abuses of fringe benefits, and more efficient management. With ever lower costs we must reach out for more export markets. The continuance and consolidation of stable political and social conditions, sensible relations between labour, management and government, these are the pre-conditions for increased investments in the industrial and servicing sectors to create meaningful jobs for ever-increasing numbers of our more educated citizens seeking better jobs.

    Third, enhancement of national consciousness of the problems that will come upon us in the next few years. We must all learn to place national interests above personal or sectional interests. Internally, high morale - internationally, confidence in our continuing stability and efficiency - this combination will enable us to achieve new records in high performance.

    The broad outlines of policy for each Ministry in my Government are set out in an addendum* to this Address. To sum them up, it is to get full value for every dollar spent. To do this, everyone must put in a full day's work for a full
* Cols. 15-32.

These problems appear daunting only to the faint hearted.

MEMORANDUM PRESENTED TO PARLIAMENT AS AN ADDENDUM
TO THE ADDRESS BY THE PRESIDENT


    Outlines of policy on matters of finance, defence, foreign affairs, industrial and technical training and technological education, have already been set out in speeches by respective Ministers.

MINISTRY OF LABOUR


Comprehensive Labour Legislation

    A comprehensive Employment Bill will be introduced in Parliament. The Bill will standardize and regulate the terms and conditions of employment for all employees irrespective of whether they are workmen, clerks or shop assistants and the existing Labour Ordinance, 1955, the Shop Assistants Employment Ordinance, 1957, and the Clerks Employment Ordinance, 1957, will be repealed. Among other things the Bill will provide a standard working week of 44 hours for all employees, regulate overtime work, to enable a wider spread of employment, limit certain fringe benefits to lessen abuses and increase productivity and promote more industrial growth.

Central Provident Fund

    It is necessary to mobilise domestic savings to be employed in our counter recession strategy. Under consideration are selected increases in the rates of Central Provident Fund contributions and a new range of contributors covering all employees including those in Government and Statutory Boards in addition to their pensions or other provident fund benefits and self employed persons.

    Concurrently a scheme is being worked out between the Ministry and the Housing and Development Board to enable balances in the present Central Provident Fund to be set off against the first instalment of Housing Board flats. The additional Central Provident Fund contributions can be used to pay off monthly instalments of both Housing

Column: 16

and Development Board and privately built homes. It is estimated that about 60 per cent of the 2, 3 and 4-room tenants of the Housing and Development Board flats will thus be enabled to purchase their present flats. The lowering of assessments is also being considered to make the home ownership scheme attractive.

Training

    The Hotel and Catering Training Centre will be extended to train waiters, barmen, room stewards, receptionists and cashiers for the expanding hotel and catering industry. Technical assistance had been sought under the Colombo Plan and the I.L.O. for the expansion of the Training Centre. Soon the training of cooks and courses for supervisory and management personnel will also be conducted at the Centre.

    A crash programme for the retraining of redundant base workers was started in April this year at the Singapore Vocational Institute, the Economic Development Board's Workshop, Polytechnic and at 5 secondary technical schools. These training courses will equip retrenched workers with the basic skills in various trades. Further, steps have been taken to establish industrial training centres which will be equipped with modern machinery to enable trainees to gain actual production experience. These centres will offer training and retraining facilities not only to our citizens retrenched by the British Services but also to school-leavers who desire to acquire industrial skills to get employment in blue collar jobs.

Manpower Planning

    A Manpower Planning Committee will be established to study the supply and demand position in depth and breadth. The studies will embrace educational and training requirements and supply the necessary information for comprehensive manpower planning.

Column: 17

MINISTRY OF EDUCATION


    In education, the available resources of the Government will be directed mainly towards the following targets: -

    (a) A reorganisation of the secondary school system to divert a much larger number of students to technical education and vocational training. A Cabinet Committee with the Minister for Education as Chairman, Minister for Science and Technology, Minister for Labour and Minister for Finance has been set up to supervise the planning and co-ordination of technical and vocational training on a national basis with the object of avoiding duplication of effort and ensuring that technical education and vocational training will be keyed to manpower needs and employment opportunities.

    (b) A concerted effort to produce effectively bilingual school -leavers to ensure wider opportunities for higher education and employment in our multi-racial society. Increasing use will be made of the second language as a medium of instruction. As far as possible, students in non-English language schools will study mathematics, science and technology in the medium of English and the humanities through one of the other, either their mother language Malay, Chinese or Tamil. In English language schools, the mother tongue will be more extensively used to make students effectively literate and fluent in a second language.

    (c) Increasing emphasis on quality at all levels of education to make up for shortcomings in the past necessitated by the

unprecedented rate of expansion in the school system to provide a place for every child. Now that this quantitative target has been achieved, attention will be turned to qualitative improvement in such fields as curriculum reform, textbooks and teaching aids, teaching techniques, teacher training and re-training, examinations, health education, physical education, art and music, and all forms of extramural activity. MINISTRY OF COMMUNICATIONS


    The geographic and strategic importance of Singapore gives us an edge in the field of transportation and communications.

Marine

    Last year, a record number of 28,496 vessels or 117.3 million net registered tons of shipping used our port facilities. Singapore became the No. 1 port in the Commonwealth and moved from 5th to 4th port in the world.

    Proper terminal facilities for ocean-going container vessels at the East Lagoon have been under construction at a total cost of over $77 million. They will be operational by 1970.

Column: 19



    The efficiency and productivity of our port, both workers and management, must be demonstrated in quicker turn-round of ships.

    There has been considerable progress in shipbuilding and ship repairing business at the Port of Singapore Authority Dockyard, at Jurong, Kallang and Tanjong Rhu shipyards. This is a highly labour intensive industry. The naval base at Sembawang will be converted into a commercial dockyard, even whilst the Royal Navy is using the Base. This conversion work may commence this year.

Telecommunications

    Singapore has long been a major regional and world centre for telecommunications. To keep abreast of satellite communications, we have decided to install an Earth Satellite Station in Singapore to be operational by 1970. This station will be supplementary to the Seacom cable system. It will enable Singapore to obtain direct link via the Indian Ocean Satellite with other similarly equipped countries from Japan and America in the East to Britain and Europe in the West.

    In addition to normal telecommunication facilities, the Earth Satellite Station can provide a means for the reception and transmission of television programmes, thus enabling Singapore viewers to link up with the main centres of civilisation the world over.

    The Singapore Telephone Board has over 108,000 telephones and 76,000 direct lines are in service. 98 per cent of subscribers are now connected to the automatic Subscriber Trunk Dialling Service (STD). The Singapore Telephone Board will have not only to expand its services to keep pace with the increasing demand for its services but also to improve its standards of maintenance and efficiency.

Aviation

    1967 was a record year with 964,244 passengers (25 per cent increase over the previous year) 8.6 million kilos of air cargo (11 per cent increase) and 1.8

million kilos of mail (26 per cent increase) being handled by our airport authorities.

    Twenty-one international airlines run scheduled services through Singapore and work is in progress for the extension of the runway from 9,000 feet to 11,000 feet to get Singapore Airport to receive the jumbo jets.

    Modern equipment such as Visual Approach Slope Indicators, Instrument Landing System and an Airport Location Beacon have been or are being installed to maintain high standards of safety and efficient operation. An aircraft engineering facilities building is nearing completion.

Postal Services

    A record 192 million articles of mail were handled by our Postal Services during 1967. We must maintain high standards of efficiency under even heavy loads of mail traffic.

    The following agency services were continued during the year, namely: -

Legislation is being drawn up to permit renewal of driving licences at post offices, on behalf of the Registrar of Vehicles.

Road Transport

    The offices of the Registrar of Vehicles at Middle Road will be reorganised in order to streamline work procedures and to ease congestion for large numbers of licence holders who regularly pay their dues at this office.

Dental Health

    Dental health services require to be improved and expanded. The standards of dental care amongst the population are low. Only the younger generation of the population show the beneficial effects of fluoridation of the water supplies which started in 1958. Although the main effort for dental care is directed at school children (there are 50 school dental clinics) it reaches less than half the primary school-going population.

    Improvement in dental health will be achieved if there is greater attention to dental hygiene and if dental services become more universally available. Great stress will therefore be placed on an intensive and sustained dental health education programme. This will be started in the schools and then directed to the general public. Dissemination of dental health knowledge is regarded as urgent at this stage as our population is not conscious enough of the importance of maintaining dental health.

    The dental care services will be expanded as rapidly as resources are available. The limitation is the shortage of dentists (there being only 156 Division I registered dentists in Singapore; 60 of whom are in Government service). Priority will be given to the establishment of dental clinics for school children.

Medical Charges

    The maintenance of the Health Service and its plans for development make an ever growing demand on the public purse. Already 12 per cent of the Republic's budget (or $78,000,000) is apportioned to the Health Service. The rise in costs cannot be sustained without limit without either contracting the services or a fall in the standards of health practices.

    Neither alternative is acceptable. Hence some other means of holding down costs is essential.

    Since the services are almost entirely free a token proportion of the costs will have to be borne by patients if the services are not to be limited.

    A revision on medical fees is therefore being undertaken on the principle that services must be partially paid for. Examples of the proposed revision will be: -

    (a) extending charges for hospital care to classes of patients who have hitherto been treated and fed free;

    (b) increasing the number of paying class beds in hospitals (there are insufficient B class beds to meet the increasing demands chiefly due to the increasing number of collective agreements where employees, are entitled to admission to paying class wards);

    (c) charges for workmen injured at work and persons injured in road traffic accidents who are covered by Workmen's Compensation or third-party insurance schemes, respectively;

    (d) increasing outpatient charges per attendance and for attendances during Sundays and public holidays;

    (e) enhanced accouchement charges for the third and further child to discourage families who can least afford to increase their numbers.
