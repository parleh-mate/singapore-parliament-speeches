COMPLAINTS AGAINST NOISE
(Particulars)


    1. Mr Yeo Choo Kok asked the Minister for Home Affairs (a) how many cases of complaints against noise have been reported to the Police since 1st January, 1981; (b) what were the causes of the noise in all these cases; and (c) whether the existing laws are adequate for the Police to deter people from making excessive noise in housing estates as well as on the road.

    The Minister of State for Law and Home Affairs (Prof. S. Jayakumar) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, there were 15,611 cases of complaints against noise reported to the Police between 1st January, 1981 and 2nd February, 1982. This works to an average of 1,115 cases per month or 37 cases per day. This is not an alarming figure considering that we have some 500,000 households, 110,000 business establishments and 400,000 motor vehicles.

    The complaints are usually made against noise emanating from:

    (1) weddings, parties and other social functions;

    (2) the inconsiderate use of musical instruments and audio equipment;

    (3) family disputes and quarrels;

    (4) children crying or playing;

    (5) dogs barking;

    (6) motor vehicles, and

    (7) other sources, such as construction activity.

    The answer to the problem of noise pollution in housing estates as well as on the road is not legislation alone. Landscarce Singapore is a busy modern metropolis with considerable industrial and commercial activity. It is densely populated, and we have no choice but to live close to one another in high rise buildings. It is, therefore, inevitable that there will be a certain amount of noise from roads construction sites and our neighbourhoods. There must be a tolerance to a certain amount of noise which will result from our living conditions.

Column: 940



    The problem, however, is to prevent noise levels from becoming excessive or intolerable. There are several laws which deal with the problem of noise. The law under which the Police usually take action is the Minor Offences Act. Under the Act, the Police can only take action when excessive noise occurs between the hours of midnight and 6 a.m. As such, the Police are sometimes hampered by this limited provision. The other laws on noise pollution are as follows:

    (1) Motor Vehicle (Construction and Use) Rules, 1974, which deal with excessive noise from vehicles.

    (2) Environmental Public Health Act which deals with industrial noise, and

    (3) Public Entertainments Act, which controls the sound level of loud speakers.

The Police have been working on proposals to amend the laws but this is a complex matter and may take some time to finalize, as other departments controlling and regulating noise pollution activities are involved.

    People living together in a community such as ours, should learn to be considerate and refrain from making loud noises unnecessarily. At the same time, they should also be tolerant towards the occasional noises which are not intended to annoy. This will not only create a more friendly and pleasant neighbourhood to live in, but the Police will also have fewer complaints of this nature to investigate and can, therefore, deploy more men to combat crime.

     Prof. S. Jayakumar (for the Minister for Home Affairs):

The Misuse of Drugs Act 1973

    Mr Speaker, Sir, let me point out at the outset that under the Misuse of Drugs Act, 1973, drug addicts are committed to approved institutions, which are not detention centres, for compulsory treatment and rehabilitation. They are not detained for criminal offences. The Act provides for committal for treatment for six months at a time, subject to a maximum of 36 months. As of 15th February, 1982, 3,356 persons were receiving treatment in Drug Rehabilitation Centres (DRC). None of them had been detained for more than five years.

    Compulsory treatment and rehabilitation of drug addicts is similar to the compulsory isolation and treatment of those suffering from infectious diseases as well as those committed to mental institutions for violent behaviour. We consider drug addiction to be a pernicious disease which, if not checked, will erode the very fabric of our society. It is necessary to commit drug addicts for treatment and rehabilitation because drug addiction, like an infectious disease, spreads rapidly. If drug addicts are not identified and sent for treatment swiftly, they will continue to spread the habit to others in our community.

Safeguards

    There are adequate safeguards to ensure that non-addicts are not committed under the Misuse of Drugs Act. First, under the Act, the Director and the Deputy Director of the Central Narcotics Bureau (CNB) can order a drug addict, who has been confirmed to be a drug addict either through a positive urine test or by the medical doctor through medical examination and observation, to be admitted for treatment and rehabilitation at a DRC for a period not exceeding six months. Secondly, an addict's further stay in the DRC will be assessed by a Review Committee which is chaired by a medical doctor. This Committee will either transfer him to the Day Release Scheme or extend the committal period.

    The third safeguard is an elaborate system of collecting two urine samples from suspects and this has been in operation

Column: 942

since 1977. The system is fully laid down in the Misuse of Drugs (Urine Specimens and Urine Tests) Regulations, 1977. Under this, a suspected addict whose first urine test is positive can apply for a second urine test on the same sample. It ensures that no mistakes occur during urine collection.

    Fourthly, a drug addict can make a complaint under the Act, to a Magistrate pursuant to section 33D of the Misuse of Drugs Act that he has been improperly committed for rehabilitation. The Magistrate will conduct an inquiry into the complaint and he can order the discharge of the inmate from a DRC.

    Fifthly, as a further safeguard, of course, any person, through his counsel, can file a writ of habeas corpus in the High Court for the release of an inmate from the DRC on grounds of improper committal.

The Immigration Act

    Persons detained under the Immigration Act are prohibited immigrants, illegal immigrants or persons remaining unlawfully in Singapore. As of 15th February, 1982, there were 10 persons detained under this Act. Of this number, only one has been in detention for more than five years. Prohibited and illegal immigrants have to be detained. They have no right to be here and should not be allowed to stay here. Singapore cannot. be a haven for those who may be a burden to society and are unable to qualify for entry under the normal immigration regulations. Detention is an effective deterrent.

    As regards safeguards, there are sufficient safeguards to ensure that innocent persons are not detained under the Act. Each case is thoroughly examined and investigated to determine whether the person should be detained pending removal from Singapore. The person is brought before a magistrate in Chambers who authorizes the Detention Order signed by the Controller of Immigration if he is satisfied that the detention is lawful and valid. The Immigration Department reviews the detained cases regularly. Where the identity card and nationality of the detained person can be established, the appropriate foreign mission in Singapore is approached and requested to issue a travel document to facilitate repatriation.
