VALUATION LIST FOR 1960
(Variation of)


    1. Mr A. P. Rajah asked the Minister for National Development whether, in view of the present position of the law that, in the matter of fixing the annual value of premises in Singapore the rents of which are controlled under the Control of Rent Ordinance (Chapter 242), such annual value must be assessed on the basis of rents permitted under the said Ordinance, he will take appropriate steps to have the Valuation List for 1960 in respect of such premises varied to conform with the law.

    Mr Ong Eng Guan: Mr Speaker, Sir, due to the corruption in the City Council in the past ten years, we found in Singapore that assessment of the annual values of properties had not been properly done and not on an equitable basis. The result has been that a considerable number of property owners have benefited over a period of years from the undervaluation of their properties while others less fortunate have had their properties valued correctly.

    Due to the correct valuation taken by the City Council since the new Government came into existence, many millions of dollars of revenue which were lost hitherto have been recovered and which can be used for the benefit of the poor classes in erecting more standpipes, providing more electricity, constructing parks and playgrounds, kampong roads and many other amenities.

    The revaluation of properties has been done in certain areas which we believe have many vested interests connected with the City Council in the past ten years and there were many areas where many properties were not valued properly. At the same time, in our revaluation of such properties, the Assessment Department also touched on rent-controlled houses in which certain anomalies in the payment of assessment had been created due to rent control. Because of the existence of rent control, there are some people, mostly chief tenants, who

are now reaping rich profits from such rent-controlled houses. We think it is high time that the chief tenants of these houses should be made to bear a part of the cost of maintaining the essential services and providing better amenities to the poor. The revaluation for assessment purposes of rent-controlled houses has been done in some States of the Federation of Malaya. During the past few weeks, after the valuation list has been published, we found out that some landlords and chief tenants tried to exploit the situation by threatening to increase the rents of sub-tenants who are mainly the poor people.

    Mr Speaker, I am quite aware, as is pointed out in the question tabled by the Member for Farrer Park, that rent-controlled premises are in a special position in that the annual value must be assessed on the basis of the rents permitted to be charged under the Control of Rent Ordinance. VALUATION LIST FOR 1960
(Access to public)


    2. Mr A. P. Rajah asked the Minister for National Development whether he is aware that, despite the provisions of section 112 of the Local Government Ordinance, 1957, members of the public are being denied full and free access to the Valuation List for 1960 for purposes of inspection, and what steps he has taken or will take to ensure that the City Council complies with the provisions of the aforesaid section.

    Mr Ong Eng Guan: Mr Speaker, on 1st September, 1959, an estate agent who had been working as an officer in the City Council Assessment Department, when the Member for Farrer Park was there, wanted to take down a list of valuation of properties but was refused access to the 1960 valuation list because he could not produce letters of authority from the property owners. This was in keeping with previous departmental procedure based on the Municipal Ordinance. The City Council assessment officers were suspicious of their former colleague, who is now an estate agent, and who in 1958 whilst in the employ of the City Council had used City Council information for a High Court bank assessment case, that is the famous Chartered Bank case, and appeared as a witness on behalf of his clients while he was still in the service of the City Council. Because of his past conduct, the City Council Assessment Department refused him access to the

Column: 639

1960 valuation list until he could produce letters of authority from the particular property owners who would be his clients to look into the assessments of their particular properties. Unfortunately, Mr Speaker, the Local Government Ordinance promulgated in 1957 was not clear on the matter as to whether any member of the public could go to the City Council and look at the valuation list of all property owners with whom he has no connection whatsoever. Mr Speaker, the Municipal Ordinance on which the Local Government Ordinance, 1957, was based did not allow any member of the public to look at the valuation of any property unless he has got a letter of authority. It is based on the same principle as the Income Tax Department in not allowing any accountant or lawyer to have access to the Income Tax Department's assessment list of taxpayers. This precaution will restrict unscrupulous persons from touting for business by looking into other people's assessments where there will be many chances of corruption, particularly in the case of persons who have previously been engaged by the Assessment Department or the Income Tax Department.

    Mr Speaker, the matter was brought up to the High Court and we found that the Local Government Ordinance is different from the Municipal Ordinance on this matter and allowed access to the valuation list to any person even though he has no connection. We are studying the law and until such time as the relevant section of the Local Government Ordinance can be amended to prevent touts and unscrupulous agents from making use of such free access for their own ends, our hands are tied. If the Member for Farrer Park wants to know, this estate agent is Mr C. H. Williams.

CINEMATOGRAPH FILM HIRE DUTY
BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.50 p.m.

    The Deputy Prime Minister (Dr Toh Chin Chye): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have the Yang di-Pertuan Negara's recommendation to proceed with the Bill. Sir, I move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The cinema business is in a flourishing state. The revenue from entertainments duty in respect of admissions to cinemas has been increasing during the last two years. There have been built a number of new cinema theatres during

Column: 642

this period, and it has been found that the present rates of entertainments duty cannot be increased without the burden being passed to the patrons. The local exhibitors who have to bear a number of expenses would pass any additional taxation on the cinema industry to the patrons, unless legislation were passed to control the admission prices. On the one hand, the local distributors who have a share in the profits are subject to income tax. But the overseas distributors who obtain greater profits are not subject to local taxation. Large amounts of royalty are therefore being remitted overseas, tax free, as they are not within the charging provisions of the Income Tax Ordinance.

    Furthermore, it has been found that the greater the amount paid by the local distributors to the overseas producers, the smaller is the chargeable profit of the local distributors here, as these royalties are deducted from receipts to arrive at income. As the local distributors are, in the main, subsidiaries of the overseas producers, they can conveniently so arrange their business that the local distributors pay little income tax, and the great bulk of their gross income receipts are paid and remitted overseas without being subject to local taxation.

    At the moment the law is such that the royalty received by the producer is not subject to tax, since he is non-resident, and the income is derived outside Singapore and therefore not income chargeable within the scope of the Income Tax Ordinance.

    It is usual that agreements are made outside the country and payments effected outside, 50 that it does not suffer from local taxation. This exempts payment of tax on royalties received by producers resident overseas and in addition in respect of producers resident in the United Kingdom. The United Kingdom/Singapore Double Taxation Agreement exempts royalties from income tax when they are received by producers resident in the United Kingdom from Singapore. It is considered desirable that some measures be taken to extract a due from these overseas producers, and this objective is what the proposed Bill intends to achieve.

Column: 643



    It is based on similar legislation in force in New Zealand since the 1930's. The main contents of this Bill are indicated in the Explanatory Statement. But there are some main provisions in the Bill which should be mentioned.

    In clause 4, it is proposed to levy with effect from today a film hire duty in respect of proceeds which are derived from the renting of foreign films. It is not the intention of Government to impose this tax on films which are locally produced.

    "Renter" and "rent" are defined in clause 2.

    This duty is not allowed for deduction under subclause (8) of clause 4 for purposes of income tax on account of loss in revenue.

    Renters of these films will be required to be registered under clause 5, and to keep records as specified in clause 6.

    They are liable to pay duty at the rate of one-quarter of 60 per cent of such gross receipts as laid out in clause 9. It may be suggested why one-quarter of 60 per cent is used instead of a straight 15 per cent. Perhaps this ought to be explained. Film hire duty is in nature a customs duty, and therefore the country of origin of the contract has no bearing on the liability. In strict theory, it should be levied on the value of the film imported. It is virtually impossible, however, to ascertain the value of films at the point of entry by ordinary methods of valuation, such as actual cost, occurrent domestic value in the country of origin, and so on. The imposition of a film hire duty seeks to overcome this difficulty of valuation by making the gross rentals derived, the determining factor for valuation. How gross receipts from rentals should be calculated is set out in clause 10. From experience elsewhere, it has been found that 40 per cent of such gross receipts would be considered quite an adequate allowance to cover expenses and for profit. It is, therefore, considered that the levy should be imposed on the balance of 60 per cent. In other words, for every $1 that a non-resident producer takes out of this country, he is charged 15 cents.

Column: 644



    Nonetheless, I would like to say here that this quantum can be adjusted in the light of our experience. One-quarter is an elastic quantity and according to subsequent events and experience, if necessary, we shall adjust it.

    In clause 11 there is provision whereby the Minister may grant exemption in respect of all or any of the provisions of the Ordinance. The reason for this clause is this: for example, if a film society or a body or any other association wishes to exhibit a film, we would wish to exempt him from the tax defined in this Bill, and therefore discretion is left to the Minister to so exempt.

    It is not the purpose of Government to go at the small fry. We consider that tax should be borne by those who can bear it, and in this particular instance, taxation is imposed upon non-resident producers, those who make the money in this country escape from income tax by devious ways. Now it is natural that we would expect that when this film tax is imposed, the renter would try to pass it on to the cinema-going patrons. This has been forestalled by a clause here which says that the existing admission charges cannot be increased without the approval of the Minister. This clause is in clause 13. In other words, when this Bill comes into force, there should not be any increase in cinema charges, which, as it is, are already high. There are other clauses which make provisions for an effective collection of the duty. We do not pretend that this Bill is absolutely perfect. Someone may find loopholes. But when we discover that someone has used these loopholes, we shall plug them quite quickly.

    We estimate that in a year, the Government should be able to collect $1 million revenue from this taxation.
