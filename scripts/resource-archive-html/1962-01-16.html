    1.     Dr Goh Keng Swee: Mr Speaker, Sir, I have the Yang di-Pertuan Negara's recommendation that the Bill be proceeded with. I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Hon. Members may recall that this Bill was first introduced to the Assembly at the June 1961 sitting, and since then the Bill has lapsed. The contents of the Bill before the House now are the same as those introduced in June last year, and except for minor changes in dates and in the bringing of the Schedule up to date, there has not been any material change. The main purpose of the Bill is to secure proper control over the activities of the banks in Singapore and to provide proper supervision to ensure a sound basis for banking business which is so important to the State.

    The provisions of this Bill are modelled on the lines of the Banking Ordinance and the Central Bank Ordinance now in force in the Federation of Malaya. It is desirable that Singapore and the Federation should have parallel banking legislation as the banking system is common to both territories. The Banking Bill now before the House has been held in abeyance since June 1961, because of more urgent business that has occupied the House since the present Session began. It is now being re-introduced on a Certificate of Urgency. I am proceeding with this Bill throughout its three stages because of the recurrence of industrial disputes between certain local banks and their employees. These disputes have resulted

Column: 122

in considerable inconvenience to the public and to the trading and business community in the inter-bank clearance of cheques, and ths has interfered with the efficiency of the banking business in the State. At the moment, the Malayan Banking Association operates a mutual inter-bank clearing house with three of the bigger banks serving as clearing hunks. There is provision in the Bill for the setting up of a Clearing House by the Government and, if this Clearing House is set up, on the coming into force of the Ordinance, the inconvenience that arises out of any industrial dispute will be restricted in its effect.

    The Bill before the House is a fairly straightforward one and only a few words of explanation are necessary.

    Part I makes the necessary definitions. Banking business is defined very broadly and is not intended to limit any legitimate banking business.

    Part II is devoted to the licensing of banks. This Part provides that only banks holding licences from the Minister can carry on business in Singapore. Provision is made for conditions to be attached to licences and these conditions can be modified as and when necessary. The object of this section, is to ensure that banks which operate in the State have a sound financial structure and that their business integrity is not open to question. All new banks - whether local or foreign - have to obtain a licence. Existing banks which were incorporated before 1st January, 1962, will be given a licence. They will, however, have to satisfy certain capital requirements. In the case of new banks incorporated after 1st January, 1962, if its Head Office is in Singapore, its paid-up capital must be at least $2 million. If the Head Office is situated outside Singapore, its paid-up capital must be at least $5 million and it must hold at least $2 million in Singapore in respect of business in Singapore either in the form of immovable property or other approved assets. Existing banks incorporated before 1st January, 1962, will be given a period of grace of up to 10 years under Part VI to comply with the capital requirements.

    Part III sets out the main duties of licensed banks. Most of the duties set out in this Part will not in any way cause well established banks to depart from their existing practices and policies. There is provision for a licensed bank to inform the Minister of any alteration in the Bank's constitution or of any proposed change in ownership. Every licensed bank is also required to maintain a reserve fund to be built up out of its profits in the manner prescribed n the Bill. The reserve fund will of course greatly increase the soundness of the bank. Licensed banks are also not allowed to pay dividends until its capitalised expenditure has been written off; nor can it grant unsecured loans exceeding $5,000 to any of its directors or any firm in which the directors are interested. Certain other requirements are also set out to ensure the financial soundness of the bank. Certain statistics are also required to be furnished to the Inspector of Banks for the protection of depositors and to assist in the control of banks. A bank that considers that it is unable to meet financial obligations is required to inform the Minister. Licensed banks are also required to appoint auditors to be approved by the Minister.

    Part IV of the Bill deals with the powers of the Minister. In this Part the Bill differs from that of the Federation of Malaya Ordinance, where the Central Bank exercises the principal functions laid down in their Ordinance. Since we do not have a Central Bank, it is necessary to set up some other authority which can perform these functions. Provision is made for the Minister to establish a clearing house.

    An Inspector of Banks is to be appointed to be the authority to undertake investigations of banks both on a routine basis and when the Minister has reason to believe that a licensed bank is carrying on its business in an unsatisfactory way. The Inspector may also, on the advice of the Minister, assume control or exercise supervision over the business of a licensed bank in certain circumstances to protect the interests of depositors and creditors. But these are reserve powers which will be used only when the need arises. The powers of the Minister, which can be delegated to the
