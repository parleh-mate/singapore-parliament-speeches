    Dr Goh Keng Swee: Mr Speaker, Sir, the proposal to establish a Common Market arrangement in respect of a limited number of commodities produced either in the Federation or Singapore was first mooted by the Singapore Government in September 1959. As a result of subsequent discussions both at official and ministerial levels, the two Governments agreed to form a Joint Committee of Officials to discuss the proposal submitted by the Singapore Government for the establishment of a Common Market.

    Three meetings of the Joint Committee of Officials were held between 10th June, 1960, and 25th August, 1960. In the course of these meetings, the officials of the Federation Government sought elucidation on certain points, and both sides examined in detail the implications of the proposal. After the conclusion of the first series of meetings, the Federation representative asked for time to consider these proposals. It is hoped that talks on this question will be held shortly between Ministers of the Governments of Singapore and the Federation.

    The Bill seeks to introduce into Singapore the doctrine of what is commonly known as "diminished responsibility". This was originally a Scottish doctrine but was imported into England by the Homicide Act, 1957. The present law relating to insanity is contained in section 84 of the Penal Code which reads as follows:

    `Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of unsoundness of mind, is incapable of knowing the nature of the act, or that he is doing what is either wrong or contrary to law.'

Column: 1510



    This provision, Sir, is based on the so-called M'Naghten Rules, which summarised the advice given by the Judges to the House of Lords in M'Naghten's Case in 1843. According to this provision, the relevant test of insanity is a legal test and not a medical or psychiatric test. For the defence of insanity to succeed, it is necessary to establish that the accused was suffering at the time of the offence from a defect of reason due to disease of the mind so that he either did not know what he was doing or did not know that it was wrong. If the accused is not suffering from a disease of the mind or is not suffering from a form of insanity which affects the intellectual powers as distinguished from the will or emotions, he is not within the M'Naghten Rules.

    Clause 2 of the Bill, which is based on section 2 of the Homicide Act, 1957, of England, provides that culpable homicide shall not be murder if the offender was suffering from such abnormality of mind (whether arising from a condition of arrested or retarded development of mind or any inherent causes or induced by disease or injury) as substantially impaired his mental responsibility for his acts and omissions in causing the death or being a party to causing the death. The effect, Sir, is that diminished responsibility will be a mitigating factor and, to quote from the report of the Royal Commission on Capital Punishment in England, it is a "device to enable the courts to take account of a special category of mitigating circumstances in cases of murder and to avoid passing sentence of death in cases where such circumstances exist."

    The exception of diminished responsibility is quite different from that of insanity. Insanity covered by the M'Naghten Rules and section 84 of the Penal Code is a complete defence leading to the verdict of acquittal while diminished responsibility merely reduces the offence to culpable homicide not amounting to murder.

    The provision of the new Exception 7 to section 300 of the Penal Code, which are proposed to be inserted by clause 2 of the Bill, cover mental abnormality whether induced by mental or physical disease or injury or arising from mental

Column: 1511

deficiency or other inherent causes. These words would appear to cover mental abnormality due to any form of insanity, to mental defectiveness, epilepsy, psycho neurosis arising from inherent causes and forms of psychopathic personality arising from arrested or retarded development of the mind. The onus of proving that he comes within the exception is by virtue of section 106 of the Evidence Ordinance on the accused, and it is for him to satisfy the jury not only that abnormality within the definition contained in the Exception existed at the time of the offence, but that it was of such a degree that it substantially impaired his mental responsibility for the acts with which he is charged. The use of the term "mental responsibility" makes it clear that it is not legal responsibility which is in question at this point. Legal responsibility is diminished if the criteria contained in the Exception are satisfied, but diminution of legal responsibility is not itself one of those criteria.

    The doctrine of diminished responsibility has been applied in England for over five years and has on the whole been applied successfully. The doctrine has also been adopted by a number of Commonwealth countries and recently the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council has given authoritative advice on the application of the doctrine in the case of Rose vs the Queen, in an appeal from the Supreme Court of the Bahama Islands. In the English case of Regina vs Byrne (1960) 2 QB 396 Lord Chief Justice Parker in delivering the judgment of the Court of Criminal Appeal said:

    `"Abnormality of mind" which has to be contrasted with the time-honoured expression in the M'Naghten Rules "defect of reason" means a state of mind so different from that of ordinary human beings that the reasonable man would term it abnormal. The expression "mental responsibility for his acts" points to a consideration of the extent to which the accused's mind is answerable for his physical acts which must include a consideration of the extent of his ability to exercise will power to control his physical acts'.

    The Lord Chief Justice then went on to cite from Scots cases indicating that "such abnormality as substantially impairs his mental responsibility" involved a mental state which in popular language (not that of the M'Naghten Rules)

a jury would regard as amounting to partial insanity or being on the borderline of insanity. Lord Tucker, in giving the opinion of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in Rose vs the Queen, referred to the case of Regina vs Byrne and said:

    `Their Lordships respectfully accept this interpretation of the words "abnormality of mind" and "mental responsibility" as authoritative and correct. They would not, however, consider that the Court of Criminal Appeal was intending to lay down that in every case the jury must necessarily be directed that the test was always to be the borderline of insanity. There might be cases in which the abnormality of mind relied upon could not readily be related to any of the generally recognized types of "insanity". If, however, insanity was to be taken into consideration, as undoubtedly would usually be the case, the word must be used in its broad popular sense. It could not too often be emphasized that there was no formula that could safety be used in every case - the direction to the jury must always be related to the particular evidence that had been given and there might be cases where the words "borderline" and "insanity" might not be helpful.'

    The doctrine of "diminished responsibility" was considered at the United Nations Seminar on the role of the Substantive Criminal Law in the protection of Human Rights held at Tokyo last year and the Seminar in its report recommended the adoption of the doctrine.

Detainees


    4. Dato Abdul Hamid Bin Haji Jumat asked the Minister for Home Affairs to state the number of persons detained during the period 3rd June, 1959, to 30th April, 1961, under (i) the Preservation of Public Security Ordinance, and (ii) the Criminal Law (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance.

    Mr Ong Pang Boon:

    (i) On 3rd June, 1959, there were 61 persons under detention under the Preservation of Public Security Ordinance. Of these, 35 have since been released.

    During the period 3.6.1959 to 30.4.1961, an additional 27 persons were detained, of whom 18 were in connection with A.R.T.I.S. and 2 were connected with K.M.T. activities. Of these 27 persons 9 have been released.

    As at 30.4.1961, there were altogether 44 persons under detention.

    (ii) The number of persons detained under the Criminal Law (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance in the period 3.6.1959 to 30.4.1961 was 513.

Housing: cost of building, land and subsidy


    5. Dato Abdul Hamid Bin Haji Jumat asked the Minister for National Development what will be the building costs of the following types of accommodation: - (i) those to be let out at

Column: 1562

$20 p.m., (ii) those to be let out at $40 p.m., and (iii) those to be let out at $60 p.m.; and what will be the proportionate cost of land and the approximate subsidy of each of the above categories.

    Mr Tan Kia Gan: The average building costs of the following types of accommodation are:-

    (i) 1-room unit to be let out at $20 p.m. - $3,000 per unit

    (ii) 2-room unit at $40 p.m. - $3,700 per unit

    (iii) 3-room unit at $60 p.m. - $4,700 per unit

    I should explain that the building costs include the following three elements: construction cost, cost of site work and cost of land. The subsidy applicable to units of any particular housing project will depend very much on the cost of the land involved, which can vary considerably, from 5 cents per sq. ft, to as high as $10 per sq. ft. It is therefore not possible to say generally what the subsidy will be for a particular type of unit; the information can be given only in respect of a particular housing estate.
