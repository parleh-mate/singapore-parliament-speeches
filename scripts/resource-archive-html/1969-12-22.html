BRAIN DRAIN FROM
SINGAPORE


    4. Mr N. Govindasamy asked the Minister for Education whether he is aware of any brain drain from the Republic as a result of persons going abroad for further education or specialised training and failing to return to Singapore on completion of their courses; and if he will consider taking steps to minimise this brain drain.

    Dr Lee Chiaw Meng (for the Minister for Education): Mr Speaker, Sir, the problem of a brain drain from the Republic has not been seriously studied because it is not regarded as a serious one at present, particularly in regard to scientists and technologists going to other countries.

    Persons going abroad for further education or specialised training are either government scholars or private students. For government scholars in respect of whom proper records are maintained, the percentage who have failed to return to Singapore in the past on completion of a course is insignificant. For private students, no specific records are maintained. But the number who choose not to return has not been noticeable. This is perhaps because the number is small.

    Appropriate measures will be taken to minimise the brain drain from the Republic should the problem become serious.

PUBLIC TRANSPORT SYSTEM
(Committee to review)


    5. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister for Communications whether he will consider setting up a committee to review the public transport system in the Republic; to assess public needs in the next five years, and to make recommendations.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Communications (Mr Phua Bah Lee) (for the Minister for Communications): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Member for Punggol may recall that in July 1968, the Minister for Communications announced in Parliament that he would establish a Transport Advisory Board to study ways and means to improve the present public transport service in Singapore. This Board was, in fact, set up the following month and has since been holding regular monthly meetings.

    The Board submitted its first interim report to the Minister in February this year and Government's acceptance of most of the Board's recommendations was announced in his speech at the opening ceremony of the Transport and Communications Exposition on 1st August. The most important recommendation, being the compulsory wearing of crash helmets by motor cyclists, scooterists and their pillion-riders, is being implemented in stages as from November 1969.

    The Board is currently studying the problem of uniform legislation for bus companies in Singapore and also the possible re-routing of existing bus routes so as to provide more efficient and direct services for commuters from their homes to their places of work.

    As for long-term solutions of our transport problems, this needs the attention of better qualified professional experts and planners. Two senior officials from the London Transport Board have already submitted "a preliminary study of possible new measures to improve and expand Singapore's mass transport facilities", which report is being examined by officials from my Ministry and those from the Ministries of National

Column: 168

Development and Finance. Furthermore, a team of United Nations advisers attached to our State and City Planning Office have in the past two years been studying and assessing Singapore's long-term needs in public transport, including the long-range aspects of providing a mass transit system for Singapore.
