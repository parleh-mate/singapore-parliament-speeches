PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS



     The President (Mr Wee Kim Wee): Honourable Members:

A New World

    We are at a turning point in international affairs. A major war is in progress in the Persian Gulf. Its outcome will determine whether there can be a durable peace in the Middle East, and whether the United Nations and the United States emerge strengthened from the crisis. Already the decisive response of the multinational forces to the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait has established the principle that armed aggression is not an acceptable way to further national ambitions.

    Last year, after the Communist regimes in Eastern Europe collapsed, and the Soviet Union committed itself to perestroika and glasnost, prospects for world peace seemed favourable. Major international tensions had been resolved. The super-powers would disarm, and nations would enjoy a peace dividend.

    But recent events in the Soviet Union show that the transition from Communism and a command economy to democracy and a free market is difficult and uncertain. The shape of the new landscape is still unclear. While overall the change is undoubtedly for the better, the post Cold War world will have its own dangers and tensions.

The Next Lap

    Within our region, prospects are favourable. The tremendous economic

Column: 7

growth of East and Southeast Asia is likely to continue. In Singapore, we have started a new chapter in our young history. Mr Goh Chok Tong took office as Prime Minister in November 1990. A new generation of leaders is in place.

    The Independence generation of Singaporeans created the prosperous and secure nation that we know. They started out with precious little, but have handedon to us many valuable assets. The creation of an Elected Presidency to safeguard our accumulated savings and national reserves symbolises this transformation. Today we can plan ahead for the next 25 years, and marshall the resources needed to turn bold ideas into reality.

    Over the past year, my Government has been working on long term plans to secure Singapore's future. The main proposals are contained in the book "The Next Lap" which is attached to my Address this evening.

Common Ownership and Collective Responsibility

    In the next lap, we must strengthen the sense of common ownership and collective responsibility among Singaporeans. We want to change attitudes and develop civic pride. Every citizen should treat Singapore as he would treat his own home.

    Singapore is our home. It belongs to us, all of us. Each one shares in Singapore's security and future. Each has a stake in its wealth and prosperity. We must not only maintain our common home. We must work together to build, expand, and upgrade it.

    To achieve this, my Government will widen participation in policy making, invest more in our people, and increase the public and private assets of Singaporeans.

Widening Participation

    Every citizen should contribute to make Singapore a better place for all of us. When Singaporeans take an active interest

Column: 8

in public affairs, and see their own suggestions making a difference to the community, they will identify more closely with the success of Singapore. When policies incorporate ideas from many citizens, and take into account their reservations and concerns, the policies will not only be less imperfect, but will also command wider support and acceptance.

    For several years, the Government has promoted greater consultation. Feedback Unit forums and Select Committee hearings have become established avenues for collecting views and improving policies. Members of the public participate in many advisory groups rendering advice to government ministries. We will widen the circle of public participation in national issues.

GPCs.

    Government Parliamentary Committees will play a more prominent role. We will encourage the public to give their views to the GPCs, especially on issues which do not go to Select Committee. The GPCs should not only express these views in Parliament, but also follow them through by proposing amendments to improve legislation.

Service Quality Improvement Unit.

    Besides discussing policies, many citizens are directly concerned with the quality of service they receive in Government offices - when they pay bills, apply for permits, or write to ministries. The Government will set up a Service Quality Improvement Unit under the Prime Minister's Office, to monitor and improve upon the standard of public administration. All Singaporeans who come into contact with Government departments and statutory boards should suggest ways for these organisations to improve their service. The aim is to maintain the highest possible standards in our public services.

Human Resources

    People are our most precious resource. We will devote a larger percentage of our GDP to education. We will build more schools and train more teachers. We will

Column: 9

convert all secondary schools to single-session, and later primary schools as well. The ratio of teachers to students will improve.

    We will invest in tertiary education. Eventually, 40% of each age group should qualify for university or polytechnic, without compromising academic standards. This year, Nanyang Technological Institute will become a full university, Nanyang Technological University. We have set up a third polytechnic, Temasek Polytechnic. By the turn of the century, we may need a third university and a fourth polytechnic.

    The present structure of our education system is sound. But there is room for improvement and fine-tuning. Our aim is to give every child at least 10 years of primary and secondary education. The system should offer children a wider range of educational choices, adapted to their individual aptitudes and needs. It should give more help to weaker pupils, who presently go to the extended and monolingual streams. It should guide such pupils to build on their strengths, especially through technical and vocational education, in order to do better both in school and later on the job market.

    We will focus on moral and civic education. The values we share determine how competitive we are economically and how strong we are as one nation. Children should grow up imbued with sound values, aware of Singapore's strengths and vulnerabilities, and conscious of what Singaporeans must do to earn a living for ourselves.

    Every Singaporean should be bilingual in his mother tongue and in English, but not all can achieve this with the same ease or competence. While few Malay and Indian children have problems mastering their mother tongues, some Chinese children experience considerable difficulty learning Chinese. We will improve the teaching of Chinese to help pupils achieve a working competence with less stress and effort.

    As an investment in the next generation, we are establishing an Edusave Scheme. Every schoolchild from the age of 6 to 16 will have an Edusave account. Each year the accounts will receive some money

Column: 10

from an Endowment Fund. Children can use Edusave for part of their school expenses. This will help to equalize opportunities for every generation, and allow parents to decide how best to use the money to educate their children.

    Education is also the key to further Malay progress. My Government supports the efforts of MENDAKI and other community organisations to uplift the Malays. MENDAKI plays a key role, but to succeed many groups must contribute, not MENDAKI alone. Through MENDAKI, the Government will channel assistance to individual community groups whose programmes support the mission and objectives of MENDAKI. The measure of their success is not whether Malays have caught up with other Singaporeans. Rather it is whether the community can achieve successive fixed targets which they set for themselves. In other words, how far the Malays have progressed in absolute terms.

Investing in Infrastructure

    My Government will work to increase the assets of Singaporeans. The best way is to generate economic growth and invest in our infrastructure.

    We will reclaim more land from the sea. The new land can be used to expand the port and airport, and for industries, housing and recreation.

    We will extend the MRT to Woodlands. Work will begin this year, and the extension will be completed in 5 to 7 years. The 16 km line will link the Yishun and Chua Chu Kang stations via Woodlands New Town. It will benefit 130,000 residents in the Northern part of Singapore.

    We will make Teleview more widely available, and help families gain access to the system. Eventually, Teleview terminals will be as common and indispensable as telephones are today.

    These will be shared assets of Singaporeans.

Increasing Individuals' Assets

    We can also give every citizen a more direct sense of ownership, so that he will

Column: 11

feel that he in fact owns a part of Singapore. One major asset Singaporeans own is our HDB flats. We are now almost a 100% home-owning society. Over the next two decades, we will modernize the older HDB estates in a multi-billion dollar programme. Many HDB blocks will be refurbished to higher standards. Others will be rebuilt.

    Some poorer families living in rental flats still cannot afford to own their own homes, sometimes after working and saving for many years. My Government will introduce new schemes to enable as many of them as possible to own their homes before they are 45 years old.

    While incomes of Singaporeans have generally gone up, a few will always need special assistance, either for themselves or for their children. Our philosophy is to provide many helping hands to those in need. The Government will work with charity and community organizations to help them.

The International Position

    Our preoccupations must not be only domestic. Most important, we must look outwards to secure our place in the international community. In a shrinking world, to think in terms of Singapore International is a matter of necessity, not choice.

Singapore International Foundation

    We will help Singaporeans overseas to organize themselves in clubs and associations, to keep them conscious of their national identity. We will set up a Singapore International Foundation to build an overseas network of friends of Singapore.

Talent

    Singapore is what it is today because we have attracted talent from all over the world, especially Asian talent. No city can maintain its excellence by drawing only on indigenous talent. Singaporeans must welcome talent from abroad. Our island and economy are big enough to accommodate all the talent that we can find.

Column: 12



    Nowadays, talent is internationally mobile. Singapore competes against other countries not only to attract foreign talent, but even to retain our own. Whether in terms of infrastructure, housing, or culture, our total environment must compare well with other leading cities in the world.

The Growth Triangle

    Singapore is cooperating actively with Indonesia and Malaysia to promote economic development in the Riaus and Johor. All three parties have more to gain by working together as one Growth Triangle, than by competing singly, both against one another and against others. In the last year, this cooperation has made dramatic progress. Joint tourism, industrial, and water projects are taking off in Batam and Bintan. Singapore and Johor are discussing cooperation in manpower training, investment promotion, and development of water resources. The Growth Triangle promises to mark a new and historic phase in our economic and political relationship with Indonesia and Malaysia.

    Beyond our immediate neighbours, Singapore will continue to support ASEAN and wider regional groupings like the Asia Pacific Economic Cooperation (APEC) forum, and Malaysia's proposal for an East Asian Economic Grouping (EAEG). We also support a stronger UN, which will be a key institution maintaining international law and order.

National Security

    Prospects for continued peace and stability in the region are good. But we should never forget the lessons of Kuwait, and neglect our national security. Singapore took a firm stand against the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait. We have a vested interest in not condoning a stronger country using force against a weaker one.

    We will continue military co-operation with friendly countries, both bilaterally and through the Five-Power Defence Arrangements. We encourage the US to maintain a military presence in the region for as long as possible. But we cannot depend solely on others for our security. No diplomatic treaties or friendly forces will preserve us, unless Singaporeans are

Column: 13

prepared to fight and die to defend Singapore.

    National Servicemen and reservists make a vital contribution to our security. We must recognise their contribution, although we can never compensate them fully for their heavy responsibilities. Total Defence and National Service must remain our way of life.

Conclusion

    Before the Gulf crisis, the immediate future appeared full of promise. Now it is more uncertain. The next 25 years will bring many more surprises. We may not be able to control the external environ-

Column: 14

ment, but we must respond effectively to changes in it.

    While we develop our economy and city, we must also secure our international position. Widening participation in civic life, investing in people and infrastructure, and increasing the assets of Singaporeans will help to strengthen our sense of common ownership and collective responsibility. The basics have not changed: be united and look after one another, work hard and invest, stay vigilant and seize opportunities. These fundamentals will secure our future.

MINISTRY OF EDUCATION
DR TONY TAN KENG YAM
Minister for Education


    The quality of education will be further improved by providing good educational infrastructure and facilities, training enough teachers and fine-tuning the education system to meet the needs of our people.

Single Session Schools

    By 1999, all secondary schools in Singapore will be single session. Ten primary schools will take part in a trial in 1992 on extended single session day schools. If successful, the system will eventually be extended to all primary schools. With additional staff, we will change the school hours to provide children with a fuller and more varied education.

Teachers

    The key to better education is more and better trained teachers. The National Institute of Education (NIE) will be established as part of the Nanyang Technological University (NTU). It will offer degree level teacher training courses and diploma courses in specialised fields. To attract enough good teachers, salaries must be competitive. In addition, because of limited local manpower, we will have to rely on some overseas recruitment.

Preparatory Year

    The Preparatory Year Programme launched in 1990 provides children with a firm foundation in the mother tongue. Eventually all 5-year olds will attend the Preparatory Year Programme. All children will receive at least 10 years of general education before they decide whether to pursue an academic or a vocational/ technical career.

Column: 16



Secondary Schools and VITB

    Secondary schools will offer a technical stream to complement the existing academic stream. Students in both streams will have the opportunity to progress to institutions of higher learning, including the universities. As at present, children will be allowed to transfer from one stream to another as they mature and discover where their talents lie.

    The Vocational and Industrial Training Board will be revamped. It will take in only students with secondary education. Its courses will be correspondingly upgraded.

    More independent schools will be established. Government and Government-aided schools will have more autonomy to run their own operations and structure their educational programmes to suit the needs of their pupils.

Tertiary Education

    Temasek Polytechnic will be completed by 1995. We will study the need to establish more polytechnics and possibly a third university by the turn of the century. Our target is for 40% to 50% of each cohort to receive tertiary education. This must be done without lowering standards.

Resources

    These improvements require more investment by Government in education. We will raise the percentage of GDP devoted to education from the present 4% to 5%.

    The Government will establish an Edusave scheme for every child from the age of 6 to 16. Every year, a sum will be paid into the child's Edusave account to help meet his educational expenses. What is not used will be transferred into the CPF when the child becomes an adult. The Edusave scheme will give parents more choice in the type of education they want for their children.

Column: 17

MINISTRY OF LABOUR
MR LEE YOCK SUAN
Minister for Labour


    Together with employers and workers, we will maintain a conducive work environment, and create more and better jobs for our workforce. We will protect the safety, health and social security of our workers.

Labour Relations

    We will strive to preserve our excellent industrial relations climate.

    The flexible wage system is in place in both the unionised and non-unionised sectors. Companies are in a better position to weather an economic downturn.

More Efficient Use of Manpower

    We need to make better use of scarce manpower resources. To promote part-time employment, we have issued guidelines to help employers and workers work out suitable part-time schemes.

    70% of unionised firms have raised the retirement age of their workers to 60 years. Progress in non-unionised firms, however, has been slow. We will decide soon whether to legislate the raising of the retirement age.

Foreign Workers

    We will continue to permit a controlled inflow of foreign workers to complement the local workforce and facilitate economic growth.

    We will promote the recruitment and assimilation of skilled and compatible foreign workers. For the construction industry, we have introduced a two-tier levy system with a lower levy for skilled foreign workers.

    The Employment of Foreign Workers Act has been enacted. Employers caught with illegal workers will face higher penalties, including mandatory jail sentences for repeat offenders.

Column: 18



Industrial Health and Safety

    We will protect workers' health by preventing and controlling occupational health hazards and diseases.

    We will encourage factories to adopt self-regulatory measures to prevent accidents. We will request more industries to run safety orientation courses as is being done for construction and shipyard workers.

Social Security

    We will continue to improve services to CPF members and extend CPF to more Singaporeans, including the self-employed. We will gradually restore the CPF contribution rate to the long-term target of 40%, with equal contributions by employers and employees.

Column: 18

MINISTRY OF HEALTH
MR YEO CHEOW TONG
Minister for Health


    We aim to build a healthy population and to provide a high standard of health care for all Singaporeans.

Hospital Services

    The restructuring programme gives government hospitals greater management autonomy, to respond better to the needs of the patients. Patient surveys have shown that the restructured hospitals have indeed improved their service. Tan Tock Seng Hospital will be restructured this year.

    Hospitals will continue to improve their efficiency and productivity, to contain increases in health care costs. While Class A and B1 patients will eventually have to pay for the full cost of the services, the Government will continue to subsidise the Class B2 and C wards heavily, to ensure that they remain affordable to lower income groups. The poor and indigent will continue to enjoy waiver of medical fees.

    This year, we will begin rebuilding Tan Tock Seng Hospital and Kandang Kerbau Hospital, and constructing a new general

Column: 19

hospital in the eastern part of the island. These new hospitals will have enough B2 and C Class beds to meet the needs of the lower income groups. We will also upgrade Alexandra Hospital. The community hospital in Ang Mo Kio and a new psychiatric hospital to replace Woodbridge Hospital will be completed in 1992. We will begin construction of the National Dental Centre and the National Institute of Health in 1992.

Primary Health

    We will continue to educate the public to lead a healthy lifestyle. We will intensify efforts to discourage smoking, and to emphasize the importance of a balanced diet, regular exercise and management of stress. We will expand screening programmes to detect diseases early, and genetic counselling services to prevent hereditary diseases.

    The Hougang Polyclinic will open in March 91, and Yishun and Woodlands Polyclinics in 1992. We will expand the training programme for family physicians to further upgrade the standard of primary health care in Singapore.

Elderly

    We will set up three more Senior Citizens' Health Care Centres in the next two years. The community health care programme for senior citizens and the Home Nursing Service will be expanded to meet the needs of the elderly.

Children

    We will improve health services for children. The health surveillance programme to detect early any physical and developmental abnormalities in children will be expanded. A developmental stimulation programme will guide parents to help their children develop to their maximum potential.

Birth Rates

    Response to the new population policy has been encouraging. The total fertility rate increased from 1.4 in 1986 to 1.9 in

1990. We will continue to encourage Singaporeans to have three children and more if they can afford it, in order to achieve replacement fertility levels.

MINISTRY OF TRADE AND INDUSTRY
BG (Res) LEE HSIEN LOONG
Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Trade and Industry


The Economy

    The Singapore economy grew by 8.5% in 1990. We can sustain this high growth by developing our human resources and R & D capabilities, upgrading our infrastructure, co-operating with our neighbours, and establishing linkages with our international and regional markets. Our competitive advantage must continue to be a competent and hardworking workforce and excellent infra-structure.

    Singapore will be a total business centre with first-class services to back up her excellent physical infrastructure. We will make Singapore a global hub for air and sea transport, and telecommunications.

Developing Human Resources

    People are our key resource. We will develop a culture for quality in our workers. We will encourage companies to increase spending on training to 4% of payroll with the help of the Skills Development Fund. The NPB will create more training opportunities for our people to continually upgrade their knowledge and capabilities.

Developing R & D Capabilities

    We must harness technology to give our workforce a competitive edge, and help us move into a new phase of industrial development. The National Science & Technology Board (NSTB) will promote development of scientific and technological capabilities. It will co-ordinate national R and D efforts. R & D expenditure should reach 2% of GNP by the year 2000.

    We will draw up a Science and Technology Masterplan to identify the areas Singapore can enter, e.g., bio-technology,

Column: 21

micro-electronics, information technology, and how to strengthen our capabilities in these areas. To build up a critical mass of R & D personnel, we are considering creating a Science Habitat, which will be a conducive place for scientists and researchers to work, and live in.

Upgrading Infrastructure

    JTC will start to renew its older industrial sites. Plans for the first business park, to be located at Jurong East, will be finalised this year.

    JTC is studying enlarging and merging the group of islands south of Jurong into a site for chemical industries. Engineering studies have begun.

    The PUB will invest $3.4 billion over the next three years to expand its generation, production, transmission and distribution capacities for electricity, water and piped gas. Agreements for the supply of water and natural gas from Malaysia have been signed. Discussions for the supply of water and natural gas from Indonesia are in progress.

    TDB will initiate new projects to help Singapore companies compete in international markets. A Trade Intelligence Information Hub will give our traders an edge in a rapidly changing international trading environment. A Fashion Resource Centre will help local designers, manufacturers and fashion houses to keep abreast of the latest developments in the international fashion scene.

Establishing International Linkages

    Singapore will continue its outward-looking economic strategy. We will continue to search for investments which introduce new technology to Singapore and generate activities with higher value added and skills content.

    We will encourage more local companies to seek opportunities abroad. We will help local enterprises to upgrade their operations and become more efficient. SISIR will step up its efforts to help Singapore-based companies meet the required standards to export to the European Community and elsewhere.

Co-operating With Our Neighbours

    We will continue to strengthen economic ties with countries in the region. The Growth Triangle is a tangible example of economic co-operation with our neighbours. We will expand the co-operation to cover other areas, eg, developing tourist resorts, industrial estates and water catchments in Bintan, and developing a Science Park with Johor. We will continue to co-operate with neighbouring countries to make the Growth Triangle a dynamo for the Asia-Pacific region.

Tourism

    The STPB will intensify its promotional and marketing efforts, especially in the fast-growing Asia-Pacific region. Singapore welcomed 5 million visitors in 1990. This year will see the opening of the Tang Dynasty Village, Bugis Street, Telok Ayer Market, Underwater World at Sentosa and the renovated Raffles Hotel. These will make Singapore a more attractive tourist destination and provide more recreational outlets for Singaporeans. The Southern Islands will be developed into a major tourist resort.

MINISTRY OF FINANCE
DR RICHARD HU
Minister for Finance


Fiscal Policy

    The Government's fiscal policies will continue to support investment and economic growth.

    The private sector will remain the engine of growth. The public sector will be kept small to limit its claims on financial and manpower resources.

    Our tax structure will encourage savings, promote investments and stimulate economic growth. Corporate tax rates will be kept internationally competitive. Tax on individuals will remain broad-based, equitable and geared towards rewarding hard work and entrepreneurship.

    We will publish a White Paper on the Government's proposals for a broad-based consumption tax, although actual

Column: 23

implementation of the tax will be deferred while the revenue position permits.

    Recurrent expenditures will be restrained, to free more resources for investments in infrastructure and education and to improve the quality of life. Public housing will be upgraded. Substantial funds will be allocated for new facilities for the arts, sports and recreation.

Monetary Policy

    High economic growth and a tight labour market have generated some inflationary pressures. Our monetary and exchange rate policies will continue to be geared towards containing inflation and maintaining price stability.

Regulatory Policy

    The integrity of the financial system is the sine qua non of a financial centre. We aim to maintain high regulatory standards without imposing unnecessary restrictions on financial institutions.
