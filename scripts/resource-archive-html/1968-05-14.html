    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Education (Dr Lee Chiaw Meng): Mr Speaker, Sir, as I was saying yesterday when the House adjourned, it is time to reorganise our educational system to keep pace with our industrialisation success.

    Let us review our present position. Last year, of the 144,000 students in secondary schools, only 18,000 were in technical and vocational schools. The ratio is about seven to one. Last year, we produced 417 engineers and scientists as compared with 178 at the sub-professional level.

    We compare very poorly with other industrialised countries. Take Japan for example; for every five secondary school students, two are in the technical and vocational schools, and for every professional engineer they produce, there are about five supporting skilled technicians.

    At the same time, we need skilled workmen to build and repair ships and sailors and officers to man them, personnel to run our hotels, builders to meet the demands created by our plans

on housing, urban renewal and development. In short, in order that our plans on industrialisation and economic development may continue to be successful, as it has been, we must have sufficient and capable human talent in the top strata, but, more important, we must also provide a much larger group of supporting personnel in the lower echelons. We need to reorganise our educational system to meet this demand.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, may I digress a bit? When we reorganise our secondary education system, we also keep in mind the womenfolk of this country. I can assure the Member for Delta that the only outlet for women is not in bars as waitresses or as dance hostesses. For example, at the Productivity Centre of the Economic Development Board, a course is run exclusively for girls.

    I now come back to the main issue. Sir, may I be permitted to go briefly into the types of personnel that we require to meet our industrial needs? We have engineers, planners, etc, at the professional level, and down the ladder, we have senior technicians, technicians, craftsmen and operatives. The senior technicians, whom we generally call technical assistants, are trained at the Singapore Polytechnic for the Technician Diploma. Already, we have the cream of the secondary school students taking these courses. Some of them even have the Higher School Certificate. Last year, for example, the Polytechnic received about 2,000 applications for about 700 vacancies.

    Then we have technicians and craftsmen, such as the Craft Diploma graduates of the Singapore Vocational Institute. Already two such institutes are being built and more will be constructed in the next few years. They are, in fact, the key personnel in factories, and we are very short of them. Lower down, we have the vocational schools which unfortunately are considered to be for the "drop-outs". We will rectify this. After leaving school, these students will have a period of in-plant or industrial training to qualify as operatives specialising in one type of trade. The institutional

training in a vocational school provides a general background education, while the in-plant training will be so orientated as to meet the requirements of our industries from time to time.

    There will be a new department created within the Ministry of Education to plan, co-ordinate and administer our technical education and industrial training at the sub-professional level. This department will be supervised by a Cabinet Committee, as outlined in the Addendum to the President's Speech under the head Ministry of Education.

The present vocational schools will be reorganised, converted and their syllabuses modified. They will no longer be regarded as schools for drop-outs. Industrial Training Centres will be established to provide the in-plant training facilities to complement the institutional training. We hope that by next year we will be able to see physically the first of these changes, and by 1971, we will have some 30,000 students in the technical and vocational streams. By then, the ratio will be in the region of three and a half to one.
