REPORT ON ECONOMIC
DEFENCE SCHEME
(Statement by the Minister for Finance)


2.48 p.m.

    Mr Speaker: Before we go on to the introduction of Government Bills, is there any Minister wishing to make a statement?

    The Minister for Finance (Dr Goh Keng Swee): Mr Speaker, Sir, you may recollect that on the 28th November last year I described to the Assembly, in the course of my Budget Speech, the situation facing the economy of Singapore following upon Indonesia's economic confrontation. I also outlined the measures that the Government proposed to take to offset the adverse effects of economic confrontation. I said that a study was made in July 1963, some months before the trade suspension actually took place, to assess the pos sible effects of an economic blockade of Singapore by Indonesia. Our calculations showed that some 8,200 workers will eventually he laid off in the event that the Indonesian blockade is completely effective. There may be a reduction of the national income by some $208 million or 8.7 per cent of the gross national income.

    Just over six months have passed and both Members of this House and the general public outside may be interested to know what damage economic confrontation did actually inflict upon our economy.

    Before I go into details, it may be useful if I were to remind the Assembly of the gross exaggeration indulged in by some Members of the Opposition in assessing the losses that we would suffer by way of income and employment. One Member of the House placed an increase in unemployment as high as 20,000. Barisan Sosialis Members in particular predicted with great relish a collapse of the economy which they had gleefully expected. This is, of course, entirely in keeping with their pro-Indonesia anti-Malaysia political line.

    May I stress that the estimates which emerged from our researches last July were based on the assumption of a total

Column: 12

closure of firms engaged in processing raw materials imported from Indonesia as well as the complete laying-off of ships formerly plying on Indonesian routes? In other words, they were the maximum possible effects, in the event, the worst did not happen. Ships that were temporarily laid-off soon resumed work on new shipping routes to Burma, to Thailand, to ports of Malaysia, in cluding those in Sabah and Sarawak, Hongkong, and other places. The closing down of all processing factories also did not take place. For some months stocks of raw materials were adequate to maintain production and employment, and after that alternative supply lines were opened to enable a number of these firms to continue in business.

    In October last year the Department of Economic Defence was established and it put into operation two schemes. The first was to pay laid-off workers a monthly allowance equivalent to two-thirds of their income from employment. The total number of workers who received benefits under this scheme was 4,202 at its maximum. The figure as on 30th May was 2,545, a decline of sonic-thing like 1,700. This substantial reduction was principally due to the large number of workers who have regained employment either in new or existing industries under the Direction of Employment Scheme of the Government or who returned to work when their former employers resumed production.

    The total amount of money paid up to 30th May this year was $1,438,830.10. This amount was shared equally between employers and Government.

    In the second aspect of the Economic Defence Scheme, Government tried to find work for these laid-off employees. To do this, certain powers were given to the Director of Economic Defence to require specified employers to recruit from these laid-off workers on the register. In February this year, the Direction of Employment Scheme was put into operation, and the present position is as follows.

    In addition to the 4,202 workers who have previously mentioned had received these monthly allowances, another 619 were registered for re-employment

purposes. These persons did not qualify for the allowances either because they were casual workers not attached to any particular employer or they were not engaged in industries which were totally affected by economic confrontation. Examples of these are the water-front workers engaged in the Roads traffic of the Singapore Harbour.

    So in all the Government was faced with the task of finding jobs for 4,821 registered persons, victims of Indonesian confrontation one way or the other. It will be noted that this is substantially less than the estimate of 8,200. The reasons for this I have already discussed.

    As on the 30th May this year, a total of 2,740 vacancies were notified to the Director of Economic Defence. All pioneer industries are required to recruit their workers under the scheme, as well as all firms engaging 50 employees or more each. These are the industries which the Advisory Committee established under the Economic Defence (Direction of Employment) Ordinance recommended as suitable for redirection purposes. Public Authorities and Government departments were also required to notify the Director of vacancies in Division IV and daily-rated occupations. Of the total of 2,740 vacancies notified to the Department, pioneer industries form the largest group, offering 1,036 jobs. Government and Statutory Bodies reported 932 jobs. Private firms offered 708 places and the Armed Services 114.

    A part of the notified vacancies could not be filled up from the register of the Economic Defence Department because the skill and experience required were not available. Exemptions were, therefore, granted in respect of these vacancies and the Department and firms were authorised to recruit from outside the scheme. The total number of exemptions granted as on 30th May was 1,045. This leaves a total of 1,745 vacancies into which laid-off workers may be absorbed.

    Of this number, 878 workers have either been employed or are in the process of being employed, and this leaves 867 vacancies still unfilled and available for posting.

    Of the total figure of 4,821 now on the Economic Defence register for employment. 2,177 have already been reemployed or are in the process of being re-employed. Together with unfilled vacancies of 867, we get a total of 3,054 jobs as against 4,821 workers on the register. Therefore, as on 30th May the position was a surplus of workers over jobs in the number of 1,767.

    The number of vacancies notified to the Economic Defence Department conies in at the rate of about 700 a month of which 450 to 500 may be suitable for employment of persons on the register. At the rate progress is being made, it will not be very long before all those who could be employed will be absorbed and redirected under the scheme.

    I have some observations to make on the implementation of the plan to re-direct laid-off workers. This operation has proved to be more complex than we had expected it to be. It is not sufficient to offer the worker any job anywhere even if he or she is capable of performing such work, and even if the prospective employer is willing to accept the worker. There is the important question of travelling distance, it is no good offering a worker living in Sembawang a job in Jurong if a large slice of his daily pay will be absorbed by travelling to and from work. So even if there are suitable vacancies and suitable workers, it is sometimes not possible to match them together and if the worker refuses for good reason to take on work offered, it is not reasonable to disqualify him from the financial benefits of the Economic Defence Scheme.

    Similarly, certain employers are particular about the type of worker they want to engage in. Our textiles mills and the Garment factories insist on engaging young girls and women. Their preference is for those aged between 18 and 21. We have persuaded them to accept women workers up to the age of 25. These employers argue that the type of work requires manual dexterity and a capacity to learn new techniques which are often absent among older women.
