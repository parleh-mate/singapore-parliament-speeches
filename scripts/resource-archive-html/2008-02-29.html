KPIs and Concrete Results from Arts Events

 

     Miss Penny Low (Pasir Ris-Punggol): Sir, I beg to move,

 

     That the total sum to be allocated for Head Q of the Estimates be reduced by $100.

 

     Sir, what is a place with production lines but no arts and culture?  It is called a plain vanilla factory.  Sir, what is a nation without arts and culture?  It will be without a soul and imagination.  Would this be a place to attract the top talents?  Obviously not.  In the race for top talents, a strong arts and culture sector turns a high-skill, high-innovation economy into a great, liveable place.  I applaud MICA’s effort to promote the Renaissance City plan.  In 2006, MICA spent $31.2 million to support hundreds of artists and events.  Revenue generated amounted to $3.45 billion in 2006 and $3.67 billion in 2007 – a tremendous value-add to our economy.  However, I fear the efforts at promoting arts and culture could be shortchanged by the narrow criteria set as definitions of success and KPIs.

 

     MICA prides itself on supporting hundreds of arts-related activities with thousands of attendees.  Funding criteria are very much numbers-focused.  However, quantity is not always quality.  Arts and culture take time to evolve and be integrated into everyday life.  A more sustainable way of nurturing the arts is to identify key stakeholders and focus on building the Renaissance City eco-system.  To this end, why not have yearly awards to glam up and focus the sectors on measurements that build the eco-system?  Awards can be open to all arts-related events, including artistic productions, fashion shows, corporate and grassroots events, indoor and outdoor performances, and so on.  Some suggestions are:

 

     (1)  "The most experiential and creative arts production of the year Award", judged based on the innovative idea, impact an event has on its audience, audience participation strategy, inspiration quotient, and so on.
     (2)  "The arts or event organiser of the year Award", judged based on the evidence of clear strategic thinking, marketing effectiveness, cost per audience, return on investment and audience interaction.

 

     (3)  We could also have "The best backroom support provider of the year Award", given to the props or audio visual technical experts that enhance the artists’ works.  Criteria include how innovative solutions to a client’s brief were arrived at and executed, cost effectiveness and audience endorsement.


     (4)  We could also have "The most buzz event of the year Award", judged based on its media hit rates.  

  
     The list goes on, but it suffices to show that arts and culture can be measured with a more liberal set of KPIs than mere audience numbers.  Otherwise, events that offer goodie bags and lucky draws may overwhelm the funding pie.  KPIs should aim at nurturing the critical paths and stakeholders, involving the private, public and people sectors to produce a multiplier effect.  Nurture the stakeholders, provide a favourable tax and open environment and allow the arts and culture eco-system to flourish. 

 

     Finally, can the Minister provide an update on:


     (1)  The excellent Renaissance City Plan, 3.0?
     (2)  The School of the Arts?
     (3)  National Arts Gallery?

 

Renaissance City Plan

 

     Ms Irene Ng Phek Hoong (Tampines): Sir, I applaud MICA for its bold step to turn Singapore into a globalised city through its Renaissance City plan.  Amidst the flurry of the infrastructure built and the tickets sold, I would like to pause and ask:  Are we a creative city or a karaoke city? 

 

     A creative city is marked by innovation, ideas and an authentic cultural vibrancy.  A karaoke city is one which excels at stylish imitation, taking a well-sung song and putting its own rendition on it.  The Integrated Resorts, the Singapore Flyer and the F1 race, these add to our buzz, but I would argue they do little to project Singapore as a creative city.  They may be good ideas, but they are not ours.  We have the Arts Festival, the Biennale and many arts events.  But we should take stock.  How have we invested in creating something unique which builds on our strong traditions and heritage and which will leave a cultural legacy not only for Singapore but for the rest of the world?  How are we tapping our indigenous cultural capital to recreate our city to turn public spaces into aesthetic experiences that move our spirit?  How generously and actively do we commission artistic and musical works to Singaporeans, take risks with them and pioneer their work in Singapore and other parts of the world? 

 

     We need to devote more funds to our Singapore arts groups.  The budget for our National Arts Council seems puny, compared to that of the arts councils in major cultural cities.  This year, I see that it receives 2.6% more over last year's budget.  This will hardly keep up with inflation.  What progress has been made to inculcate an appreciation of our heritage and to strengthen the Singapore heartbeat to the creation and sharing of Singapore stories, be it film, theatre, dance, music, literature or the visual arts?

 

     Sir, cities need stories of cultural narratives about themselves, to both anchor and drive identity as well as to galvanise residents.  A city which describes itself as we do, as a global city of opportunity with its economic undertones fosters different behavioural patterns in citizens than a city that projects itself as a city of culture, a creative city or a city of ideas.  Adelaide, which started the Festival of Ideas in 1999, explicitly conceives of itself as a thinking city.  It invites thinkers from all over the world to assist South Australia to build on its climate of creativity.  The ideas festival has since been copied by other cities, such as Bristol and Aspen.  We have the Renaissance City plan.  But my sense is that we do not see or project ourselves as a renaissance city as such.  "Renaissance" is French for rebirth.  It implies a flowering of ideas, an openness to novel and even strange ones.  It implies a period in which artistic, social, scientific and political thought turn in new directions. 

 

     If we are serious about becoming a Renaissance City, we should take a more relaxed approach to some of our old policies which may be too cautious for this great task before us.  The incident involving the Complaints Choir highlights the historical mindset against foreigners that seems out of place in our arts scene today.  Collaborations between different arts groups from different parts of the world are part of the creative process in this globalised world.  With the better educated and more cosmopolitan population, should we not allow our citizens to listen to different views, wherever they come from, and make their own judgment?  Beyond the two subjects which we should stay off-limits, ie, race and religion, why should we fear foreign talents working with our arts groups to produce something that may touch on life and politics in Singapore? 

 

     To be honest, I was not too exercised about the Complaints Choir incident because:

 

     (1)  I do not understand what we need to learn from two Finns on how to complain.  We are very good at that already; and

 

     (2)  I do not see what is so creative about singing complaints that Singaporeans are already mouthing to themselves every day, ie, karaoke. 

 

     But I thought that MICA's decision to clamp down on the foreigners in this group jarred like a chalk screeching across the board.  The notion of a creative city, as opposed to a karaoke city, implies a level of openness that might potentially be unsettling to some.  We should be ready for that and be more relaxed if we are serious about our quest to be a global arts city.

 

     Another event we should prepare for is the 2010 Youth Olympic Games.  We should treat this major event not only as a sporting event but also as a major cultural festival with effects lasting long after the Games are gone.  I would urge MICA to start planning for this and to involve our creative and cultural sector.  We should use this opportunity to regenerate the cultural life of our cityscape, reposition Singapore and project ourselves a truly Renaissance City – A New City Reborn.

Fusion Arts

 

     Mr Chan Soo Sen (Joo Chiat): Sir, I must say that Singapore has a better "cooling" spell now culturally.  We have been successful in organising the Youth Olympics Games.  That makes us cool.  Last year, I was in China and I had the opportunity to be at the Singapore Seasons in Shanghai as well as in Beijing.  I remember telling the Minister at the end of the show that I felt very good as a Singaporean because we were demonstrating to the Chinese that we were there not only for economic mutual benefits but also for cultural exchange.  And that was cool.

 

     For the two Singapore Season events, there are two things that made the events very cool.  One is our fusion food which I thought everybody enjoyed – Chinese, Singaporeans, everybody.  There was also this thing called the fusion arts which I saw – big cultural items, multi-racial artistes going up, putting on each other's costumes, dancing, and I think the opinion for that one is a little more mixed.  Some said it was neither here nor there, it was a weird mixture.  Some of my Chinese guests who were there told me that actually they did not know what it was all about but that it was interesting.  I suppose "interesting" is a very polite way of saying that, yes, it is something that I cannot understand, but thanks for the effort anyway. 

 

     I think fusion art is something that we can have a unique opportunity to be innovative about, because Singapore is really a place where a lot of cultures meet – East and East, East and West, North East vs South East, South East vs South West – everybody seems to meet here because the monsoon meets here and brought our own forefathers together.  So we have the opportunity to appreciate each other's arts, and a lot of our traditional artists come here with the immigrants and they meet, make friends, exchange and create unique brands of arts.  Fusion art is not, as what I have said earlier, people of different ethnic groups putting on each other's costume and dancing, performing western dance.  That is, in a sense, a kind of fusion art.  But then we remember chemistry.  I remember when we studied, one of the first experiments that we did was to mix iron powder with sulphur and, after mixing it, that is called a mixture.  A mixture can be separated through physical method.  You use a magnet and you can remove all the iron dusts.  Or you use – was it called carbon disulphide? – you can dissolve all the sulphur, let it evaporate and you separate the sulphur from the iron.  But if you heat it up, it becomes some kind of iron ferric type of compound.  It can never be separated again. 

 

     I think, for our fusion arts, we have achieved the mixture stage.  The challenge is whether we can achieve some kind of a chemical reaction compound stage so that we will create something that is really unique.  As I have said, this has already happened in the past.  We have this very famous style of painting called the Nanyang style.  This is basically the Chinese artists who came from China to Southeast Asia and who were influenced by the Malay way of painting as well as the Western way of painting.  They looked at the Southeast Asian subjects and then they evolved into some unique way of painting.  We have people like, for example, Liu Kang and Chen Wen Hsi.  These are very, very famous world-class, or at least regional class, fusion artists.  In the case of writing, we also have a group called the Malayan Chinese literature.  Frankly, Malayan Chinese literature, in large part, means Singapore Chinese literature, because a lot of them actually worked in Singapore.  It is just that, at that time, this place was called Malaya.  So Malayan Chinese literary style.  Again, Chinese writers coming to this part of the world influenced by each other's way of creativity and looking at the local subject matters and issues and evolved into a unique style of writing.

 

     If this has happened in the past, I am sure it can happen in the future so long as we get the compound right.  The correct compound consists of people who are steep in multi-cultural creativity, in the sense that you have somebody who is equally good in Chinese arts as well as Western arts, there is a chance that he would evolve into a kind of unique fusion art.  If you have somebody who is very good in Chinese arts as well as Malay arts, eg, Chen Wen Hsi and Liu Kang, it evolved into a Nanyang style of painting.  What we need are multi-cultural types of artists.  We now have an opportunity.  We are going to have an Arts School.  Part of the objectives for our education is to be multi-cultural.  I would like to urge that the Arts School pay much attention in the multi-cultural approach to arts education so that we may have a chance to evolve our unique style of fusion arts that we can truly call Singaporean.  Of course, this is for the future.  If this happens, then we will have something that we can truly be proud of and feel very, very cool about – our unique fusion art.  It is not only East-West.  It is also East-East.  It can also be North East-South East, South East-North East, South East-South West.  In case you do not know what I mean, we can have many, many styles of fusion arts for the future.

The Singapore Totalisator Board and Asia Pacific Breweries are sponsors for these awards.  According to the answer by MCYS to my Parliamentary Question (PQ) last month, I quote, "The purpose of the MAP award is to recognise our athletes' individual and team efforts and sacrifices in training intensively at the elite level and attaining medal honour for the country." Cultural Heritage

 

     Dr Ong Seh Hong (Marine Parade) (In Mandarin ): [For vernacular speech, please refer to Appendix A *.] Sir, the UNESCO World Cultural Diversity Declaration 2001 states: "Cultural diversity is one of the original sources of development.  It is important to mankind, just as  biological diversity is important to mother nature."  UNESCO also pointed out that cultural heritage includes not only the materialistic historical sites or relics, but also covers the various communities and customs that are left behind by our ancestors, that include the various languages and dialects as well as the performing arts.

 

     Singapore was founded in 1819, less than 200 years ago. We do not have awesome relics, such as the Pyramids or the Great Wall but we have something that is bestowed to us by our ancestors – the cultural heritage passed on to us by our forefathers. In fact, we have the various customs and traditions from the Chinese, Malay, Indian and Eurasian communities, as well as our native Peranakan culture.


     Unfortunately, we do not cherish and preserve them.  Many of these have diminished, such as our roadside dialect wayang, and the Malay Gamelan music. Since they are not supported by us, many of these have diminished.


    As far as language is concerned, many of our younger generation people  could not speak their own dialect and the Eurasians are also beginning to lose their unique Kristan language. Many people have forgotten the real meaning of their traditional festivals.  Many Chinese families go overseas during the Chinese New Year festival period instead of staying at home for reunion and new-year visitations to their relatives and friends. Many of our roadside crafts and delicacies have also diminished.

    During a recent Chinese New Year gathering, I had the pleasure to taste the "Gi-ah kueh " made by the 75-year-old mother of my friend. This is a traditional food from Ann Kway county of Hokkien province.  I have not tasted this delicacy for more than 30 years in Singapore. This food brought back memories of my childhood days, and it reminded me of the urgency of preserving our cultural heritage.


    This morning, in the Lianhe Zaobao, a reader wrote that the Korean people felt extremely guilty when they were not able to preserve the Nan An Men, a national treasure which was lost in a recent fire. The author asked if such a thing were to happen in Singapore, would we have the same kind of feeling?


    While developing ourselves into an international commercial metropolis, we must also do our best to preserve our cultural heritage. We have to preserve the very precious heritage and pass it on to our children and grandchildren. This is what I call, "Seek the root while the root is still here, but do not pass the lantern only after the light has been put out."

     The Minister for Defence (Mr Teo Chee Hean): Mr Chairman, Sir, I would like to thank the hon. Members for their comments and questions, and for their strong commitment to Singapore's defence and security.

 

     Ms Indranee Rajah and Mr Hawazi Daipi asked about the trends in our regional security environment.  I will begin by sharing MINDEF's perspective on this. 

 

     While the US remains the pre-eminent power in the Asia-Pacific, the rise of China and India is transforming Asia's strategic landscape.  China has become the largest trading partner of many countries in the region, including Japan, South Korea and, most recently, Australia.  China is also India's second-largest trading partner.  In addition to trade, China and India are also becoming important sources of foreign direct investment (FDI).  Since 2004, their share of the total outflows of FDI from Asia has risen from 10% to 25%.  

 

     The expansion of China and India's economic space and linkages means that they will seek to secure their interests beyond their immediate neighbourhood.  Their growing economic might allows them to progressively develop the military capabilities and diplomatic influence to do so.

 

     As a consequence, Japan too is feeling the need to exert greater geo-political influence as a "normal" country.  Japan has sought a permanent seat at the UN Security Council, and in January last year made their Defence Agency a cabinet level Ministry of Defence.

 

     Russia will also grow increasingly assertive in our region, just as it has in Europe.  While Russia and Europe have their own security concerns mainly on their peripheries, it is only a matter of time before both Russia and the EU embark on a more coherent strategy to engage our region given Asia's economic dynamism and strategic potential.

 

     The US remains a key player in the security and stability of our region.  Although the US is currently preoccupied by Iraq and Afghanistan, it is vital for the US to be actively and constructively engaged in Asia.
 

     Mr Chairman, Sir, all countries tied to the global economy are becoming increasingly interdependent and have a common interest in peace and stability.  However, friction and strategic competition between the major powers pose risks that need to be managed in the on-going shift in power relationships.  Potential flashpoints in regional security remain, such as the situation across the Taiwan Strait; and transnational threats like terrorism must also be managed in a globalised world.

 

     MINDEF's response to this complex and fluid regional security environment is to strengthen both diplomacy and deterrence. 

    

     First, defence diplomacy.  Ms Indranee Rajah and Mr Hawazi Daipi asked about the regional security architecture, and the ASEAN Defence Ministers' Meeting.

 

     Multilateral cooperation is required to counter transnational threats as well as to create a culture of constructive engagement, and peaceful dispute avoidance and resolution.  Singapore seeks to facilitate the development of a regional security architecture, which provides a robust and flexible framework for peace and stability in Asia.  

 

     A multi-layered and overlapping security architecture is developing – made up of both formal and informal forums – at the strategic as well as the functional levels.

 

     ASEAN lies at the heart of many multi-lateral groupings, such as the ASEAN Regional Forum, the East Asia Summit and the Shangri-La Dialogue.  We want an open and inclusive architecture, with ASEAN facilitating and shaping this architecture and its agenda.

 

     At the broadest level, the ASEAN Regional Forum (ARF) brings together both foreign and defence officials from 27 countries from the Pacific to South West Asia.  With its wide membership, the ARF provides an important forum for strategic dialogue and confidence building. 

 

     Also providing a forum at the broadest level, but in a more informal format, is the Shangri-La Dialogue held annually in Singapore, which is a key multilateral event for defence and security dialogue in the region.  Today, it is the only forum where Defence Ministers from the region and beyond gather, along with senior officials and academics.  The informal setting of the Shangri-La Dialogue has facilitated a frank exchange of views, and allows new concepts and ideas to be floated and discussed.  Last year, 23 countries, including 20 Ministers, took part.

 

     At the regional level, we have the East Asia Summit (EAS), with the 10 ASEAN countries at its core, as well as Australia, China, India, Japan, New Zealand and the Republic of Korea.  The EAS' priority is currently mainly economic.  But with its focused membership, the EAS has the potential to address complex and multi-faceted security challenges in the region, such as energy security, trade security and maritime security.

     Ms Indranee Rajah asked about the ASEAN Defence Ministers' Meeting, or ADMM.  The ADMM is an important step towards building an ASEAN Security Community.  Singapore had the honour of hosting the second ADMM last November.  At that meeting, we made significant progress.  Chief of Defence Force LG Desmond Kuek updated the ASEAN Defence Ministers on the two-year activity workplan.  This plan has been adopted by the ASEAN CDFs, and will foster practical cooperation in areas such as humanitarian assistance and disaster relief, counter-terrorism and maritime security.

 

     Importantly, we also agreed on the principles for what is called the ‘ADMM-Plus’.  The ADMM-Plus is part of an evolving, open and inclusive regional security framework that allows the ASEAN defence track to engage countries beyond Southeast Asia.  Security issues in Southeast Asia such as maritime security, proliferation of weapons of mass destruction, or combating international terrorism, cannot be solved only by countries within our region.  Extra-regional countries that have a stake in the region can bring expertise, perspectives and resources to bear on such shared transnational security challenges in Southeast Asia.  It is also important that we engage the major powers and facilitate constructive and cooperative norms of behaviour in our region.

 

     Complementing this web of security forums is the development of a collection of operational arrangements and functional groupings which address specific challenges, such as maritime security.  Dr Ong Chit Chung and Mr Hawazi asked about the security of the Malacca Straits.

    

     Operational arrangements like the Malacca Straits Patrol, where the three littoral states, Malaysia, Indonesia and Singapore, conduct coordinated sea and maritime air patrols, enhance the security of the Straits of Malacca.  According to the International Maritime Bureau (IMB), the number of piracy attacks in the Straits of Malacca has declined, with only seven incidents in 2007, down from a recent peak of 38 in 2004.  The IMB attributed this to the vigilant efforts of the littoral states.  The commitment and political will of the littoral states remain strong.  We are pleased that Thailand has announced its intention to join the Malacca Straits Patrols.  The littoral states agree that user states can also contribute to the security of the Malacca Straits.

 

     Other functional groupings like the Regional Cooperation Agreement on Combating Piracy and Armed Robbery Against Ships in Asia or, in short, ReCAAP, which currently has 14 members, have also contributed to improving the maritime security situation.  The Singapore-based ReCAAP Information Sharing Centre (or ISC) facilitates the sharing of best practices amongst countries.  The ISC recently hosted a Capacity Building Workshop in Singapore last October, in which almost all the ReCAAP member countries, as well as Indonesia and Malaysia which are not yet members of ReCAAP, participated.

 

     Finally, to complement the various multilateral and functional groupings, an extensive web of bilateral defence relations between the countries of the Asia-Pacific provides a strong base for the regional security architecture.

To Adopt Plant-wide Portfolio Approach on CO2 Emissions

 

     Mr Alvin Yeo (Hong Kah): Sir, our national energy policy seeks to address the objectives of energy security, economic competitiveness and the protection of the environment.  The need to balance often competing concerns, calls for flexibility of approach on the part of the various agencies involved, including the National Environment Agency (NEA).  I should disclose my interest as an independent Director of Tuas Power, presently a subsidiary of Temasek Holdings but in the process of being divested.  Tuas Power has an interest in the matters to be discussed as a significant player in the power generation sector.

 

     At present, Singapore's electricity is generated primarily for natural gas, around 80%, which is amongst the highest dependency on gas in the world.  With rising oil prices, oil-fired power plants are no longer competitive.  While the move to diversify to liquefied natural gas (LNG) goes some way to addressing this issue, Singapore's fuel mix is still much less diversified compared to the global average.  Given our heavy dependency on gas imports, we are extremely vulnerable to supply disruption and price escalation due to political tension and natural disasters in our neighbouring producing countries. Our economic growth can also be undermined by rising energy prices.  We need to further diversify energy sources to buffer us against disruption, price increases and other threats to our supply.

 

     There are limited options in our drive for diversification.  Hydro geothermal and wind power are not feasible in Singapore while nuclear energy brings its own problems.  Solar power is potential but its contribution is limited.  The only other viable alternative fuel source is coal which constitutes about 40% of diversified power around the world and is substantially cheaper than gas or oil.  The main issue, however, surrounding the use of coal lies in the CO2 or carbon dioxide emissions.

 

     Currently, the most advanced ultra super critical plant today in the world has a CO2 emission rate of about 790 grams per kilowatt hour.  This is in excess of NEA's standard of 730 grams per kilowatt hour.  However, that standard is on a per unit basis, that is, for each generating unit.  Without detracting from the need to protect the environment, we need to consider if there are other ways to manage the differing concerns.  One way is to adopt the portfolio approach to CO2 emissions whereby a power station has access on the overall rate of emissions taking into account the rate of emission of each generating unit.

 

     To illustrate, a power station might have one unit of co-plant with the CO2 emission rate of about 800 grams per kilowatt hour and two units of gas-fired combined cycle plant with the CO2 emission of about 400 grams per kilowatt hour.  The combined emission rate would work out to significantly less than the emission standard of 730 grams per kilowatt hour.  So the coal unit on its own would not.  The plant-wide portfolio approach on CO2 emission is being practised in countries like Japan.  A utility company, such as J Powers' 16,000 megawatt capacity, comprising 50% coal-plant and 50% hydro-plant, their plant-wide CO2 emission rate is 720 grams per kilowatt hour which would meet NEA's standard but, on its own, its coal-plant portion would not meet it.

 

     The use of coal will complement gas and LNG and enhance Singapore's energy security and competitiveness.  Modern coal plants have incorporated features to reduce pollutants with the exception that its carbon intensity is higher than coal-fired plants.  However, this would be addressed by the adoption of a portfolio approach to CO2 emissions, keeping emissions within overall allowable limits.  It is hoped that NEA will consider this approach to allow Singapore to secure its energy supply, bring down the cost of electricity and still protect the environment.

     By the third quarter this year, visitors to the Marina Barrage will be able to learn about Singapore’s water story and understand how the barrage works.  This is the Visitors' Centre on the screen.  They will also get to see green features in the building design, such as the turfed roof, the use of solar energy, and water and energy-efficient fittings.  So, Sir, we walk the talk.  Through these, we hope the public can learn more about the Marina Barrage and other projects, and how they can play a part in our water and environmental sustainability story.

 

     Sir, last year, as pointed out by Dr Muhammad Faishal, the PUB launched the ABC Waters programme to bring people closer to water so that they will better cherish this precious resource.  Under the programme, our drains, canals and reservoirs will be transformed into vibrant, beautiful and clean streams, rivers and lakes, raising the quality of life of Singaporeans to an even higher level. 

 

     I am happy to report good progress on the first three projects which will be completed this year - at Kolam Ayer, Bedok Reservoir and MacRitchie Reservoir.  Singaporeans can also expect more exciting projects across the island, including those at Lower Seletar Reservoir, Jurong Lake, Pandan Reservoir, Sungei Punggol and Kallang River near Bishan Park. 

 

     Let me now give a preview of some of these projects, so that after a long week and a long day, we end up on a high note, Sir. 

 

     In the eastern part of Singapore, at Lower Seletar Reservoir, the rich heritage of the area will be preserved.  Along the heritage bridge, visitors can take in the scenic view of the reservoir and, through story panels, learn of the interesting history of Lower Seletar Reservoir and its previous Orang Seletar settlement.  This will connect the young with the past and bring back memories of the place for the older generation.

 

     In the western part of Singapore, Jurong Lake will be transformed into a family recreational attraction. Here, school children can learn how wetlands function and families can get close to the water along the boardwalks.  As a connection to the loose translation of Jurong as a fish - as I understand, its local name is Ikan Julong - there will be a fishing promenade for anglers.

 

     Finally, in the central part of Singapore, Kallang River along Bishan Park will be transformed into a scenic river, which will be integrated with the adjacent park.  Families can enjoy a quiet stroll along the new footbridge connecting the residential estates from both sides of Bishan Park.  They can also enjoy picnics by the water while children have fun at the water play areas. 

 

     With these projects, we hope to bring waterfront living to the HDB heartlands, improve the quality of our living environment and enhance property values.  It will also bring closer to realisation our vision of being a City of Gardens and Water, which is one of the key thrusts of Clean and Green Singapore.

 

     Sir, while the Government carries out the physical infrastructural works, the success of these projects will depend on the community.  We want people to visit our waters, have fun and, in doing so, develop a sense of ownership and play their part to keep our waters clean.

 

     PUB will engage the community to encourage them to take ownership of these projects.  A one-day workshop was held for the pilot project at Kolam Ayer, where the community groups brainstormed how they could sustain the project in their estate.  They are now developing those ideas into community bonding projects which they will run themselves.  Similar workshops will also be conducted for the other ABC Waters projects.

 

     Sir, let me conclude.  I would like to thank the Members for their views and suggestions.  We can all be proud that Singapore has done well to achieve the clean and healthy environment that we enjoy today. While we continue to take care of our environment, we are also moving towards our vision of becoming a city of Gardens and Water to further enhance the quality of life of all Singaporeans.

 

     Looking ahead, my Ministry will maintain a strong focus on environmental sustainability to ensure that we deliver an even higher quality environment in tandem with a growing population and economy.  We will continue with efforts to develop and position Singapore as a global hub and a thought-leader for environment, water technologies and solutions.  At the same time, we will encourage the private sector to adopt more environmentally-friendly business practices, especially where these result in cost-savings and greater resource security.  To facilitate such efforts, we will look into providing incentives where necessary, for instance, to address market barriers and failures.  We will continue to take a sensible approach by adopting measures that reap effective returns and long-term benefits for our people and industries.

 

     While the Government will actively do our part, achieving environmental sustainability will require the participation of each and every Singaporean.  It can be as simple as not littering, keeping our homes free from dengue, bringing our recyclables to the nearest recycling bin, and choosing water and energy-efficient appliances.  I urge everyone, including Members of this House, to be more mindful in the little choices that we make in our daily lives.

 

 

If we work together to sustain a better environment, quality of life, the result of our joint efforts will be enjoyed not just by ourselves but also our future generations will benefit from a Singapore that is economically vibrant and has a high quality living environment with clean air, clean land and water for all.
