(Policy)


    1. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Minister for Home Affairs what the policy of his Ministry is on applications from non-citizen spouses of Singapore citizens to live in Singapore with their spouses.

    The Minister of State for Law and Home Affairs (Prof. S. Jayakumar) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, our immigration laws do not confer upon any foreigner the rights of entry or residence in Singapore. The husband of a Singapore citizen is not entitled to Singapore citizenship or permanent residence by virtue of his marriage to a Singapore citizen. If he desires to settle in Singapore permanently, he will have to obtain permanent residence on his own merits. It is the law and common practice in many countries of the world that a wife should follow her husband, and Singapore is no exception. When a Singapore girl marries a foreigner, she cannot expect the State to grant her husband the right of residence in this country. The husband will have to qualify for residence on his own merits based on the economic contribution he can make to Singapore.

    Our policy on the granting of permanent residence to foreign wives of Singapore citizens is less stringent. However, every case will have to be considered on its own merits. Among the factors to be considered is that we have to ensure that the husband is not only able to support his wife and children but they will not later become a burden to the State if they are granted permanent residence. This is important because we must discourage our citizens who can barely support their families to marry foreign wives, therefore increasing the strain on our public housing, medical and other social services. We must not forget that a person granted permanent residence will be able to form a family nucleus together with one other Singapore citizen for public housing. The grant of permanent residence to each

DRUG ABUSE AMONG NATIONAL SERVICEMEN
(Measures to eliminate)


    4. Mr Ng Kah Ting asked the Minister of Defence what progress has been made on efforts to prevent drug abuse among National Servicemen, and what are the measures taken to eliminate this menace.

     The Minister of State of Defence (Dr Yeo Ning Hong) (for the Minister of Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, in the mid-1970s, drug abuse became a serious problem in Singapore and in the Singapore Armed Forces. At the end of 1976, there were about 2,000 drug abusers among those in the national service. A Special Committee was set up to tackle this problem. Mindef decided to adopt some tough policies and measures which are still enforced today.

    The implementation of these policies and measures have resulted in a rapid and dramatic decline in drug abuse incidents in the military forces.

    631 servicemen were arrested for drug abuse in 1977. The following year, follow-

Column: 1042

ing the measures that I have mentioned earlier, there were only 342 cases; in 1979, 174 cases and in 1980, 168. For 1981, the figure has been further reduced to 132. Thus, in five years from 1977 to 1981, the SAF has succeeded in containing the menace of drug abuse. The number arrested in 1981 was about one-fifth of that for 1977, some five years ago.

    The relapse rate in the SAF, i.e. the percentage of SAF servicemen returning to drug abuse a second time, averages about 15%. This is lower than the national average rate of approximately 45%. This lower relapse rate is due mainly to the strict measures taken in drug abuse detection and to the severe penalties levied on those who were found guilty on the second or subsequent occasion.

    Measures taken against the drug menace in the SAF are a combination of education, detection, severe penalties and rehabilitation.

    Recruits are given talks on the dangers of drug abuse, soon after their enlistment. At that stage, if they admit to having taken drugs before enlistment, they will not be punished. They will be given treatment, rehabilitation and counselling, if necessary.

    Recruits with previous records of drug abuse are identified promptly on enlistment. Their urine is tested every other day for the presence of drugs. This is done throughout their full national service period. It means they have no chance of taking drugs without being detected. In addition, they undergo periodic counselling by trained social workers of the SAF Drug Abuse Rehabilitation Unit.

    Servicemen are also regularly given talks on the ill-effects of drug abuse. They are warned that there will be severe penalties against those who are detected. Publications on drug abuse are distributed throughout the SAF. A book entitled "Curbing Drug Abuse - The SAF Experience" was published in 1981. SAF conducts an annual three month-long anti-drug campaign, during which exhibitions and talks will be organized, and special publications and easily readable pamphlets will be distributed to press home the message of drug abuse and its evils.
