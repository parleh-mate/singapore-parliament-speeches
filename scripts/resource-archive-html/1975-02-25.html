    Mr Ong Teng Cheong (Kim Keat): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move,

    `That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the opening of the Second Session of this Parliament.".'.

    Sir, I am sure that hon. Members in this House wilt rise in support of my Motion thanking the President for his speech which reviews the state of the nation and outlines the major policies and measures to be implemented in the coming years.

    I am also confident that I speak for all hon. Members that we welcome the proposed increased income tax relief for those with aged parents living with them, the measures to eliminate industrial friction and improve compensation rates for workers involved in industrial accidents. It is a relief to note that, despite the prevailing world-wide inflation coupled with economic recession, we have managed to achieve a real growth

Column: 39

rate of about 7%. It is also reassuring to note from the President's speech the cautious optimism that "Singapore is likely to reverse the downtrend in her economy" with the prospect that we will probably do better by the later part of this year than now, provided there is no major unexpected catastrophe.

    We owe our present well-being to our decisive will to succeed and hard work. It is with the able leadership, imaginative policies and tough measures that our Government has been able to- minimise our economic and social dislocations under the current recessive climate. Although economic growth by itself does not necessarily generate happiness all round, the absence of such growth will undoubtedly create much unhappiness. More hard work, unpopular policies and tough measures there shall be for the continued well-being of our society for generations to come. There is no question of any of this Government's policies and measures being swayed by short-term political considerations.

    There are new challenges and problems ahead of us, as pointed out in the Addendum to the President's speech; and to quote the President, "there will be much to keep Honourable Members busy in 1975." One of the pressing problems generated by our rapid progress of urbanisation and industrialisation is that of urban transportation, in particular, peak hour travelling. The daily traffic congestion has caused much social frustration, unnecessary fuel expenditure and an adverse effect on business activities in the central business district due to a lower degree of mobility. There is no easy and simple solution to overcome the complex problem of urban transportation which has become a major concern of all metropolitan and public policy in this century. The Government will soon introduce harsh traffic management measures such as the park and ride, city shuttle bus and area licensing schemes to relieve traffic congestion. The comprehensive scheme with unpopular management

Column: 40

measures, though not a new idea, is attracting much international attention because few governments in the so-called free-world have dared to venture into such unpopularity due to short-term political considerations. Our bus service is improving, but the standard is still far from satisfactory. The introduction of the park and ride scheme will no doubt relieve the traffic congestion in the central business district. However, careful regulation or adjustment may be necessary to maintain a balanced set of management measures to ensure, among other things, that the scheme would not unnecessarily attract the normal public transport riders to opt for the park and ride alternative, in which case, a shift of traffic congestion from the central business district to the outer urban areas may result.

    Another growing concern is the increasing crime rate and the insufficiently staffed police force as expressed by the President. Although the introduction of full-time national servicemen for the police force will lessen the problem of manpower shortage, the effectiveness of our police force will depend more on the quality and maturity of the personnel than sheer numbers. It is imperative to stress the need to carefully train our selected young national servicemen for police duty. In the course of their active duty, their contact with the public is unavoidable, and their ability and tact in handling human situations will determine the goodwill or ill-will between the public and the police.

    It is without doubt, to quote the Addendum to the President's Speech, that "The Police will require the support of the public to be fully effective." As it is, the degree of mutual trust and cooperation between the public and the police leaves much to be desired. No doubt severe punishment must be meted out to law offenders, but more severe punishment should be meted out to the law enforcement officers who flout the law if we are to make Singapore a safe and orderly place to live in. The current world economic situation has made it very clear that the economic well-being of any country depends more than ever

But interdependence goes beyond economics to the question of food, population and jurisdiction over the seas, all of which requiring urgent attention but none of which could be solved by any nation acting alone.

    The importance of international goodwill as an overture to greater cooperation and increased trade cannot be over-emphasised if we are to truly establish Singapore as a "Global City". Our traders and those who have active business dealings with our neighbouring countries should be made to realise this, and I would urge them wherever possible to avoid sacrificing our long-term international goodwill for any immediate trading gain. Our traders should be encouraged to conduct "fair trade" not only intra-nationally but also internationally if this tiny City-state of ours is to continue to thrive under the "Global City" concept.
