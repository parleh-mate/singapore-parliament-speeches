PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS

Debate on the Address
(First Allotted Day)


    Mrs Lim Hwee Hua (Marine Parade): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move,

    That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:-

    "We, the Parliament of the Republic of Singapore, express our thanks to the President for the Speech which he delivered on behalf of the Government at the Opening of the First Session of this Parliament.".

    Mr Speaker, Sir, we Singaporeans have, in the short space of the last three to four years, received many wake-up calls in our relatively cosy rooms. In his Address at the Opening of the First Session of this Parliament, the President has elaborated on the challenges confronting Singapore. I would like to relate these challenges to what I see as the three wake-up calls at the individual Singaporean level. I shall begin with the one that is ringing the loudest and most incessantly.

Wake-up Call Number 1

    What we thought were unshakeable areas of competitive advantage that Singapore has are now vulnerable, and this is largely due to a confluence of several factors: firstly, the global slowdown led by the US economy; secondly, challenging regional economic and political conditions; and, thirdly, increasing competition.

Column: 82



    As a regional hub, Singapore will face intensifying risks of losing key parts of the critical mass. I would like now to discuss this against a backdrop of shifts in economic growth concentration and investments.

    Sir, economists are projecting that economic growth in our immediate region of around 3-4% for each of the next two years will be overshadowed by the North Asian region, led by a potential 7-8% growth in the huge China market. If we were to consider this concentration on an even wider perspective and think GNP rather than GDP, then China's reach extends even further. China has taken a strategic decision to invest abroad for a variety of reasons, including plans to secure new supplies of natural resources. According to the Far Eastern Economic Review, Chinese state-owned offshore oil company, CNOOC (China National Off-Shore Oil Corporation), plonked US$585 million for some Indonesian oil and gas assets. In Australia itself, Chinese investment, primarily in iron ore and aluminium businesses, stood at US$1.2 billion at last count.

    Secondly, long-range planes, when available, or huge shipping alliances, may not need to call at Singapore, if there is no business reason to do so. Therefore, an excellent Changi Airport or a successful PSA alone will not get us very far. Likewise, our stronghold on derivatives trading in the region will constantly be challenged unless we give reasons to traders to always use us, and so on.

    I would now like to move on to the twin effects of technology and globalisation on competition itself.

    Sir, globalisation itself is already increasing competition, and technology is accelerating the pace of globalisation. So businesses are now growing slowly, if at all, and only if they adapt. Businesses cannot sit still and let uncomfortable trends destroy them. Not to react would only mean they die out. Businesses really have no choice but to react ruthlessly, and this would come in many forms, eg,

Column: 83

downsizing in response to a dip in demand, very much like what we have seen in the travel business in the aftermath of September 11 (911); secondly, relocating lower-end production out of Singapore; thirdly, bringing in talent from elsewhere to keep the business going; fourthly, merging for critical mass, such as that we have seen in the banks; or fifthly, even getting out of certain businesses altogether.

    Sir, all these factors have an impact at the individual level. Whilst retrenchment at the personal level is painful, it would be better to accept such restructuring as harsh realities than to hope that something can be done by the Government to these seemingly unsympathetic banks or ungrateful large companies. Even if our jobs were intact, we would have to accept that we would probably have to do more, learn new skills and adjust to new working conditions, perhaps all at the same time. For those of us who have been retrenched, new employment would be difficult to come by and would most likely mean taking a lower pay and position.

    Sir, at the individual level, self-esteem will go down and this is where the moral support of the family or the community can be extremely helpful. There are anecdotal signs that more marriages come under stress when one partner, especially the breadwinner, loses his job.

    So what are some of the hopes we can hold on to and those that we should inject a sense of reality? Sir, we can certainly cling onto the assurance that the Government will continue to devote plenty of resources to developing human capital to the whole spectrum of pre-school to tertiary education, for skills upgrading, re-skilling and job-matching whilst ensuring at the same time that we anchor enough economic activity in and from Singapore into the global or regional market. As long as we are proactive and receptive, we will be able to receive help on adapting to changing job and market demands.

    However, Sir, we would also have to manage our wage expectations. For the past decade, our wage growth has

Column: 84

averaged more than 7% in nominal terms and about 5% in real terms. This will no longer be a sure thing, which means that we can no longer use this assumption to make investments or consume the way we could have in the past.

    Going forward, there will also be a change in the employer-employee relationship. We now have less control over our working lives and loyalties to companies will weaken as we realise that companies can no longer be paternalistic and avoid laying off workers.

    Sir, in a nutshell, this wake-up call is all about change, and having to cope with a more rapid pace of change, much of which is uncomfortable. Most of the jobs that are lost are gone forever so we should not hold out for a US-led recovery and for a return of the same old jobs. Instead, we have to continually make ourselves relevant and think in terms of our skills, ie, what we can do and contribute, rather than the jobs we would like.

Wake-up Call Number 2

    We Singaporeans have to work towards preserving our racial and religious harmony, that harmony will not continue as a matter of course, and that external events and stresses can attack our harmony. Until the infamous 911 terrorist attacks in the US, the war in Afghanistan, the fighting in the West Bank and some other events, we did not realise that we need to pay attention to our own racial and religious harmony. Now, Sir, we realise that these external events can surface or resurface old issues stemming from differences.

    The wake-up call can cut both ways. If we allow it, external stresses can undo whatever precious harmony we have built up, or we can become even more conscious of the differences between races and religions and seek to proactively integrate and understand even more.

    Sir, we have come a long way in fostering inter-racial and inter-religious ties. As a small country, we can ill-afford

Column: 85

to let external events and external influences exert pressure on us. In a nutshell, we must jealously guard our racial and religious harmony.

Wake-up Call Number 3

    This wake-up call, which probably takes on a more subtle and dull tone, is that we Singaporeans have to take ownership of our future and strengthen our sense of belonging to the country and to one another.

    Sir, there is a need to bond even closer to increase our survival as a small nation with no natural resources. There is also a need to give the Government full moral authority and support so as to bring about the much-needed wide-ranging and transformational changes.

    The sense of ownership to Singapore and one another is all the more important because of our aging population and the need to help fellow Singaporeans cope with the digital, information and wealth divide.

    Sir, the key really is, how do we react to these three wake-up calls? Do we break out in cold sweat or have we been tossing and turning and dreading the wake-up call, or do we arise and take these changes in our stride?

    If we break out in cold sweat, we should check why we have fears and concerns and how we can address them. If we have just lost a job that we have had for the past 15-20 years, then we can try examining what skills and experience we have accumulated and seek to apply these skills to jobs outside of the natural industry. If we now shudder at how insecure our surroundings have become, no thanks to terrorism, then we should discuss with our friends and community how we can look out for each other.

    However, if we have been tossing and turning, and dreading the calls, then we should no longer wait for the seemingly inevitable events to happen. Instead, we should actively look for alternatives and explore opportunities to learn new skills for other jobs. We should also not allow

Column: 86

isolated incidents of inter-racial discord to fester. We should actively reach out to friends and fellow Singaporeans to gain a better understanding and build common ground.

    Above all, if we have been alerted to these challenges, we can then take all these in our stride - assess what we have to do and how we can respond to yet another set of challenges or changing circumstances.

    Sir, I would like to conclude by observing that we have a lot going for us. We have huge savings as a nation, a sound financial and physical infrastructure, substantial goodwill from the Singapore branding - in terms of integrity, transparency and an excellent workforce - and most importantly, political and economic stability.

    We can therefore embrace the changes to policy and strategies that the Government will have to painstakingly undertake and be receptive to the need for a change, especially at the personal level.

    Sir, I believe that Singaporeans are a pragmatic and sensible lot and the resounding mandate of 75% at the last GE reflects, firstly, a common destiny and appreciation for the need for survival in challenging times and, secondly, the trust the Singaporeans have in the PAP Government to take us through the necessary transformations, and to help us cope with change.
