    Until now, the Chief Medical Officer has been combining the duties of Medical Superintendent (General Hospital) with those of the Chief Administrative Medical Officer for all hospitals and out-patients services in Singapore. The expansion of this service has been considerable, fantastically so, over the last few years and I will, in a moment, illustrate that by giving a short table of the overall increases in our 4 main Hospitals from 1938. In 1938, the in-patients were 25,913 and the out-patients were 87,447. I will skip over a few years because each year shows us an increase. In 1951, the in-patients increased to 40,833 and the out-patients increased to 612,095. In 1952, that went up to 48,550 in-patients and 726,310 out-patients. In 1954, it jumped up to 58,859 in-patients and 1,169,689 out-patients and in 1955 it rose to 62,024 in-patients and 1,271,165 out-patients.

    Now, since the beginning of 1955, 522 hospital beds had been added to existing institutions and the buildings now under construction will produce another 672 beds before the end of 1956: that is, by that time, there will be 1,194 hospital beds. Besides that, there are three new outdoor dispensaries which have also been added to the hospitals and during the coming year, a start will be made on a new 500-bed acute general hospital and on a chronic hospital for 200 beds. It is hoped also to establish two new out-patient clinics.

    Now on tuberculosis, I will answer the query raised by the hon. Member on the Grade E post at page 227. That tuberculosis is a serious menace in Singapore is well known. This appointment of Assistant Director of Medical Services (Tuberculosis) appearing at page 227 is a new appointment. For some years, an effective control organisation has been under consideration. In 1954, a request was made for expert advice from Australia and in May 1955, after I assumed office, Sir Harry Wunderly, the Director of the Division of Tuberculosis of the Australian Commonwealth Department of Health came to Singapore to assess the problem. After a preliminary survey, he recommended that we should set up a control organisation to trace, register and keep under surveillance all proved cases of tuberculosis so that we can ensure that all these patients will continue under treatment until the disease is arrested. Secondly, he advised that once such an
