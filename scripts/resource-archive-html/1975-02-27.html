    `Our foreign policy must take into consideration the new situation arising from the gradual scaling down of American and Western military presence in the region. The overwhelming Western influence in the region has been correspondingly reduced.'

The President has alerted us that this situation "will lead other powers to seek greater influence for themselves in the area".

    Hon. Members are aware that detente has replaced the cold war, but detente does not necessarily mean ending of tensions and conflicts.

    During the cold war, small nations steered clear of the conflicts between tile two blocs by simply taking a neutral stand, and for the most part the great powers tolerated this neutralism. Also, some small nations joined one of the two camps for shelter and protection. The great powers were also willing to take risks and even military adventures on behalf of their small allies. In future the contest for influence among the great powers continues, despite detente. But with detente, the rivalry among the great powers has become a complex web of simultaneous rivalry and cooperation.

    Before the onset of the energy crisis, the United States and their allies had a slight edge, in terms of economic and military power, over the Socialist bloc. However, the energy crisis has adversely affected the Western countries more than their Socialist counterparts. In other words, the energy crisis has slowed down the economies of the Western countries, and this has the effect of weakening their will to maintain their dominant presence in Asia and elsewhere in the world.

    Thus, in South-east Asia the contest for influence and supremacy by the great powers continues. Until a new balance of power is established in our region, there will be uncertainty and instability in our area. While the US has disengaged herself militarily from Vietnam and continues to be under domestic pressure to cut down her military commitments in Asia and elsewhere, the Soviet presence has become more pronounced.

    China and Japan will, with the passage of time, become factors likely to influence events in our part of the world. If the contest for influence and power is conducted peacefully and rationally, then we need have no great fears and even some benefits could come out of it all.

ASEAN has grown from its modest beginnings to an effective and lively regional body able to meet the challenges of changing times. With its combined population of more than 200 million, abundant natural resources, political stability and an ever improving developmental infrastructure, ASEAN has the potential for increased economic development which in turn will help to promote peace and prosperity in the region.
