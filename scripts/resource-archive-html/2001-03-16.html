ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

FLOODING IN SINGAPORE


    1. Mr Harun bin A. Ghani asked the Minister for the Environment (a) how much has been expended so far in making Singapore free from floods; and (b) why has there been flooding whenever there are heavy downpours in spite of this expenditure.

    The Acting Minister for the Environment (Mr Lim Swee Say): Sir, ENV spends $60-$70 million a year to improve the drains and canals in Singapore. Since 1984, ENV has spent a total of $1.3 billion on drainage improvement works, including the widening and deepening of main drains and canals to cater for increased storm water runoff arising from new land developments.

Column: 1280



    ENV is committed to reduce flooding in Singapore and has over the years, progressively implemented many drainage improvement projects to alleviate and prevent flooding. This investment in drainage infrastructure has paid off. In spite of rapid urbanisation and intensified redevelopments, the flood situation in Singapore has continued to improve significantly. There are now 238 hectares of flood-prone areas as compared to some 3,200 hectares in the 1970s, a reduction of more than 90%. The floods are less severe and are of relatively short duration.

    Sir, ENV will continue to improve the existing drains and canals to alleviate flooding in flood-prone areas, whenever it is cost-effective to do so. However, it is not feasible to completely rid Singapore of all floods for two reasons.

    Firstly, some of the areas such as those in Boat Quay and Chinatown are low-lying and are barely above high tide level. These premises remain prone to flooding, especially when heavy rain coincides with high tide. The only effective flood control measure is to raise the ground levels of these premises in conjunction with redevelopment plans.

    Secondly, there is a limit to which we can widen drains because of land constraints. Very intense rainfall, which may occur once every five years or so, could overwhelm the capacity of the drains and cause flash floods. Such floods, however, could be minor and of short duration.

    I would like to assure Mr Harun and the House that ENV is committed to press on with our drainage improvement projects to reduce flooding in Singapore, and to keep the flood situation well under control.

"CLEAN TOILET CAMPAIGN"


    2. Mr Harun bin A. Ghani asked the Minister for the Environment how successful the "Clean Toilet Campaign" has been and what further improvements can be made to ensure clean public toilets.

    The Senior Minister of State for the Environment (Encik Sidek bin Saniff) (for the Acting Minister for the Environment): Sir, our public toilets are cleaner now than they were a few years ago. However, there is still room for improvement.

    Surveys conducted annually, since 1996 when we launched the Clean Toilet Campaign, showed that there have been gradual improvements in the cleanliness of our public toilets.

    Members of the public interviewed during the surveys have given higher scorings consistently over the last five years. Cleaners who maintain these toilets have also consistently rated the toilets to be cleaner over the years. ENV officers participating in the surveys have also independently rated the toilets and have also come up with similar scorings.

    A number of public toilets in ENV and HDB hawker centers have been extensively upgraded and users interviewed have expressed that they are pleased with them. More will be done.

    We will continue to push for clean public toilets in Singapore and will continue to require building managements to keep public toilets in clean and good working condition. Through public education programme, we will continue to remind the public to cooperate and help keep our public toilets clean.

    Mr Harun bin A. Ghani: Sir, the Senior Minister of State mentioned that surveys were conducted. Could the Senior Minister of State give details about ratings given by ENV for the last five years?

    Encik Sidek bin Saniff: Sir, the rating is based on a score of 0-10. On our part, we are very lucky that the scoring happens to be between 6 and 8. This score is given by three parties: ENV officers, cleaners and the users. For instance, in 1996, ENV scored 6.0; cleaners, 6.1; users, 6.8. Recently, we have 8.1 by ENV, 8.0 by cleaners and 8.3 by users. The scoring system maintains that 9.1-10 points is excellent; 8.1-9 points, good; 6.1-8 points, satisfactory; 4.1-6 points, not quite satisfactory; and 4 points and below, poor. We will try our best to do even better.

Evolving towards a Competitive Energy Market

In addition, a combination of other factors such as a surge in electricity demand because of the economic boom and problems in getting new power plants approved created a situation where generation capacity was outstripped by demand. The main objective of the Bill is to designate the EMA as the regulator for the provision of a pilot district cooling service in an area at the New Downtown at Marina South.

    A district cooling system supplies chilled water through a network of pipes for the air-conditioning of buildings located within the service area. The conventional method is to have individual buildings put up their own stand-alone air-conditioning plants. A centralised district cooling system has two major benefits. First, it takes up less land space, an important consideration in land-scarce Singapore. Second, it significantly reduces water and energy consumption through economies of scale. There is also an aesthetic advantage, as the roof-tops of individual buildings can now be better designed, besides being put to better use.

    District Cooling Systems have been successfully implemented in certain parts of Yokohama and Osaka of Japan. Based on such experience, the advice is that a District Cooling System should serve a total gross floor area of about 1.25 million square metres for optimal efficiency. URA has therefore parceled out a section of the New Downtown at Marina South for a pilot District Cooling System to be tried out. The projected saving in gross floor area in the pilot area is about 7,000 square metres. The remainder of the New Downtown can be parceled out into four zones of similar size for future District Cooling Systems, if the pilot proves successful.

    The Minister for National Development (Mr Mah Bow Tan): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, this Bill seeks to abolish rent control in Singapore. Rent control operates in two ways. First, it restricts a landlord's right to raise the rent. Second, it restricts the landlord's right to take back possession of the rent-controlled property.

    The history of rent control goes back to 1947, just after the Second World War. At that time, much of the basic infrastructure like roads and utilities were severely damaged. Housing was in short supply. The population had risen sharply, especially with a massive influx of refugees. Against this backdrop, rent control was introduced to prevent landlords from exploiting the situation and raising rents drastically.

Why rent control is no longer needed

    The situation today is totally different. The Government has successfully implemented its public housing programme to provide affordable housing for Singaporeans. Almost nine in 10 Singaporeans now own their homes. Subsidised rental flats are available for those who still cannot afford home ownership. No family need go without a roof over its head. After 54 years, rental control has outlived its purpose to protect families from unscrupulous landlords.

Column: 1377



    On the other hand, rent control is a disincentive for landlords to redevelop or restore their properties. The Controlled Premises (Special Provisions) Act was introduced in 1970 to allow landlords to recover their rent-controlled properties for redevelopment. However, as landlords are required to pay compensation to the tenants, some landlords have not moved to recover their rent-controlled properties. As a result, some rent-controlled properties, including those designated for conservation, remain in a poor state of maintenance. The complete lifting of rent control will give landlords additional incentive to recover their properties and to spruce them up.

Paving the way to rent decontrol

    In April 1997, the Government announced its intention to abolish rent control in four years' time, that is, on 1st April this year. This long period of notice was to give sufficient time for tenants to find alternative premises or to reach agreement with their landlords to stay on after lifting of rent control.

    The early announcement in 1997 aimed to strike a balance between the interests of the tenants and their landlords. Tenants were assured that they could continue to pay low rent and stay in the rent-controlled properties for another four years while landlords were given indication that their properties would cease to be rent controlled by 1st April 2001.

    The 1997 decision to abolish rent control was not a sudden decision. In 1988, the Government used the Controlled Premises (Special Provisions) Act, which was introduced to facilitate the redevelopment of the Golden Shoe area in the 1970s, to accelerate the phasing out of rent control. The Controlled Premises (Special Provisions) Act was made applicable to all rent-controlled properties over four phases, between October 1988 and December 1991. This allowed landlords to recover possession of their rent-controlled properties for purposes of development subject to payment of

The Urban Redevelopment Authority has been sending reminders about the abolition of rent control to the tenants every year since April 1997. From April 2000, that is, last year, URA has been sending these reminders every three months.

    Since the Government's announcement in 1997, the number of rent-controlled premises has shrunk further from approximately 1,600 to about 700 as at January this year.
