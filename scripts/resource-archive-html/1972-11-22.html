PENSIONS (AMENDMENT) BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

2.54 p.m.

    The Minister for Finance (Mr Hon Sui Sen): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Honourable Members will recall that a 13th month salary was paid to civil servants in March this year. In fact, for reasons which I have already stated in this House, a second 13th month salary, which will otherwise be paid in March next year, will be paid in December of this year instead. This payment is intended to be an annual non-pensionable allowance, an allowance which would attract a Central Provident Fund contribution at the revised rate of 12 per cent. At the same time, because of the increase in the CPF rate from 10 per cent to 12 per cent, all pensionable employees in the Government service were required to make a two per cent CPF contribution with effect from April 1972 on their monthly emoluments, with a matching contribution from the Government. These contributions to the CPF are intended as additional benefits `over and above the pension benefits.

Column: 366



    As the Pensions Act stands, any period of service during which a Government employee was a member of the Central Provident Fund cannot be reckoned for benefits under the Pensions Act, unless he first pays back to the Government any CPF contributions made by the Government on his behalf. This is a requirement under section 60 (I) of the Act. In view of the changed circumstances since April 1972, when pensionable employees were required to contribute two per cent of their emoluments to the Central Provident Fund in respect of their pensionable service, it is necessary to amend section 6 of the Pensions Act. This amendment will allow a pensionable employee to retain the two per cent CPF contribution paid by Government on his account when he eventually retires from the service.

    Section 16 of the Act is also similarly affected, and a consequential amendment is required. This section provides for the payment of a death gratuity when an officer dies in service, or within one year of his retirement. The amending Bill, when passed, is to be made retroactive from 1st April, 1972, which is the date on which contributions to the Central Provident Fund were made for pensionable Government employees.

    Sir, I beg to move.

    Question put, and agreed to.

    Bill accordingly read a Second time and committed to a Committee of the whole House.

    The House immediately resolved itself into a Committee on the Bill.-[Mr Hon Sui Sen].

INDUSTRIAL TRAINING BOARD BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.00 p.m.

    The Minister for Education (Dr Lee Chiaw Meng): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

Column: 367



    Sir, in 1959 when the Government embarked on transferring Singapore's economy on to an industrial base, we determined that the large proportion of the workforce would in due course be industrially employed. This meant that the development of a skilled industrial workforce would assume growing importance, as Singapore's national income became increasingly dependent on the output and productivity of industry. Industrial training has therefore been recognised as a major instrument of economic policy.

    Thus, as a matter of national urgency, the National Industrial Training Council was established in April 1968 to deal with the immediate problem of developing and co-ordinating technical education and industrial training to meet this need. Two months later the Technical Education Department was specially formed within the Ministry of Education to execute the targets established by the Council. In the short space of four years, the Technical Education Department has substantially achieved the initial targets set out and laid a firm foundation for the development of a skilled industrial workforce.

    Sir, I should like, at this juncture, to correct certain mis-impressions about our vocational institute graduates and the value of the training they have received, which would otherwise undermine what has so far been accomplished.

    Before the new vocational institutes were established, the main supply of workers with any vocational training at all came from the vocational schools. Because of the similar name, vocational institutes have often been confused with vocational schools, which now no longer exist. This confusion is most unfortunate. Admittedly, many former vocational school students were drop-outs from the school system. The vocational institutes, on the other hand, cater for those with a reasonably high level of education who wish to learn a practical skill. Industries show a distinct preference for these graduates. The value of their

Column: 368

training has also been recognised by the Public Service Commission.

    The vocational institute graduates have been trained at heavy public subsidy specially to undertake industrial employment and I am, therefore, naturally perturbed lest doubts about their value should take hold. No effort has been spared to explain to the public, through the mass media and other means, the many developments which have taken place in industrial training since 1968. Any misconceptions and prejudices which still persist, even among a small sector of the population, could be very damaging.

    The best way to stamp out these unfavourable attitudes and to ensure that training programmes receive the fullest acceptance and recognition is to develop close co-operation between the Government, industry and labour. The growth of such co-operation will become increasingly necessary in the next stage of industrial training, as it is envisaged that the further skill-development of the basic-trained worker can most effectively be undertaken by and within industry. It is for these reasons that an Industrial Training Board is being proposed to centralise, co-ordinate and intensify industrial training. A statutory board will enable industry and labour to participate more fully towards common goals. It will have more flexibility in an area where slowness to respond to changing conditions can result in high training wastage on the one hand and critical skill shortages on the other. A board will also be better able to deal with the problems of disseminating the right kind of conceptions about industrial training.

    When the responsibility for industrial training becomes vested in the Board, technical education in schools will remain the responsibility of the Ministry. The Technical Education Department, with all its functions taken over, will then be dissolved.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, this Bill is essentially a straightforward Bill. Its main purpose has already been outlined in the Explanatory

Column: 369

Statement, and I do not wish to go into the details again.

    While industrial training is primarily the responsibility of industry, it is imperative to ensure that national interests are served. A major function of the Board will be to encourage industry to play its proper role in the national industrial training effort.

    It is envisaged that the future pattern of industrial training to be promoted by the Industrial Training Board will be as follows: -

    (1) The training of operatives and artisans will gradually be transferred back to industry.

    (2) On the other hand, the further development of the necessary training capacity for the required output of skilled manpower, both institutionally and within industry, will be a major preoccupation of the Board.

    (3) The Board will increase the supply of persons institutionally trained up to Trade Certificate and Industrial Technician Certificate levels.

    (4) The further skill-development of the basic-trained worker will be accomplished by the employer with the help of the Board through on-the-job training and, wherever possible, within the framework of an apprenticeship training scheme.

    (5) The emphasis in industrial training will be placed on the attainment of recognised standards. The Government envisages that the Industrial Training Board will develop a national system of trade testing. This will not only improve the quality of training but also establish uniformity of standards within the work-force.

    (6) The upgrading of adult skilled workers will be progressively achieved through programmes of off-the-job Industrial Training (OJIT), based on the module system already introduced.

    (7) To cater for the smaller industries, group training schemes, encompassing both apprenticeship training and OJIT, will be developed for the upgrading of key personnel, and finally,

NATIONAL CADET CORPS BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.28 p.m.

    The Minister for Education (Dr Lee Chiaw Meng): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Sir, the Cadet Corps movement in Singapore was founded in 1902 when the first Army Cadet Corps units were formed in Raffles Institution. It was

Column: 376

only after the Second World War that the movement gained momentum. In 1947, Army Cadet Corps units were resuscitated; in 1948 the Sea Cadet Corps was formed, followed in 1949 by the formation of the Air Training Corps. Each of these organisations functioned independently of the other. With independence in 1965, the need to inculcate the youth of the Republic with a sense of patriotism and to develop in them ruggedness of mind and body and leadership qualities became imperative. It was considered that an effective way of bringing this about lay in the training provided by the uniformed movement in the schools. An expansion programme of the Cadet Corps movement was therefore embarked upon in 1965. The combined membership of the three Cadet Corps then stood at just over 1,500 with 24 schools participating. Today, the strength of the Cadet Corps has risen to over 14,000 members (9,000 boys and 5,000 girls) with 102 out of 113 secondary schools participating. Next year, the Cadet Corps membership is expected to reach 18,000.

    Membership in the Cadet Corps extends from Secondary I to Pre-University I students. It is no exaggeration to say that many of our top Singapore Armed Forces personnel once served as cadets when they were in school. I am informed that cadets, on becoming national servicemen, have acquitted themselves well, setting high standards in discipline, bearing, turnout and performances. That the Cadet Corps provides sound pre-national service training is no modest claim.

    With increasing numbers of students wishing to join the Cadet Corps, at one time the movement was faced with the difficulty of training sufficient numbers of officers to meet new demands and to replace those leaving the Corps on promotion, transfers, secondment, study leave and resignations. However, the problem is gradually being overcome with the introduction of cadet training at the Teachers Training College. Last June, 36 TTC trainees graduated as qualified teachers as well as trained

Column: 377

cadet officers. Currently, there are 99 final year TTC students undergoing cadet training. Steps are being taken to relieve the shortage of officers in the Cadet Corps through the appointments of SAF reservists who are teachers. Attention is paid to Pre-U I students who are given advanced training for appointment as Cadet Lieutenants.

    The expansion of the Cadet Corps movement necessitated a complete review of the Cadet Corps organisation as a whole. Hence on 1st January, 1969, the National Cadet Corps was formed by the integration of the Army, Sea and Air Cadet Corps to strengthen the administration of the Cadet Corps movement and to raise the level of training of all cadets in schools. This move ensures that the best use is made of manpower, buildings, training areas and facilities and of other logistical requirements, resulting in maximum utilisation of available resources. With this integration, new legislation has to be promulgated to replace the existing Acts and Regulations which govern the Army, Sea and Air Cadet Corps separately. Hence the introduction of the National Cadet Corps Bill. In passing, I would like to mention that the Police Cadet Corps does not form part of the National Cadet Corps. It remains under the control of the Ministry of Home Affairs. However, steps are being taken to update the Police Cadet Corps Act and the Police Cadet Corps Regulations to bring them in line with the main provisions of the National Cadet Corps Act and Regulations.

    With the enactment of the National Cadet Corps Bill, the Cadet Corps will be accorded the status befitting the role it is playing in training our youth to become loyal and responsible citizens as part of our effort to build an organised and disciplined society.
