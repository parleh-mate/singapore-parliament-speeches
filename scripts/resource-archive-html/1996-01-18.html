DELIVERY OF HEALTH CARE
(Improvements)


    1. Dr S. Vasoo asked the Minister for Health to specify some of the improvements in delivery of health care since the restructuring of hospitals.

     The Senior Minister of State for Health (Dr Aline K. Wong) (for the Minister for Health): Mr Speaker, Sir, restructuring has brought about many improvements in the delivery of health care service in public hospitals. Some of these improvements include more patient oriented services,

Column: 421

better communication with patients, improvements in patient waiting times, new clinical services and improvements to infrastructure. I would describe a little the improvements in each of these areas.

(i) More Patient Oriented Services

    One significant area of improvement is in the reorganisation and simplification of operational systems, procedures and work flow which have made hospital services more convenient to patients and more responsive to their medical needs. For patients who require admission, a one-stop admission service at ward level has been introduced at Toa Payoh Hospital (TPH) and Singapore General Hospital (SGH). At the outpatient level, specialist outpatient clinics have been reorganised into multi-disciplinary one-stop clinics, eg, Urology Centre, Brain Centre, Heart Centre and Diabetes Centre at SGH; Arthritis Clinic at Tan Tock Seng Hospital (TTSH) and Oncology Centre at Kandang Kerbau Hospital (KKH).

(ii) Better Communication with Patients

    Through an extensive service improvement programme, all hospital frontline staff and supervisors have been trained to be more caring and courteous in their dealings with the public. There has been significant improvement in the way doctors, nurses and counter clerks relate to the patients, their relatives and the public. Financial counselling is routinely provided to all patients and their relatives before admission. Information booklets on hospital services and certain treatment procedures have been made available to patients. This has helped to allay the anxiety of patients before admission or surgery. iii) Improvements in Patient Waiting Times

    There has been a general improvement in waiting times for appointments for surgery and specialist outpatient clinic consultation and waiting times to see the doctor at the specialist outpatient clinics and at the pharmacies to collect medicines.

(iv) New Clinical Services

    New clinical services have been introduced in the hospitals' departments since restructuring. These include Medical Oncology, Neurosurgery and Neurology in SGH, Ear, Nose and Throat and Eye services in TPH, genetic counselling and menopause clinics in KKH, and Podiatry service in TTSH. SGH and TTSH also have walk- in clinics to treat non-emergency patients who would normally have been attended to at the Accident and Emergency (A&E) Department. This has allowed the doctors at the A&E Departments to spend more time on the emergency patients.

(v) Improvements to Infrastructure

    With the opening of ambulatory surgery centres in the hospitals, more patients can now undergo surgery as day patients, thus reducing their hospital cost. New technology such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) and Computerised Tomography (CT) scans have been introduced. The operating theatre and intensive care facilities have been upgraded. Physical improvements have been made to the wards and specialist outpatient clinics in all hospitals so that patients can now enjoy a better ambience and greater comfort as an inpatient or as an outpatient.

    All these improvements have led to a high level of patient satisfaction. In general, more than 90% of feedback from patients have rated the hospital care as good or excellent.

    3. Assoc. Prof. Walter Woon asked the Minister for Finance - in the light of the Minister's statement in Parliament on 1st March 1995 that SIMEX would appoint experts from the global futures industry to advise it on protecting itself against future crises like that involving Barings Futures - (a) what recommendations (if any) were made by the experts; (b) whether these recommendations have been implemented by SIMEX; and (c) what other steps (if any) have been taken by SIMEX to prevent a recurrence of the problems that caused the collapse of Barings Futures.

     The Minister for Finance (Dr Richard Hu Tsu Tau): Mr Speaker, Sir, in March 1995, SIMEX appointed an International Advisory Panel comprising experts from the global

Column: 428

futures industry. They were appointed to advise on best practices in the futures industry worldwide that could be adopted to improve SIMEX's operations and systems and to enhance customer protection. The Panel concluded its study in October 1995 and made the following recommendations to SIMEX:

    (i) To establish rules and evaluate those in place regarding customer protection with respect to proper margining of positions, segregation and trading floor practices;

    (ii) To upgrade SIMEX clearing policies, systems and procedures to incorporate real-time clearing and settlement and critical risk management systems;

    (iii) To establish confidential information sharing arrangements with mutual offset partners and other exchanges;

    (iv) To establish a requirement for clearing members that a senior officer, who must be in a position of full authority for all business of the firm at SIMEX and related activities on other exchanges, be registered with the Exchange;

    (v) To enhance SIMEX's Market Surveillance Department which is charged with responsibilities including monitoring market positions and their potential impact; and

    (vi) To establish a comprehensive large trader reporting system which will enable the Exchange to determine the ultimate owner of large positions on SIMEX including those maintained in omnibus accounts.

Column: 429

settlement operations and its system for market surveillance so as to improve its clearing and risk management functions. The key measures recommended by the consultant included:

    (ii) Improving internal risk analysis procedures to identify accounts and members carrying large positions which may result in substantial losses, and perform tests on these accounts to determine the potential risk to both members and large account holders;

Staffing for the division has been strengthened from 48 to 68 members currently, and an eventual total of 78.

RELIGIOUS/QUASI-RELIGIOUS ORGANISATIONS

    4. Assoc. Prof. Walter Woon asked the Minister for Home Affairs how many religious or quasi-religious organisations have been banned in Singapore under the Societies Act or other legislation.

     The Senior Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Home Affairs (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Mr Speaker, Sir, three religious or quasi-religious organisations have been deregistered under the Societies Act.

Those provisions were more suited to mainframe computers sited in specially designed premises, especially in the 1960s and 1970s. The use of personal computers at home and in the office is now common place, as is the linking of such computers to form vast networks that allow information to be entered, stored, altered and retrieved by a host of users in a global environment. Developments in document imaging technologies have also enabled users to keep images of their paper documents in electronic media, thereby

Need for computer-related amendments
