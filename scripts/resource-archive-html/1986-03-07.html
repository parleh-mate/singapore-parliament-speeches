PULAU LANGKAWI TRAGEDY
(Returning bodies to Singapore)


    4. Encik Wan Hussin bin Hj Zoohri asked the Minister for Foreign Affairs what steps the Government took to facilitate the bringing back of the bodies of the victims of the Pulau Langkawi tragedy to Singapore.

     The Minister for Foreign Affairs (Mr S. Dhanabalan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs learned of the Pulau Langkawi tragedy at about 5 pm on 10th February 1986, which was a public holiday in Singapore. The Singapore High Commission in Kuala Lumpur was immediately alerted and the Ministry took action to inform the families of the deceased. The Ministry made arrangements, through the High Commission, to have the bodies flown back on 11th February for burial in Singapore. As there were no scheduled flights available from Pulau Langkawi to Singapore, the High Commission chartered an aircraft on behalf of the families to fly the bodies to Singapore. The bodies arrived at 5.50 pm on 11th February and they were buried on the same day under special arrangements made by the Police and the Ministry of the Environment.

REVIEW OF BUS FARES


    2. Mr S. Vasoo asked the Minister for Communications and Information if there are plans to review the bus-fare structure, in view of the pending reduction of operational cost of the Singapore Bus Service.

     Dr Yeo Ning Hong: Bus fares are determined by bus companies. Government's role is to encourage bus companies to operate efficiently and productively and to ensure that fares are reasonable in the light of operational costs.

    Recent increases in productivity has enabled bus fares to be maintained at the same level since January 1982.

    Anticipated decreases in employers' CPF contribution and fuel costs will help the profitability of bus companies in the short term. If these reductions are of a permanent nature and are of a level that would permit a reduction in fares, then bus companies should consider a fare reduction at the appropriate time. However, at the moment, these reductions do not appear to be long term.

    Bus fares cannot be changed frequently because they require a long time for adjustments to be made. Otherwise, passengers and drivers alike will be confused about the correct fares. Therefore, bus fares should be changed only when changes in operating costs are reasonably permanent.

    To be practical, the change to any fare must be in steps of 5 cts or 10 cts per passenger trip. With more than 700 million passenger trips a year, even a reduction of 5 cts per trip requires long term profitability of bus companies to be in excess of $35 million per annum before this reduction can be absorbed. These conditions are not present at the moment.

    What bus companies should do is to plough back the extra profit and use it wisely to further improve their services and productivity, and hence ensure that the present fares can be kept at this level for as long as possible, even after operating costs go up again when the economy recovers.
