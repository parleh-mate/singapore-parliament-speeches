ORAL ANSWER TO QUESTION
INCREASE IN PETROL PUMP PRICES
(Rationale)


    1. Mr Tan Soo Khoon asked the Minister for Trade and Industry what is the rationale for the recent increase in petrol pump prices.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, first let me say how honoured I am to have been asked the first question by the former Speaker.

    With growing tension in the Middle East, oil prices have been going up and we are seeing this reflected in the prices of petrol.

    Generally speaking, petrol pump prices are determined by market demand and supply. Consumer demand for the various grades of petrol determines the overall demand. On the supply side, costs depend on several factors, including the costs of storage and distribution, the prices of crude oil and wholesale petrol, and the foreign exchange rates. Crude oil prices account for about 20% of the pump price. In the short run, as consumer demand does not adjust rapidly, changes in pump prices generally reflect changes in these cost factors. However, because of the oligopolistic structure of the industry, prices tend to be sticky both ways.

    The Consumers Association of Singapore (CASE) has asked the oil companies

Column: 347

to justify the recent increases in petrol pump prices. One of the oil companies, ExxonMobil, has explained in detail in the Straits Times on 28th March that the rise in wholesale petrol prices since January this year was the main factor that contributed to its decision to raise petrol pump prices.

    The key question is whether there is price fixing by the oil companies. Looking at the fluctuations, the Ministry of Trade and Industry finds no evidence of collusion by the oil companies either to restrict supply or to raise prices. Either would be frowned upon by the Government and would be illegal once we put in place a Competition Law.

    The recent price increases follow decreases in petrol prices last year. For the period between July 2001 and December 2001, crude oil prices fell by approximately 26%. Around the same period, and after taking into account a time lag, petrol pump prices fell by 14%. Part of this reflected the reduction in petrol taxes announced in October 2001 as part of the off-Budget measures. I have circulated a chart showing changes in the prices of crude oil and petrol from 1993 to February this year. Last year, I circulated a similar chart.

    Mr Tan Soo Khoon (East Coast): Sir, if there is no price fixing among the major petroleum companies, why is it that price increases are always more or less by the same quantum amongst the oil companies, and why is it that the price increase takes place almost on the same day?

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Mr Speaker, Sir, this is a well known phenomenon in economics. When an industry structure reflects high fixed cost and, where there are few players involved, normally, the lowest cost producer will be the price leader and the rest will follow suit. In such an industry, there is a tendency for price instability and for price wars to happen. So the smaller players have to be very careful not to trigger off a chain of events which may lead to price wars and they will normally take the lead from the price leader. This is called monopolistic competition by some economists. It reflects the nature of competition in oligopolistic structures. The airline industry faces the same problem. Many chemical industries face the same problem.

    Mr Tan Soo Khoon: Sir, if that is the case, why is it that the smallest petroleum company in Singapore, which is the Singapore Petroleum Company, with only five or six outlets in Singapore has managed to keep its price low and has not increased price and, in fact, is offering a discount?

Oil companies compete in different ways, some offer free tissue paper, some offer loyalty programmes. So there are different ways to compete and to hold on to your customers. Debate on the Address

    'That the following Address in reply to the Speech of the President be agreed to:

    Mr Ong Kian Min (Tampines): Mr Speaker, Sir, thank you for allowing me to join in the debate. Today is the fourth day of this debate. As more than 40 speakers have already risen to speak, I would just like to focus on a couple of specific issues. I support the Motion of Thanks to the President moved by Mrs Lim Hwee Hua.

Competition is global

    In his Address, the President has highlighted the immense challenges we face and the impact of global competition. Indeed, competition is global and is coming not just from China and the region. The World Economic Forum recently reported that Singapore's global growth competitiveness has slipped two notches from second to fourth placing.

    For many years, Singapore has been holding the No. 1 or No. 2 position, and our good rating has been well publicised. But little has been said about the drop in our ranking this time round. For the first time, we have been overtaken by Finland and Canada. Fast closing up behind us include Australia, Norway and Taiwan, whose rankings have improved by three to nine positions within one year.

The need to stay ahead

    What we do to tackle the present challenges determines our future. Do we despair and lose hope, do nothing and allow others to overtake us? Do we dismiss it and be content with the slack,

Column: 355

thinking that things must somehow get better when the world economy gets moving again? Or are we content to say there is no need to stay at the top all the time? If our answer to the above questions is yes, we run the risk of being tossed out of the race and get left behind. We have to regain speed, make bold strides to catch up and stay ahead.

    We must not be daunted by the challenges before us. We must not be disheartened or feel hopeless. On the contrary, we can turn them to our advantage. We can do with a healthy dose of the "siege mentality". We, like the employees of successful global companies, should be driven with a sense of urgency and purpose. We must not be complacent. Microsoft, the No. 1 PC software company, has been described to behave as if it were No. 2 and trying to play catch-up all the time. Nokia succeeded in overtaking its much larger competitors who had the first-mover advantage. Now that Nokia is No. 1, it fights even harder to stay ahead. These companies all know that once you slip, you can go all the way down and never return to the days of glory.

Challenges not unique

    The challenges Singapore faces are not unique. I would like to add to what Assoc. Prof. Koo Tsai Kee said yesterday about Hong Kong with my own personal observations. I was in Zhuhai, China, last week. I took the ferry to Hong Kong on Good Friday to catch the SQ flight back home. Going the other way were thousands of Hongkongers leaving Hong Kong for mainland China. The ferries were packed and we had to fight through long queues of people waiting to buy tickets. Hongkongers go to Macau, Zhuhai and Shenzhen by the hundreds of thousands everyday for dinner, movies and other forms of entertainment. On a bad day, one has to stand more than one hour to clear immigration at the Hong Kong-Shenzhen border crossing. Things are cheaper across the waters. People go where they find better value for their money, especially in these lean times. When money

People - the differentiating factor

    How then do we achieve competitive differentiation in this global environment? Others can import the same technology and talent to build the same or even better factory, airport and seaport. It must be the people factor that is our strategic resource. Responding to the challenges we face means that we, the people, have to change our mindset.
