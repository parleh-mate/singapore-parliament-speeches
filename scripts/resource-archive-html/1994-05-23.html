MEDISAVE
(Contribution by part-time self-employed people)


    3. Dr Wang Kai Yuen asked the Minister for Health why is a person engaged in a second job of a self-employed nature on a part-time basis required to contribute to Medisave even though that person is already contributing to Medisave in respect of his regular full-time job.

     The Minister for Health (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, currently, a person who holds multiple employment is required to contribute to CPF from all sources of employment income.     The same principle applies to a person who is concurrently an employee and a self-employed person. He is required to contribute to Medisave based on all sources of income. EMPLOYMENT OF PART-TIME FEMALE WORKERS
(Incentives)


    7. Mr Chia Shi Teck asked the Minister for Labour (a) whether more incentives will be introduced to attract females who cannot go into full-time employment to work part-time and (b) whether a lower employee Central Provident Fund contribution rate will be introduced for part-time workers so as to allow them a higher take-home pay whilst maintaining the 20% contribution from the employer.

     The Minister for Labour (Dr Lee Boon Yang): Mr Speaker, Sir, success in attracting the economically inactive females to join the part-time workforce depends on many factors. These include the benefits offered by the employers and the scheduling of part-time work for the convenience of housewives.

    My Ministry will ensure that our labour laws do not stifle the employment of part-time workers. In August 1990, the Ministry introduced a set of guidelines to help facilitate part-time employment. The Guidelines on Part-Time Employment were drawn up based on tripartite consultation and consensus. Under the Guidelines, statutory benefits are pro-rated based on the part-timer's contractual working hours in a year vis-a-vis that of a full-timer. Employers are also given the flexibility to encash certain benefits such as annual leave and public holiday entitlement for the part-time workers. The Ministry is conducting a review to formalise these guidelines into Employment Regulations so that the rights and obligations of the employers and part-timers are more clearly spelt out.

Column: 18



    With the Regulations in place to facilitate part-time employment, employers should come up with innovative schemes to meet the convenience of housewives so that more would come forward to work part-time. This would also help to alleviate our manpower shortage and reduce our reliance on foreign workers.

    On the Member's question of reducing the CPF contribution rate for part-time workers, my Ministry is not in favour of this. This is because CPF contribution is not the sole factor influencing the attractiveness of part-time employment. Reducing CPF contribution may not result in a larger number of people engaging in part-time work. On the other hand, any reduction in CPF contribution rates for part-time workers will affect some 47,000 part-time workers who are currently making full CPF contributions. Part-time workers also need to save for retirement and Medisave as much as full-time workers. The CPF scheme, as it is presently structured, helps them to do so. To lower CPF rate for part-time workers will create anomalies. A person working part-time but earning more may end up paying less CPF than another person working full-time but earning less.

    Finally, reducing the CPF contribution rate, even if it is the employee's contribution rate, may lead some employers to take into account the lower CPF contribution rate, hence, higher take-home pay, and therefore offering a lower basic wage to the part-time worker. The part-time worker may still not end up with a higher take-home pay.
