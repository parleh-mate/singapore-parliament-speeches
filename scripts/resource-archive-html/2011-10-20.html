Inflow of Foreign Workers

1 Mr Christopher de Souza asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower whether the Ministry would consider calibrating the flow of foreign manpower into Singapore in accordance with the ability of our public infrastructure to cope with such an inflow and, if so, how does the Government carry out this calibration exercise.

The Minister of State for Manpower (BG [NS] Tan Chuan-Jin) (for the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower) : Mr Speaker, Sir, the capacity of a public infrastructure is a key consideration when planning for the growth of our overall workforce. We are therefore seeking to moderate the growth of the foreign workforce at no higher than one-third over the long term. Besides the social and economic reasons for this target, which I have explained yesterday, there are also real physical and infrastructural limits that we do need to take into account.

The Government has already introduced various measures to moderate the inflow of foreign manpower, in particular, at the Work Permit and S Pass levels. We have announced the schedule of Foreign Worker Levy increases which began from July 2010 and will continue until July 2013. The increases in levies and other measures such as the phased tightening of levy tiers send a clear signal to companies to avoid growing our reliance on foreign workers and instead invest in productivity improvements so that they can remain more competitive in the long run. The Government is providing full support for this productivity effort. At the Employment Pass (EP) level, qualifying salaries were raised in July 2011. From January 2012, EP applicants will be subjected to more stringent criteria including better educational qualifications and higher qualifying salaries.

Although managing the inflow of foreign workers has to remain a finely balanced exercise, our key objective must be to ensure that our economy remains diversified and competitive, and thus is able to provide good jobs for Singaporeans. At the same time, the Government is ramping up our public infrastructure development plans to meet the needs of a growing population.

Mr Christopher de Souza (Holland-Bukit Timah) : I would like to thank the Minister of State for his response to my question. Just one clarification. We all appreciate that Singapore needs to remain open and vibrant in order to be a global city. However, one concern is that when a foreigner comes and works in Singapore, and he or she enjoys it, then, the family comes also. While we should obviously be open to this, is the Ministry of Manpower looking at, after someone comes and settles here, whether there is sufficient infrastructural support in transport, in health services, to accommodate the families coming also?

BG [NS] Tan Chuan-Jin : Mr Speaker, that is indeed the idea. Going forward, we do need to be cognisant of the overall demands. Clearly, not every foreign worker and person on Employment Pass who comes here, will bring the family, but that is something that we do have to bear in mind. Going forward, looking at the demands placed on our infrastructure would be something we need to consider.

Housing Foreign Workers

2 Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower (a) what is the current number of foreign workers in Singapore; (b) how many bed spaces are allocated to foreign workers (excluding foreign domestic workers who live in their employers' homes); (c) how does the Ministry manage any shortfall in bed spaces for foreign workers; and (d) where are foreign workers without bed spaces currently accommodated.

BG [NS] Tan Chuan-Jin (for the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower) : Mr Speaker, there are currently 670,000 foreign non-domestic work permit holders accommodated in a variety of housing options including purpose-built dormitories, workers' quarters on construction sites, converted industrial properties, HDB flats and private residential premises.

Housing of foreign workers in proper accommodation is a multi-faceted issue which involves close collaboration between different Government Ministries and agencies. A Foreign Worker Housing Coordinating Committee, jointly led by MOM and MND, oversees this issue. Since 2007, the Government has been launching new sites for dormitories to meet the need for foreign worker housing. There are currently over 140,000 bed spaces for foreign workers in purpose-built dormitories which mainly house workers from the construction, marine and processed industrial sectors.

Page: 337

In spite of the housing options available, there are irresponsible employers who continue to breach the housing requirements and compromise on foreign workers' well-being. In the worst instances, workers have been found, housed in make-shift huts or bin centres. To ensure that foreign workers are housed in acceptable housing, MOM, together with agencies such as URA, SCDF and NEA, conducts regular inspections at locations suspected as being used for illegal foreign worker housing. These efforts are producing positive outcomes. Since 2010, almost 15,000 foreign workers have been relocated to acceptable housing. Over the same period, enforcement action was taken against more than 2,300 employers for breaching the Work Permit conditions by housing their workers in unacceptable conditions.

We will keep up the enforcement efforts as we develop more foreign worker housing. At the same time, as Members know, we are also taking measures to reduce our reliance on low-skilled foreign workers over time.

Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef (Marine Parade) : Sir, for those foreign workers who are staying in the less favourable sites and in unhygienic situations, what are our plans to decant them in the medium term? And secondly, Sir, can the Minister of State update us on the plans for social and recreational areas specifically for foreign workers?

BG [NS] Tan Chuan-Jin : Mr Speaker, Sir, where we do find foreign workers housed in unacceptable conditions, we do put in place measures to relocate them, which is why expediting the construction of some of these dormitories and housing options is something that we are pressing ahead with. We are speaking to the various advisers on this. Secondly, it is an important feature in the construction of these living quarters that we provide recreational and eating facilities, and so on, within those premises. Going forward, we are putting those in place to make sure that the foreign workers do have viable options. We think that is one important step in providing them a hospitable and good environment to not only live in, but to spend their free time in as well.

Mr Christopher de Souza : Sir, I would like to ask the Minister of State to consider a proposal I had put up during the last Parliament session: for employers who do not house their foreign workers in appropriate accommodation and do not provide proper bed spaces, then, going forward, that they be penalised where their quota for employing foreign workers in the years ahead is concerned. That would be a sufficient deterrent and would ensure that proper bed spaces and accommodation are given.

Economic Impact of Pulau Bukom Oil Refinery Fire

3 Ms Sylvia Lim asked the Minister for Trade and Industry if he would provide an interim assessment on the impact of the Shell oil refinery fire and facilities shut-down at Pulau Bukom on the Singapore economy, in particular on energy supplies and prices.

The Second Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr S Iswaran) (for the Minister for Trade and Industry) : Mr Speaker, Sir, our assessment at this juncture is that the shutdown of the Shell refinery on Pulau Bukom is likely to have a minimal impact on the overall economy. And there are several reasons for this assessment.

Page: 338

First, even though there is a loss of petroleum output in the short term, the impact on downstream chemicals plants is small as there are alternative sources of feedstock. Furthermore, while the refinery accounts for a significant portion of the chemicals cluster, the cluster as a whole accounts for less than 3% of Singapore's GDP.

Second, Shell's efforts at restarting the refinery remain on track. On 10 October, Shell partially restarted its largest Crude Distillation Unit which accounts for 20% of its refining capacity. Shell is working closely with the relevant authorities to safely re-start additional processing units of the refinery as soon as possible.

Third, the incident is not likely to affect the chemicals cluster in the longer term. Investor confidence in Singapore remains strong and new investment projects will continue to be developed.

Finally, the Pulau Bukom fire incident does not affect Singapore's electricity supply. The primary fuel for generation of electricity is natural gas. While fuel oil is used for some power generation, our generation companies do not have any existing supply contracts with Shell for fuel oil. The generation companies also maintain a stockpile of fuel oil and diesel, in line with the Energy Market Authority's licence requirements. These are not stored on Pulau Bukom and hence the fuel reserve for electricity generation was unaffected by the fire.

Fuel oil prices are used to set our electricity and town gas tariffs. Members would be aware that this is because of the link between the natural gas we use for our power generation and fuel oil prices. The Pulau Bukom fire has not had any impact on fuel oil prices, which continue to be driven by global demand and supply trends. Based on currently available data, we do not expect the Pulau Bukom fire incident to have an impact on electricity and gas tariffs.

Ms Sylvia Lim (Aljunied) : Sir, two supplementary questions for the Minister. First, there have been private sector economists' estimates about how much has been lost to the economy due to the shut-down. The figures that were cited were between $100 million and $200 million, depending on how long it goes on and what facilities are affected. So, I would like to ask the Minister whether the Government has an estimate of how much has been lost so far, in dollar terms, to the economy in terms of output. Second question, Sir, is: there are obviously Singaporeans working in Shell and Singapore businesses supplying services to the Shell operations. Given that these livelihoods are affected, how is the Government working with Shell to ensure that the operations come up online and in the safest way possible?

Mr S Iswaran : Sir, I thank the Member for the supplementary questions. Let me take the second point first. As far as the coordination of the recovery effort is concerned, there are two aspects to this: one is the fire safety and technical aspects, which we will address subsequently in another Parliamentary Question. As far as the economic aspects are concerned – because this is part of an integrated value-chain for us – EDB is working very closely with Shell and with the downstream companies that rely on some of the products in order to ensure that there is a smooth transition. I think, in the first instance, the key point is that there are feedstock supplies. So, in other words, there are inventories that can support the activities. So, a lot of the measurement of the outcome or the impact will really depend on how smoothly and quickly Shell can restart its operations. So, when the Member refers to private sector estimates, well, it really depends on the assumptions on which they rely. As of now, the Government's estimation is that the impact would be minimal but we are reluctant to put an actual dollar value until we know the exact duration and rate of response in recovery.

Ms Sylvia Lim : Sir, thank you and just one more supplementary question. I understand that it would take time for this thing to pan out, but what I was asking was whether, to date, the Government has an estimate of how much has been lost.

Mr S Iswaran : Sir, if we think about the economy and the size of it – 3% of GDP and this is just a component of that – and then we can do some estimations based on that. But, really, this is guesstimation. I do not think they are reliable numbers because you are not really looking at what is happening on a day-to-day basis. You want to look at the total impact. Emergency Response to Pulau Bukom Oil Refinery Fire

4 Ms Sylvia Lim asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs with regard to the fire at the Shell refinery on Pulau Bukom on 28 September (a) whether the Government had worked with Royal Dutch Shell PLC or its subsidiaries to draw up or approve contingency plans to respond to the risk of various types of fire at Shell's facilities on Pulau Bukom and, if so, how was this undertaken; and (b) what findings have been made on the preparedness of the relevant responders, from both the public and private sector, in dealing with the incident.

The Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Teo Chee Hean) : Mr Speaker, Sir, the Royal Dutch Shell refinery at Pulau Bukom (Shell Bukom) is required under the Fire Safety (Petroleum & Flammable Materials) Regulations to put in place appropriate safety measures to address the risks inherent in its operations. One key requirement is to have an Emergency Response Plan, which Shell Bukom is required to update regularly, to deal with various types of incidents, such as spillage, leakage, accidental discharge and fire. This plan is reviewed by the Singapore Civil Defence Force.

Shell Bukom is also required under the terms of its licence to establish and maintain an in-house emergency response team known as the Company Emergency Response Team (CERT). The SCDF conducts annual audits on the CERT to assess its competency. Failure to meet basic standards may result in composition fines or even the revocation or non-renewal of the licence that permits the storage of petroleum and flammable materials.

SCDF has developed and dovetailed its contingency plans with Shell Bukom. These plans are validated through joint exercises at least once a year. In 2011, two major deployment exercises coupled with CERT audits were conducted by SCDF in February and September. These exercises were critical in testing evacuation procedures, transportation of SCDF assets to Pulau Bukom, and their respective command and control systems.

On the preparedness of the responders to the fire at Pulau Bukom, our preliminary assessment indicates that both Shell Bukom and SCDF responded expeditiously and in an organised and well-coordinated manner. This was largely due to the close relationship established between Shell Bukom and SCDF through their regular joint exercises. Shell Bukom's CERT responded according to established protocols practised in its regular training, while SCDF sized and deployed its resources on-site quickly and appropriately.

Page: 340

In addition, pre-established mutual aid contingency arrangements between Shell Bukom and other petrochemical companies, including those on Jurong Island, were activated in good time to share foam and fire-fighting resources.

Throughout the incident, SCDF worked closely with other public agencies, including the National Environment Agency on environmental pollution control issues, and MINDEF which had offered its Fast Craft Utility and Super Puma Helicopters to support ongoing operations if needed. SCDF also worked with the Singapore Police Force to ensure the smooth evacuation of personnel on the island early on during the emergency. In addition, the Transport and Health Ministries were engaged to look into the impact on nearby shipping traffic and medical response-related matters respectively.

Ms Sylvia Lim : Three supplementary questions for Deputy Prime Minister Teo. Mr Teo Chee Hean : Sir, I do not want to comment on what was reported in the media or the press because it may or may not be accurate based on hearsay, but what the Ministry of Home Affairs would be doing is conducting an inquiry into the events, the events that led up to it, whether the safety arrangements were properly followed, then the response, both of Shell and SCDF, and whether those processes were properly followed. Deferment of National Service for Medical Studies

(Dr Patrick Tan's disruption from NS)

5 Dr Lim Wee Kiak asked the Minister for Defence if he could clarify (a) whether Dr Patrick Tan's prolonged disruption from full-time National Service, as well as his deployment to the Defence Medical and Environmental Research Institute, adhered to the then prevailing policies; and (b) whether Dr Patrick Tan had been accorded any preferential treatment.

The Minister for Defence (Dr Ng Eng Hen) : Mr Speaker, Sir, MINDEF had issued two public replies, in July and August this year, relating to Dr Patrick Tan's disruption and deployment during his National Service. In those replies, we stated that he was treated according to prevailing policies. He was not given any preferential treatment. Let me provide further details on how these policies were applied to all full-time National Servicemen. I use the term "NSFs" for full-time National Servicemen over the years. I beg Members' indulgence as there are quite a lot of details because the period is long. Please bear with me because I think details are important.

Since 1973, nearly four decades ago, MINDEF has allowed NSFs to disrupt from medical studies in Singapore because we need military doctors to serve in the SAF. This policy continues today. So, I think it is quite well known that NUS medical graduates are disrupted while they are NSFs to finish their medical degrees.

From 1981, the disruption policy for medical studies was extended to allow NSFs to study medicine overseas because the number of local graduates was insufficient. From 1981 to 1986, 38 NSFs were disrupted for overseas medical studies.

From 1986, the criteria were tightened to take into account their performance during Basic Military Training and Physical Employment Standards (PES) screening. Eight-six applicants were disrupted under this latter scheme, including Dr Patrick Tan in 1988. He had completed his BMT and was part-way through Officer Cadet School. From 1992, disruption for overseas medical studies was no longer allowed as the number of local medical graduates met SAF's needs. All in all, in the 12-year period, namely, 1981 to 1992, when disruption for both local and overseas medical studies was a prevailing policy, 902 NSFs were disrupted to study medicine in Singapore and 124 for overseas medical studies, for a total of about 1,026 NSFs. So, in that 12-year period, 1,026 full-time National Servicemen were disrupted for medical studies, 902 locally and 124 overseas.

Page: 342

MINDEF granted disruption so long as the medical degree was recognised by the Singapore Medical Council. And the length of disruption varied according to the time required to obtain a medical degree. For those who studied medicine locally, the period of disruption taken was typically seven years − five years for the medical degree, an additional year each for housemanship and hospital postings.

For those who studied overseas, almost all of the 124 medical students chose to study in the United Kingdom, Ireland or Australia. And these were disrupted for about seven to eight years. Only three NSFs applied and were approved for disruption to study medicine in the United States of America (US). By way of explanation, let me say that in the US system, the medical course is a graduate programme. In other words, to enter into a medical school, you will be required to have a pre-medical degree. So, the disruption period is typically longer – four years to obtain the pre-medical degree and then five more years for the medical degree proper.

I mentioned that only three applied for and were approved for medical studies in the US. One of them was deferred and disrupted for nine years. He obtained his US pre-medical and medical degree. The second case was deferred for four years to obtain his pre-medical degree and then decided that he did not want to do his medicine in the US but wanted to do it in Ireland instead. MINDEF granted it to him, so he was deferred and disrupted for a total of 12 years – four years pre-medical degree in the US, eight years for medical degree in Ireland. The third case, Dr Patrick Tan, was also disrupted for 12 years, for his pre-medical course in Harvard University from 1988 to 1992, and thereafter, for his combined medical degree and PhD program in Stanford University's Medical Scientist Training Program.

MINDEF had written to Stanford University to enquire if Dr Patrick Tan could shorten his course for just an MD – a medical degree. Stanford University replied that it was not possible, "the Medical Scientist Training Program is an integrated MD/PhD program in which both the MD and PhD degrees will be simultaneously conferred".

Several others NSFs have been deferred or disrupted from NS to obtain their basic degree followed by a PhD. These are few and each case was assessed and approved based on individual merits. Let me cite some examples.

In 1983, a serviceman was disrupted to pursue medicine locally. After he obtained his medical degree, he was disrupted again for his PhD studies in Oxford. He did Onco-pathology under the Rhodes Scholarship. In total, he was granted disruption for ten-and-a-half years.

In 1990, an exceptionally bright pre-enlistee entered University at age 14 and was accepted into a PhD course in Cambridge before he was due for enlistment. MINDEF allowed him to defer his NS until he completed his PhD at the age of 21. There were two other servicemen enlisted in 1988 and 1993 respectively, who were disrupted twice, first to obtain an undergraduate degree under the Public Service Commission (PSC) scholarship, followed by a second disruption to obtain their PhD under the Rhodes Scholarship. Both disrupted for a total of eight years.

Page: 343

So, in summary, for disruption, Dr Patrick Tan was disrupted in accordance with prevailing policy to obtain his medical degree. A longer period was granted to those who were admitted to US medical schools. Disruption for overseas medical studies has been discontinued since 1992. We also have not granted disruption or deferment for PhD studies since 1999.

Now, let me speak about Dr Patrick Tan's deployment in answer to Dr Lim Wee Kiak's question. Dr Patrick Tan was re-enlisted in the year 2000, having obtained both his MD and PhD degrees. He was deployed to the Defence Medical Research Institute (DMRI), in view of his training and qualifications.

DMRI was a new institution then, established in 1995 to coordinate and conduct human science and biomedical research aimed at enhancing the safety, survivability and performance of SAF personnel.

Other full-time National Servicemen had been posted to DMRI in the five years preceding Patrick Tan's posting, from 1995 to 1999. Thirty-two NSFs were posted to DMRI, including another MD/PhD holder, some medical officers and the rest with A-levels, basic and Masters degrees. Fifteen of these 32 that I have mentioned were combat-fit. Each case was again approved on its own merit as having the qualifications that met the needs of DMRI. Some served in DMRI for the bulk of their full-time NS, while others were medical officers posted there on shorter stints of about six months.

After serving his full-time NS, Dr Patrick Tan was deployed to 3rd Combat Support Hospital and served his remaining NS liability till 2009.

MINDEF is acutely aware that all NS men must be treated equitably, regardless of background. I want to assure all Singaporeans that equity of treatment for National Service has been and must remain a cardinal principle. Without this strict adherence to this fundamental tenet of fair and transparent treatment, commitment to NS will be severely eroded and the institution of NS weakened.

We had or have explicit schemes, open to all applicants, which allow NSFs to disrupt for medical studies here or overseas which I have outlined in detail. There was no preferential treatment given to Dr Patrick Tan, or any other individual who was disrupted because of their connections. All NSFs were disrupted to obtain their medical degrees based on stated criteria applicable to all who qualified.

The way the competition is coming is threatening entire industries and jobs. We call it hyper-competition. With the Internet, it pervades every nook and cranny and it is reaching out to the world of what are called "PhDs" – poor, hungry and driven people – everywhere. It cannot expand in the same adolescent way as it used to, 7%-8% a year, effortlessly year after year. We have domestic constraints of population and space. Population growing, Singapore getting crowded, space being allocated. We have to scramble to find space for new workers' dormitories, new factory land, new space for highways, for parks. Everything which you put in, something else has to be displaced. We are slowing down the inflow of foreign workers and immigrants, but that means squeezing out more businesses and reducing more growth. That is a very high opportunity cost. There is a trade-off. Already, many employers are feeling it, especially small and medium enterprises (SMEs), and especially those which are run by Singaporeans because they tend to be the less generous pay masters and the first ones to run into difficulties, not able to get workers.

If you cannot find staff from the Philippines or from Indonesia or wherever the nurses come from, you will not find enough Singaporeans to do this. Not just individual pieces work, but the whole system works well. Our unions work with our employers and the Government, we have tripartism. Our business environment – everything clicks. You can register your company for $2 and, in 20 minutes, it is done. You want telecommunications, you can have it. If you want an SIA flight, you can have it. If you want utilities, it is there. It is a tight, high-functioning system where the whole is more than the sum of its parts. And this is not just bragging rights. If you cross the Causeway, you work somewhere, or just go overseas, work somewhere, one of our neighbouring countries, any of our neighbouring countries, you would not get the same wage. So, we have to keep that and, through that, I think our Singaporeans can have a premium. And if they can strengthen that advantage, that premium can go up.

Even though yesterday somebody said past record is not a promise of future performance, in Singapore, they say, "you have this past record, I think we will bet on them. Weekly Rest Day for Foreign Domestic Workers

1 Mr Christopher de Souza asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower whether the Ministry will consider the proposal to provide foreign domestic workers working in Singapore one day off per week.

Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam : The Ministry of Manpower (MOM) is currently studying a proposal to legislate weekly "off-days" or "rest days" for foreign domestic workers (FDWs), with compensation in-lieu should the FDW and employer mutually agree that the rest day be foregone.

MOM is committed to safeguarding the well-being of FDWs in Singapore. FDWs are currently protected under a comprehensive piece of employment legislation called the Employment of Foreign Manpower Act, which prescribes that FDWs must receive adequate rest. Employers who fail to comply with this requirement can be fined up to $5,000 and/or jailed up to six months. They will also be barred from hiring another FDW.

In 2006, a standard contract was introduced by the employment agency accreditation bodies – Association of Employment Agents (Singapore) and CaseTrust – requiring employers to stipulate the pay and number of rest days per month for FDWs. This contract continues to be the market standard for employment contracts between employers and FDWs, and requires at least one day per month to be stipulated. Should the FDWs consent to work during their rest days, employers are required to pay their FDWs a mutually agreed amount of compensation.

The idea of raising the minimum standard by mandating a weekly rest day for FDWs attracts diverse views and requires careful consideration. FDWs, like other workers, need enough time to rest and relax. Recent interviews with over 900 FDWs indicated that a large majority felt they had adequate rest each day. However, only a minority had a weekly rest day.

On the other hand, some employers cite family circumstances that make it genuinely difficult to cope without an FDW. A large number of employers have also expressed concern about FDWs' activities on their day off.

We are in the process of consulting all stakeholders, including employers, domestic workers, employment agencies and non-governmental organisations on this issue. This includes prescribing the amount of compensation that should be given in-lieu of the rest day and allowing FDWs and employers the flexibility to mutually agree which day of the week the rest day should fall on.

MOM will also continue to work closely with stakeholders as we make other enhancements to our FDW management framework, to better meet the needs of FDWs, employers, agents as well as wider national interests.

Plans to Regulate Maid Recruitment Agencies

2 Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef asked the Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Manpower whether there are any plans to regulate maid recruitment agencies.

Page: 421

Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam : All employment agencies (EAs), including those that place foreign domestic workers (FDWs), are currently regulated and licensed under the Employment Agencies Act (EAA). Following the conclusion of a review of the EAA early this year, an enhanced EA regulatory framework has been put in place since 1 April 2011.

EAs that place FDWs require a Comprehensive Licence under the EA licensing framework and are subject to more stringent requirements. For example, EAs that have consistently high placement volume of FDWs have to furnish a security deposit amount of at least $40,000 and up to $60,000. In addition, all EA personnel need to attain a Certificate of Employment Intermediaries (CEI) in order to conduct EA work. EA personnel that place FDWs have to attain the version of CEI which covers the relevant legislation and regulations, in particular the Employment of Foreign Manpower Act that spells out the rights and obligations of FDWs and FDW employers.

Under the EA Licence Conditions, there is also a specific section dedicated to the obligations of EAs in the placement of FDWs. It requires, inter alia, the provision of acceptable food and accommodation prior to the issuance of Work Permits, as FDWs need to pass all the entry requirements before they can be deployed. EAs are also obliged to furnish prospective FDW employers with the employment history of FDWs. This ensures employers are able to make an informed decision when choosing which FDW to hire.

To help employers to make more informed decisions when selecting an EA, the EA Directory hosted on MOM's website has been revamped since 1 January 2011 to include useful indicators for FDW employers. For example, the directory displays each EA's success rate in placing FDWs who stay with their employers for a year or more as well as the FDW Entry Test passing rate of the FDWs brought in.

Beyond these recent improvements to the EA regulatory framework, MOM is also working with the industry to develop a new voluntary trustmark scheme for EAs which aspire to higher levels of service excellence.

MOM will continue to monitor the EA industry and, if necessary, make further refinements to the EA regulatory framework, taking into account the diverse operating models in the EA industry.

Water Pressure Levels in Areas of High Development

4 Assoc Prof Fatimah Lateef asked the Minister for the Environment and Water Resources with the increase in residential and commercial developments (a) how does the PUB address issues of lower water pressure in areas of these developments, especially during peak-utilisation hours; (b) what are the options available to residents to improve their water supply pressure; and (c) whether the PUB has a regular review mechanism to monitor this.

Page: 423

Dr Vivian Balakrishnan : PUB works closely with agencies such as URA and HDB in implementing water supply infrastructure ahead of new developments so that the distribution network is able to support the growing water demand.

There is a Code of Practice for Water Services that stipulates the requirements for the plumbing system so that the water pressure at customers' premises is adequate even during peak utilisation hours. Developments located on high ground are required to supply water through water tanks located on top of the buildings.

However, there are a small number of older private residences built prior to the introduction of the Code of Practice which are located on high ground and do not have a water tank. Over time, as new developments gradually increase the overall water demand in the area, these older premises could experience intermittent lower water pressure during peak usage hours. PUB monitors these areas closely and when such a situation occurs, PUB has boosted the water pressure to the area. For example, in the Tai Keng residential area, PUB has increased the water pressure to ensure residents continue to have adequate water pressure throughout the day.
