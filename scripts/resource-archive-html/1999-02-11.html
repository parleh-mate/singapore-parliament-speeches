TELECOMMUNICATION AUTHORITY OF SINGAPORE

(Free market competition)



    1. Mr Chng Hee Kok asked the Minister for Communications why the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore is against free market competition.

Mr Ong Kian Min asked the Minister for Communications on what grounds the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore (TAS) will disapprove a tariff reduction package or permanent promotion proposed by a mobile telephone operator and whether the TAS has in the past rejected any proposal for price cuts submitted by either SingTel Mobile or M1.

    3. Mr S Iswaran asked the Minister for Communications what is the rationale behind requiring telecommunications service providers to seek approval from the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore for changes to their charges and whether there are plans to discontinue with this practice when the market is further liberalised from 1st April 2000.

    4. Mr Chuang Shaw Peng asked the Minister for Communications why the Telecommunication Authority of Singapore considers the latest price cutting by SingTel Mobile anti-competitive and not in line with the free market policy practised by the Government.

    Mr Chng Hee Kok (East Coast): Mr Speaker, Sir, before I ask Question No. 1, let me declare my pecuniary interest as

I am a director of Singapore Power Ltd. Singapore Power is one of the partners of StarHub. Question No. 1, Sir.

    The Minister for Communications (Mr Mah Bow Tan): Mr Speaker, Sir, may I have your permission to take Question Nos. 1 to 4 together as they are related?

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    Mr Mah Bow Tan: Sir, since its formation in 1992, TAS has actively and consistently adopted a pro-competition approach. It is committed to fully liberalising the telecommunication industry to ensure Singapore's competitiveness and to allow consumers to enjoy the benefits of competition, ie, a wider range of quality services and products at competitive prices. In line with this philosophy, TAS has liberalised the basic telecommunication services market earlier than planned, by bringing forward the expiry of the monopoly licence of SingTel by 7 years from 2007 to the year 2000 at a cost of $1.5 billion. It opened the mobile phone and paging market in 1997. It has further liberalised the mobile services market from April 2000. The Internet services market has been completely opened since last year. The basic services market will also be opened up to further competition from April 2002.

    In liberalising the market, TAS has a duty to ensure that the competition is sustainable or healthy. It has to ensure that incumbents do not adopt anti-competitive measures, such as predatory pricing, to keep out competitors. This is to safeguard the interests of consumers in the longer term by ensuring that we do not regress to the former monopolistic situation. It is especially important in an industry like telecommunications where significant natural barriers to entry already exist.

    Recent developments in the mobile phone market serve to illustrate this point. There are only two players in the current mobile communication market. Until such time when there is more and freer competition in the market, TAS needs to approve operators' proposals to ensure that (1) they do not engage in predatory pricing,

Column: 2011

ie, operators which enjoy certain monopolistic privileges are not using these to unfairly drive out other market players and (2) they do not lock consumers into schemes that prevent them from switching operators freely.

    TAS has not rejected SingTel Mobile's proposal in this particular case. What it has said is that the proposed scheme was unclear in some respects, and that SingTel Mobile has to revert with more details to address TAS' concerns. These include whether its loyalty programme is sustainable, and whether tie-in conditions would be imposed on subscribers to prevent them from switching operators when the market is further liberalised in year 2000.

    If SingTel Mobile can satisfactorily address TAS' concerns, TAS will allow such a scheme to be marketed to the consumers as permanent. But until it is approved as a permanent scheme, TAS has to ensure that SingTel Mobile does not mislead its consumers by marketing it as a permanent scheme, when it is currently approved as a promotion only valid till March 2000. Let me say that TAS has since received clarification and financial information from SingTel Mobile on its loyalty programme. TAS is satisfied with the clarification and the business case and will approve the loyalty programme as a permanent scheme. TAS will be issuing a formal announcement later today.

    TAS encourages any proposal that will benefit consumers in the long run. However, in keeping with its role to protect consumer interests, there have been past instances where TAS had disapproved schemes or proposed modifications to schemes to prevent customers from being tied down or having to suffer penalties for switching operators. In some cases, competitive services had been bundled with monopoly services, to the disadvantage of competitors without monopoly rights.

    TAS will take a progressively relaxed regulatory stance as the telecommunication industry is further liberalised from 1st April next year. However, it will

continue to impose minimum service standards, as well as minimum safeguards to protect the consumers and maintain an oversight role over market developments to ensure a sustainable competitive environment.

    Mr Mah Bow Tan: Sir, it is not possible for TAS to set market caps for any player in the market. This is for the individual players to work out, ie, for them to go and gain as much market share as possible within the rules of the game as set out, in other words, there is no anti-competitive pricing especially when the incumbent operator has got certain monopolistic privileges.

    The second question which the Member asked was whether the Government would bail out an operator. The answer obviously is no.

COST OF HAJ PILGRIMAGE BY APPROVED TRAVEL AGENTS


    The following Question stood in the name of Mr Hawazi Daipi -

    6. To ask the Minister for Community Development and Minister-in-charge of Muslim Affairs (a) what is the average cost of going on the haj pilgrimage for the coming haj season; (b) whether the cost is too high, considering that airlines have lowered their airfares; and (c) how the Islamic Religious Council (MUIS) ensures that approved travel agents which provide haj packages do not profiteer, especially at a time when workers have been called to make sacrifices by accepting wage cuts.

    Mr Ahmad Mohd Magad (Pasir Ris): Question No. 6, Sir.

    Mr Abdullah Tarmugi: The cost for the haj pilgrimage for the coming season, excluding airfare, ranges from about $4,400 to $6,650. These are based on quotes sent in by 28 travel agents or GSAs. The lower range of $4,400 is actually lower than the lower range last year. If you include the airfare, the packages will range between $6,710 and $8,960.

    The airfare for a return trip from Singapore to Jeddah by Saudi Airlines or Singapore Airlines has not changed for the last two years and most Singapore pilgrims travel by these two airlines.

    MUIS's role in haj matters is regulatory and supervisory. It ensures that there are sufficient approved travel agents to provide competition, and that the services offered by these travel agents are delivered as promised. It also sends a medical team to look after the pilgrims while they are in Saudi Arabia. However, it

does not determine the prices offered by the travel agents. Travel agents have to compete among themselves to offer their best prices and services to travellers. Several years ago, MUIS requested travel agents to offer budget economy packages to cater to cost-conscious pilgrims, but they were not popular. This year, in response to MUIS's request for travel agents to offer economy packages, one travel agent is offering an economy package which affected about 100 pilgrims out of 3,000.

    Mr Ahmad Mohd Magad (Pasir Ris): Sir, I would like to ask the Minister whether GSAs have not been granted approval to date, as a result of having breached MUIS's guidelines or standards and, if so, how many over the past few years.

    Mr Abdullah Tarmugi: Sir, over the past three years, four established GSAs were not granted approval to manage haj pilgrims for one or more of the following reasons:

    (a) financial mismanagement;

    (b) deviation from the haj tender package;

    (c) poor performance; or

Many of them handle and discharge chemicals which, if not controlled properly, can pose serious threats to our environment as well as to the health and safety of our people.

    While the total pollutants emitted into the air today are within safe limits, the amount of pollutants will increase with the continued expansion and growth of our industrial sector in the years ahead. We therefore cannot afford to be complacent, and must ensure that we have adequate and appropriate regulatory powers to deal with any new environmental development.

    Over the years, my Ministry has tackled pollution problems through regulations made under various Acts. For example, we introduced the Poisons (Hazardous

Substances) Rules, 1986 under the Poisons Act and the Environmental Public Health (Control of Noise at Construction Sites) Regulations, 1990 under the Environmental Public Health Act. Such controls will now be consolidated into the EPC Bill. This Bill will also give my Ministry additional powers to control or prohibit the emission of any particular air pollutant from any industrial or trade premises.

Engagement of registered inspectors to check completed developments

    Sir, currently, ENV officers carry out inspections of completed developments to ensure compliance with environmental requirements. To encourage self-regulation, the Bill will provide for the engagement of registered inspectors who are professional architects or engineers to undertake such checks.

Putting existing practices into law

    At present, before major projects such as oil refineries, petrochemical plants and land reclamation are implemented, my Ministry will ask the developer to submit details of pollution control measures and, if necessary, risk assessment studies. We also require installations using hazardous substances to carry out regular safety audits on the safe handling and management of such substances. Industrial premises discharging air pollutants into the atmosphere or trade effluent into the water-course or sewer are required to monitor such discharges, treating them first, where necessary. All these administrative requirements will now be provided for in the EPC Bill and thus, be legally enforceable.

    The Bill will enable the Ministry to control air pollution from sources other than industrial and commercial premises, such as smoky vehicles. The particulates emitted by diesel-driven vehicles are very fine and a large proportion of them are less than 2.5 micron in size (and these are commonly referred to as PM2.5). Our studies indicate that diesel particulates make up more than 50% of the PM2.5 in

our air. Studies in the US and other countries have linked PM2.5 to an increase in respiratory diseases and increased mortality. The studies indicate that it is prudent to keep PM2.5 levels as low as possible in order to protect the health of the population. Poorly-maintained diesel-driven vehicles give off excessive amount of particulates. My Ministry has worked closely with the Land Transport Authority (LTA) and the Traffic Police to take enforcement action against smoky vehicles. Although the proportion of smoky diesel-driven vehicles has gradually reduced over the years, the number has nonetheless remained high. My Ministry will continue to step up enforcement action against smoky vehicles in Singapore. This Bill will result in my Ministry taking over from the LTA the responsibility for enforcement against smoky vehicles.

    The Bill will extend my Ministry's controls over noise from construction sites and other sources. It will empower my Ministry to order construction sites and workplaces that generate excessive noise affecting residential neighbourhoods to stop work if they fail to comply with a notice to take appropriate measures to control noise.

    My Ministry vets building plans for compliance with pollution control requirements and gives its clearance to the Building Control Division which then issues approval under the Building Control Act. The Bill will include provisions requiring the direct submission of building plans to ENV for pollution control purposes.

Air Pollution Control (Part IV)

    Clauses 10 to 14 of the Bill deal with the control of air pollution. Clause 10 requires the occupier of any industrial or trade premises to maintain and efficiently operate any fuel burning equipment and any air pollution control equipment installed in the premises. Clause 12 provides for the introduction of caps on total emissions of air impurities. Any emission in excess of such caps will be an offence. Clause 12 also allows the Minister to make regulations to control or prohibit emission of air impurities from any other source, eg, motor vehicles.

Water Pollution Control (Part V)

    Part V of the Bill deals with water pollution control and these are covered under clauses 15 to 19. Under clause 15, any person who intends to discharge trade effluent, oil, chemical, sewage or other polluting substances into any drain or land has to obtain a licence. This clause has been strengthened to incorporate the presumption that if there is evidence of any discharge of trade effluent, oil, chemical, sewage or other polluting matters from a premises into a drain or land without a licence, the occupier of the premises shall be guilty of the offence unless he can prove otherwise. Clause 16 requires any occupier to treat such trade effluent before it is discharged.

    Clause 17 prohibits the discharge of any toxic or hazardous substance into any inland water. Any person who contravenes this provision is liable on the first conviction to a fine not exceeding $50,000 or imprisonment for a term not exceeding 12 months or to both, and on a second or subsequent conviction, to a fine not exceeding $100,000 and imprisonment for a term of not less than 1 month and not more than 12 months. The heavy penalties, including mandatory jail sentence for the second and subsequent

convictions, are necessary as any discharge of toxic or hazardous substance into our inland water will not only contaminate our limited water resource, but also endanger the health and lives of our people.

    Clause 18 empowers ENV to require any person who had discharged any toxic or hazardous substance or other polluting matter onto any land or into any drain or sea to remove and clean up such substance or matter within a specified time. Clause 19 empowers ENV to require any person to take measures to prevent water pollution due to the storage or transportation of toxic substances or other polluting matters. Currently, these requirements are enforced administratively.

Land Pollution Control (Part VI)

    Sir, clause 20 empowers the Minister to make regulations to control the pollution of land. These would include regulations to require remedial measures to be taken on contaminated sites.

Control of land contamination

    In land scarce Singapore, land use is reviewed regularly to ensure optimal use. Changes in land-use zoning may result in industrial sites being re-zoned for residential, recreational or other developments. Such land-use conversions have not posed any problems so far. In some developed countries, there have been occasions when industrial land had been converted to residential use, and subsequently found to have been contaminated only after the adverse effects on the health of the residents showed up many years later. By that time, it was a bit too late.

    Currently, owners of industrial land, such as JTC and the Land Office, require their tenants to carry out soil studies and clean up contaminated land before the lands are returned to them. This is done administratively. My Ministry would advise JTC and the Land Office the procedure and standard for soil studies and clean up. This Bill will empower the Minister to make regulations to require that land found to be contaminated will have to be made good before change of use is allowed.

Hazardous Substances Control (Part VII)

Licences and Industrial Plant Works (Part IX)

    Clauses 31 to 34 contain provisions on licences and industrial plant works. Currently, a person requires separate licences, permits and approvals to carry out various activities, such as discharging trade effluent, occupying and using scheduled premises, importing and selling hazardous substances, etc. Under clause 31, he may apply for a single licence to carry out these activities. This again is to instill discipline and self-regulation in the industries.

    Clause 38 empowers the Minister to make regulations to require any industrial or trade premises or person who deals with hazardous substances to take out and maintain insurance against environmental risks.

    Clauses 41 to 50 contain provisions which confer on ENV powers which are necessary for the enforcement of the Bill. Clauses 43 to 45 give authorised officers powers of investigation and arrest. Compensation, Damages and Costs (Part XII)

    Clauses 51 to 64 relate to the recovery of compensation, damages and costs payable under the Bill. Miscellaneous Provisions (Part XIII)

SINGAPORE STUDIES


    7. Mr Shriniwas Rai asked the Minister for Education if he will consider introducing "Singapore Studies" as a subject in the Universities.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    Various aspects of Singapore studies are already incorporated as elective or compulsory modules in the academic curriculum of our universities. In addition, Singapore history will be offered as a module in NUS's Core Curriculum, which is due to be implemented in July this year.

Compulsory modules

    At NUS and NTU, individual Faculties and Schools require their respective students to take compulsory modules which cover a range of topics on Singapore studies. For example, the recent history of Singapore is an area of study covered in the "Foundation Skills Course", a Faculty Enrichment Module taken by all first year students in the NUS Faculty of Arts and Social Sciences. In NTU, the Schools of Engineering and Applied Science require their students to take a module on "Engineers in Society" that covers issues pertaining to the history, politics, social structure and challenges of modern Singapore.

Elective modules

    Issues related to Singapore are covered in a wide range of elective modules that span diverse subject areas such as Sociology, Geography and History. At NUS and NTU, some of these elective modules are also offered to students of other Faculties and Schools within the respective universities.

INTERNET FOR EDUCATION


    8. Dr Lee Tsao Yuan asked the Minister for Education what is the current status regarding the use of the Internet for the teaching and transmission of educational material by educational institutions.

    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    Under the Masterplan for IT in education, the Ministry will install whole-school networking for all schools by next year. This will provide schools with good access to the Internet. At present, about 60 schools already have school-wide networks. The majority of the remaining schools, however, still have limited access points (about 10 points per school and at least 2 points for SingaporeONE).

    Teachers and students use the Internet mainly to source for information, especially for project work. The Ministry helps schools by evaluating websites. Websites, which are relevant to the curriculum, are listed in the Ministry's Intranet, together with suggestions on how these websites could be used in the classroom.

    While the Internet is an important resource, pupils are taught to be discerning on the factual correctness of the information downloaded from the web. In addition, they are also taught to synthesise the data instead of doing a cut-and-paste job when writing their project report.

    The Internet provides new possibilities for technology-facilitated collaborative work. Many of our schools have participated in collaborative projects with pupils from other countries. They discuss issues and questions with one another through e-mail. Many of these projects culminate with the pupils creating and developing their own theme-based websites.

    The Internet has also been used to disseminate information to schools. Some schools have set up their websites on the Internet and include educational resources for pupils to use.

CHINESE LANGUAGE AND UNIVERSITY ADMISSION


    9. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Education whether his Ministry has collated any statistics on the number of students who scored excellent grades in other subjects but were unable to enter local universities simply on account of their failure in the Chinese language subject at their O-level and A-level examinations.

Column: 2195



    RAdm Teo Chee Hean:

    Admission into NUS/NTU is based primarily on A-Level points. The maximum is 76 points, 20 points each for 3 A level subjects and 8 points each for General Paper and Mother Tongue (MT) taken at the AO level. Applicants compete on their total points. A student who scores excellent grades in other subjects but fail their Mother Tongue may have higher points than an average student who passes Mother Tongue. However, a student who does not meet the Mother Tongue requirement (D7 or better) will be offered provisional admission. During his 1st year undergraduate vacation, he must undergo an intensive 1-month mother tongue course and pass the course.

    There are thus no students with excellent grades who could not get into the university because of MT. Those who failed MT and were not accepted into University because their results for the other subjects were poor as well.

    For academic year 98/99, a total of 21 students were provisionally admitted into NUS/NTU on the grounds that they had failed to obtain at least a D7 in their Chinese Language. This is 0.3% of the 7000 (approx.) JC/CI students who were admitted into NUS/NTU. The percentage of students who are provisionally admitted because of poor performance in MT is very small.
