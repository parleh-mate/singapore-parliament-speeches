GOVERNMENT ASSISTANCE FOR FISHERMEN AND FARMERS


    2. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Minister for National Development what does the Government propose to do to assist fishermen and farmers in Singapore and its surrounding islands to increase their income.

    Mr Ong Eng Guan: Mr Speaker, as in most countries, the life of the fisherman is a most difficult and hazardous one. In the case of Singapore, the situation is further aggravated by the fact that Singapore's coastlines do not contain much fish, and also during the past years, due to over-intensive fishing, the

Column: 417

catch has become smaller and smaller year by year. To improve the catch of our fishermen, it is necessary for them to take up deep-sea fishing and seek new fishing grounds, for example, in the South China Sea or the Indian Ocean. This is not an easy task, because deep- sea fishing requires both capital and technical skill.

    There are other problems facing the fishermen; for instance, the exploitation by the middlemen and unscrupulous moneylenders, the lack of capital to buy the necessary gear and equipment, the poor living conditions of the fishing villagers, the lack of jetties where fishermen can land their catch.

    These are some of the many problems which are receiving Government's consideration. At present, we can only do whatever we can to assist these fishermen. For instance, we have tried to improve the living conditions of the fishermen in the Southern Islands. We are proposing to construct a jetty at the 18th Mile, Jurong Road, for fishermen to land their catch. The situation cannot improve appreciably until our fishermen can also go to the Federation and Indonesian waters to catch their fish.

    Mr Speaker, neither is the solution of the farmer's problems in an island like Singapore an easy task. The main problems of the farmers are, briefly, the shortage of land, the lack of cheaper fertilisers and seeds, the need for a better marketing system which will reduce the exploitation by the middlemen, better credit facilities and the lack of real co-operatives.

    These problems cannot be overcome at present due to various difficulties. We are attempting to do whatever we can to help the farmers. For instance, we are carrying out experiments to obtain better strains of pigs and chickens, and experiments in pig-stuff and fertilisers. We are also providing better veterinary services for pigs and chickens, offering a ploughing service at cheaper cost and converting more prawn ponds from Singapore's swamp land.

    Apart from the charges made by the Singapore Harbour Board, the only charge levied on shipping for the varied services provided in our port has taken the form of Light Dues at very nominal rates. These Light Dues yield approximately $480,000 per annum. This sum of money is paid into a special fund called the Light Dues Fund and this Fund is used exclusively for the maintenance and improvement of harbour lights and navigational aids. The present cost of maintaining the port, of dredging and survey, outside the Singapore Harbour Board, is approximately $1,500,000 per year. This, sum makes no provision for development or improvement expenditure to the harbour. All these years the annually recurrent costs and cost of development projects have been met from General Revenue.

    The Government is well aware of the need to nurture the port, to improve its facilities and maintain efficient administration so that the services provided are highly competitive, but with the present need to provide for the adequate overall development of a rapidly growing community, Government finds it necessary to provide that the port user also contributes towards the annually recurrent and development expenditure of the port. The dues to be collected under this Bill will be paid into general revenue until such time as a Port Authority is set up when the question of paying the dues to a fund administered by the Authority will be considered.

    The principle on the levy of Port Dues incorporated in this Bill is that ships and harbour craft which derive earnings from calling or operating at Singapore or who occupy a berth within the port for a period in excess of 30 days, shall be the only ones which are called upon to make a contribution towards maintenance and development of the port. Ships which call at Singapore solely for the purpose of taking on board fuel, stores and water will not be required to pay Port Dues. These ships which derive no earnings from their call in Singapore do, however, already indirectly contribute to the economy of the State to an important extent.

    Mr Lee Kuan Yew: The total number of failures between June 1959 and 25th March, 1960 is 66, of which 37 were due to technical fault, 20 due to underground cable damage and 9 due to fault on overhead lines.

    Malay Schools
(Religious Instruction)


    3. Inche Mohd. Ali Bin Alwi asked the Minister for Education how many periods per week are given to religious instruction in Malay Schools, and whether the syllabus is in line with that in the Federation of Malaya.

    Mr Yong Nyuk Lin: One period per week of 30 minutes is given to the teaching of the Islamic Religion in Malay Schools. This conforms with the curriculum structure common to all Primary Schools. The religious instruction syllabus has been carefully drawn up by a small committee of religious teachers in the Malay Schools who are advised by Inche Ustaz Ahmad Sunhaji bin Muhammad, teacher in the Al-Junied Religious School. The syllabus is designed to meet the needs of Singapore pupils and the pattern of the Federation syllabus is not followed in every respect.
