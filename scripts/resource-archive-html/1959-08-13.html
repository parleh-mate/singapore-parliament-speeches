    The Minister for Labour and Law (Mr K. M. Byrne): Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    The Factories Ordinance provides for the regulation and supervision of health, safety and welfare of persons employed in factories and other workplaces. An amendment to section 6, subsection (4) of this Ordinance is now considered necessary to provide that small factories employing less than ten persons will only be exempted from the provisions of the Ordinance if no mechanical power, steam boiler, steam container, steam receiver, cast iron underfired vulcaniser, air receiver, refrigerating plant pressure receiver, or gas plant is used, or where no highly inflammable or noxious substance is manipulated, used or created.

    Mr Speaker, Sir, as the Ordinance stands, no legal action can be taken against a small factory employing less than ten persons even though it uses the kind of machinery mentioned above. It has been found that there are a large number of factories having less than ten persons using out-of-date machinery which did not originally have safety guards incorporated, or where they have had safety guards, these could easily be removed, Sir.

    The Government is merely seeking power to take preventive action against serious accidents to life and limb in small factories employing less than ten persons where dangerous machinery of the type mentioned above is used, and which are now excluded from the Factories Ordinance, Sir.

    In a recent case, a locally assembled grinding machine burst its grinding wheel, a piece of which hit the operator in the face, killing him instantly.

    The present amendment to the Factories Ordinance is designed to enable proper control to be exercised in respect of such activities where dangerous machinery is used in small factories employing less than ten persons.

    As the explanatory statement to this Bill shows, the purpose of this Bill is three-fold. Clause 2 of the Bill merely seeks to regularise what is the present procedure of the Board of Film Censors in the matter of censoring duplicate films. The Board has, since the war, been regarding 16 m.m. and 8 m.m. copies of censored 35 m.m. films and 8 m.m. copies of 16 m.m. censored films as duplicates in this respect and has authorised screening of these duplicates without viewing by the Board. This practice, however, is not strictly in accord with section 16 of the Ordinance which requires a duplicate to be identical in detail, subject matter and footage. A 16 m.m. film is, however, only one-third the length of an otherwise identical 35 m.m. film, and an 8 m.m. film is proportionately shorter.

    Clause 2, therefore, repeals and re-enacts section 16 of the Cinematograph Films Ordinance to enable copies of different sizes to be regarded as duplicates for purposes of censorship. Administrative arrangements preclude the chances of evading censorship of duplicate films. Mr Speaker, you will note also that a proviso is added to this clause which prohibits a larger size

Column: 411

copy to be regarded as a duplicate of a smaller size censored film. The reason is obvious and is meant to prevent the evasion of proper censorship fees which are based on footage.

The Committee of Appeal of 17 members, as constituted by the existing section 21 of the Ordinance, hardly exists at the moment and this is the main reason for the urgency of this Bill. There have been a number of vacancies since 7th January this year.
