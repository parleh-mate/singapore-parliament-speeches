PERSONAL CREDIT LINES

(Review of eligibility criteria)

     1.  Mr Seah Kian Peng asked the Senior Minister in light of the significant increase and accumulation of debts incurred by individuals in the last 12 months, whether MAS will review the eligibility criteria of personal credit lines being extended to these individuals.

     The Minister for Finance (Mr Tharman Shanmugaratnam) (for the Senior Minister):   Mr Speaker, Sir, total consumer loans grew by 10% year-on-year in

September 2008.  Two-thirds of the increase over this period were due to an increase in housing loans, which is not surprising given the buoyant property market in 2006 and 2007.  Housing loans are, of course, secured on the property that is being financed and are subject to the Monetary Authority of Singapore's (MAS) prudential requirements on their maximum loan-to-value ratios.  In addition, banks will conduct rigorous credit assessments and consider the risk profile of the borrower, his debt servicing to income ratio and the value of the collateral before granting a housing loan.  Car loans, which are also secured credit, have only increased marginally over the same period while share financing, which is the third major component of personal credit lines typically, has fallen.  So the bulk of the increase being 10% year-on-year in September was due to housing loans.

 

     As for unsecured credit to individuals, total outstanding balances on credit and charge cards have grown over the past 12 months, consistent with the growth in previous years.  However, reflecting the use of credit and charge cards more as convenient payment instruments than anything else, the percentage of outstanding balances rolled over for more than one month, in other words, where it is really a form of credit, has remained fairly constant at around 15%.  Moreover, the charge-off rate, which measures credit card debt written off by banks and card issuers as a percentage of the rollover balance, remains low at 3% to 4%.

 

     MAS' current rules on the eligibility criteria for personal unsecured credit are not too loose, and are in fact stricter than in most other countries.  Firstly, applicants for credit cards and other unsecured credit facilities must meet the minimum annual income requirement of $30,000.  Secondly, the maximum credit that can be

     To ensure that its regulatory controls continue to be an effective deterrent, NEA has increased the maximum fine for construction noise violations from $20,000 to $40,000 with effect from October 2007.  NEA also takes action against errant contractors that repeatedly violate the permissible noise limits, by issuing work restriction orders to restrict the operation hours at their construction sites.

After a complaint is received, NEA will send officers down to the construction site to investigate the complaint, and will inform the complainant of NEA’s findings. Contractors found to have exceeded the noise limits will be fined, with work restriction orders imposed on recalcitrant offenders. Where the noise levels are found to be within the regulated limits, NEA would still advise the contractors to limit their activities during noise-sensitive periods. For example during school examination periods, NEA would advise contractors to liaise with the nearby schools and limit their construction work during the examination period, where possible.

FINES ON BUS OPERATORS
(Objectives and adequacy of quanta)

     16.   Mr Siew Kum Hong asked the Minister for Transport what is the objective of fines imposed by the Public Transport Council on bus operators for non-compliance with Quality of Service standards and whether the quanta are adequate.

     Mr Raymond Lim Siang Keat:

     Since October 2007, the Public Transport Council (PTC) has put in place a penalty framework to enforce its set of Quality of Service (QoS) standards for basic bus services. The penalty framework sends a strong and clear signal to the operators that they should consistently strive to do better than what the QoS standards stipulate.

 

     The penalties vary with the nature of non-compliance. Broadly, an operator could fail to comply either at the level of an individual bus service, for example, services that are overcrowded, or at the level of its entire bus operation, for example accident rate and bus breakdown rate. Penalties range from $100 per day for each non-compliant bus service in the former, to $10,000 per month for each non-compliant standard in the latter. Given that there are about 260 basic bus services, the financial penalties for non-compliance can add up to a significant sum if the operators are not vigilant in their operations.
 
     In the PTC’s recent assessment of the operators’ performance from December 2007 to May 2008, the bus operators largely met the QoS standards set but also had some instances of non-compliance. These include non-compliance with standards, such as scheduled trips operated, adherence to scheduled headways and passenger loading. As a result, financial penalties of $9,300 and $1,000 were imposed on SBS Transit and SMRT Buses respectively.

 

     But such instances of non-compliance do not just penalise the operators financially. As Mr Cedric Foo had noted in a report in the Straits Times, the operators "carry a reputation risk in not meeting the standards". He highlighted an important aspect of the penalty framework, which is the signalling and reputation effect, which goes beyond the direct cost of financial penalties. In this regard, I note that SBST had earlier said it was "sorry and disappointed" over its instance of non-compliance in the latest QoS assessment, and that it will step up efforts to improve.


     The bus operators are purchasing more buses, as well as accelerating the recruitment and training of bus drivers. Regular commuters on public transport would have noticed the newer buses already plying our roads.  Such efforts by the operators show that the penalty framework helps in inducing the operators to enhance their operations to comply with the QoS standards.

 

     Going forward, the PTC will review the penalty framework as and when necessary to ensure that it remains effective.
