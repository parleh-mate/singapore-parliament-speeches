SEPARATE HDB SHOP RENTALS FOR RESETTLEMENT CASES


    5. Mrs Yu-Foo Yee Shoon asked the Minister for National Development whether he will consider having a separate system of Housing and Development Board shop rentals for resettled farmers and shopowners who, despite the discounts granted by the Board, are still finding it difficult to pay their present rentals based on the tender system, in order to alleviate their problem.

     The Minister for National Development (Mr Teh Cheang Wan): Mr Speaker, Sir, at present, there is already a separate system of HDB shop rentals for resettled shopkeepers. While the rental for public applicants is based on tendered amounts, the resettlement rental is pegged at 90% of the rental paid by the lowest successful tenderer. Furthermore, under this system, resettled shopkeepers pay only 50% of the resettlement rental for the first year. Thereafter, the rental is increased by 10% each year until it reaches the full resettlement rental in the sixth year. Resettled shopkeepers thus enjoy rental concessions for a number of years. Resettled farmers are normally paid a cash grant of $26,000 as part of the resettlement compensation. If they opt for shop premises in lieu of the cash grant, they are also given similar rental concessions for a period of five years. Generally, these concessions work out to be more than the $26,000 cash grant.

    The concessions enjoyed by the resettled shopkeepers are best illustrated by an example. Assuming the market rentals for shops in a neighbourhood centre range from $2,000 per month to $3,000 per month, the rentals for shops reserved for resettlement cases in the same neighbourhood would be pegged at 90% of the lowest market rental of $2,000 per month. The resettlement rental is therefore $1,800 per month. In the first year, a resettled shopkeeper would only be paying a monthly rental based on 50% of the resettlement rental, ie $900 per month. This works out to be only 36% of the mid-point of the rental range for public applicants in the neighbourhood. Although the rental for the resettled shopkeepers would be gradually increased to the full resettlement

Therefore, a resettled shopkeeper with normal business acumen will be able to conduct his business successfully. However, not everyone is endowed with business acumen, be he a resettled shopkeeper or a public applicant of HDB shops. No amount of rental concessions can help the person without the necessary business acumen to succeed in his business.

IMPORT DUTY ON REFINED SUGAR
(Rationale)


    9. Mr Chng Hee Kok asked the Minister for Trade and Industry if he will explain the rationale for retaining the import duty on refined sugar after abolition of the excise duty.

     The Minister of State for Trade and Industry (BG Lee Hsien Loong) (for the Minister for Trade and Industry): Mr Speaker, Sir, I am answering on behalf of my Minister.

    Before the 1st September of this year the Sugar Industry of Singapore (SIS) was the sole supplier of refined sugar to the domestic market. SIS concluded favourable long term contracts with Australia and Fiji for the supply of raw sugar. In the last 10 years, world sugar prices fluctuated widely but the long term contracts guaranteed us regular supplies of sugar at stable and comparatively low prices.

    This situation has now changed. There is a world sugar glut, as governments in Europe and the United States are subsidizing their sugar growers.      Mr Lee Yock Suan: Mr Speaker, Sir, the basic objective of the Central Provident Fund (CPF) is to provide for the old-age financial security of our citizens and for contingencies. CPF savings also help to achieve our goal of a 100% home ownership society as well as to meet hospitalization expenses.

    Whilst maintaining the high savings rate through the CPF, members can be given the flexibility to manage their own CPF savings. Once they have saved a minimum reserve for old age and contingencies, they can be allowed the option to invest their CPF funds in excess of this minimum reserve in other types of investment.

    The Government has therefore approved the setting up of a scheme to allow CPF members to invest a portion of their Ordinary Account savings in shares, loan stocks, unit trusts and gold.

Savings which can be used for such investments

Column: 596



    A CPF member should have at least a minimum reserve of $30,000 in his CPF Ordinary and Special Accounts before the excess balance in his Ordinary Account will be allowed for such investments. The minimum reserve of $30,000 will include CPF amounts which have been invested in a home. It does not include the Medisave Account which is meant for hospitalization expenses.

    This reserve of $30,000 is estimated to be the amount necessary for an individual to meet his barest living expenses after retirement. The minimum reserve of $30,000 will be adjusted periodically for inflation.

    Above the reserve of $30,000, the excess balance which can be used for investment will be termed "Investable CPF Savings". For a start, members will be allowed to invest up to 10% of their investable CPF savings in the local stock market or in gold. There will be a sub-limit of 5% on the amount which can be invested in gold. They can only use amounts in their Ordinary Account for this purpose. The ultimate intention is to allow CPF members maximum discretion in the way they deploy their investable CPF savings, subject to minimum essential safeguards.

Type of shares and loan stocks

    Let me outline the salient features of the scheme for investment in shares, loan stocks and unit trusts. It is desirable that members be allowed to choose from as wide a variety of stocks as possible, exercising their own judgment and discretion. However, as many CPF members may be investing in the stock market for the first time, certain minimum standards should be set. Investable CPF savings will only be permitted for investment in shares which satisfy the minimum conditions laid down under the Trustees Act and are listed in the Stock Exchange of Singapore. I will refer to them as trustee shares. These conditions include a minimum paid-up capital of S$5 million and require the companies to have paid dividends for each of the last five years.

    Investment in the convertible loan stocks of companies whose shares are classified as trustee shares will also be allowed.

Column: 597



    Other companies with good track records but whose shares do not meet the conditions to be classified as trustee shares can apply to the Securities Industry Council (SIC) which will be asked to assess their applications for inclusion under the scheme. Those companies that are seeking listing for the first time can likewise apply to SIC for consideration.

    As the liability of CPF funds is wholly in Singapore, the scheme will be confined to stocks of Singapore-incorporated companies. This is similar to the present situation where CPF funds can only be invested in properties located in Singapore.

Unit Trusts

    Investable CPF savings can also be used for investment in unit trusts. Unit trusts will similarly have to apply to SIC to be considered for inclusion in the scheme. The CPF savings invested through unit trusts can only be invested in shares or convertible loan stocks which are included under the scheme.

Limits for investing in shares, loan stocks and unit trusts

    Of the 308 companies listed on the local stock exchange, 59 are trustee shares of Singapore-incorporated companies. Their total market capitalization is about $20 billion. Their turnover last year is estimated at about S$2.5 billion.

    To avoid artificially inflating the stock prices, the liberalization of CPF savings for such investments will be done gradually. For a start, members will be allowed to invest up to 10% of their investable CPF savings under the scheme. The intention is to gradually allow the limit to go up, subject to evaluation of the scheme.

    It is estimated that initially up to about S$1 billion, or S$1,000 million, will thus be made available for investment under this scheme. It can be used for the purchase of qualifying stocks from the local stock market as well as for re-financing of such stocks already purchased.

Trading in shares, loan stocks and unit trusts

    I now turn to some other details of the investment scheme. CPF members will be allowed to use their investable CPF savings up to the initial limit of 10% to pay for the full price of the Singapore trustee shares, related convertible loan stocks and unit trusts, as well as the brokerage, administrative and other charges incurred. If they already own such stocks, they can use their investable CPF savings up to the approved limit to re-finance these assets. Where the available CPF funds are insufficient, they can top up with cash.

    Members will be allowed to trade freely in the shares, convertible loan stocks and unit trusts under the scheme. However, as the scheme is to enable members to invest but not to speculate in the stock market, there will be administrative guidelines to prevent the use of CPF monies for speculation and trading on margins. These guidelines will be formulated so as not to hinder the flexibility required for non-speculative investments.

    As this is a scheme to enable CPF members to manage the investment of their old-age savings, all gains derived from CPF funds must be kept in the CPF accounts. Dividends received (net of corporate tax) must be returned to the member's account. Upon the sale of the shares, loan stocks and unit trusts purchased under the scheme, the amount realized from the use of CPF funds, including any capital gain, must also be returned to the CPF accounts. However, members will not be required to make good trading losses or to reimburse the CPF interest forgone. Any cash top-up and related earnings will be returned to the member.

With CPF members investing under the scheme, it is expected that the number of small investors will increase. They will benefit from sound advice on investment. Banks and other financial institutions will be encouraged to develop the capacity to provide such services for a fee.

SINGAPORE DOLLAR


    24. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Minister for Finance whether he is of the view that the strength of the Singapore dollar may be a contributing cause for the present downturn in Singapore's economy and if it is, does the Government propose to do anything about it.

     Dr Hu Tsu Tau: The strength of the Singapore dollar is not responsible for the present downturn in the Singapore economy.

    In general, in the absence of foreign exchange controls, the exchange rate of a country's currency is dependent on the country's balance of payments, its foreign exchange reserves, and the direction and magnitude of capital and monetary flows.

    Over the past decade, the Singapore dollar has appreciated against most major international currencies as a natural outcome of the strength of our economy, strong capital and monetary inflows, a tight fiscal policy which led to steady fiscal surpluses, and large and increasing foreign reserves. The value of the Singapore dollar is determined by these fundamental economic parameters. The Monetary Authority of Singapore intervenes only when foreign exchange speculators distort the value beyond its normal range against a basket of currencies of our main trading partners.

VOCATIONAL REHABILITATION AND EMPLOYMENT OF DISABLED PERSONS


    27. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Acting Minister for Labour if he will outline in detail against each recommendation why the Government has not felt able to ratify and adopt any of the recommendations contained in the: (i) Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (Disabled Persons) Convention 1983 (No. 159); and (ii) Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (Disabled Persons) Recommendation, 1983 (No. 168), adopted at the International Labour Conference in June 1983.

     Mr Lee Yock Suan: As stated in Miscellaneous Paper No. 5 of 1985 presented to Parliament on 1 August 1985, the Government has decided not to ratify or adopt the Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (Disabled Persons) Convention, 1983 (No. 159) and the Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (Disabled Persons) Recommendation, 1983 (No. 168) for the following reasons:

    (i) Employment of the disabled is best left to market forces and enlightened employers rather than enforced through legislation.

    (ii) With the tight labour market, disabled persons have been able to secure suitable employment after appropriate training.

    Each country is run differently depending on its people, resources and state of development. It is not appropriate to adopt in total conventions drafted to meet the standards of more advanced Western countries.

    Singapore has its own programmes for the vocational rehabilitation and employment of disabled persons. Thus the Disablement Resettlement Scheme of the Ministry of Labour assists persons disabled by industrial accidents to undergo vocational rehabilitation for the purpose of gainful employment. Under this Scheme, disabled persons are helped in securing on-the-job training in light industrial establishments as well as in sheltered workshops run by voluntary organisations.

    The Ministry of Community Development administers an Incentive Scheme funded by the Singapore Chinese Chamber of Commerce and Industry to help disabled people to gain employment. Under the Scheme, the employer is reimbursed for the first 1 month's salary paid by him to the disabled employee. The Scheme also provides a transport and meal allowance of $5/- for the disabled employee during the first two weeks of his employment or until he receives his first salary payment.

    The Handicaps Welfare Association has set up a Vocational Assessment and Placement Centre and a centralised job placement service with the support of the Ministry of Community Development and the Singapore Council of Social Services.

JURONG SHIPYARD
(Early Retirement of Selected Employees)


    2. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Acting Minister for Labour how his Ministry views the practice by some companies to force early retirement on selected employees and if he will make a statement in particular on the exercise carried out by Jurong Shipyard noting especially the fact that all the selected employees were Singapore citizens and the Malaysian employees were left untouched.

     Mr Lee Yock Suan: The view of my Ministry is that no company should force its employees to accept early retirement. However, employees may agree to accept early retirement.

    In the case of Jurong Shipyard, workers were offered benefits as specified in the Collective Agreement if they agreed to retire early to help the company cope with the downturn in the shipbuilding and repairing industry. A total of 275 workers took up the offer and collected the benefits provided under the scheme.

    Shortly after accepting the offer, some of the workers approached my Ministry for assistance alleging that they were forced to accept early retirement. They also wanted to claim extra benefits. My Ministry took up the matter with the Management and was informed that the union and the workers had agreed to the early retirement scheme.

    As the Management had given the assurance that the scheme was voluntary, my Ministry and the NTUC requested them to accept any worker who wished to return to the Shipyard without any break in service. The Management agreed to do so provided that the worker refunded the retirement benefit and other payments which he had collected under the scheme. 18 workers have since refunded the benefits they received and returned to work. Some other workers insisted on being allowed to return to work without having to pay back the retirement benefit and other payments they had received. This the Management could not accept.

    It is not true that only Singaporean workers were involved in this scheme. The company had 139 Malaysian workers of whom 10 accepted early retirement. The company has over the past 3 years almost halved its foreign workforce through resignations and non-renewal of work permits when they expired.
