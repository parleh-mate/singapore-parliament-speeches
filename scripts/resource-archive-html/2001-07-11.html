    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Deputy Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I would like to answer Questions Nos. 2, 3, 4 and 5 together as they are on the same issue.

    Mr Deputy Speaker: Yes.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: Thank you. The Singapore economy shrank by 0.8% in the second quarter, following the weaker external environment. The slow-down is more rapid than what we had earlier expected.

    All the major economies have slowed sharply. US growth in the first quarter was revised downwards significantly from the initial estimate of 2% to 1.2%, as companies run down inventories at a much faster pace. The EU has also slowed significantly while the Japanese economy is near or may be in recession.

    At the same time, the global electronics industry has deteriorated even more sharply in the last few months. Global semiconductor sales fell by 29% in April and May, down from -4.4% in the first quarter this year and +22% growth in the fourth quarter of last year. For the whole year, industry analysts at Gartner Dataquest expect the industry to shrink by 20% to 25%.

    The negative environment has a large impact on Singapore's growth as external demand accounts for 80% of our total demand. In particular, our manufacturing sector has declined by 10.9% in May, and is expected to remain weak in the second half. Export-oriented services have also slowed as demand from regional economies fell. We have therefore revised our growth forecast this year down to 0.5% to 1.5%.

    We can expect unemployment to worsen. Already, we have seen more companies putting their workers on shorter work-week and compulsory leave. There has also been a significant fall in job advertisements in recent months. Anecdotally, I think all of us would have heard or received feedback from friends and relatives that there is a fear that they may lose their jobs because of the retrenchment that they see going on around them.

    In view of the sharper than expected downturn, the Government intends to implement some specific measures to cushion its impact on companies and workers. However, this will not be like the $10 billion package in 1998. The 1998 package was necessary to restore Singapore's competitiveness following the Asian financial crisis and the sharp devaluation of regional currencies. The current downturn is a cyclical slowdown caused by a severe drop in external demand, especially in electronics. We are encouraging Ministries to speed up their development projects, but we should be realistic that such pump priming would only be of marginal benefit; leakage is high and it would not be able to offset the drastic fall in external demand. The objective of the measures we are working on this time is to ease the burden on businesses and workers, while we await the recovery of the US economy and the global electronics industry.
