    8. Mr Leong Horn Kee asked the Deputy Prime Minister if he will give an update on the status of the Clob issue, and what are the possible solutions being considered to resolve the matter.

    The Deputy Prime Minister (BG Lee Hsien Loong): Mr Speaker, Sir, I last updated Parliament on the Clob issue in May 1999. Since then, Mr Akbar Khan's cash offer has closed with 359 investors out of more than 172,000 accepting. Three more private sector offers have been announced, but none have obtained regulatory approvals and been formally made. Akbar Khan's Straits Fund Sdn Bhd is offering units in a closed-end investment fund in exchange for Clob investors' securities. It is not yet known at what price the Clob securities will be valued or how Clob investors are to trade these units. larger investors, and custodial services for non-accepting investors. And the third private sector offer is for Clob investors to swap their securities for an equal proportion of shares in Telekom Malaysia and United Engineers of Malaysia ("UEM"). The two companies have said that they will purchase the Clob securities at a discount to market prices, and sell shares in Telekom Malaysia and UEM at a premium to market prices.

    Any offeror intending to offer Clob investors shares or units in investment funds for their Clob Malaysian securities will need to issue a full prospectus in Singapore. This prospectus must be approved and registered with the Registry of Companies and Businesses ("RCB"). The RCB verifies that the prospectuses comply with the disclosure requirements set out in Singapore's Companies Act, and will register the documents if it is in the public interest to do so.     The Stock Exchange of Singapore (SES) has stated that it will not stand in the way of private sector offers. However, there should be clear and reasonable arrangements for non-accepting investors to trade their shares on the KLSE. The agreement between CDP and SCANS contains arrangements for Clob investors to transfer their securities to individual account with the Malaysian Central Depository, and thereafter trade these securities on the KLSE. SUZHOU PROJECT


    9. Mr J. B. Jeyaretnam asked the Deputy Prime Minister whether the Government is still hopeful of recovering all monies invested in the Suzhou project.

    BG Lee Hsien Loong: Mr Speaker, Sir, Mr Jeyaretnam last asked about investment of public monies in Suzhou Industrial Park (SIP) in January 1998. In my reply, I explained the composition of SSTD, that is, the Singapore consortium participating in the SIP project. I also provided the amounts its shareholders had invested. I pointed out that as the shareholders include publicly listed companies and MNCs, the SIP project had to be viable. Any losses sustained would have to be made public and available for scrutiny by the international investment community.

    I also explained the problems faced by the SIP as a result of aggressive promotion by the Suzhou Government of a rival industrial park next door, the Suzhou New District (SND).

Column: 1828



    Since January 1998, both sides have carried out extensive consultations to resolve the problems. They thoroughly evaluated the progress of the SIP and the experience gained so far, and sought to work out jointly the best way to advance the project, given the circumstances. The discussions were successful, and resulted in a memorandum of understanding (MOU) signed by the two sides in Singapore on 28th June 1999, and published.

    Initially the Singapore side suggested that SND, that is, the rival, suspended its marketing for five years, so that full attention could be given to accelerate SIP's development. The Chinese side explained why they could not suspend SND, but expressed their commitment to SIP's priority status by offering to coordinate the marketing of the two parks. The Singapore side accepted the Chinese explanation of their constraints. Hence, the need for a fundamental review.

    The MOU is a comprehensive package redefining the objectives and structure of the project, and resolving many long outstanding problems. Its main points include:

    a) Refocusing the SIP project on completing the first 8 sq km by 1st January 2001.

    b) Transferring majority ownership and management control of CSSD, the joint venture company from Singapore to China on 1st January 2001.

    c) The Suzhou Government and the joint venture company to continue to look after the interests of SIP tenants and investors. They guarantee that the changes to the project will not affect their commitments to these tenants and investors.

    d) The Suzhou Government will respect SIP's priority status by recommending all suitable projects to the SIP, encouraging investors to choose SIP, and taking concrete measures to pre-empt disorderly competition between SIP and SND.

Column: 1829



    e) The Suzhou Government will encourage population from Suzhou old city to move to SIP so as to accelerate SIP's development and growth.

    f) Both sides will accelerate the adaptation of Singapore's experience. Beyond 1st January 2001, software transfer will continue through attachments and training of Chinese officials in Singapore.

    g) Both sides have also settled all the outstanding commercial issues, to enable the project to go forward with a clean slate and the best chance of success.

    h) Both sides will jointly assist CSSD to enhance its financial performance and to enable CSSD's early listing.

    These measures will put the SIP project on a workable track and improve the joint venture company's financial viability. The Chinese side have assured us that they will do their utmost over the next 18 months to implement the agreements and make the 8 sq km area a success. The Singapore side remains committed to the project and will do all it can to achieve a positive outcome.

    The shareholders in the Singapore consortium have fully endorsed the MOU. They include American, Japanese, Dutch and Korean corporations. The consortium has slightly increased its investments in the joint venture companies since January last year.     The strategic objective of the SIP project was not just to earn a profit for the joint venture, but to be a vehicle for software transfer.
