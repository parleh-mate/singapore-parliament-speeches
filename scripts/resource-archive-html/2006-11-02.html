     The Leader of the House (Mr Wong Kan Seng): Mr Speaker, Sir, on behalf of all Members of this House, I extend to you our warmest congratulations on your re-election as Speaker of the Eleventh Parliament.

 

    Sir, you bring to the office of Speaker a distinguished parliamentary record of some 22 years, with your experience on both the front and backbenches.  Members have often benefited from your gentle persuasion and enjoyed the good humour, qualities which you have brought to the Chair since 2002.

 

    Sir, since your election as Speaker in 2002, your quiet patience and determination in discharging your duties fairly and impartially at all times has gained the respect of Members of both sides of the House.  In presiding over the proceedings, you have upheld the prestige and decorum of this House in your rulings.  With your firm hand in applying the rules and Standing Orders, you have also ensured that proceedings of the House are carried out expeditiously and orderly.  Your unanimous re-election today reflects the confidence that the House has in you.  We have no doubt, Sir, that under your guidance, the good standing of this House will continue to be maintained.

 

     On this note, I would also like to take this opportunity to welcome the 24 new PAP MPs and one Non-Constituency MP to the House. With this new term of MPs, and another 17 and 21 PAP MPs elected in 1997 and 2001 respectively, this House has undergone a transformation. This group of MPs makes up 73% of the Members in this Chamber. As a result, Parliament is able to remain relevant to the people.

 

      Just one word of advice to our new Members.  It is a privilege and an honour to be a Member of the Parliament.  This role comes with a great responsibility.  We are elected by the people to represent their interest and we must perform our duties with a high standard of honesty and integrity.  We must never abuse the privileges accorded to us as Members and must be worthy of the House which we are elected to.  Indeed, I urge Members to maintain a measure of discipline and decorum at all times in this august Chamber, especially on occasions of robust or provocative debate, so as to uphold the dignity of the House.

 

  We eagerly look forward to the maiden speeches of the new Members next week and their contribution to the debates in this Chamber in the coming years.

PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS

 

A Competitive Economy, an Inclusive Society

 

Singapore at a turning point


     The President:  Honourable Members, forty-one years ago, Singapore was thrust into independence and an uncertain future.  At that defining moment of our history, we resolved to succeed.  Together, Singaporeans made key choices that made us what we are today – a united people of different races living and prospering in harmony.

 

     Singapore now stands at another turning point.  Globally, our environment is changing rapidly, and often unpredictably. With globalisation, countries cooperate to prosper together, but also compete more intensely.  Moreover, new problems cross borders with greater speed and virulence, including diseases like avian influenza and security threats like extremist terrorism. In Asia, the rise of China and India will transform the entire continent, and indeed the rest of the world.

   

     Within Singapore, fundamental changes are taking place.  First, our economy has undergone a significant shift.  Globalisation is putting a higher premium on skills, knowledge and flexibility.  Professionals and highly-skilled workers are in demand and can command global wages. But those who are less-skilled will have to compete with the large population of low-wage workers in China, India and Vietnam, as these countries attract more foreign investments and increase their exports.


     Second, our age profile is changing rapidly. From being a young population and workforce with few aged dependants, we are becoming a more elderly society, with older workers and many more retirees.  Singaporeans are living longer but not producing enough babies to replace themselves.

 

     Third, we are experiencing a generational change.  The founding generation that lived through the upheavals of separation, independence and nation-building is giving way to the P65 generation, born after independence, who have grown up amidst peace and prosperity.

 

     Finally, the generational shift will require a new social and political compact.  The P65 generation is better educated and more globally exposed than their parents, and completely at home in the internet age.  They need avenues to try out their ideas and fulfil their ambitions.  The most successful ones must learn instinctively to identify with their fellow citizens, and lend a hand to those in need.  We must also deepen the sense of mission in this new generation, and provide them more opportunities to take responsibility for our country and build our future together. 

 

     The General Election this year was thus a milestone in Singapore’s political history.  Singaporeans of all races, young and old, strongly endorsed a new leadership team, which includes many new MPs from the post-independence generation.  Voters gave them a clear mandate to take Singapore forward.  My Government will work with all Singaporeans to set the agenda and directions for the next phase of our nation’s development. 

Fostering security

 

     The first imperative is to maintain a safe and secure environment for our people to pursue their dreams and aspirations. A small state needs an active foreign policy, for it to be a responsible and effective player in the international arena. Singapore enjoys good relations with all the key powers.  We participate actively in ASEAN, APEC, the East Asia Summit and other regional forums. These are important platforms to strengthen cooperation as well as to manage conflicts and transboundary problems like haze. 

 

     Sound foreign policy and diplomacy are only effective if we are strong enough to fend for ourselves.  A compact but credible SAF deters potential aggressors, protects our national interests and secures us against both conventional and unconventional threats. An effective Home Team, as well as the vigilance of every citizen, ensures a safe and secure home for all.

 

     One critical aspect of security is social defence.  We have worked long and hard to foster peace and harmony in our multi-racial and multi-religious society.  We must never take this for granted.  Instead we must do more to strengthen cohesion, trust and confidence between the different communities.  At the same time, we must uphold the fundamental principle of meritocracy, so that all citizens, regardless of race or religion, enjoy fair and equal opportunities. 

Growing the economy

 

     An orderly and safe environment will enable us to generate the wealth for our needs. Without economic growth, we cannot invest in our future or realise any schemes to help Singaporeans.  When conditions are favourable, as they are now, we must grow as fast as we can.  Then even when conditions are tough, we can weather the storm and help those in need.


 

     To achieve our full potential in this ever-changing environment, we must be nimble, flexible and alert.  Our companies must build their capabilities and expand both locally and abroad, especially in Asia.  Our people must continue to work closely together, with a strong tripartite partnership among workers, employers and the Government. 


 

     We must also maintain a stable and predictable political climate, tackle problems rationally and constructively, and take a long-term view when making decisions for Singapore.  This is the way to build a better life for all. 

 

     To sustain growth and vitality, there must be enough people living and working in Singapore.  So we must encourage Singaporeans to marry, and couples to have more babies.   At the same time, we need to attract more new immigrants.  They will not only augment our numbers but also add talent and diversity to our city, and help us all to prosper.  So each of us must make an effort to welcome new immigrants as part of our Singapore family, and help them to integrate into our society.

 

Maintaining an outstanding public service

 

     The private sector will take a leading role in creating wealth.  However, the Government creates the preconditions for the private sector to prosper.  It is critical for Singapore to have an effective government and an outstanding public service, with high standards of competence and integrity, able and determined to deliver results for the country.  The public service must offer competitive and attractive careers, in order to recruit and retain talented and committed people and maintain the quality of government that Singaporeans have come to expect.  This is a key competitive advantage for Singapore which makes up for our small size and other disadvantages. 

 

Investing in our future


     One important way the Government can facilitate growth and prosperity is by investing in the future of Singapore.  We will invest in our people, by upgrading and transforming our education system at all levels – primary, secondary and tertiary.  All students will have opportunities to  pursue their diverse interests and develop their creative talents.


 

     We will invest in R&D to build new capabilities, particularly in the emerging areas of biomedical sciences, interactive and digital media, and environmental and water technologies.  We will also continue to deepen our capabilities in the electronics, chemicals and engineering manufacturing industries.  These R&D efforts will move our economy up the value chain, spur growth and create fulfilling and well-paying jobs for Singaporeans.


 

     We will invest in our infrastructure to meet the business needs of tomorrow.  Singapore will be fully connected to the digital highway, as we build a pervasive wireless network and high-speed broadband connections to every home and office.  We will enhance our air and sea ports to entrench our position as a major transport hub.  We will build a high-quality, affordable public transport system to serve commuters.

 

     We will invest in our city to make it vibrant and cosmopolitan, energetic and exciting.  Arts and culture will add a creative buzz to Singapore.  We will transform Marina Bay and the business district to create something special that will attract visitors and create a sense of pride and belonging in our people.  At the same time, we will renew and rejuvenate our HDB estates to improve on our first-class living environment.  Singapore will become the best place in Asia to live, work and play.

Building an inclusive society

 

     Even as we meet competition and pursue growth, we need to strengthen social cohesion.  We will build an inclusive society which leaves no one behind.  Because of globalisation, incomes in many countries are stretching out.  Singapore faces this problem too.  While most Singaporeans will benefit from the opportunities that globalisation brings, some segments of our population are finding it more difficult to cope. 


 

    We cannot solve this problem by resisting change, or hiding from competition.  This is futile and will only reduce growth and make things worse for all Singaporeans.  Instead, our basic approach must be to stay open to the world and pursue growth and competitiveness, while doing more to assist Singaporeans who are adversely affected. 


 

     Education is the best way to level up our society.  The Government already invests heavily in each child.  Going forward, we will continue to provide every child access to a top-rate education, so that he or she gets the best possible start in life.  We will further reduce attrition among students, and help those who are at risk of dropping out.  Children from low-income families will not face a digital divide, because we will help them to own computers and access broadband services.


 

     To help lower-skilled workers, particularly the older ones, the Government will provide them with more skills training and upgrading, in close cooperation with unions and employers.  We will redesign low-wage jobs so that such workers can become more productive and earn more.  The workers too must make the effort to learn new skills and keep themselves employable as the needs of industries change. 


 

     The Government will also do more to assist lower-income Singaporeans, to help them progress with the rest of society.  We will target our assistance, and encourage them to work and upgrade themselves, to build up their assets, and to support their families.  


 

    A second looming social issue is the ageing of our people.  Ours is one of the fastest ageing populations in the world.  On current demographic trends, the number of Singaporeans aged 65 years and above will double in 15 years.  This will be a dramatic change, with far-reaching implications for our society and economy. 


 

     The post-World War Two baby-boomers will soon be retiring.  We need to tackle holistically, on many fronts, all the challenges of a greying society.  Singaporeans need to stay active longer, retire later, and lead productive and fulfilling lives in their golden years.  We need to build a living environment with more user-friendly features to help the elderly and the disabled.  We must help older Singaporeans to unlock the substantial asset value of their homes and convert it into income for their retirement.  We must keep good basic medical care affordable to all.  Most of all, we need fundamental mindset changes – on the part of families, workers, employers, and the Government.


 

     The growing needs of lower-income and older Singaporeans require us to strengthen our social safety nets.  This calls for many helping hands.  Individuals have to make the effort to help themselves, and to provide for their future needs.  Employers have to adapt workplaces to suit older workers.  Families have to stay together, sharing responsibility for one another and helping to care for elderly parents.  Community and voluntary welfare organisations have to support troubled families and the disadvantaged, and provide avenues for Singaporeans to volunteer and contribute in a spirit of cohesion and generosity. 


 

     For its part, the Government will do more.  Already, we have implemented schemes such as Comcare, the CPF and Medisave Top-ups, the Utilities Save, the Additional CPF Housing Grant, the Workfare Bonus, and the Progress Package.  These measures have helped many lower-income households to weather their recent difficult years.  We will introduce more such measures to help needy Singaporeans more systematically, learning from our experience about those measures which work and those which do not.

 

     Our social security system is based on three pillars.  First, the CPF helps Singaporeans save for their retirement.  Second, the 3Ms – Medisave, MediShield and Medifund – help us afford good quality healthcare.  Third, HDB home ownership provides a roof over the heads of all Singaporeans, and gives every household a stake in Singapore.  This approach is sound.  It enables almost every Singaporean to be self supporting, and avoid becoming dependent on the state.  We will adapt, reinforce and update these three pillars of our social safety net.  We will provide more support for those facing difficulties, and help them to save more in their CPF and Medisave accounts.

Emphasising achievement and work

 

     However, in building an inclusive society, we must avoid the mistakes other countries have made in pursuing welfare schemes.  Starting off well-intentioned, many are now stuck with increased dependency and higher unemployment. The work ethic of their people has been eroded and their motivation sapped. With this heavy burden of welfare, these economies have stagnated.


 

     We would all like the Government to do even more, but the Government can only spend within its means.  We have kept taxes in Singapore low, especially direct taxes on income.  This keeps us competitive, and ensures strong incentives for Singaporeans to work, to excel, and to make the most of their abilities.  That is a main reason why people and companies want to live, work and invest here. 


 

     We have to maintain this approach.  Therefore, we must be very cautious in taking on new state-funded welfare commitments.  We must ensure that our programmes are sustainable and affordable, and that we have the resources to fund and sustain the schemes we want.


 

     However, Government spending will inevitably rise over the next decade, as we invest more in Singapore’s future and spend more to meet our growing social needs.  We have to provide for this, in order to balance the budget and command the resources that we will need.  Every citizen must contribute to our shared well-being, but better off Singaporeans have a responsibility to do more to help their less successful fellow citizens.  Only then can we maintain social cohesion, and foster an inclusive society where every citizen has a stake in Singapore.


 

Conclusion


     Today, as in 1965, we face major choices that will profoundly shape our future.  But we are far better equipped today to make these choices and act on them than our founding generation.  We have built sound institutions, our people are better educated, and our economy is one of the most competitive in the world. 

 

     Our choices will determine Singapore’s future in the 21st century.  If we are fearful and reluctant to adapt to new challenges, we will lose ground amidst a changing world, and Singapore will become a nation divided by wealth, race and religion.  But if we respond boldly and creatively to our challenges, we will make Singapore secure and prosperous, harmonious and cohesive – a country that all Singaporeans are proud of and love, and that other nations respect and admire.  Let us resolve to stay together and move ahead.  Let us build a competitive economy and an inclusive society for ourselves and our children in Singapore.

MINISTRY OF HEALTH

MR KHAW BOON WAN

Minister for Health

     Our mission is to build a healthy nation of Singaporeans. Based on the principles of personal responsibility, early intervention, and integrated evidence-based health services, we work with the private and people sectors to ensure that high quality and appropriate healthcare is available and affordable to all.

Keeping healthcare affordable

     Our healthcare financing framework ensures that Singaporeans can afford to pay for care. There will always be a social safety net for the poor through targeted Government subsidies and Medifund. Beyond this, healthcare is a shared responsibility between the individual and society.

     Government will strengthen the 3Ms (Medisave, MediShield and Medifund) framework to address the concerns of (a) Singaporeans who worry if they get hit with high medical bills; (b) older Singaporeans whose Medisave has run out; and (c) Singaporean workers who do not have employers to help cover the costs of their outpatient care. We will also try to accommodate the desire of Singaporeans with substantial Medisave balances to use them for private healthcare.

     We will get Medisave to cover a greater share of costly medical care. If successful, the initial Medisave coverage for four chronic diseases at outpatient level will be progressively expanded to other chronic diseases.

     We will expand the use of insurance for low-probability but large bills by further enhancing MediShield. MediShield policies with different scopes of coverage, deductibles and premiums will be developed to better suit individual needs. We will also partner employers, unions and insurance companies to improve medical benefits for workers.

     We will take care of needy Singaporeans. We will build up Medifund as an effective safety net for the needy and review our financial support for intermediate and long-term care. Where the Budget permits, we will consider Medisave top-ups for low-wage workers through Workfare.

Strengthening our healthcare system through long-term and integrated care

     We presently deliver excellent care when people fall ill. We can improve further by partnering Singaporeans to look after their health and improving integration between healthcare providers across the spectrum – primary, step-down, acute; the private, public and people sectors.

     Singaporeans must take responsibility for their health. We will develop programmes to support and empower Singaporeans to actively manage their illnesses, especially those with chronic diseases. National health promotion efforts will be complemented with more targeted programmes for at-risk groups.

     We will support family physicians to deliver better primary and preventive care as they are the best and most trusted patient advocates and long-term care co-ordinators. We will promote stronger partnerships between family physicians and specialists to better manage the major illnesses afflicting Singaporeans.

     We will review intermediate and long-term care for our ageing population. We will improve the quality of rehabilitation so people can return to independent and productive lives in the community. Government will support the training and deployment of professional and administrative manpower. We will encourage more private sector participation to improve performance and efficiency.

     We will accelerate IT adoption across all healthcare providers. MOH will publish bill sizes, clinical outcomes and service standards from public and private providers, so that patients can make better choices for themselves.

Improving healthcare accessibility and service standards

     We will renew our healthcare infrastructure to meet the needs of our growing population. The new regional hospital in Yishun will open in 2009. We will rebuild the National Heart Centre and expand the National University Hospital, while opening new beds in existing hospitals to meet rising demand. Where appropriate, we will co-locate community hospitals with acute hospitals to improve integrated care. We will continue to pilot innovative infrastructure such as the newly opened Jurong Medical Centre.

     We will deploy more quality manpower to improve service standards and clinical quality. We recognise that anxious patients and their families require attention and reassurance, and will improve workflow within our institutions and patient interaction skills of our front-line staff, particularly when communicating medical conditions and providing financial counselling.

Pursuing medical excellence

     We will drive clinical quality improvements to advance patient safety. Excellent health services research will lead to evidence-based clinical improvements. Clinical outcomes will be tracked in electronic medical records. The Ministry will also reinforce the culture of teaching and education that have made our public institutions icons of quality and progress.

     The Government will invest in our professional and healthcare manpower – doctors, nurses and allied health professionals – so that we continue to deliver quality care to a growing population. New schools and courses will be provided locally. Continuing education and accreditation will cover more groups of professionals. We will review our regulatory frameworks to attract world-class foreign talent to Singapore. We will also encourage more private sector professionals to contribute to the people and public sectors.

     To keep up with advances in biomedical science and develop new clinical treatments for Singaporeans, we will invest in clinical and translational research in partnership with A*STAR, MOE and the National Research Foundation. We will strengthen our supporting infrastructure by promoting academic medicine and strengthening our biomedical ethics regulations.

     MOH will develop new programmes for our future healthcare needs. This includes a broader approach to elderly care, such as facilitating affordable solutions for ageing-in-place in the community. We should help our patients spend their final days and moments with their families in peace, comfort and dignity. We should acknowledge the limitation of medical science and address end-of-life issues based on good sense, realism and compassion, as the alternative of delaying the end will merely be at great physical, social and emotional costs to the patients and their families. We will also upgrade our mental health programme to make Singapore an emotionally resilient community with good access to effective mental health services.

Tackling infectious diseases

     The Ministry remains vigilant to the threat of global infectious disease outbreaks. We will invest in a national public health laboratory, and partner the WHO and the international community to counter infectious diseases and keep Singapore safe. We will tackle emerging communicable diseases in our local population, such as HIV/AIDS and sexually transmitted diseases amongst youths.

Singapore medicine

     As healthcare becomes more globalised, Singapore should leverage our reputation for quality and trusted medical care to re-position ourselves as the regional medical hub. We will collaborate with other Government agencies and healthcare institutions to maximise our potential without compromising our core mission of delivering good quality and affordable healthcare to all Singaporeans.

Building a quality living environment

Ensuring water for all

MINISTRY OF EDUCATION

 

MR THARMAN SHANMUGARATNAM

Minister for Education

 

Opportunities for all through quality and diversity

 

    Education is our best hope for the future.  We are shifting our focus to quality and diversity in education.  We will develop the potential of every child to the maximum, so that we create a mountain range of talents in Singapore, with many peaks of excellence.

 

     We are introducing more choices for students, and more opportunities to develop diverse strengths and interests in sports, arts and intellectual endeavours.  Within the next five years, we expect a third of our schools to have niches of excellence of their own.

     At primary levels, we will implement further refinements to our approach to ability-driven education in Primary 5 and 6 by adopting Subject-based Banding to replace the EM3 stream.  These changes build on the experience that schools have gained from the changes that we have put in place over the years, including the integration of EM3 students with others in Mother Tongue Language and non-core subjects.

Levelling up through education

 

     Education is the best gift we can offer our children, especially those from lower-income families.  We will work together with social agencies to minimise non-attendance at pre-school education, and reduce dropouts at primary and secondary school levels.  Within five years, we aim to halve the proportion of students who drop out of our schools, from the current 3% to 1.5%.

 

     The Financial Assistance Scheme (FAS) and the Edusave Merit Bursary (EMB) awards have been expanded to benefit more students from lower income families.  With the revisions earlier this year, more than twice the number of families now qualify for FAS and about a third more will qualify for the EMB awards.  We have introduced a new Government bursary to help needy university students pay for living expenses during their studies.  The universities have also increased the quanta they have set aside for financial assistance by almost threefold.

 

     We will raise the quality of education for children with special needs.  To allow children with mild to moderate special learning needs to benefit from being part of mainstream schools, we will deploy Special Needs Officers (SNOs) as well as provide training in special needs to selected teachers in mainstream schools.  MOE has also significantly increased its recurrent funding to Special Education (SPED) schools, and is accelerating the redevelopment of nine purpose-built SPED schools to be completed in 2008.

 

Strengthening rootedness and preparing for the world

 

     Through the school experience, we will strengthen heartware and rootedness among our young.  Our schools will continue to find new ways to foster a strong sense of belonging and pride in Singapore, and create more opportunities for interaction, multiracial bonding and partnerships with the community.  We will also encourage students to initiate activities, and help them develop the desire to write the next chapter of the Singapore Story.

  

   We will also expand twinning relationships between our schools and those overseas.  These programmes will help our students gain confidence in working across cultures, and build collaborative networks from young.

 

     MOE is making it easier for Singaporean children abroad to join our schools when they return home, so as to support and encourage Singaporeans in their efforts to venture overseas.  At the same time, we will strengthen the integration of foreign scholars and students into our schools and tertiary institutions.  Over time, these students will either contribute to Singapore’s cosmopolitan and diverse make-up, or build up a network of friends for Singapore when they return to their home countries.

 

Supporting our teachers

 

     Our teachers are at the heart of our efforts to improve the quality of education.  We will provide them with more time and space to interact with their students, reflect on their teaching, and engage in effective professional development.  We are making significant improvements in pupil-teacher ratios and have allowed schools greater flexibility to recruit additional qualified teachers directly through the Adjunct Teachers Scheme.  Schools will also be receiving greater administrative support, for example, through provisions of new staff to support co-curricular activities.

   

  The GROW package, launched in September 2006, will support teachers in their professional development and better recognise their contributions.  Teachers will have benefits such as sabbaticals, which give opportunities for fresh learning and growth.

 

     Going forward, improvements in the quality of education will come from innovations on the ground – new teaching practices and curricula, driven and owned by schools, responding to the needs of their students.  MOE’s role will be to provide top-down support for these ground-up initiatives.

 

Building a vibrant tertiary sector

 

     In tandem with the changes in our economy, ITE training will continue to shift from a predominantly manufacturing focus to a broad-based focus including the services sectors.  We will also work to maximise the number of students who go on to ITE, who would otherwise not have post-secondary education.

  

   MOE will provide more local university places for Singaporeans, reaching 25% within a few years.  Our three autonomous universities will aim to provide a top quality education at affordable cost.  Polytechnic graduates will have more choices to pursue quality degree courses locally.  Besides the growing university sector, they will have opportunities for further study in niche areas of an applied nature, to be offered by foreign specialised institutions in collaboration with the polytechnics.

 

     Our universities and polytechnics will also work to develop world-class capabilities in research and development, as part of Singapore’s broader thrust towards R&D excellence.  MOE has also put in place an enhanced academic research framework to support the development of Research Centres of Excellence in our universities.  The polytechnics will also establish Centres of Innovation in selected areas, so as to further deepen their links with key industries and boost local research and innovation efforts.

Our psychological response would be even more crucial, for it is our resilience and social cohesion - built on our strength and resolve as a people - that will enable us to emerge stronger after a crisis. Total Defence will continue to serve us well as a framework for how we respond to the threats and challenges in a cohesive and comprehensive manner. MINDEF will continue to strengthen our Total Defence through national education efforts, to involve all Singaporeans in the security and defence of Singapore.


Prevention, protection and consequence management


     In fighting terrorism, the SAF will continue to work closely with home-front agencies to enhance our defences and responses, to deter attacks and protect ourselves. Robust inter-agency cooperation and frequent exercises prepare the response forces and our people to deal with an attack.


Defence and deterrence


     Even as the SAF builds up its non-conventional capabilities, we will have to ensure that it continues to have the conventional hard edge to be an effective deterrence. The SAF safeguards Singapore’s security, sovereignty and territorial integrity. A strong defence also gives Singapore the political space to chart our own destiny and pursue the best interests of our country and our people.


     The 3rd Generation SAF is evolving into a leaner, more flexible and more tightly-networked force that can undertake the spectrum of missions necessary to ensure Singapore’s security. The SAF’s ongoing transformation will ensure that we can fight and win the wars of the future. Transformation involves new technologies, operational concepts and training methods. With transformation, the SAF’s capabilities will be magnified.

 

     Technology is a force multiplier that enables us to surmount our limitations in people and resources. It is also a catalyst of new ideas for organising, equipping, training, and fighting. We will continue to exploit technology by leveraging on our robust defence eco-system, so as to maximise synergy and collaborative opportunities.


Building trust and connectivity


     Diplomacy is the other pillar of Singapore’s defence policy. MINDEF will continue to actively develop positive and mutually beneficial relationships with friendly countries to enhance Singapore’s security and regional stability. The SAF will continue to work closely with friendly forces to enhance our professional skills, improve inter-operability, and benchmark ourselves against the best armed forces in the world. The SAF will also continue with its extensive overseas training which helps overcome our land and airspace constraints.


     At the wider level, it is clear that countries must work together to deal with common security threats. International cooperation is essential and Singapore can play a role in such cooperative efforts to contribute to regional security and stability, and also protect our national interests and ensure our security. MINDEF will continue to participate actively in multilateral defence efforts which bring international and regional partners together to deal with common threats, especially in such areas as counter-terrorism, maritime security, and counter-proliferation. Singapore will also continue to do our part where we can make a useful contribution to multinational peace support operations or humanitarian assistance and disaster relief missions.


People


     The strength of the SAF lies in our servicemen and servicewomen - their spirit, their commitment and their professionalism. We will continue to develop them to achieve their highest potential. With 80% of each cohort having post-secondary education, we have the human resources to make good use of the technology that is core in the 3rd Generation SAF.


National Service


     National Service is critical for national defence and remains the backbone of the SAF. Maintaining the motivation and morale of our National Servicemen will continue to be our focus.

 

Public support and consultation


     MINDEF will continue to seek feedback and consult Singaporeans through channels such as SAFRA, ACCORD and RECORD, and the National Education Outreach programme. The recommendations of RECORD IV to recognise the contributions of our National Servicemen are being implemented.

 

 

MINISTRY OF HOME AFFAIRS

 

MR WONG KAN SENG
Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs

 

     The Ministry of Home Affairs' mission is to keep Singapore safe and secure. This is a fundamental quality of life which all Singaporeans and visitors to Singapore have come to value.  Safety and security are also the critical conditions which underpin Singapore’s continued economic prosperity and growth. 

 

Sustaining good security

 

Staying ahead of the terrorists

 

     MHA will continue to develop strong practical competencies to deal with extremist terrorism and its various violent forms. Just as the terrorists are evolving and are adapting their tactics, we need to keep up or better still, stay ahead of them. We must constantly seek to limit the weaknesses in our society which can be exploited by terrorists. For instance, MHA recently moved to require registration of pre-paid mobile phone SIM card users. This will deny the anonymity that such cards provide to users, something which terrorists and criminals have exploited to conduct their activities.


 

     We must always be ready to develop new capabilities and learn new skills. Although Singapore is a not a country with nuclear energy, the prospect of a “dirty bomb” which exploits radioactive material, is not an implausible threat. To deal with such a scenario, the Singapore Civil Defence Force (SCDF) has developed capabilities to identify, contain and neutralise such threats.  The SCDF is working closely with the SPF and SAF to enhance integration and tap synergies so as to improve our capacity and capability to meet such threats.  

     Ultimately, however, vigilance and preparedness are our strongest defence against the terrorist threat. Governments alone do not defeat terrorism; leaders and the communities defeat terrorism. This need for vigilance and preparedness, therefore, applies not only to our front-line officers but also to our citizen-partners in business, industry and the community.

 

Security as a way of life

 

The people as security stakeholders

 

     MHA conducted Exercise Northstar V in January 2006. It involved, for the first time, a significant participation by grassroots as well as members of the public who happened to be present at the scene at the time of the exercise.  This is a direction MHA will continue to pursue. The SCDF will organise more large-scale ground deployment exercises not only to raise awareness and get Singaporeans mentally prepared for an incident, but also to persuade them to better equip themselves by learning emergency preparedness skills. In every major catastrophe, it is typically ordinary people at the scene who are the first to respond to help victims and save lives. Such people make a timely and crucial difference complementing the efforts of the emergency forces.


     The Community Engagement Programme (CEP), launched in February 2006, is aimed at enlarging and deepening, across various sectors, what has been previously pursued by the efforts of organisations like the MHA and the grassroots. The CEP will involve not just grassroots organisations, but also religious groups, voluntary welfare organisations, clan associations, educational institutions, businesses, unions, and the media. The CEP establishes a broad framework which can manage and minimise racial and religious tension in the aftermath of a terrorist incident. This is a long-term programme which hopefully will grow to become a natural and permanent feature of our societal landscape, reflecting our people’s commitment to stay cohesive and resilient against any threat.

  
     There is urgency to engage our communities and people over security matters. The threat of extremist terrorism is not going to go away. It will remain a serious threat for a long time. The most dangerous aspect of this threat is that it exploits religion. As such, the damage it can cause is not just material loss but the deeper injury of tearing apart our harmonious inter-communal relations. If this happens, even if we heal the wounds, the scars will remain for a long time and will continue to breed mistrust among our different communities. MHA recognises that Singaporeans have the most critical role to play to prevent the threat, to mitigate its impact and to be resilient through emergency preparedness and a commitment to our social cohesion. Every Singaporean must ultimately be an active stakeholder in Singapore’s security.

Balancing security and efficiency

 

     The world is not the same one we lived in before 11th September 2001. Heightened security will be a part of life for a long time to come.  Enhanced security measures will cause inconvenience to the public but Singaporeans must learn to accept this as a part of life in the current security climate. We must be prepared to make the necessary adjustments at both the personal and organisational levels.  MHA would, however, strive to minimise the costs of security wherever practicable. Life must go on. We must not be paralysed by fear or by rigidities in our security responses which can choke off the dynamism and efficiency of our society which makes us competitive and vibrant. 

    

     A key challenge we must confront is how to strike the right balance between security and efficiency.  We cannot protect everything. A risk management approach must be pursued based on threat and vulnerabilities. Wherever possible, we should exploit technology. Smart use of technology can offer win-win solutions. For example, ICA’s Biometric Identification of MotorbiKErs (BIKES) system will enhance the security screening of motorcyclists at our land checkpoints.  This system uses biometric technology to screen motorcyclists and is expected to allow bona-fide motorcyclists to enjoy speedier clearance.  Similarly, in order to facilitate travel while maintaining high security, ICA will be installing enhanced biometric-based automated clearance gates, known as eIACS, at all checkpoints which allow automated self-clearance by Singapore Citizens, Permanent Residents, Long Term Pass holders and Frequent Visitors using only their passports.


Security as a competitive advantage

 

     Going forward, we should leverage on our good security and position it as a competitive advantage. Companies and organisations choose to locate their headquarters or conduct international conventions in Singapore not only because of the many attractions we offer in terms of organisational efficiency, good infrastructure and so on.  They must also have considered the fact that Singapore is a safe and secure venue where the safety and security of their delegates can be assured.  The hosting of the International Olympic Committee Session last year and the 2006 Annual Meetings of the Boards of Governors of the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank Group in Singapore is testament to our reliability and capability to ensure the security of the thousands of delegates while they were in Singapore. Their decision to choose Singapore as a venue reflects the trust these international organisations have in the security of Singapore.  This is one of the key ingredients in transforming Singapore into a convention centre, a knowledge hub and a connector of people and regions. 

 

Policing change and maintaining fundamentals

 

     Our society is changing and policing has become a more complex challenge.  MHA needs to be in tune with the pulse of these changes and find ways to accommodate the aspirations and needs of Singaporeans within the boundaries of law and order. Some of these boundaries may need to be revisited and reviewed.  MHA will develop more calibrated approaches to address the challenges of, for example, youth crime.  Likewise, the Police will review its role in regulating business and entertainment activities and confine its role to law and order parameters. The private security industry should be developed to be a partner in addressing the law and order and security needs of business corporations and organisations. 

     We must encourage citizen participation. However, personal autonomy and empowerment must be balanced with attendant accountability.  Even as we move to liberalise and refine the boundaries, we must be clear what our fundamentals for survival as a small multi-ethnic society are.  We will not tolerate racial and religious extremism or bigotry; we will not go soft on our zero tolerance for drugs, whether synthetic or otherwise. CNB will remain vigilant against possible new drugs and patterns of abuse. 

     On the flip side of enforcement and policing, MHA will seek to reintegrate ex-offenders with their families and with society so that they can recover and live meaningful and productive lives.  Prisons and SCORE will work towards the creation of a seamless through-care system to help inmates reintegrate. They will also raise awareness and engage the community to take action to support ex-offenders and their families.  For example, Family Resource Centres were set up in July 2006 to help inmates' families cope with the incarceration of their loved ones and to facilitate their role in the inmates’ eventual reintegration.  SCORE will also continue its efforts in raising awareness and engaging the community to take action to support ex-offenders and their families through the Yellow Ribbon Project.

 

Conclusion

 

     Our constant challenge must be to globalise without losing our local identity.  Building a competitive economy and an inclusive society must mean sustaining our fundamental values while we grow and change as a modern society plugged into a global economy.  We want a safe and secure Singapore; a society where different communities live in harmony; a vibrant society which upholds the rule of law; and one which seeks to rehabilitate and recover those who have transgressed.  We want a land of opportunity where everyone has a chance, if not a second chance, to grow and to succeed. This is a vision worth aspiring to. A strong and trusted partnership between the leaders and the people will make this vision achievable.

Ensure stability and security in an uncertain world

     The threat of transnational terrorism is undiminished. Some terrorist attacks have succeeded, in London, Bali, Jordan, Egypt, while others have been foiled through arrests in Canada and the US. In Singapore, our security agencies have remained vigilant. Major exercises like Exercise Northstar were conducted to prepare us for the worst. We have taken a whole-of-government approach to tackle this long-term threat. The National Security Coordination Secretariat (NSCS) created in the Prime Minister’s Office facilitates this process through its two agencies - the National Security Coordination Centre (NSCC) and the Joint Counter Terrorism Centre (JCTC). 

     At home, NSCS works with the various security agencies in strategic planning and key policy issues. Various programmes, like courses and seminars, are conducted to raise awareness, to familiarise the agencies with national security plans and policies, and to foster a collaborative culture. A Centre of Excellence for National Security (CENS) has been set up to help NSCS explore new policy thinking and innovative ideas to further enhance national security. Abroad, NSCS will continue to strengthen ties with our regional partners in ASEAN, as well as with our international partners, to combat this shared security threat.

Seize opportunities in a time of rapid change and increasing competition

     In the face of growing complexities and rising uncertainties,   NSCS is developing a risk assessment and horizon scanning capability, through a development project. The project will help build and track a range of possible scenarios and pick up weak signals of surprises that can cause us great disruptions. The system will also facilitate inter-agency collaboration and horizontal communications among the various security agencies.

Foster an inclusive society

       In the people sector, NSCS coordinates the various agencies involved in the continuing effort to raise the public’s awareness of the threats to Singapore’s security. This will help enhance individual and community resilience. Citizens are encouraged to play a more active role in combating terrorism. MINISTRY OF LAW


PROF S. JAYAKUMAR
Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Law

 

     MinLaw will further strengthen Singapore’s legal infrastructure and develop the legal services sector. We will also (a) enhance our insolvency regime; (b) optimise our land resources to support Singapore’s efforts to seize new opportunities; and (c) leverage and build on our Intellectual Property (IP) reputation and strengths to contribute to the growth and development of Singapore as a knowledge hub.

Legal

 

     To ensure that our legal system remains relevant and responsive to the needs of Singapore, we will work closely with the Attorney-General’s Chambers to further strengthen the legal infrastructure. We will also work with other Government agencies to ensure that legislation keeps pace with socio-economic developments and are updated to combat emerging threats such as terrorism and other trans-national crimes.  It is also important that the public have trust and confidence in the legal profession, and we will work with the Law Society to ensure this.

 

     A more globalised economy, strong economic growth in Asia and the rise in cross-border transactions present significant opportunities for our legal services industry.  International arbitration in particular presents enormous potential for growth.   Given our reputation and strengths, Singapore can be a location of choice for the conduct of international arbitration work, especially for the Asian region.  MinLaw will work with other agencies to achieve this goal.  We will set up an international dispute resolution complex, encourage major international arbitration institutions to be based here, and work with local and foreign institutions and law firms to attract more international arbitration work to Singapore.

     Growing opportunities in arbitration and other international legal services will require us to build up the necessary legal talent pool in Singapore.  We will ensure that existing and recently introduced schemes provide not only the numbers but also the quality of lawyers needed to meet the demands.  We will also continue to explore new schemes for the supply of high quality legal talent to support the growth of our legal services industry.

     In addition to measures already announced, we will also examine longer term strategies to enhance Singapore’s position as an international centre for the provision of legal services.  These will include identifying core areas of excellence for the legal sector and how they may be developed; promoting Singapore as a key provider of legal services in Asia, particularly for cross-border commercial transactions, and setting future directions for the legal profession as a whole.


     MinLaw will continue to ensure the provision of quality legal aid and assistance to persons of limited means.  We will ensure that the civil legal aid system has the latitude and flexibility to help all those who are deserving of legal aid.  We will also work closely with the Law Society and other partners to encourage greater volunteerism in the provision of legal aid and assistance to the needy.

     In support of cultivating a more harmonious society, MinLaw will continue to promote the resolution of community-based disputes via mediation.  We will enhance our efforts to create and maintain public awareness of community mediation and to encourage greater use of the schemes available for mediation of social and relational disputes.  We will continue to ensure that the demand for mediation services is met.  Towards this end, we will also work to increase the capacity and capabilities of our mediators. 

     To promote enterprise and entrepreneurship, our debt recovery system must balance between instilling financial discipline and providing a fresh start for debtors.  While we increase efforts to promote prudent financial management, we will also put in place alternative schemes for financially distressed individuals to repay their debts without having to go into bankruptcy.  We will also review and improve the corporate insolvency regime to balance the interests of creditors and failed businesses.


Land


     In support of Singapore’s economic growth and development, MinLaw will continue to play an active role in optimising our land resources.  We will work closely with other Government agencies to identify and facilitate the use of State land and properties to better meet the needs of new industry sectors and businesses.

     MinLaw will also do its part to make Singapore a great global city to work, live and play in.  We will continue to devise new schemes and innovative mechanisms for letting out State land and properties to encourage the development of a vibrant cityscape and revitalise our neighbourhoods.  We will encourage more innovative use of State land and properties, and will tap the public for new ideas.  We will also make available land resources to promote social harmony and community bonding, for example for sports and recreational uses.

Intellectual Property (IP)

 

     With the advent of the knowledge economy, there is great potential for us to build on our reputation and strengths in IP to spur Singapore’s economic growth and development. MinLaw will intensify efforts to leverage and improve on the good reputation of our IP regime to support the development of Singapore as a knowledge hub and a centre for creative and high-tech industries.  We will also further promote the growth of a vibrant IP services industry in Singapore, including working with the industry to deepen core IP capabilities such as in patent drafting and IP strategy advisory services.  We also aim to develop Singapore into a recognised IP education and training centre for the region.

 

 

PRIME MINISTER'S OFFICE (NATIONAL POPULATION SECRETARIAT)

                                           

MR WONG KAN SENG
Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Home Affairs

 

     At the heart of every successful society is a core of dedicated and committed people, who stay and move ahead together. This is especially vital for a small city state like Singapore. Our people are our critical and only resource. For Singapore to continue to thrive and succeed, we will need a core of Singaporeans who will form our social, political and economic foundation, and drive the nation forward.


    The National Population Committee (NPC) was set up following the announcement of the parenthood package in August 2004 to deal with the wide range and multi-faceted challenges of population issues. A full-time National Population Secretariat (NPS) was established in the Prime Minister’s Office in June 2006 to support the NPC and coordinate the work of Ministries in key areas such as promoting marriage and parenthood, engaging overseas-based Singaporeans, and encouraging naturalisation.

Increased number of births

 

     While all the schemes in the parenthood package announced in August 2004 have been fully implemented, it is still too early to assess the effectiveness of the measures. The declining fertility rate has reached a plateau in 2005 at 1.24 (the same as in 2004), although the number of births has increased slightly from 35,135 in 2004 to 35,528 in 2005, a positive reversal after four years of consecutive declines. The number of registered births in the first eight months of 2006 (23,516) is also higher than that in the corresponding period in 2005 (23,065). We are, however, still a long way from the replacement level of 2.1.

 

Encouraging naturalisation

 

     It is not realistic to expect local fertility to significantly reverse in the near future, much less to return to the replacement level, which was last recorded in 1976. As such, in addition to raising local fertility, we would also need to raise immigration and the subsequent naturalisation of suitable immigrants in our efforts to enlarge our population stock so that we could grow our workforce in both size and vibrancy to sustain a viable economy. The Citizenship and Population Unit (CPU) has been set up under the NPS to coordinate and drive the efforts of the relevant Government agencies in this area.

 

     The CPU has recently identified three broad strategies to encourage naturalisation, viz. (a) eliminating and reducing process hindrances, (b) enhancing outreach to foreigners, permanent residents (PR) and new citizens, and (c) more effective branding of Singapore overseas. By making Singapore a more attractive place to global talent to live, work, study and play, this three-pronged approach aims to encourage and enable more suitable foreigners and permanent residents to consider taking up permanent residency and citizenship respectively. The infusion of new residents and citizens will bring greater diversity of ideas, experiences and skills to Singapore. This will make for a more vibrant and robust society, one that is able to spot and seize opportunities in a time of rapid change and intense competition.

 

Engaging overseas Singaporeans to foster an inclusive society

 

     Besides augmenting Singapore’s population, the NPC also seeks to foster an inclusive society by helping Singaporeans working and studying overseas to stay connected with one another and with Singapore. The Overseas Singaporean Unit (OSU) was launched in March 2006 for this purpose. Since then, it has held a number of introductory roadshows to reach out to overseas Singaporeans in major cities such as London, New York, Beijing and Sydney, to seek feedback on the services and information Overseas Singaporeans wish to receive from Singapore. Underpinning this effort is the Government’s belief that every Singaporean matters. The OSU’s vision is to facilitate the development of an interconnected Singaporean diaspora with Singapore the global city at the heart of this vibrant and diverse network.


 

     In August 2006, the OSU launched a website, www.overseassingaporean.sg, which serves as a one-stop information, e-services and community-building portal for Overseas Singaporeans. For Singaporeans who wish to return, they will be able to find links to useful information such as job and internship opportunities in Singapore and the region, and the latest school admission policies for their children.


    The OSU has also been working closely with the private sector to better engage Overseas Singaporean students and professionals. To provide Overseas Singaporeans with first-hand information on the economic opportunities in Singapore and the region, the OSU has held dialogues and networking sessions bringing together corporate leaders, professionals and students, and set up internship and job-link programmes. At the community level, the OSU also encourages and facilitates the various Overseas Singaporean associations, clubs and organisations to network and link up with each other.

Future direction

 

     Going forward, the NPC, supported by its full-time Secretariat, will continue to find ways to augment our population stock while engaging Overseas Singaporeans and work towards a Singaporean society that is not limited by geographical bounds.

 

 

PRIME MINISTER'S OFFICE (NATIONAL RESEARCH FOUNDATION)

 

MR LIM HNG KIANG
Minister for Trade and Industry

 

     The National Research Foundation (NRF) was set up as a department under the Prime Minister’s Office on 1st January 2006 to coordinate the research activities of different agencies within the larger national framework and to fund strategic R&D initiatives that would generate economic benefits for Singapore.  The NRF has also appointed a Scientific Advisory Board comprising  renowned scientists, researchers and entrepreneurs, who advise the NRF on key areas of R&D.

   

     The Government has approved a funding stream of S$5 billion to enable the NRF to undertake its work from 2006 to 2010.  The sustained funding is a strong signal of the Government’s commitment to invest in R&D as a driver for economic growth and as a foundation for our long-term competitiveness to ensure that Singapore maintains our edge in an increasingly knowledge-based and innovation-driven economy.

Seize opportunities and foster growth

 

     The NRF’s aim is to fund areas where there is most potential for R&D breakthroughs that will yield economic benefit for Singapore, by generating industry growth and enterprise creation.  The Research, Innovation and Enterprise Council (RIEC), chaired by the Prime Minister, has approved the allocation of S$1.4 billion from the National Research Fund to fund three strategic programmes - Environmental and Water Technologies, Interactive and Digital Media and Biomedical Sciences Phase II.   The development of these three programmes is targeted to create 86,000 jobs and S$30 billion of value added by 2015.  The NRF will continue to explore other emerging areas beyond these three programmes, and also look at long-term technology, social and demographic trends to identify new strategic areas for R&D investment.  

 

Build a vibrant global city


     To support Singapore’s ambition to become a vibrant, global city, the NRF aims to transform Singapore into a talent magnet for scientific and innovation excellence and build up a concentration of research talent to foster innovation and creativity. It is also important for the NRF to establish strong linkages with global institutions to enhance Singapore’s connectivity to other centres of research in the US and Europe, and accelerate our thrust towards an inventive, innovative and entrepreneurial economy. The RIEC thus endorsed the concept to establish a Campus for Research Excellence and Technological Enterprise (CREATE) in Singapore, and the proposal to set up the Singapore-MIT Alliance for Research and Technology (SMART) Centre as the first centre within the Campus.    

     The SMART Centre will be the largest international research endeavour that MIT has ever established.  CREATE will also foster joint research programmes between the world’s top research universities and Singapore-based research institutions, and recruit young graduate and postdoctoral talent to nurture a cadre of extraordinary scientists, whose discoveries and innovations could generate new growth engines for Singapore.
