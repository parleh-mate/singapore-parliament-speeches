MediShield for the Elderly

Mr David Ong (Jurong) : Mr Chairman, Sir, in our many helping hands approach supported by the 3Ms of healthcare financing and our family as the cornerstone of elderly care, the presence of MediShield has been a source of much comfort and support for Singaporeans. MediShield for Congenital Diseases

Dr Janil Puthucheary (Pasir Ris-Punggol) : Mr Chairman, I declare my interest as a paediatrician working in a restructured hospital. The issue of MediShield for children with congenital diseases has been brought up in this House in previous Parliaments as well as in this debate. I would just like to add my voice to the chorus. Sir, the problems faced by these children and their families are significant. But many of the diseases can be treated such that these children can lead long, healthy and normal lives.

The current regulatory framework lumps together all congenital diseases, when in truth there are a significant number of conditions that can be treated in a straightforward manner with a reasonable prediction of cure and a normal life, and a relatively short hospital stay. If part of the current policy position for excluding these children is that these children are expected to not survive, then the policy has not kept up with advances in medical science.

Let me offer some examples. A ventricular septal defect, a hole-in-the-heart, has about a 95% chance of complete cure and an expectation of a long and healthy life. In contrast, a child born with a hypoplastic left heart has the odds stacked against him. The current policy pushes these two together. Let me move to an alternative example. An adult, however, who suffers a significant heart attack has about a 80% chance of survival. In contrast, another adult who has a ruptured aortic aneurysm has less than 30% chance of getting out of hospital alive. Both of those conditions are currently claimable under MediShield. So, the policy pushes those two conditions together.

This subject has been brought up in the House previously, and from previous replies, the main concern is one of risk-pooling and the effect on premiums. This is true if one treats all congenital diseases as being equal, and I would suggest that the assumptions underlying that position are based on out-dated science and medical knowledge.

I would like to suggest that a couple be allowed to apply for MediShield for their unborn child. And as part of that application, there should be a requirement for high quality antenatal screening, which can detect most of these conditions in time for appropriate preparation and counselling, with the final decision about being allowed to have MediShield being made after the child has been born. These criteria need not be subjective and can be independently verified.

Would the Minister consider extending the use of MediShield to cover children with congenital disorders? Would the Ministry consider examining the position that categorises all congenital diseases together?

Strengthening of Primary Healthcare
