    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Mr Speaker, Sir, this Bill and the following Bill which I will be moving for Second Reading concern certain esoteric points in the law of conveyancing.

    As regards the Land Titles Ordinance, I would like to remind the House that it was first passed in 1956 and it was not brought into operation until 1st December, 1960. As from that date, under section 8 of the Ordinance, all land alienated by the Crown will be brought under the provisions of the Ordinance and certificates of title will be issued in lieu of Crown instruments. However, since 1956, there have been a number of surrenders by land developers under section 11 of the Crown Lands Ordinance for the purpose of sub-dividing their land into smaller lots for building purposes. Some 1,200 lots in the case of the Sembawang Hill Estate and another 1,000 odd lots in the case of the Serangoon Garden Estate have already been sub-divided. In both these cases, Crown grants have not yet been issued for each subdivided lot as time is taken for the survey of the land to be completed. However, in the meantime, buildings which have been erected on the lots have been sold and most of these properties are under mortgage. Under section 8 of the Land Titles Ordinance, there is no provision for the registration of mortgages or other encumbrances created before the issue of the Crown grants. The purpose of the present Bill is to provide the machinery whereby these mortgages or other encumbrances can be protected by notification on the land register kept in the Land Titles Registry.

    The new section 8A. (b) [clause 2] of the Bill puts the responsibility of informing the Registrar of the existence of any mortgage or other encumbrance on the person interested, and paragraph (c) enables the purchaser of the mortgaged property to be registered as proprietor without an order of the Court. This provision is similar to section 13 (6) of the Ordinance.

CROWN LANDS (AMENDMENT) BILL

    The Prime Minister (Mr Lee Kuan Yew): Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Section 8 of the Land Titles Ordinance States that after the commencement of the Ordinance any person who has become entitled to land upon alienation by the Crown for an estate in fee simple or for an estate in perpetuity or for a leasehold term of not less than 21 years, can require the Collector of Land Revenue to furnish to the Registrar of Titles a certificate to that effect. The Registrar shall thereupon bring the land under the provisions of the Ordinance as therein directed, and shall deliver the triplicate certificate of title to the person entitled thereto. This complicated provision really means that the Collector of Land Revenue is under a duty to give a certificate to the Registrar of Land Titles who then registers it. But in the case of a statutory land grant issued under section 4 (1) of the Crown Lands Ordinance, the Collector of Land Revenue will be faced with this difficulty. Under section 2 of the Ordinance, a statutory land grant is defined as a grant in perpetuity, but in Schedule A, the Form provides for an estate in fee simple by virtue of the

Column: 1192

words of limitation on "his heirs and assigns for ever". This means, Mr Speaker, Sir, that the Collector will either have to follow the words of the Form, in which case it will be contrary to section 2; or if he goes according to section 2, he will not be going in accordance with the Form and Schedule A. This anomaly has existed since the original Crown Lands Ordinance was passed in 1886. I do not wish now, Mr Speaker, Sir, to labour the House with the intricacies of the difference between a grant and a fee simple and "his heirs and assigns for ever". For the peace of mind of conveyancers and the Registrar of Land Titles, it is now proposed to remove this anomaly so that the Collector of Land Revenue can certify with certainty that the person to whom Crown land is being alienated is entitled to a grant in perpetuity.

DIPHTHERIA IMMUNISATION BILL


    Order for Second Reading read.

3.08 p.m.

    The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Health (Dr Sheng Nam Chin): Mr Speaker, Sir, -

    Mr Speaker: Will a Minister signify the recommendation of the Yang di-Pertuan Negara?

    The Minister for Home Affairs (Mr Ong Pang Boon): I so signify.

    Dr Sheng Nam Chin: Mr Speaker, Sir, I beg to move, "That the Bill be now read a Second time."

    Diphtheria is a serious health problem in Singapore and some 600 cases of the disease occur annually with over 50 to 60 deaths. Both the occurence of the disease and deaths are preventable by

Column: 1195

immunisation, and this Bill is designed to improve preventive measures by making immunisation compulsory for infants and children.

    Since 1938 in the City area and after the Second World War in the rural area, it has been the policy to encourage parents to accept inoculation against diphtheria on a voluntary basis. Unfortunately, the response has not been as encouraging as expected. Only approximately 50 per cent of the new-born every year obtained immunisation by the voluntary method. For the effective eradication or reduction of diphtheria, it is necessary that 90 per cent of the children should be immunised. Thus, it is considered to be appropriate at his stage to require compulsory immunisation of all infants and children by primary and booster diphtheria inoculation as defined in clause 2 of the Bill.

    Compulsory immunisation by legislation is neither new nor unorthodox. Children are required to be immunised against diphtheria in such countries as Japan, Canada, Germany, France, the U.S.S.R. and Yugoslavia, and it has been the experience of these countries that such compulsory immunisation sharply decreases the incidence of the disease.

    In the main immunisation will be effected by requiring every child to be administered with primary diphtheria inoculation within 12 months of birth. Alternatively, every child resident in Singapore, who is under the age of seven years and who has not been immunised against diphtheria, will be required to do so under clause 5 of the Bill. Booster diphtheria inoculation, which means a further inoculation against diphtheria for a person who has received a previous inoculation, will be made compulsory for every child residing in Singapore on attaining the age of four years. Provision is also made for a child to obtain a second booster inoculation upon entry to Primary School. Clause 6 refers to booster inoculation.

    Clauses 3 and 4 of the Bill are to ensure that inoculation and intra-dermal test (to test the susceptibility of a person against diphtheria) shall be carried out by qualified persons. Also notification of
