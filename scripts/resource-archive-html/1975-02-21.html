PRESIDENT'S SPEECH


8.01 p.m.

    The President (Dr Benjamin Henry Sheares): Honourable Members, there will be much to keep Honourable Members busy in 1975. We started 1975 with the United States and Western Europe easing credit to avoid more un employment and deepening recession. Japan will probably do likewise later in the year. Singapore is likely to reverse the downtrend in her economy. Our economy will probably do better in the second half of this year than in the first half.

    We have not fared too badly last year, with real growth around 7%. Two rough indicators are usage of our harbour and airport. General and bulk cargo, excluding bunker fuel, handled by the Port of Singapore Authority increased in volume by 4%. Aircraft arrivals and departures increased by less than 1% in 1974. But passengers increased by 9%. Thus the world and Singapore adjusted to higher fuel prices and higher fares.

    Singaporeans cannot expect to enjoy real growth rates of 10-12% which lasted for over five years up to 1973. The Japanese, who achieved 12-15% growth for the decade before the energy crisis, now plan for a 4~% growth rate. If our growth rates are between 5% and 7%, it is because we have a lower base from which to climb up.

    Provided there is no war and another oil embargo or other major catastrophe, Singaporeans will adjust to slower

Column: 6

growth rates. This is one of the advantages of a relatively young population.

    Our birth rates continued to fall in 1974. Experts had predicted more births because of more young people entering the fertility age group. But, in fact, total births declined in 1974 to 44,000 from 48,000 in 1973.

    On the other hand, the problems of the old will grow more difficult as young families leave out their aged parents when they move into flats. Already, over one million people, or more than 45%, live in Housing and Development Board estates. Last year, 26,000 homes were completed. My Government will take measures to make it more attractive for young couples to have their parents live with them. If nothing else, the grandparents help in bringing up the children whilst father and mother are at work.

    A more pressing problem is travelling at peak hours. Once we live in high-rise and work in high-rise, we have increased the density of traffic between the home and office. And when so many. want to get into the Central Business District at the same time, then traffic jams at peak hours are. inevitable. Fringe car parking will have to be made to work, to free the roads in the Central Business District for more efficient use by public passenger vehicles. Peak hour travelling times and the charges for entry will be adjusted to give maximum room for public passenger vehicles. The bus service is improving. More improvement can be made in another three to five years. In the late 1980's the beginnings of a Mass Rapid Transit may lessen this congestion.

    One beneficial side effect of the energy crisis is a slowdown in the number of new cars registered. However, within a few years, car manufacturers will design lighter cars with more economic engines, cheaper to buy and cheaper to run. So it is necessary to be vigilant and to adjust road and petrol taxes to prevent our roads from over-congestion.

    Another problem Honourable Members will have to tackle is the growing

Column: 7

crime rates. High crime rates used to correspond to high unemployment rates. This is no longer true. A generation is growing up that does not know what it is to be unemployed. They get jobs easily, despite the slowdown in world trade and in Singapore's economy. They want the good things in life, but without working to pay for them. On the other hand, the police force is undermanned. My Government will introduce full-time National Service for the police force. In this way, the manpower shortage will be lessened. We shall also have people in the reserve police service living all over Singapore. They will enlarge our law enforcing capacity. We must catch criminals and punish them. More severe punishment must be meted out, most of all to drug traffickers on whom any mercy is misplaced. We must make Singapore a safe and orderly place to live in. And this we can achieve because we have a people responsive to their collective needs, a public service and police force not tainted by corruption, and a Government prepared to take action before things get out of hand.

    The primary duty of government is to establish a society in which everyone has an equal chance to live, learn, train,

Column: 8

work and earn to the best of his or her ability. We shall complete 16 years of representative government this June. Our population growth went down from 4.0% in 1959 to 1.5% in 1974. On the other hand, our external reserves have grown 20 times, from a mere $336 million in June 1959 to over $6+ billion on 1st January, 1975. This could never have been achieved without self-discipline, hard work, thrift and enterprise.

    Honourable Members, I am confident that in your deliberations, you will not be swayed by short-term political considerations, and that, instead, you will be guided by the long-term interests of all our people.

MINISTRY OF FINANCE


    The primary aim of economic and financial policy is to counter the effects of the current world recession and to prepare ourselves for economic recovery. Provided that we use our resources intelligently, we shall continue to achieve relatively high rates of growth.

    We will offer an attractive package of fiscal incentives for new types of industries such as machine tools, precision instruments and petrochemicals. Pioneer industry tax exemption will be extended from five to ten years for the most deserving industries. Not only do they require heavy investments in plant and machinery; they also need highly skilled workers. Our workforce must be better educated and better trained.

    We will help existing industries to improve and expand. At the same time, we will not overlook other sectors of the economy such as construction, trade, tourism, transportation and other service industries.

    Spare construction capacity and labour now available will be used to expand our physical assets. Large amounts of capital will be required to expand wharves, industrial sites, telecommunications and airport facilities.

    Such investments have to be financed by public funds. A sizeable contribution averaging $500 million annually is being made from the Main Budget to the Development Budget. But with a less buoyant economy, existing tax revenues may not be sufficient to meet capital needs. We may therefore have to draw upon the foreign exchange reserves we have built up. These fortunately are considerable and should be more than adequate.

    The Monetary Authority of Singapore will continue to deal with the problems of a disordered international monetary system. It will supervise and regulate banks and finance companies more

Column: 10

sensitively. For more efficient management of our reserves, the Monetary Authority of Singapore and the Board of Commissioners of Currency will be merged into a central bank with powers of currency issue. The 100% backing in gold and foreign currencies of the Singapore Dollar will be maintained.

MINISTRY OF DEFENCE


    The defence build-up is levelling off. Further improvement in our defence capacity will be achieved not by greater numbers but by higher quality. The Singapore Armed Forces will therefore concentrate on improving its training programmes, methods of instruction, logistics administration and methods of utilising manpower.

    Wherever possible in non-combat vocations, national servicemen will be posted to work which they intend to do after leaving the Army. Special units have been established in Mindef to plan the career development of regular officers and NCOs. It is gratifying that growing numbers of young Singaporeans have come to regard service as regular officers or NCOs as a rewarding and respected career.

MINISTRY OF FOREIGN AFFAIRS


    Our foreign policy must take into consideration the new situation arising from the gradual scaling down of American and Western military presence in the region. The overwhelming Western influence in the region has been correspondingly reduced.

    This will lead other powers to seek greater influence for themselves in the area. It will be important to cooperate with our neighbours to safeguard our common interests. ASEAN can play an important role in lessening the leverage the great powers can otherwise have in exploiting differences and conflicts within the region. It can also quicken

Column: 11

the pace of regional co-operation and economic development. This in turn will strengthen stability and peace in the area. ASEAN can help member states adjust to the rapid geo-political changes in the region as the consequences of American withdrawal from direct involvement with the problems of Indochina work themselves through, and a regional balance is established without any great power hegemony. ASEAN will soon have a permanent Secretariat which will coordinate the many regional economic, social and cultural projects being implemented.

    With the growing wealth from their oil resources, the countries of the Gulf have clearly increased in importance for trade and investments. A Trade Mission was sent to West Asia and an official Economic Mission is proposed for Iran. Arrangements are also being discussed for a Singapore-Iran Joint Economic Commission.

    Another area where trade can be developed to the benefit of both parties is Eastern Europe. Relations between Governments there and Singapore have been very friendly and it is proposed there be more exchanges of trade and economic missions with these countries.

    Sea communications are vital to Singapore. Our interests lie in the maintenance of existing rights of passage through all major waterways, especially the Straits of Malacca and Singapore. We support all changes which will facilitate speedier and increased communication to improve world trade. Fishermen from Singapore should continue to enjoy the right to fish in Southeast Asian waters in the same way as do fishermen of other countries. Singapore therefore favours the setting of internationally acceptable standards which will take fair account of the needs of countries like Singapore.

MINISTRY OF HOME AFFAIRS


The Police Force

    The Police Force will use new techniques to deal with the rising crime rate and new patterns of crime. A

Column: 12

centralised Criminal Intelligence Unit has been established to improve criminal investigation. Officers will be trained in "scene-of-crime" procedures and the use of sophisticated equipment.

    Team policing which enables patrolmen to maintain radio contact with their patrol cars and move swiftly was successfully tested in two Police Divisions. It will be extended to the other six Divisions.

    To meet the manpower shortage in the Force more civilians and women police officers will be used. Full-time National Servicemen will be taken into the Force. Work methods will be reviewed to save manpower.

    The Traffic Police will implement the Points Demerit System and Ticketing System soon. Under the Points Demerit System, drivers who commit serious traffic offences will be awarded points. Those who accumulate 12 points or more within 12 months will have their licences suspended for up to 6 months. The Ticketing System will enable the Traffic Police to issue summonses on the spot for certain offences. Both these systems have been computerised.

    The Police will require the support of the public to be fully effective. Thus nationwide educational campaigns such as last year's "Keep Singapore Crime-Free" and "Keep Singapore Accident-Free" campaigns will continue to be organised from time to time.

    To cater for shifts of population to new towns and housing estates and to replace dilapidated Divisional Police Stations, four new Stations will be built in Toa Payoh, Paya Lebar, Orchard Road and Jurong Town. Construction is expected to start in the next financial year.

Vigilante Corps

    The Vigilante Corps has grown not only in numbers but also in its usefulness to the community. Apart from its civil defence role, it has also established the Community Security Force to help the regular Police in crime prevention.

Prisons Department

Immigration Department

MINISTRY OF EDUCATION


    The education system will be restructured by stages with the objective of reducing educational wastage, improving teaching and learning, and increasing the supply of trained manpower. The new education system will have the following features:

    (1) Primary education will be extended to seven years, with provision for pupils who make good progress to complete it in six years.

Column: 14



    (2) The Primary School Leaving Examination will be a grading exercise instead of a pass-or-fail examination. Every pupil who completes primary education will proceed to post-primary education and training of a type best suited to his aptitude and ability.

    (3) The secondary curriculum will be diversified to include a variety of vocational training and apprenticeship schemes.

    To provide the necessary supporting facilities for the restructured education system, the following measures will be implemented:

    (i) A new Education Act will be enacted.

    (ii) The building of new schools and junior colleges will be speeded up. Additional workshops and science and language laboratories will be built.

    (iii) The Industrial Training Board's capability will be strengthened. It will conduct vocational courses and implement apprenticeship schemes for secondary students.

    (iv) Teacher training will take into account the revised staffing needs under the new system. In-service courses will be conducted to familiarise principals and teachers with the philosophy of the new system.

    (v) Textbooks and other teaching materials will be produced to meet the requirements of the new system.

MINISTRY OF LABOUR


    Our ability to take into our stride the adverse effects of the ills afflicting the world economy depends on the continued tripartite co-operation of the Government, labour and management. The two most important factors are the discipline, hard work, efficiency and skill of our workers, and the enterprise and innovation of our entrepreneurs.

I   MODERNISATION


    We will continue to pursue our twin objectives of upgrading skills and increasing productivity. The National Productivity Board will assist those who are prepared to set up productivity committees. It will shortly convene the National Industrial Relations Committee to consider the issue of productivity and wages.

    The National Productivity Board will continue to assist small and medium industries to improve the expertise of their lower and middle management. It will also continue to help to improve the skills of their supervisory staff. To help local industries to upgrade their technology, the Board has established a Low Cost Automation Unit. It has also planned additional services in the fields of design engineering, metal trade engineering, quality control, preventive maintenance, management information system and electronic data processing.

II   INDUSTRIAL RELATIONS


    The effects of inflation and recession. the pursuit of modemisation of our economy and the strive for higher productivity can disrupt our present industrial peace and stability. To ensure that this will not happen, our tripartite structure of industrial relations has to be strengthened and the quality of industrial relations improved. This will call for positive personnel management and sound trade union policies. It will also require capable personnel managers and trade unionists. The Ministry has decided to set up an Industrial Relations Division with high level staff. It will provide conciliation services as well as preventive mediation to eliminate areas of industrial friction.

III   SAFETY, HEALTH AND WELFARE OF WORKERS


Safety and Health

    The Ministry is determined to raise standards of industrial safety and health especially in building construction, shipbuilding and ship-repairing. These have

Column: 16

the highest rates of industrial accidents. A special unit each has already been set up for these two industries. A Study Group on accident prevention in the shipbuilding and ship-repairing industry is expected to submit its report shortly. Industrial dermatitis and noise-induced deaf ness will be added to the list of diseases for which workmen's compensation is payable. The Factories Act will be amended to provide for more stringent safety measures to be observed for work in hazardous enclosed places. Enhanced penalties will be imposed where contraventions are likely to cause death or bodily injury.

Workmen's Compensation

    The Ministry will present to Parliament a new Workmen's Compensation Bill which will increase the quantum of compensation. Procedures will be simplified to ensure expeditious payment. The maximum compensation will be increased from $21,600 to $35,000 for a fatal case and from $28,800 to $45,000 in the case of permanent incapacity.

Placement

    The Ministry has set up a special retrenchment unit in August 1974 to assist retrenched workers to seek alternative employment.

Employment of Young Persons

    The recent Committee on Crime and Delinquency found that the requirement that young persons seeking employment in industry must obtain Certificates of Registration from the Commissioner for Labour has contributed to the delinquency of young school dropouts. The Ministry will amend the Employment Act to allow young persons between 14 and 16 years of age to work in industrial establishments without the prior approval of the Commissioner for Labour. Children between the ages of 12 and 14 will be allowed to work in industrial undertakings under approved apprenticeship schemes. However, the existing provisions on safety, health and welfare governing their employment will be re-enacted in separate regulations.

Population

    Compounding all these is our large and growing population. Population density is 3,664 persons per square kilometre, about the highest in the world. The number of women in the fertile age group 15 to 44 years is 529,200, double that in 1957. If each couple limits its children to two only, population growth will continue for another 50 years before it stops growing. By then the population will be about 4 million. We have to achieve our goal of two children per family. Otherwise the standards of our social services and environment must go down by sheer pressure of population and overcrowding.

    Fortunately, the birth rate continued to decrease last year to 19.8 per thousand. The rate of natural increase was 1.43% the lowest recorded rate since the War. The National Family Planning and Population Programme will be intensified, complemented by sterilizations and abortions.

Costs

    Costs of the medical services escalated from an annual increase of 5% in 1972 to 25% in 1974. This will continue to escalate even higher as prices of medicine, equipment and staff salaries follow inflationary trends and as new hospitals, outpatient and other medical facilities are added.

    The cost of these increases will be paid from taxes. Those who utilise the services will have to bear higher nominal charges.

MINISTRY OF NATIONAL DEVELOPMENT


The Construction Sector

    Oil crisis, inflation and economic recession adversely affected the construction industries. A two-pronged effort to counter this recessive trend is underway. Public works are being stepped up and measures are being implemented to stimulate private sector construction.

Public Housing

    The HDB completed a record of 26.000 units in 1974. One million Singaporeans (45% of the population) are now living in HDB estates. There is, however, a waiting list of 80,000 intending purchasers and 20,000 applicants to rent HDB flats. The HDB's building

Column: 24

programme will be further accelerated towards completing 30,000 units, and eventually 40,000 units per year. This year, new designs for 3 and 4-room flats (which are 10% larger) will be introduced.

Middle income Housing

    For our middle income citizens, ineligible for public housing and unable to afford the high prices of private dwellings, the Ministry, through its Housing and Urban Development Company, will build condominium housing for sale at prices within their reach. Some 4,200 housing units in nine residential areas will be constructed within the next two years. This programme will continue so long as demand continues.

Urban Renewal

    The Urban Redevelopment Authority was established on 1st April, 1974 to improve, through comprehensive planning and systematic redevelopment, the living and working environment in the central area. The 4th Sale of Sites was launched in April 1974 and a 5th Sale of Sites will take place this year to generate more private sector development.

Building Managetment

    The Ministry has set up a Building Management Unit under the Buildings and Common Property (Maintenance and Management) Act to ensure that all common areas and services of buildings such as corridors, car parks, fencing, lifts, etc. are properly maintained. In cases of default, the Unit will carry out all necessary maintenance work and charge the costs to the owner(s) who may be private individuals, corporations, Statutory Boards or institutions.

Reclamation

    Reclamation work along the east coast and west coast continues. The latest Marina Centre reclamation project which will create a large lagoon and sizeable areas for open space and recreation will also enable the construction of a highway linking Changi in the east to Jurong in the west, thus casing traffic congestion in the city centre.

Parks and Recreation

    A major park and beach recreation area - The East Coast Park - being developed on 500 acres of reclaimed land from Bedok to Tanjong Rhu, will have cycle tracks, footpaths, a swimming lagoon, sports facilities, camping sites, holiday chalets and food centres.     Singapore continues to be self-sufficient in fresh pork, eggs and poultry meats in spite of occasional shortages and high prices of imported raw materials. Work is in hand to resettle pig farms from Kranji to Ponggol.

Manpower
