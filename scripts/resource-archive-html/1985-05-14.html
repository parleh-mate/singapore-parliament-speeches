ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

LOW-INCOME FAMILIES
(Plans to improve their lot)


    1. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence what plans the Government has to improve the lot of families in our society whose gross income is less than $750.00 per month.

     The First Deputy Prime Minister and Minister for Defence (Mr Goh Chok Tong): Mr Deputy Speaker, Sir, the Member for Anson has asked how the Government intends to improve the lot of families in our society whose gross income is less than $750 per month. A family who earns $750 per month is not poor. It can afford to own its own 3-room HDB flat. The lot of such a family has improved as the fortunes of Singapore have risen.

    In his Address at the opening of Parliament, the President said:

    'My Government will pay particular attention to those who find it difficult to keep up with the pace of change.'

These are the older families earning less than $500 per month and whose capacity to earn more is limited. These are the families I would regard as the relatively poor in Singapore. They form about 12% of the population.

    Even here, we have made dramatic progress. In 1977, 475,000 persons earned less than $500 per month. In 1980, the number dropped to 400,000. In 1982, the figure dropped further to 306,000, a reduction of 36% in five years. This has been achieved through economic growth and education.

Column: 9



    Our economic growth has been rapid - from 1974 to 1984, it has been 7.9% per year. The expanding economy meant more jobs and better wages. Everyone could move up, provided he made the efforts. The incomes of the poor went up even faster than that of the general population. In the same decade, they increased nearly twice as quickly as incomes of the average wage earner: by 4.2 times as compared to 2.4 times, in real terms. Without growth, there would have been widespread poverty in Singapore. Our Government will therefore continue to target for economic growth in the years to come.

    Education is the other key to unlock families from poverty. The better educated and trained a person is, the more productive will be the job which he can do, and the more will he earn. The statistics show this clearly. Among the poor wage earners, fully 56% have less than PSLE qualifications, while among average and above average wage earners, only 32% have less than PSLE. This is why the Government has emphasized, and will continue to emphasize, education as a means of upgrading the next generation.

    Education is of course not only a matter for the schools and the teachers. It is also a matter for the homes and the parents. Poor families with many children will have great difficulty giving all of them adequate parental care and attention. Furthermore, each child will only have a small share of the limited resources in the family. As a result, the children's school work will suffer, and so will the chances of the family making good in the next generation. This is why our Government strongly encourages poorer families not to have more than two children. The $10,000 cash grant scheme is designed with this purpose in mind.

    The scheme also serves the purpose of helping poorer families own their own homes. The sum of $10,000 is more than adequate for a down-payment on a 3-room flat. Helping the poor to own their own homes is an important objective of our Government. Then they will not feel left out. Their sense of commitment and belonging to our society will increase. Their children will have a better environment to grow up in.

    Besides the cash grant scheme, the HDB is considering making it easier for the poor to buy flats. At present, applicants of HDB home ownership flats are required to pay a 20% down-payment at the time of application. The HDB is considering reducing the down-payment to 5% for tenants of HDB rental blocks, if they buy a 4-room Simplified or smaller flats. If the tenants wish to buy a 4-room Model A or bigger flats, the down-payment will be 10%, with another 10% payable upon allocation of flats. The HDB will grant loans to the buyers for the remaining sum. It will also increase the maximum repayment period, from the present limit of 20 years, to 25 years.

    Even with the easier repayment terms, the families will have to earn enough income to pay for the flats. One way to increase the family income is for both husband and wife to work. Many wives from poorer families presently do not work. They cannot afford to pay for child care services. To help them to do so, the Ministry of Community Development is embarking on a programme to provide more child care centres in housing estates at affordable prices.

HONGKONG AND MACAU WORKERS IN SINGAPORE


    3. Mr J.B. Jeyaretnam asked the Acting Minister for Labour the reasons for the Government's plans to encourage workers from Hongkong and Macau to come and settle down in Singapore.

     Mr Lee Yock Suan: It is Government's policy to encourage highly skilled workers to be recruited not only from Hongkong and Macau but also from other Asian countries and to offer such workers permanent residence if they are found suitable and can blend into our society.

    Unfortunately, not many Singaporeans are willing to acquire construction skills and make a career in the industry. It is therefore unavoidable that we have to rely on foreign workers to supplement our limited pool of local construction workers.

    Hongkong and Macau are good sources of highly-skilled workers for construction and other trades. With their wage levels higher than Singapore's, they will not depress Singapore wages as when we rely on workers from low-wage countries. We are aware that Singapore contractors are not enthusiastic over their wages.
