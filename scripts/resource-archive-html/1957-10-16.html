ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

ALIEN MUSICIANS AND BANDS


    Policy on entry and on protection of local musicians

    1. Mr Lee Kuan Yew asked the Minister for Labour and Welfare what is the present practice of the Government with regard to the entry of alien musicians and musical bands seeking employment in Singapore; and what is the Government's policy on the question of protecting local musicians from unemployment as a result of imported alien musicians.

    Dr Lim Yew Hock: Sir, the entry of alien musicians and musical bands seeking employment in Singapore is governed by the provisions of the Immigration (Prohibition of Entry) Order, 1953. Among the categories of persons set out in the Schedule to that Order who may be admitted into Singapore are persons with professional or specialist qualifications which would enable them to follow their profession or occupation in the Colony without prejudicing the interests of persons already resident in Singapore and possessing corresponding or similar qualifications.

    The present policy of the Government, Sir, designed to protect local musicians from unemployment as a result of imported alien musicians, allows for the admission of not more than two foreign musical bands into Singapore at any one time, subject to the local sponsor who is desirous of importing the foreign band engaging an equal number of local musicians to play with imported bands. The maximum stay in Singapore for each imported band is six months. This policy is at present under review in the light of the experience gained and of the Government's policy on the tourist trade.

    Mr Lee Kuan Yew: Will the Hon. the Minister inform us whether the review is intended to widen or to narrow the limits of entry of alien musicians?

    Dr Lim Yew Hock: It is intended that the number fixed at present of two bands may be increased. One must not overlook the fact that a foreign band here means employment for local musicians.

POST OFFICE SAVINGS BANK INTEREST RATE


    Increase suggested to match higher Bank Rate and encourage savings

    10. Mr William Tan asked the Financial Secretary whether in view of the recent increase in the bank rates and in view of the necessity to encourage more savings he will take steps, if necessary by amending legislation, with the object of raising the rate of interest payable on deposits in the Post Office Savings Bank.

    Mr T. M. Hart: Sir, the matter has been considered but it has been decided that it would not be appropriate to change the rate of interest paid by the Post Office Savings Bank.

    The P.O. Savings Bank does not compete with commercial banks or borrowers appealing to investors in the stock market. It does not raise or lower its rate of interest on deposits in sympathy with movements in the stock exchange. It aims at giving people in the lower income group easy facilities for saving small amounts in circumstances of absolute security, and guarantees a rate of interest which remains steady and certain over the years and which is reasonable having regard to the security and the facilities afforded by the Bank. In this connection it is perhaps interesting to note that despite booms and depressions, disasters and victories, the rate of interest paid by the P.O. Savings Bank in the United Kingdom has remained at 2 per cent since 1864; while the rate of interest paid by the P.O. Savings Bank in the United States of America has remained at 2 per cent since 1910.

    It is open to doubt whether the P.O. Savings Bank could in fact raise the rate of interest at the present time even if it were regarded as politic to do so. As the hon., Member is no doubt aware; the P.O. Savings Bank is intended to be a self-supporting institution. Its deposits are required by law to be invested in trustee stocks, which yield a comparatively low rate of interest. Its income

Column: 2653

is derived solely from interest from these investments. From this income, it must meet its operating costs and pay the rate of interest on deposits, which is prescribed by law. It should also, if circumstances permit, build up a reserve against depreciation of investments. If its income is insufficient to meet running expenses and statutory interest charges, the deficit must, by law, be made good by Government. The financial position of the Bank at the present time has been examined. Indications clearly are that if the rate of interest were to be now raised to 3 per cent - which would still be low compared with the yields obtainable elsewhere under stable and secure investment - the Bank would find difficulty in meeting its running expenses and interest charges, and the deficit would have to be met from the general revenue by Government. Such a situation would not, I am sure, be in accordance with the wishes of the hon. Member.
