ORAL ANSWERS TO QUESTIONS

TERRORIST ATTACKS AGAINST USA
(Impact on Singapore economy)


    1. Mrs Lim Hwee Hua asked the Minister for Trade and Industry whether there will be an adverse impact from the unfortunate bombing incidents in the United States of America on Singapore's economic prospects and, if so, whether the Government will consider implementing further measures to help Singaporeans and businesses cope with the worsening downturn.

    2. Mr Tay Beng Chuan asked the Minister for Trade and Industry, in view of the recent devastating terrorist attacks against the United States of America, whether his Ministry can (i) provide statistics regarding the extent of their negative impact on the Singapore economy; and (ii) introduce further measures to help the Singapore economy should it be badly affected by the events.

    The Minister for Trade and Industry (BG George Yong-Boon Yeo): Mr Speaker, Sir, with your permission, I would like to take Question Nos. 1 and 2 together.

    Mr Speaker: Yes.

    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: The recent terrorist attacks against the US are bound to impact the global economy. It has already caused significant disruptions to the US economy. The financial, insurance and airline industries are badly hit, although the interest rate cuts by the US Federal Reserve and the European Central Bank have helped to cushion the shock to financial markets.

    Prior to this event, there were some indications that the US economy might be close to bottoming out, but the September 11 attacks have diminished markedly the prospects of an early recovery. Consumer confidence and spending are expected to decline, increasing the risk of a US and a global recession significantly. The

Column: 2048

Singapore economy will be adversely affected.

    In Singapore, the immediate impact of the attacks was felt by our financial markets. The Straits Times Industrial Index fell by 7.3% while the Singapore dollar rose by 1.1% against the US dollar on 12th September as investors sold off the US dollar and stocks affected by the attacks. Air and sea transport was held up temporarily when the US closed its airports following the attack. Our export-oriented sectors like manufacturing, tourism and transport services will slow further as a result of weaker US demand in the short-term.

    The situation is still fluid and will depend on how events unfold in the coming months. The chain of events set off by the terrorist attacks has not yet fully played out, as the US considers its response, and as terrorist groups undoubtedly plan further moves. But the events last week have already changed the world. There will be greater uncertainties, and all economic forecasts will have to be reviewed. What is clear is that an economic recovery in the short term is unlikely. At this point, we expect our overall economic growth this year to become negative. Singaporeans must therefore be prepared for a more prolonged downturn.

    Over the last few months, the Government has been working on a second package of additional measures which might be needed to help ease the burden on companies and Singaporeans. The World Trade Centre attacks have increased the urgency of the second package and the scale of measures required. We are still finalising the package. The Finance Minister will address the question on the timing of the package in his reply.

    Mr Hawazi Daipi (Sembawang): Sir, in a worst case scenario, how many jobs does the Minister expect to be lost as companies are directly affected by the downturn?

    Prof. Jayakumar: Sir, let me first sketch the background to the recent positive developments in our discussions on the bilateral package. Senior Minister visited Kuala Lumpur last year in August, with Prime Minister Goh's agreement, to try and narrow the differences over the outstanding bilateral issues. Following discussions with Dr Mahathir, Senior Minister had written to Tun Daim regarding Singapore's proposals.

    The next step came, in February this year, when Malaysian Deputy Prime Minister Abdullah Badawi visited Singapore and discussed with Prime Minister Goh the package of outstanding issues. At this meeting, Prime Minister Goh agreed in-principle to Malaysia's request to move their KTM railway station from Tanjong Pagar to Kranji, and to have the KTM trains run in an underground tunnel from Johor to Kranji, as part of the larger package of issues. As Senior Minister has made clear in his comprehensive press conferences in KL, there is now accord on the basic skeletal or framework agreement, which would form the basis to resolve the outstanding issues.

    This framework agreement, firstly, spells out the elements of the package; secondly, indicates the areas where there is agreement on both sides; and thirdly, sets out areas where further discussions are needed to close the gaps.
