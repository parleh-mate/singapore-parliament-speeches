BUKIT HO SWEE FIRE


2.31 p.m.

    The Minister for National Development (Mr Tan Kia Gan): Mr Speaker, Sir, I have His Excellency the Yang di-Pertuan Negara's recommendation to proceed with the motion* standing in my name.


    *The motion reads as follows:

    That this House expresses its profound sympathy for the victims of the fire at Bukit Ho Swee and calls upon the people to donate generously to the Bukit Ho Swee Fire Relief Fund;

    That to prevent a recurrence of such disastrous fires the site of the fire be acquired by the Government for the Housing and Development Board for rebuilding, and to this end that no one be allowed to rebuild on this site structures of a temporary or permanent nature;

    That compensation to private owners for the land to be acquired should be at the value of the land in its encumbered state just before the fire, and that legislation to this end be passed;

    That the Housing and Development Board regulations relating to the prohibition of sub-letting or sharing of accomodation be relaxed in the case of these fire victims in order that rapid re-housing will be possible;

    That this House thanks the fire fighting services, the Police and Military Forces for their work in combating the fire and maintaining order on that occasion and thanks the workers in the Government Social Services and the Voluntary organisations who have helped in the relief of the fire victims.


    The Bukit Ho Swee fire took place on the afternoon of the 25th May, 1961. It was without doubt the worst that Singapore has ever experienced since the war. It devastated an area of some 100 acres and rendered homeless, according to the latest count, a total of 2,833 families - comprising some 15,694 persons, most of whom lost practically all their possessions. Hon. Members of the House have read in the Press of the immediate measures which have been taken by the Government to provide interim relief for the fire victims. They are now housed

Column: 1566

temporarily in five school buildings in the Kim Seng Road area, where voluntary workers, the Government Social Welfare Service, the Army and the Work Brigade have done all they can to relieve the sufferings of the victims and have provided them with food, clothing and blankets.

    The Government has also set up the Bukit Ho Swee Fire Relief Committee under the chairmanship of the Minister for Labour and Law. Representatives from political parties, the Trades Union Congress, student bodies of Universities, the Polytechnic, the Chambers of Commerce, the Council of Social Services, rural and other organisations are serving in this Committee.

    Sir, lessons must be learned from the cause of this fire, and this lesson should strike home with full impact. For the third time since the war, Singapore has bitterly experienced disaster caused by fire. In 1958 the Kampong Koo Chye fire rendered 379 families with 2,050 persons homeless; in 1959 there was the Tiong Bahru fire which affected 1,016 families, comprising 5,200 people; and now the most tragic of them all, the Bukit Ho Swee fire which destroyed the homes of 2,833 families, involving nearly 15,694 people. The common feature about these fires is that they occur in already packed and unauthorised structures of wood and attap. The occupants in all these cases had, in fact, been living in veritable fire traps. If only one house catches fire, then within minutes a blazing inferno would engulf a whole community before the fire fighters could arrive.

Column: 1567



    The unstinted devotion to duty during the disaster of the fire fighting services, the Police and the Military was something to be proud of, but unfortunately They were to a considerable extent hindered by curious crowds who arrived on the scene while the fire was raging and almost choked up the roads, thus delaying the arrival of the fire engines. It is hoped that our citizens would be more civic-minded in future and make an effort to subdue their curiosity and stay away from calamities of this nature which are not really spectacles for enjoyment. I am happy to say that, on the other hand, the fire victims themselves behaved in an exemplary manner and showed no panic. Though the fire took four lives, it would surely have been more disastrous if the crowds of sightseers and spectators had gone out of control and panic had resulted.

    Let us not ignore the lessons of this catastrophe. The reasons for the existence of building by-laws, the need to enforce minimum building standards, and the basic requirements of proper planning should now be painfully clear. We must not be blind to such lessons. We should now understand why the Chief Building Surveyor's Department of my Ministry conducts its campaign against unauthorised structures, which are built in exactly similar conditions to the houses that went up so quickly in flames at Bukit Ho Swee on 25th May. Most of these unauthorised structures are put up by unscrupulous racketeers who live off the miseries of the poor. It is appreciated that the poor people are forced by circumstances to live in these huts because of the acute shortage of cheap housing in the State and that it will be some time before the building programmes of the Housing and Development Board can catch up with this shortage. But I should like to appeal to these people not to fall into the clutches of the racketeers who put up unauthorised buildings for rent or sale. They should never buy or rent any of these structures. They may not only be cheated, but may also become victims of fire.

    Any building to be put up should be duly authorised by the Building Surveyor's Department. The procedure for the grant of permits for temporary buildings has now been streamlined, and applications should now be dealt with more expeditiously. Government is firmly resolved to discourage the erection of wooden buildings with attap roofs, and in the congested city area to prohibit it altogether. Firm orders have been given to the Chief Building Surveyor to pull down any unauthorised structures as soon as they are spotted.

    The Government will not be discharging its responsibilities to the people if it did not clamp down on unauthorised buildings.

    Sir, it was indeed fortunate that the Bukit Ho Swee fire was brought under control and did not spread further. If circumstances had been adverse the fire could easily have gone out of control with much more disastrous consequences. A conflagration of this magnitude can easily engulf not only attap buildings, but also brick and concrete structures as well. Many brick and concrete buildings were, in fact, destroyed on 25th May, along Havelock Road. If attap and wooden structures were allowed to multiply in the city areas, the next fire might well destroy a considerable section of the city, with considerable loss of lives. I appeal to members of the public to report immediately to the Chief Building Surveyor's Department should they see any unauthorised attap and wooden structures being put up. At the moment, there is a large number of outstanding mandatory orders against unauthorised buildings. Many of these orders require alterations to the unauthorised structures before they can be regularised. Most of the houses, however, cannot be regularised, because they are likely to become dangerous fire traps. There has been a great deal of misunderstanding about the work of the Building Surveyor's Department, particularly when it takes action to demolish unauthorised buildings. But may I repeat that one of the main reasons for this unpleasant action is to save people from the dangers of fire traps, like those of Bukit Ho Swee?

    This Government will take firm measures to prevent the site of the Bukit Ho Swee fire from being reoccupied by unauthorised structures. The site is to be acquired for re-development by the Housing and Development Board. No doubt, hon. Members will support this proposal as well as the Bill that will be introduced to acquire the land. The project is one designed for the public good and the private owners concerned can be expected not to make a profit out of this disaster.

    Sir, the relief, re-housing and rehabilitation of the fire victims cannot be an easy matter when so many persons are involved. There are not sufficient numbers of vacant Housing and Development Board flats immediately available to re-house every family. Though the public and various Governments have been most generous with their gifts and donations in cash and kind, whatever relief that the fire victims can receive will only provide temporary rehabilitation until they can definitely find their own feet again.

    The Government has decided that all available housing units of the Housing Board should be made available for rehousing the fire victims. In this connection, I should like to appeal to those members of the public who have been on the waiting list for Housing Board accommodation to make a certain amount of sacrifice. There are some 13,000 applicants on the housing register and many of them have been waiting for three, four, or even five years. Just when their chances of being allocated accommodation have improved with the completion of more than 1,000 houses, they have to be told again that the fire victims will be given priority. The Government hopes that these applicants will not grudge the fire victims the special priority given to them. These applicants at least have some form of accommodation at present, while the fire victims have lost both homes and most of their possessions. The Housing and Development

    These flats will be offered to the fire victims at the rents normally charged to members of the public. It is not the intention to upset the very complex rent structure of the Housing and Development Board by introducing rent-free periods or by waiving rents. The Government, while doing all that is right and proper for the fire victims, must not lose sight of the necessity to account to the people for the expenditure of public funds entrusted to its charge. It is considered, therefore, that fire victims should pay the normal rentals charged for the accommodation offered. To assist fire victims further, it is proposed that the Housing Board regulations prohibiting the sub-letting of rooms in flats or the sharing of flats by more than one family be relaxed. The Housing and Development Board does not normally permit its tenants to sub-let any part of their flats, but in order that re-housing of fire victims can be accomplished expeditiously, the Board will be asked to permit fire victims to share accommodation with their friends or relatives now residing in any of the Board's estates. The Board will also accept two

small families of fire victims joining together to apply for a flat. The distribution will be on a per capita and per family basis and it is felt that this method will be the most equitable.

    All fire victim families will receive (a) a rent assistance grant of $7 per person up to a maximum of $35 per family. This sum will, in effect, operate as a rent subsidy, and will be paid for a period of three months; (b) a lump sum rehabilitation grant will be paid to these families; the sum to be paid will be dependent on the monies available in the relief fund after meeting other commitments.

    All families will receive these two grants, whether they accept Housing Board accommodation or not; those not requiring Housing Board accommodation may use their rent assistance grant for finding their own accommodation.
