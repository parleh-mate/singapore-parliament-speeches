MODIFIED STREAMING SYSTEM
(Fine-tuning)


    3. Mr Lew Syn Pau asked the Minister for Education whether there is a need to fine-tune the modified streaming system after three years of experience.

     The Minister for Education (Mr Lee Yock Suan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the first streaming exercise under the modified streaming system was carried out at the end of 1991. Pupils joined one of three streams (EM1, EM2 or EM3) at Primary 5 in 1992. These pupils sat the modified Primary School Leaving Examination (PSLE) last year.

    Under the present provision, pupils in all three streams follow the same syllabuses and take common PSLE papers in English language and mathematics. In practice, however, EM3 pupils, being the weakest, can only cope with the more basic aspects of the curriculum and answer the easier questions of the examination papers.

    My Ministry has reviewed the system. It is of the view that some fine-tuning of the curriculum and examination in English language and mathematics for the EM3 stream is needed to better suit the abilities of the pupils.

    The Ministry is modifying the syllabuses and writing separate textbooks and workbooks in these two subjects for pupils in the EM3 stream. Consequently, EM3 pupils will sit for separate papers, to be called Foundation English and Foundation Mathematics, at the PSLE.

TEACHERS

    4. Mr Lau Ping Sum asked the Minister for Education what steps will be taken to reduce the administrative workload of teachers, such as that of computer data entry.

EXPRESSWAYS
(Speed limit for light goods vehicles)


    5. Mr Cheo Chai Chen asked the Minister for Home Affairs whether he will consider allowing the expressway speed limit for light goods vehicles such as vans and pick-ups to be increased from 50 km/h to 70 km/h.

     The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Home Affairs (Assoc. Prof. Ho Peng Kee) (for the Minister for Home Affairs): Sir, the Public Works Department, together with the Registry of Vehicles and the Traffic Police, regularly reviews the speed limits of the various categories of vehicles on our roads. They have found no reason to change these limits.

    The present 50 km per hour speed limit for light goods vehicles is imposed for safety reasons. Light goods vehicles are heavier and usually longer than ordinary class 3 vehicles. Given their weight and size, the potential damage and danger caused by these vehicles in the event of an accident would be greater. They would also require a longer distance to stop. Both for the safety of the drivers as well as other roadusers, it is necessary to impose a lower speed limit on such vehicles.

    I would be cautious about increasing the speed limit for such vehicles. Research in the United States has shown that increasing the speed limit on the expressways would lead to more road casualties.

EMPLOYMENT OF CHILDREN AND YOUNG PERSONS


    2. Mr Cheo Chai Chen asked the Minister for Labour whether he will prohibit employers from employing students who are below 16 years old except where they have obtained consent from their parents and school principals.

     Dr Lee Boon Yang:

    Most employers in Singapore do not employ workers below the age of 16. A minority such as fast food restaurants and retailers are known to have employed some school children during their school vacation. For example, Burger King, a fast food establishment, requires written parental consent.

    The employment of persons who are below the age of 16, including school children, is governed by the Employment Act and the Employment of Children and Young Persons Regulations. There are special provisions to protect such young workers. For example, the Regulations stipulate that when a young person aged between 14 and less than 16 years is attending school, the period of work plus the period of school attendance shall not in aggregate exceed seven hours in any one day. Young persons are also not allowed to work in any occupation or place of work or under working conditions that are likely to expose them to safety or health hazards.

    Given the safeguards in our law and the fact that employers generally do obtain parental consent, my Ministry is of the view that there is no need to disallow employers from employing school children below the age of 16 unless the consent of their parents and school principals has been sought.
