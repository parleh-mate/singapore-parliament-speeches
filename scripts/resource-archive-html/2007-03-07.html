RAISING OF COMPULSORY RETIREMENT AGE

     1.  Dr Lily Neo asked the Minister for Manpower whether he will consider raising the compulsory retirement age and, if so, when will it be done.

    The Minister of State for Manpower (Mr Gan Kim Yong) (for the Minister for Manpower):  Mr Speaker, Sir, over the past two years, the employment rates of Singapore residents between the age groups of 55-59 and 60-64 have risen significantly.  For the 55 to 59 age group, it increased from 56% in 2004 to 61% in 2006.  For the 60 to 64 age group, it rose from 34% in 2004 to 42% in 2006.   The employment rates of older Singapore male residents in particular, at 78% for the 55 to 59 age group and 60% for the 60 to 64 age group in 2006, are higher than the OECD average and many developed countries, although we still trail Japan and Sweden.  This improvement has been mainly due to the good economic growth leading to expanded employment opportunities.  The efforts by the tripartite partners have also facilitated this improvement through various schemes and initiatives.

 

     We will continue to take this approach of growing the economy and working in conjunction with our tripartite partners to seek effective ways to raise the effective retirement age for older workers. The Tripartite Committee on Employability of Older Workers in its Interim Report last year recommended a more flexible model of legislation where employers are required to offer some form of re-employment to workers who reach the retirement age, although not necessarily the same job on the same terms.   Such a system was introduced in Japan in April 2006 and a tripartite study group visited Japan in October last year to study its experiences.  The Tripartite Committee is evaluating the results of their re-employment legislation system. This will help us determine if this form of legislation is suitable and effective for Singapore.

 

     Dr Lily Neo (Jalan Besar): May I ask the Minister of State whether now is an opportune time to raise the retirement age due to the number of jobs created last year, and is it not time now to get more older workers to join the workforce or delay retirement to contribute to the workforce when the labour is tight?

     Mr Gan Kim Yong:   Mr Speaker, as I explained in my speech just now, we are taking various measures to encourage older workers to continue to stay in the workforce.  We are also introducing a lot of initiatives to encourage employers to shift their mindset and to accept older workers in their workforce. We are studying the Japan's system of encouraging older workers to work.  So I think, at the moment, our focus is on practical ways to encourage older workers to remain in the workforce, rather than to look at the retirement age.

     Mr Speaker:  One last supplementary question.

     Dr Lily Neo:  Thank you, Mr Speaker.  May I ask the Minister of State how does MOM prepare for this ageing population and ageing workforce?  Are there new avenues for upgrading and employment of older workers?  How do you encourage employers to accept older workers?  The last question is: how do you promote lifelong learning and nurture lifelong employability?

     Mr Gan Kim Yong:  Mr Speaker, through our various schemes, especially under the Advantage! Scheme which was announced some time last year, we have come out with a suite of measures to encourage older workers to continue to upgrade themselves. We are also working with NTUC to carry out job redesign so that certain jobs which were not suitable for older workers will be made suitable for them.  We are also working with the employers' representatives, SNEF as well as SBF, to work with employer groups to change the work practices in the work places to make them more suitable for older workers and to also help employers change their mindset to be more receptive to older workers. Through all these measures, we will continue to encourage our workers to work longer in preparation for an ageing population and an ageing workforce.

 

     With regard to her question on lifelong learning, we will continue to pursue lifelong learning both through the WDA as well as the training institutions.

PUBLIC PRIVATE PARTNERSHIP SCHEME AND BEST SOURCING

     2. Mr Zaqy Mohamad asked the Prime Minister and Minister for Finance (a) how will the Government rate its Public Private Partnership Scheme and outsourcing projects to date; (b) what are the measures being used to measure the success of these initiatives; (c) which projects have provided good returns and which have notably fallen short of expectations beyond reasonable limits; and (d) are there any new models being considered for the near future.

     The Minister of State for Finance (Mrs Lim Hwee Hua) (for the Prime Minister and Minister for Finance): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Public Private Partnership (PPP) and Best Sourcing efforts in the Government are progressing well. 

 

     Best Sourcing is really about finding more cost-effective alternative service providers for non-strategic functions so that the public service resources can be directed at core functions. It is not about cost cutting or outsourcing or cheap sourcing. Since April 2004, the public sector has market-tested about 230 or 18% of its non-strategic functions.  70% of these functions were subsequently outsourced with an aggregate contract value of about $1.8 billion, yielding an annual operational cost savings of at least $34 million. The success of these initiatives can be measured by the extent of market-testing and cost savings, whilst maintaining service or performance standards. 

 

     Outsourcing of car park management by HDB and URA is a good example. Through the Best Sourcing exercise, HDB and URA expect annual savings of 20%, or $4 million per year.  Another recent example is the Ministry of Manpower’s award of a 3-year contract for the provision of integrated contact centre services (comprising a call centre, email handling and web services), which brought about significant improvements in the service levels.

 

     Best Sourcing is a complex exercise and agencies have to move at a pace which allows for changes without affecting the quality of service to the public, and without creating unnecessary anxiety and uncertainty for their staff.  Nonetheless, MOF will continue to encourage and help public agencies to undertake more Best Sourcing. 

 

     As for Public Private Partnership (PPP), we have only started them not too long ago.  So far, four PPP deals have been awarded - two by PUB for water treatment, one by NEA for incineration and another by Singapore Customs for TradeExchange.  The projects are not yet in full operation but we expect the benefits to be great as the PPP providers are able to optimise the whole lifecycle costing as it designs, builds, operates and maintains the facility.  The public sector will then buy services from the provider, instead of directly owning and operating these assets. 

 

     Sir, we are working on more PPP deals.  The Sports Hub by the Singapore Sports Council and the new ITE College West by ITE have just closed their tenders on 28th  February 2007.

 

     The public sector will consider the most appropriate method of procurement and will continue to explore new ways with the objective of always deriving the greatest value for money.  

 

     Mdm Halimah Yacob (Jurong):  Mr Speaker, I would like to ask the Minister of State how does the Government ensure that the Best Sourcing does not become cheap sourcing.  Are the contracts awarded to the lowest tender or is it based on outcome?  For instance, is there any auditing for the Ministries to determine whether CPF is paid and labour laws are complied for workers so that it is not cheap sourcing?

     Mrs Lim Hwee Hua:  Sir, the Government would take all steps to ensure that in any Best Sourcing initiative, minimum standards will continue to apply, in terms of service delivery to the public, and that shall be the baseline.  Therefore, it will not always be the cheapest tender, but it will be the cheapest subject to minimum standard being complied with.  In some cases, the status quo is actually the Best Source, and it remains so.  As regards the suppliers who are ultimately awarded, it is actually a slightly different issue.  We would certainly expect the suppliers to comply with all the standard labour laws.  And certainly, if the Member is aware of anyone who is not complying with them, the Member is welcome to direct this to the attention of the Ministry.

     Mr Zaqy Mohamad (Hong Kah):  What sort of changes in KPIs have the Government adopted or is considering adopting to take advantage of our Government sector flexibility and agility through these PPP's initiatives or even Best Sourcing initiatives?

     Mrs Lim Hwee Hua: Sir, currently, the KPIs evolve over time and the KPIs would, in most cases, be the minimum standard that Ministries would have to deliver.  Should the PPPs be successful, in terms of discovering greater technologies, then the KPIs would be amended accordingly.

     Resumption of Debate on Question [6th March, 2007],

 

     "That the total sum to be allocated for Head O of the Estimates be reduced by $100." - [Mdm Halimah Yacob].

 

     Question again proposed.

 

 

     The Minister for Health (Mr Khaw Boon Wan): Mr Chairman, Sir, I will continue with my reply yesterday which was interrupted. 

 

     Mdm Halimah's queries are related to two important subjects, ie, how best to subsidise the needy, and how to ensure that care for the rich does not affect care for the poor.  We do not apply the subsidy formula mechanically, eg, every Class C bill, subsidise 80% or Class B2 bill subsidise 65%.  This is because there are many ways to treat the same illness.  For instance, we promote generic substitution, ie, the use of generic medicine which is cheaper but effective, instead of branded goods.  This way, we save on healthcare costs.  But sometimes, some subsidised patients insist on getting the more expensive branded medicine.  We oblige, but we get them to pay more for the non-standard drugs or implants.  Otherwise, the subvention budget would be depleted by such subsidised patients who obviously can afford better care and are demanding more.  This will disadvantage the truly needy who accept the basic but adequate care that we prescribe.  And there is an added reason.  For very expensive care, such as in the intensive units, we actually subsidise much more than the standard 65% or 80%.  The cost of running ICU is very, very high.  And if we were to simply apply the standard subsidy rate, many such patients would not be able to cope.  This is the reason why subsidy rates differ from nursing homes to expensive acute hospitals and why we apply a lower subsidy rate for outpatients than inpatients. 

 

     After all the mathematics are done, what patients are really most concerned about is the dollar quantum they have to pay and not so much how many percent of the bill that they are paying.  This way, we better ensure that the needy can afford their healthcare. 

 

     As for rich foreign patients crowding out the poor, I am very alive to this concern.  The solution is to ramp up supply of doctors, nurses and bed, and this is what we are doing, as I outlined yesterday.  The solution is not to say we do not treat rich or foreign patients in public hospitals.  Remember that doctors are mobile.  While they are mindful of their public duties, they also want to treat a range of patients, rich and poor.  If we unduly restrict their practice, some will simply pack up and go, and the poor will then be denied of their expertise.  But I agree fully with Mdm Halimah that our accounting should be clear and that we do not end up with subsidised patients subsidising the rich patients by unwittingly shouldering part of the cost of private care.

 

 

     Ms Denise Phua Lay Peng (Jalan Besar): Sir, I refer to the Minister's vision of one Electronic Medical Record (EMR) for one Singaporean, and also for both public and private sector medical practitioners to share the information when the database is up.  Can the Minister comment on:

 

     (1)  How Singaporeans' medical records can be kept confidential under such a system?

 

     (2)  Whether each citizen is empowered with the decision to share or not his personal medical records, because it can be quite intrusive and, in fact, dangerous for such personal medical records to be go around if it is not protected.

 

 

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan: Sir, this is indeed a very important subject, and that is part of the reason why "one Singaporean, one EMR" will take some time to implement.  Therefore, we will have to do it gingerly and carefully.  Otherwise, once information starts going around in the virtual world, it is very troublesome.  All countries are grappling with this problem.  My approach is to take it step-by-step.  That is why we focus on public hospitals first, because it is easier for us to manage our own doctors and nurses.  Once we are confident - and we are reasonably confident - my next step is going down to step-down care, eg, nursing homes, VWOs, etc.

 

     The key point is that we will have to decide what are the critical data.  Some data are so sensitive that they will never be shared.  So it does not mean that "one Singapore, one EMR" means all the gory details are out in outer space.  It cannot be.  We have to decide.  For example, issues like HIV and sexual diseases, these are data that we keep highly confidential.  In any case, do not get the wrong impression that electronic medical records will worsen this problem.  This problem exists even in paper form.  When we run hospitals, we know that with medical records on paper, any hospital staff with a badge, can walk into the records office, in the name of checking out the medical records of their patients.  You never know what happens in the office.  Whereas now, with electronic medical records, we can actually audit them.  So, let us say, we have a staff who, for various reasons, checks into the medical records of all kinds of patients who are not his patients, in the paper world, it is very hard to audit.  But in an online world, it is so easy.  There are softwares that can pick up very quickly; if you have a hundred patients, why are you accessing 1,000 patients' records?  There is no reason for you to do so.  Unfortunately, it does happen.  The system is able to pick it up.  I remember there was a case where we have to terminate the service of the staff.

 

 

     Dr Lily Neo: Mr Chairman, Sir, may I ask the Minister how does he maintain the high standard of our medical personnel?  In the past, doctors were sent overseas for training under the HMDP scheme.  Are there better avenues and more funding presently?  Does he have any plan to scale greater heights of medical expertise and medical excellence?  The Minister said yesterday that MOH is getting hundreds of foreign graduates to join our Ministry.  Could I urge him to be mindful of the quality of doctors and not just the quantity?

 

 

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan: On both points raised by Dr Neo, I totally subscribe to her views.  One is that we must continue to train, because medical science continues to evolve and we cannot stand still.  HMDP, which is a programme that we created many years ago to send doctors overseas and to attach them to some of the best centres of the world, continues to expand, and we are pumping in more money each year.  More importantly, in recent years, we have extended it beyond doctors, because the medical system comprises - no doubt, doctors form a big part - also nurses and allied health workers who are very important.  Each year, when I give away such scholarships to our staff, I am glad to see that besides a large number of doctors, also large numbers of pharmacists, radiographers, etc, and we should continue this way.

 

      The second point is about the quality of intake of foreign doctors.  Yes, that is why we study the medical degrees very carefully before we put them on the approved schedule.  I have progressively expanded it.  My operating principle is a simple one:  all the top medical schools of  major countries ought to be on our approved list.  There is no reason to exclude them.  But it does not mean that once a degree is recognised, you can just come in and set up shop, because, first, we only give a conditional registration.  So when you come in, you must have an employer and, practically all the time, it is the public hospitals.  My point to the CEOs is this:  yes, you can have a recognised degree, but you never know until the fellow appears and works for a few weeks or months.  Please watch over him very carefully in the first few months because, whether it is medical competence, or more importantly, it is his ethos and values, and those are very hard to check on paper.  If we are too strict in taking them in, then we are missing out on opportunities.  So I would rather be a little bit loose in taking in, but be very prompt, firm and decisive in throwing them out.  If you find that the fellow is just not up to our ethical standards and competence, we will be the first one to throw him out.  So, yes, it is not chasing numbers.  Numbers are important, but quality as well because, at the end of the day, it is the Singapore brand of medicine which is at stake here.

      Finally, on the possibly different criteria in means testing used in nursing homes versus the homes run by MCYS, I do not have the details with me here.  But I would certainly take the Member's feedback and we would make a check of it, and if there is a need to synchronise and harmonise them, we will do so.

 

 

     Mrs Josephine Teo (Bishan-Toa Payoh): Mr Chairman, Sir, I would just like to thank the MOS for responding to my question on mental healthcare yesterday.  I was glad to note that mental healthcare patients are given proportionately more support through Medifund.  I would like to ask the question of whether the IMH has sufficient capacity to cater to demand because, from time to time, we hear of cases of mental healthcare patients who are discharged and have returned home where it would appear that they would be better off with longer term care at the IMH.

 

 

     Mr Heng Chee How: Mr Chairman, Sir, I thank the Member for her comment.  From the perspective of the Ministry, and certainly from IMH, supply adequacy will be a factor we will always bear in mind.  In other words, it is not a question of defining a certain supply and then, once you reach that, you push everybody out even though they may need inpatient care.  But neither do you want to then make every case an inpatient case unnecessarily, because the considerations must be the early re-integration of the person into wider society so that he or she might be able to function normally and live a full and fulfilling life.  So there is the medical aspect of this, ie, the assessment by the doctors as to what might be the right thing to do for an individual case, and this is also ongoing.  Certainly, as the population changes in profile, as it ages, and as more stress comes upon society, the demand patterns may shift and this is something that is also monitored by the Ministry as well as by IMH.

     Mr Khaw Boon Wan: Sir, I thank Mdm Halimah for her further elaboration.  I spent some time yesterday in my speech talking about the diverse needs of the patient and, therefore, the need for diversity.  From my point of view, that is a critical point.  You can have a very uniform system which was what I grew up with when I was a young officer starting my career in Singapore.  From the headquarters' point of view, it is the simplest - one circular and everybody follows, everybody wears the same uniform, etc.

 

     But I think that world is gone. And I gave some illustrations yesterday - different kinds of needs.  It is very hard for us to group them into simple boxes. For general policies, we have no choice but to simplify.  But reality requires us in each case, to try to be able to meet the unique needs.  That is why we restructured the hospitals and we have clusters, and we continue to evolve.  I do not see that the current structure will go on forever.  I am sure we will continue to evolve. Even during my term as Health Minister, I am quite sure that it will continue to evolve.

     The Special Education sector has a very diverse landscape.  It covers a wide spectrum of disabilities including learning difficulties, intellectual disability, sensory and physical disabilities.  It also has a wide range of varying degrees of disabilities.  Currently, there are about 4,000 children with mild to moderate special learning needs in our mainstream schools, and approximately 4,800 children with more severe special needs in 21 SPED schools.  Of these 4,800 children, about 500 of them currently take the mainstream curriculum in SPED schools.  These schools provide additional specialist and para-medical support to help the children access the mainstream curriculum.  The other schools cater to children with more severe disabilities, and focus more on programmes to help them develop skills for independent living as well as vocational skills where possible. 

 

    Ms Denise Phua, Dr Amy Khor and Mr Christopher de Souza have asked MOE to play a more proactive role in the education of children with disabilities.  MOE already oversees the overall educational policy, direction and operation of SPED schools.  We provide the majority share of development and recurrent funding.  We also provide professional support to SPED schools, including advice on mainstream curriculum and pedagogy, as well as some training courses.


     In September 2004, MOE announced a series of measures to enhance education support for children with special needs, both in SPED as well as mainstream schools.  Through these measures, we now have enough places in SPED schools to cater to all children with special needs, compared to a waiting list of 230 children in 2004.  The construction of customised school buildings for 19 SPED schools is largely on track.  A Steering Committee on Teaching and Learning was set up jointly by MOE and NCSS in 2005 to look into improving the quality of teaching and learning in SPED schools.  MOE has deployed 81 Special Needs Officers (SNOs) to 70 primary and secondary schools since 2005, and the number of SNOs is expected to grow to close to 240 by 2010.  These initiatives are being rolled out and their full effects will be seen in the years to come.  

  

     A SPED school requires a range of professional skills, some of which reside within the relevant voluntary welfare organisations (VWOs) who specialise in different disability areas.  There is also considerable diversity of needs across the SPED sector.  Therefore, as Mr de Souza recognised, no single agency, whether MOE or other agencies, is able to provide the holistic support needed by children with disabilities.  The needs of the children will be better served through a "Many Helping Hands" approach, involving partnership among various stakeholders in education, health and social services.  MOE has partnered some 13 VWOs to operate special schools that offer customised services catering to children with different disabilities.  Indeed, SPED schools like Pathlight School and Metta School are good examples of the success of this partnership approach, where the expertise, professionalism and commitment of the VWOs have made a real difference to the SPED schools.

     We have significantly improved the Special Needs sector over the last few years.  But I agree more can be done, and MOE is constantly looking at ways to further improve the quality of education for children with special needs.  The Steering Committee of the Enabling Masterplan 2007-2011 has released its report on 12th February which proposed many recommendations relating to education for children with special needs.

     Firstly, greater involvement in overseeing and supporting the management of SPED schools, especially on educational matters.   Secondly, we will provide a diverse range of educational opportunities for children with special educational needs. And thirdly, greater training and professional support for SPED schools to achieve excellence.


    I will elaborate on each of these thrusts in turn.

 

    Let me start with the first thrust, greater involvement in overseeing and supporting the management of SPED schools, especially on educational matters.  MOE intends to play a more active role within the current framework, but we will focus on areas where we have core competence in.

    Another area is in learning outcomes.  We want to encourage SPED schools and parents to set high but realistic expectations and help these children to realise their potential.  MOE will engage the SPED schools to help develop targets for the learning outcomes of their students.  Depending on the students, these could include 'O' or 'N' level passes, or pre-vocational certification or other measurable outcomes.


    Dr Amy Khor was concerned about the quality of special education.  In addition to defining learning outcomes, SPED schools will also need to show greater accountability for the quality of their education programmes.  MOE will work with NCSS to develop a quality assurance framework, and carry out management audits of SPED schools periodically. 

 

     I will now move on to the second thrust of providing a diverse range of educational opportunities for children with special needs.  

 

    Just as MOE is offering diversity and choice in our mainstream education system, we also recognise that a differentiated set of approaches is the best way to meet the wide range of special needs. We should avoid a binary approach of either placing all children with special needs in mainstream schools, or segregating them entirely in SPED schools.  Ms Denise Phua, Dr Amy Khor and Mr Christopher de Souza talked about integrating some children with special needs in the mainstream education environment, where such interactions would be beneficial to the learning outcomes of these children.

 

   I would like to reiterate that there are some 4,000 students with mild to moderate levels of special learning needs who are already in our mainstream schools.  Close to 60 mainstream schools have been retrofitted to facilitate access for children with physical disabilities.  There are also two designated schools for the hearing impaired and four for the visually impaired, catering to students who need specialised support.

    At the same time, some children with special needs who are currently pursuing the mainstream curriculum in SPED schools can benefit from greater interaction with other children in a mainstream school environment.  Since 2006, Pathlight School has piloted a satellite SPED class for its autistic students at Chong Boon Secondary School.  The Canossian School is housed in the same Eduplex as Canossa Convent Primary School.  It has also piloted a programme to send a small group of its hearing-impaired students to neighbouring MacPherson Primary School to attend some lessons there.  

 

     These experiments have worked well, especially in helping these students transit to a mainstream school eventually.  MOE plans to implement more satellite SPED classes in mainstream partner schools.  This arrangement will allow the SPED children to interact with children in the mainstream school during some lessons, as well as other activities outside of the classroom.  At the same time, they continue to enjoy the benefits of specialised instructions for most subjects in the SPED classes.  Mainstream students would also benefit from interaction with people with disabilities from a young age, in line with our efforts to build a more inclusive society.

 

     To increase opportunities for interaction and socialisation, five SPED schools are currently located adjacent to mainstream schools.  Another five will be located next to mainstream schools when their purpose-built school buildings are ready.


    For children in SPED schools who can benefit more from pre-vocational training, MOE will be exploring with training agencies like ITE and NTUC to make available to them pre-vocational courses which could be aligned to the Employability Skills System established by the Workforce Development Agency.  This will help enhance their employability, so that they can be integrated into society and be more independent when they grow up.

Many take it upon themselves to upgrade their skills and knowledge by tapping on the wide range of training courses available.  We recognise the demands and challenges of being teachers and are keenly aware of their workload.  Let me share with you three areas in which we are working on to give them greater support.


    Firstly, we are recruiting and deploying more teachers to schools. With the additional teachers recruited over the past five years, we have, on average, been able to give each school an additional five teachers. By about 2010, provided we can sustain our pace of recruitment and keep attrition low, we target to deploy another five teachers to each school.

 

     These additional teachers have resulted in a significant improvement in the pupil to teacher (PTR) ratio over the past few years. The pupil to teacher ratio for primary schools was reduced from 24 in 2004 to 22 in 2006 and for secondary schools from 19 to 18 over the same period. By 2010, our target is to reduce PTR further to below 20 for the primary schools. I note Mr Alvin Yeo's earlier comment about reducing class sizes. Alternatively, it is to assign more classroom periods to each teacher. So recruiting new teachers of lower quality or assigning more classroom periods to each teacher, I think neither is a desirable outcome.


     Beyond the allocation of additional teachers, schools can also leverage on the Adjunct Teaching (AJT) scheme, introduced in 2004, to appoint trained teachers who had left service. The adjunct teachers can support schools when their teachers go on the Part-Time Teaching Scheme, or pursue professional development opportunities.  On average, each school managed to engage about four to five adjunct teachers last year.


     With these additional teachers, schools have greater flexibility in terms of staff deployment.  Many have introduced new programmes, or allowed greater diversity in the school curriculum to better respond to their students' learning needs.  At Guangyang Primary School, they have creatively tapped on their adjunct teachers to conduct differentiated teaching.  During these lessons in Mathematics, Mother Tongue and English, small groups of students who are weaker in these subjects are taught separately by the adjunct teachers, giving them the special and dedicated attention that they need.  The teachers focus on using strategies and activities that best suit the needs of these students.  Such flexibility and enhanced learning have been made possible because of the availability of additional teacher resources that have been judiciously used to meet specific needs.


     To further support teachers and meet the needs of the students, we have deployed para-educators like the Co-Curricular Programme Executives (CCPEs), the Full-Time School Counsellors (FTSCs) and Special Needs Officers (SNOs). These para-educators have enabled teachers to concentrate more on lesson preparation and classroom teaching duties.


     Secondly, MOE has also provided schools with more financial resources to purchase manpower or other services to take over some of the duties previously done by teachers, for example, in running CCAs, training the school choir, and so on, where an external agency may be better equipped with the skill set to do so. This has helped to further ease teachers' workload. The manpower grant had been increased over the years.  For a primary school with 1,500 pupils, it has increased from about $50,000 in 2004 to $126,000 today, a 160% increase.  Likewise, the grant disbursed to a secondary school with the same number of pupils has increased from $115,500 in 2004 to about $150,000 today.

By leveraging on IT, teachers are able to access databases of lesson materials and customise them to meet their students' needs.     The EPMS is a new system fully implemented in 2005, and it was in response to feedback from the ground and designed with their inputs. We believe it is an improvement to the previous system.  And I will talk more about this later.  As professionals, our teachers have no issue with being held to high standards of performance; indeed, they expect it of themselves.  Their request was for the appraisal process to provide a clearer idea of what competencies they were expected to show, and what areas they had to focus on to improve further.  They wanted a more explicit discussion of their performance in specific areas, and clear identification of their training and development path. 


     The EPMS was developed after extensive consultation with education officers at all levels, phasing gradually from 2003 - first to the school leaders, then to Heads of Departments and other key appointment holders, and finally, school wide, to all teachers in 2005.


     We believe the EPMS allows a holistic assessment on our educators' performance based on competencies, and follows a more structured process to help them identify areas for further development and improvement.  The system spells out the knowledge and skills required as well as the professional characteristics and behaviour patterns appropriate for each of the three different career tracks, namely, Teaching, Senior Specialist and Leadership. Let me say that there is no disadvantage in any of our teachers pursuing one of these tracks.  They are not discriminated against in any way in the performance appraisal process.  With this differentiation, teachers have the greater clarity of the expectations and behaviour that are essential for success in each particular field of excellence. 


     No assessment system is perfect, not least because there will always be some degree of subjectivity involved. Neither is the EPMS perfect nor has it been implemented evenly across all schools. We are committed to making it work even better. We are putting new teachers through EPMS workshops to help them better use and understand the tool, and we are putting also the new evaluators through the new supervisors through the system so that they can better understand their responsibilities in assessing teachers under their charge.

 

 

The Ministry will continue to take in feedback and suggestions from our teachers and school leaders on how to improve the system.  For example, we have refined the EPMS work review form to include a training and development section that will encourage a more in-depth discussion of the officers’ career aspirations and developmental needs.

     Members have touched on PMETs (Professionals, Managers, Executives and Technicians) in this Budget Debate - Dr Lim Wee Kiak, Ms Denise Phua, Ms Jessica Tan, Mr Zaqy Mohamad and Mrs Josephine Teo.  Sir, let me turn my attention to this. They are quite right.  We do need coordinated support.  We need to make sure that our programmes are coherent, and they know where to go.  But I want to sound a word of caution.  It does get more difficult. I want to be realistic.

     I mentioned earlier that the projected manpower demand over the next five years is over 450,000 jobs.  The already tight labour market will get even tighter.  Up to a point, as Mrs Josephine Teo mentioned, this is good because wages will rise.  But if costs go up too fast, as she rightly pointed out, ahead of productivity or if businesses cannot meet their manpower needs, then we become less competitive and could lose opportunities for future growth.

 

     Mrs Teo also rightly pointed out that every year, we only have about 33,000 locals.  That is the size of our supply.  And because of our ageing population and falling birth rates, it will actually decline.  Post-2010, the growth rate will actually drop.  I have circulated the charts to show that the employment rates of working age males are already as high as that in other developed countries.  We are among the highest in the world for those number of working.  Now, I need to explain "employment rate".  This is a figure which is the ultimate in employment statistics.  It just measures the number of physical bodies you have and how many in that pool is working.  This is not like unemployment rate.  Because to be unemployed, you must be looking for work, and if you are not looking for work, then you cannot be unemployed.  But the employment rate does not care whether you are looking for work or not.  It just asks a question: do you exist, first; and if you exist, how many of those who are existing are working?  So it is the absolute measurement, if you like.  It is nearly 90%.  We are among the highest in the world, on par with Japan but ahead of many OECD countries.  I have also circulated a chart on employment rates for women, just to complete the picture.  It is much better for cohorts of younger women, but it could be improved for cohorts of older women.

 

     We are literally running out of physical bodies to fill up new jobs that are coming onstream.  450,000 divided by five is about 90,000 new jobs, but we have only a supply of 33,000 and we have reached maximum.  We will have to persuade existing workers to work longer.  Fewer older women are working, so we may have to expand efforts and encourage them to come back to the workforce.  But these efforts, as Members recognise, will take time.  Depending on our local workforce alone will constrain our growth potential.  Therefore, we will have to judiciously use foreign manpower to supplement our local workforce to meet demand.  "We do not intend to shoot ourselves in the foot", a quote that Mrs Teo used.  So to maintain our economic momentum, MOM will be making certain adjustments to help businesses meet their increasing needs for manpower.

 

     In doing so, I also want to address concerns of Members of this House and the public that there must be a balance in allowing foreign workers here, so that Singaporeans will ultimately benefit as the economic pie grows.  I will, therefore, not increase foreign worker quotas.  The quotas will stay.  The only exceptions to this are for the construction and process sectors which have a large demand for manpower.  Mrs Teo asked us to be selective.  This is one selective way.  Except for the construction and process sectors, the quotas for all other industries will stay the same but within the existing cap, certain rules will be relaxed to help businesses source for and retain experienced foreign workers.

 

     First, I will raise the 'S' Pass quota.  To help meet the industry demand for skilled foreign workers, we will allow companies greater access to 'S' Pass holders.  The 'S' Pass holders will be increased to 15% from the present 10%.  But the additional 5% will come from the companies' existing work permit quota.  So, the quota is not raised, and if you want more 'S' Passes, you take it from your work permit quota and you can bring more 'S' Passes in.  In other words, they can recruit more 'S' Passes, but the total proportion of work permit and 'S' Pass holders remains the same.  This will take effect from 1st June 2007.

 

     Second, I will extend the maximum employment period for work permit holders.  Currently, skilled and unskilled work permit holders can be employed for a maximum of 15 years and four years respectively.   To help employers retain their work permit holders longer in a tight labour market, MOM will extend the maximum employment period for skilled and unskilled work permit holders to 18 and six years respectively, ie, a three and two years' extension basically.  This will take effect from 1st April 2007.

 

     Third, certain aspects relating to the construction sector.  Dr Lim Wee Kiak's example, notwithstanding, this is the sector that is really under pressure to build.  I think we need to be easier on them.  They have problems with sand and granite and I do not want workers to be another thing to be on their mind.  The Building and Construction Authority has projected that this sector's annual output will rise to between $14 billion to $17 billion for the next few years, up from the average of about $12 billion in the past three years, so almost a 14% rise.  Naturally, this will increase the demand for construction manpower and BCA estimates that we will need another 40,000 to 50,000 foreign workers over the next two to three years.  So to ease the manpower pressure, we will adopt the following measures from 1st April 2007:

 

     (i) The sectoral dependency ratio will be increased from 1:4 to 1:5;

 

     (ii) We have intended to increase their productivity by cutting their Man Year Entitlement (MYE) for each project by 5%.  We have decided to hold back this reduction and just allow them to use the existing Man Year Entitlement formula.  We do want our projects to be finished on time; and

 

     (iii) Currently, the MYE is also waived for construction workers who have worked for any employer for at least six years.  I will shorten this to four years.  This will increase the pool to about 73,000 workers who will not require a Man Year Entitlement.

 

     Next, on the process industry.  This is a sector that deals with petrochemicals related to building the plants and other aspects.  The process industry is poised for a major boom up to 2010 due to the robust growth in Asia's energy markets and China's increasing demand for petrochemicals.  To harness this growth, from 1st April 2007, we will increase the dependency ratio to 1:5 from the current 1:4, and we will also allow contractors to cross-deploy their workers.

 

     We will monitor the effectiveness of these changes and make further adjustments, if necessary, as warranted by the employment conditions.

 

     While changing or relaxing some of these measures, I want to reassure Members that we have taken their potential concerns into account.
