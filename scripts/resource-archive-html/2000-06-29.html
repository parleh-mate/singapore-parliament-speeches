    BG George Yong-Boon Yeo: The Government has always been committed to helping low income families improve their economic prospects and living standard. First, we ensure that their basic needs are met through affordable housing and healthcare. Second, we help equip them with the means to support themselves by providing heavily subsidised education and skills training. Third, we help create ample job opportunities by building a strong and competitive economy.

    This approach has worked well for us. Over the years, low income families in Singapore have seen their incomes and living standard rise steadily, in real terms.

    Among households in the bottom 20%, 95% own colour TV sets, 90% own telephones, and 77% own washing machines. These ownership rates are better than even the national average of all households in many developing countries.

    Our achievement in home ownership is also good. While many of the poor in other countries are homeless, the lowest 20% of

households in Singapore are well-housed. 75% of them own their own homes.

    We have achieved this without creating dependence or weakening the family, and in a way which gives opportunity and hope for the children of low income families to do better. All segments of our society are thus able to benefit from our economic prosperity, and this is part of the reason for Singapore's political stability.

    Given the progress we have made, many people were surprised and disturbed by newspaper headlines claiming that the lowest 10% of our households lived on only $133 a month. In fact, the headlines were misleading and inaccurate.

    The Department of Statistics had reported that, in 1999, the average income from work of the bottom 10% of households was $133 per month. This was the statistical average. 75% of these households had members who did not work at all and therefore reported zero income. This pulled down the average income for this group of households to $133 per month.

    This statistical average of $133 per month in 1999 was sharply lower than the average of $258 per month in 1998. The main reason was that the proportion of households in the bottom 10% with no working persons went up from 62% in 1998 to 75% in 1999. Put it another way, the proportion of households with persons working fell sharply from more than one-third (38%) to one-quarter.

The Ministry of Manpower (MOM) provides employment assistance, at no charge, to unemployed Singaporeans who have difficulties finding a job, including those in the bottom 10%.     I have taken some time to put the Department of Statistics' figures in context, as it is important that we get a right picture of the bottom 10% income households before we discuss how best to help them. There are poor Singaporeans in our midst but the situation is not at all as bad as the headlines portrayed. To help the less well-off, the Government has put in place a range of assistance programmes.

    The Ministry of Community Development and Sports (MCDS) has various schemes that target the most disadvantaged in our society. The Public Assistance (PA) Scheme provides long term financial help. PA recipients also receive free medical services and assistance in-kind. The Interim Financial Assistance Scheme caters to those in need of shorter term help, while families in rental flats can seek relief from the Rental and Utilities Assistance Scheme (RUAS). There are also child care and student care fee assistance schemes for low-income families. The Community Development Councils (CDCs) further complement these schemes with other assistance tailored for their needy residents.

    For the children of low income families, the Ministry of Education (MOE) renders help for their education. MOE's Financial Assistance Scheme (FAS) provides needy pupils from families receiving social assistance from MCDS a 50% waiver of miscellaneous fees, full waiver of school fees and a textbook grant or an annual bursary. Schools can augment the assistance through the School Advisory Committee or School Management Committee Fund. Children from low-income families who perform well are also awarded Edusave Merit Bursaries. Many charitable foundations and grassroots organisations provide supplementary support.

Together with the National Council of Social Service, MCDS is also encouraging more corporations to sponsor assistance programmes for the less fortunate. This `many helping hands' approach enables us to create a strong basic social safety net for the poor.

The average income ratio of the top 20% to the bottom 20% households has increased from 13.6 times in 1997 to 17.9 times in 1999. This was partly because the impact of the recent recession was much harder on the lower income households than on the higher income households. However, it is also likely to indicate a longer-term structural trend.

    With globalisation, there is now a global market for labour. At the top, scarce talents command high international wages while, at the bottom, the wages earned by those who are unskilled come under increasing downward pressure from the hundreds of millions in the Third World joining the global market who are prepared to work for a pittance. This is a worldwide trend which our system of foreign worker levies ameliorate only to a point.

The average is satisfactory if the 24 incomes are clustered fairly close together. We rank them from the highest to bottom and we choose the 24th person, who is in the middle, and we use that as the representative. This fixed rank approach will not be skewed by the presence of a few very high income earners, and will yield a more stable benchmark. Because I am choosing the income of person number 24, so even if person number 1 earns $10 million instead of $2 million, it does not affect that figure of the middle person unless the whole group moves, in which case it is fair for the median and our benchmark also to move.

GDP-related Bonus

    The GDP-related bonus will directly link the pay of political and statutory appointment holders and senior Administrative Officers to the state of the economy.

    The size of the bonus will depend on the real economic growth of the economy. The bonus will be 1 month if the Singapore economy grows by 5% in the calendar year, the mid-point of its estimated long-term growth potential (4 - 6%). It will be 0 month (the minimum) if the economy grows by 2% or less, and 2 months (the maximum) if the economy grows by 8% or more. In between, it will vary linearly with GDP growth.

    The intention is to eventually build up this bonus to an average of 3 months, with a range of 0-6 months.

Performance Bonus

    Performance bonuses were first introduced for Superscale officers in the Administrative Service in 1989. Since then, they have been extended to timescale Administrative Officers as well.

    The performance bonus norms will be raised as follows:

    Political and statutory appointment holders and Administrative Officers in Superscale C and above presently receive performance bonuses averaging 4 months of salary. This will be increased to an average of 5 months for those in Salary Range SR6 and above.

    Administrative Officers up to Superscale D1 presently receive between 1 - 2 months of performance bonus on average, depending on their grades. This will be increased to an average of between 3 - 4 months.

    How many months of performance bonus an officer actually receives will vary from person to person. Those with unsatisfactory performance will receive no bonus at all, while an outstanding officer will receive much more than the average quantum.

    Several appointments currently receive fixed bonuses instead of a performance bonus: the President, PM, SM, Chief Justice, Speaker, Chairman PSC, Attorney General and Auditor General. This will continue under the revised framework. Their fixed bonus rate will be set at the average rate for senior officers.

Bonus Pool

    Under the bonus pool system, an officer will be paid half his performance bonus for the year immediately. The Government will contribute the other half into a bonus pool. The officer will be entitled to receive the amount in the bonus pool in two equal instalments 12 and 24 months later, provided he continues in Service.

    Officers who resign or are dismissed from the Service will not be allowed to withdraw any balance in the pool. However, officers who retire from the Service will be allowed to withdraw the balance.

    The bonus pool system will not apply for the President, statutory appointment holders and judicial appointees.
