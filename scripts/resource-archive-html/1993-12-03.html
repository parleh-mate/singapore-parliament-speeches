TALKS ON GENERAL AGREEMENT ON TARIFFS AND TRADE


    1. Mr Chia Shi Teck asked the Minister for Trade and Industry (a) what are the probable negative consequences for Singapore if the talks on the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade fail to reach a successful conclusion on l5th December l993; and (b) what are the Government's contingency plans.

     The Minister for Trade and Industry (Mr S. Dhanabalan): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Uruguay Round negotiations are not going smoothly. As the deadline of 15th

Column: 1190

December approaches, there is a special effort underway to resolve the more contentious points. It is therefore likely that the major players will come to some compromise at the last minute. The compromise may result in a smaller package than what is presently being negotiated. A smaller package is better than a complete failure provided that the areas that are excised from the final agreement do not tilt the balance of interests between the developed and developing countries.

    Sir, it s not possible to predict with certainty the outcome of a failure. Certain dangers are clear. The present GATT regime has been eroded by protective measures taken by countries such as arbitrary Anti-Dumping measures, voluntary export restraints, and other unilateral actions. Failure to conclude the Round is likely to accelerate this trend and protectionist sentiments may become dominant in countries which are having difficulty competing against imports. Protectionist measures taken by one country may lead to counter protectionist measures by others. Free trade would come under strong threat, negating the achievements made by GATT in the past decades.

    A spiralling series of protectionist moves with consequent reduction in international trade cannot go on forever. At some point, countries will realise, after paying the price, that a multi-lateral trading system with free flow of goods and services is the only option for economic growth. While we await such a change, we must continue to be nimble, adapt to new circumstances, and use our resources to make ourselves more competitive. We must seek new opportunities and niches in a more protectionist regional and international market.

    To the extent that we can, we should work with countries that have a common interest, to come together and form economic groupings targeted at trade liberalisation. We have started this process within ASEAN under the AFTA Scheme. Economic linkages in groupings

Such groupings cannot replace a global trading system like GATT. But they can give us some respite until we move to an open global free trade system.

    In essence, we will continue to maintain our free trade policy and encourage our neighbours to do so. We shall continue to introduce policies and invest in those areas that will keep Singapore competitive. We will also join economic groupings that aim at promoting free flow of trade and investment.

NATIONAL SERVICEMEN
(Disruption of service for further studies)


    2. Dr Wang Kai Yuen asked the Minister for Defence what is the rationale of requiring national servicemen, whose service was disrupted for further studies, to complete their service in a continuous period lasting no less than six months, where applicable.

Column: 1192



     The Parliamentary Secretary to the Minister for Defence (Mr Matthias Yao Chih) (for the Minister for Defence): Mr Speaker, Sir, disruption for further studies is granted to national servicemen who have 2 1/2 years of full-time liability. Ideally, such servicemen should remain in the Singapore Armed Forces (SAF) for the whole 2 1/2 years to complete all the required training courses and exercises. However, disruption is allowed to this group after they have served a minimum required period so that they need not start their further studies later than those who have only two years to serve.

    Disruption is a postponement of full-time training. It is not the end of full-time national service. The balance period should be effectively used to prepare the servicemen for an operational role. As far as possible, he will be deployed to a unit which is undergoing the type of training he has missed during his disruption. All national servicemen must undergo the key phases of full-time training and be operationally ready by the time they finish national service. To achieve this, the period they spend for training after disruption has to be long enough to enable them to complete the phases they have missed. It would be wasteful and counter-productive for the serviceman if he were to return to the SAF for short spells just so that he can complete his 2 1/2 years service without receiving the appropriate training.

    Furthermore, national servicemen who are disrupted for further studies are likely to hold the rank of officer or junior commander. They must be fully trained to take on leadership roles in their reservist units. Those who miss the prescribed higher level courses before they finish national service will have to be sent for such courses during the remaining period. There will be little or no opportunity for them to take such courses when they have become reservists, as the length of most of the courses at this level is greater than the normal reservist call-up period.

    To ensure proper deployment and minimum waste of the servicemen's time, the SAF therefore requires that the remaining service be served in one

continuous period. Most servicemen have no problems over this requirement. 83% of disrupted servicemen have a balance period of only two months or less, and can complete their service during their first university vacations. The rest, who have longer balance periods, would have missed substantial portions of their training. They should return to follow the full training syllabus appropriate to their ranks and vocation in the same manner as their counterparts who are not disrupted.

     Dr Wang Kai Yuen (Bukit Timah): Sir, what are the specific reasons for not allowing overseas medical students to do their national service during their vacations, a practice which was formerly permissible?

APPLICATION FOR HDB RENTAL FLATS
(Increase in income ceiling)


    5. Mr Low Thia Khiang asked the Minister for National Development what is the rationale for setting the household income ceiling of $800 for eligibility to apply for Housing and Development Board rental flats; and whether he will consider increasing the ceiling to $1,200.

     The Minister of State for National Development (Mr Lim Hng Kiang) (for the Minister for National Development): Mr Speaker, Sir, the Government's aim is to encourage as many Singaporeans as possible to own their flats, so that they can have a stake in the country. Thus, subsidised rental accommodation is limited to those households in the lower income group, with monthly incomes not exceeding $800, who genuinely cannot afford to purchase their own flats.

    Households with incomes of more than $800 should be able to afford the monthly instalments for a new 4-room `budget' HDB flat in New Towns like Choa Chu Kang, Jurong West and Woodlands. The average prices of the 4-room `budget' flats in these New Towns range from $70,000 to $83,000. At such prices, the corresponding monthly instalments payable range from $255 to $300, and can be serviced entirely from the CPF contributions of households with incomes of as low as $850.

    Those who find difficulty buying an HDB flat are provided with assistance under HDB's Low Income Incentive Scheme, whereby families with household incomes of $1,000 or less, buying a 4-room Simplified or smaller flat, are given a loan of up to 95% (instead of the usual 80%) of the selling price of the flat, and need only pay a downpayment of 5%.

    My Ministry therefore does not see the need to revise the $800 income ceiling for rental flats for the time being.

However, the unions have pointed out that their concern is not just with average salaries, but also with the maximum points. PSD has confirmed that Division IV and DRE maximum salaries are below private sector levels by between 20% to 30%. We are therefore adjusting the maximum salary points by 8% to 28%.

Limiting Pension Liability

    In previous salary revisions, increases went into pensionable emoluments. This raised the pension liability of Government proportionately, and resulted in civil servants enjoying less of the benefits of the increase while in service rather than after retirement. Government has decided to make salary increases in this and subsequent revisions non-pensionable. Salary increases will be paid as a Non-Pensionable Component (NPC). This NPC will, however, attract full CPF, even for pensionable officers. Civil servants will therefore not lose out.

    The public sector unions have expressed to the Government their concern that the new scheme should not disadvantage officers who are nearing retirement age, and had expected to retire with the old medical benefits. This group may not accumulate sufficient extra CPF on the Non-Pensionable Component of their salaries before they retire to cover their

expected higher co-payment for medical services after retirement. To address this problem, the Government will pay in-service pensionable officers who retire after 1st January 1994 and receive medical benefits in kind an additional 1% of their salary into their Medisave accounts each month after their retirement, as for officers on the Medisave cum Subsidised Outpatients (MSO) scheme. PSD calculates that this will be sufficient to cover the additional co-payments of most pensioners and their dependants.

    Officers in the five Designated Services are recruited on pensionable terms, even though all other civil servants are now recruited on full CPF. We do not intend to erode the margin which pensionable officers in these five services enjoy over non-pensionable officers who are earning the same salaries but are on full CPF. We are reviewing the superannuation terms of the Designated Services to bring them up to date and in line with these changes.

Statutory Boards

    This revision does not apply automatically to Statutory Boards. As established in the last revision in 1989, Statutory Board salaries should also be in line with overall market levels. Statutory Board revisions, if any, should be settled in consultation with their controlling Ministries.
