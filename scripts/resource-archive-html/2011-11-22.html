Mdm Halimah Yacob : Sir, the Community Care Endowment Fund (ComCare) provides assistance for low-income and needy Singaporeans. ComCare funding and programmes are delivered through our Community Development Councils (CDCs). ComCare beneficiaries are a diverse mix of needy Singaporeans who need different forms of assistance and for varying periods of time. Examples include elderly persons who are unable to work and need long-term assistance, families who require short-term help because the breadwinners have been retrenched from their jobs, families with members recovering from chronic illnesses and parents who require financial support so that their children can attend preschool. ComCare complements other support which the beneficiary may receive from his family as well as the help provided by various other help agencies in the community.

Mrs Lina Chiam asked about the amount of help each beneficiary receives each month. CDC officers will look at each individual's or family's circumstances, including factors such as household size and composition. Depending on the specific circumstances, the CDCs put together a package of assistance to address their needs. The help can be in form of cash assistance, free or subsidised medical care, vouchers for food, rental, utilities, service and conservancy charges, and preschool and student care subsidies. Because of this, it is not meaningful to provide an average figure on how much each beneficiary receives per month. However, we can give some typical examples.

For a family of four, where the male breadwinner with a homemaker wife and two preschool age children has recently lost his job, his family may be provided with $600 a month in cash allowances as well as vouchers for utilities and service and conservancy charges for three months. In addition, his children can receive up to $340 a month each in preschool subsidies, while the parents look for work. This is on top of the universal childcare subsidies which his children are already entitled to. For a couple where the husband has to stop working temporarily due to ill health and the wife has to be his primary caregiver, but has savings and some support from their children, he may receive $300 in cash a month while he recovers at home. They may also receive assistance for utilities and service and conservancy charges as well as rental assistance if they reside in a rental flat. For a single needy elderly without family support, he or she may receive $400 in cash allowances a month. In addition, there are other support such as free medical treatment at polyclinics and restructured hospitals, rental and utilities vouchers and community help. This additional support can amount to about $1,000 a month.

Ms Denise Phua asked how help services provided by the CDCs and other helping hands can be better coordinated and communicated to minimise their falling through the cracks. We agree on the importance of the CDCs to work with the various help services within their respective districts. Currently, this is done through the ComCare Local Networks. Today, nine ComCare Local Networks bring together various agencies comprising voluntary welfare organisations, citizens consultative committees, other social service agencies and even schools, hospitals and self-help groups. Through these networks, agencies come together to collectively identify and sort out systemic issues as well as work out processes to facilitate the referral of cases among the ComCare Local Network (CLN) partners and closing of referral loops. Working together, they are better placed to decide on the best approach to reach out to and assist the needy within their communities. Nonetheless, there is room for improvement.

Electronic Road Pricing (ERP) on Weekends

(Review)

3 Mr Liang Eng Hwa asked the Minister for Transport whether the Ministry would review the need (i) to impose ERP charges on weekends and (ii) to extend the ERP cordoning strategy to weekends.

The Minister of State for Transport (Mrs Josephine Teo) (for the Minister for Transport) : I thank Mr Liang for his question. Traffic congestion at any time leads to a sub-optimal use of our road network. It undermines our quality of life, the environment, and the overall efficiency of the economy. Congestion therefore needs to be kept in check. Where possible, traffic engineering methods, such as road widening, have been implemented to relieve congestion. However, where such solutions are not available or have been exhausted, Electronic Road Pricing (ERP) has been introduced.

Page: 667

We have generally applied ERP based on ensuring an optimal travel speed either on individual roads, such as expressways and major arterial roads, or to the city area using a cordon concept. The cordon concept, where all routes entering the same area are priced uniformly, is necessary because the roads leading into the city area are fairly good substitutes for one another. The lack of a cordon would only lead to traffic diverting into roads that are not priced or priced lower, resulting in greater localised congestion.

The reasons for introducing ERP on Saturdays for the Orchard and Bugis-Marina Centre cordons in 2005 and 2008 respectively still stand today. Since its implementation, ERP has improved traffic speeds within these cordons and maintained them within the acceptable range. ERP rate reviews are carried out quarterly, as well as for the June and December school holidays, to ensure that ERP rates remain relevant to the traffic situation.

While the core principles of ERP remain essential, we will continue to be flexible at the margins where there is room to fine-tune the system. For instance, we recently shortened the evening ERP operating hours for gantries along the northbound Central Expressway (CTE) before the Pan Island Expressway (PIE) and the southbound Singapore River Line, taking into consideration public feedback, traffic conditions and travel patterns. In response to feedback on weekend ERP and cordon charging, and also to take into account the changes in the road network in the vicinity of Marina Centre area, we will be moving the Nicoll Highway gantry in 2012 and adjust the Bugis-Marina Centre cordon, to enable motorists to access the East Coast Parkway (ECP) via the Nicoll Highway without having to pass through and pay ERP at the cordon. In doing so, motorists will have a greater choice in choosing their travel routes and times. Motorists who want a smoother drive can still do so by paying the ERP charges. We will continue to monitor the traffic situation and fine-tune the ERP scheme where necessary.

Previously, suppliers of such goods needed to maintain documentary proof of the export of the goods for GST zero-rating. To promote the competitiveness of the biomedical sector in Singapore, the ACMT scheme is extended to qualifying biomedical contract manufacturers.

The scheme has also been enhanced to further reduce GST compliance cost by enabling local contract manufacturers, including those in the biomedical sector, to:

(1) disregard the services rendered on failed or excess production; and

(2) recover GST on local purchases of goods used in the contract manufacturing process.

Clause 8 of the Bill gives effect to the changes in the scheme.

A new zero-rating relief is also introduced for specified services made to overseas persons and performed on specified goods kept in approved warehouses in Singapore. The new relief aims to encourage overseas persons to store high value goods, such as art, antiques and gold, in specialised storage facilities in Singapore, and purchase-related services, such as auction, insurance and valuation in respect of the stored goods. The GST zero-rating also covers the renting of storage units used to store such high value goods. Clauses 3 and 4 of the Bill provide for this initiative.

The Customs (Amendment) Bill comprises three main categories of changes. The first category seeks to enhance Customs enforcement. The second category allows Singapore Customs to share information collected under the Customs Act with domestic public agencies for the investigation and prosecution of certain offences, or when it is in the public interest to do so. The third category improves the administration of the Customs Act. Let me now highlight the key changes.

First, we will increase the penalties for tobacco-related offences. Sir, from 2005 to 2010, the number of tobacco-related customs offences increased by about 24%, while the number of repeat offenders in the same period increased by more than six times. We will introduce mandatory imprisonment for repeat offenders caught with more than 2 kg of tobacco products, and increase the minimum court fine to $4,000 for all repeat offenders, comprising $2,000 for the unpaid duty, and $2,000 for the unpaid GST.

We will also increase the court fines for first-time offenders caught with contraband cigarettes. Currently, the minimum court fine as provided under the Act is 10 times the duty and tax payable, which could at times be lower than the composition sum for minor offence cases. The minimum court fine will therefore be raised to $2,000 for first-time offenders, comprising $1,000 each for the unpaid duty and the unpaid GST. Clause 17 of the Bill provides for these changes.

We will also amend the Act to extend liability to any person who furnishes incorrect information to another for making declarations. Currently, traders may appoint third-party agents to declare their consignments. However, under the Act, only declaring agents who make false declarations may be prosecuted, but not the traders who provide the information. Clause 15 of the Bill allows Singapore Customs to prosecute traders who have provided incorrect information as well.

Page: 695

I shall now move on to the amendment that facilitates the disclosure of information to domestic public agencies, subject to safeguards.

Currently, information declared by traders is strictly confidential. It can only be disclosed with their consent, or if the information is necessary for the purposes of an investigation, enforcement or prosecution under the Customs Act or the Goods and Services Tax Act. This is to protect sensitive commercial data and uphold our integrity as a trading hub. However, we recognise that there may be over-riding reasons for Singapore Customs to disclose information to other domestic public agencies. These could be for the purpose of investigation and prosecution of offences under other domestic laws, or when there are national security, public health and safety concerns.

The Customs Act will thus be amended to allow Singapore Customs to disclose information to domestic public agencies, where public interests are of concern. To reassure traders that confidentiality of their trade data will be respected, comprehensive procedural safeguards will be established. Agencies requesting for customs information will be required to seek approval from the Minister for Finance. Each request will be considered carefully, and will be granted only when there is a strong justification to do so. In addition, any information disclosed to the domestic public agencies will be governed by the Official Secrets Act. Clause 11 of the Bill provides for this change.

Sir, I will now touch on some of the amendments that will improve the administration of the Act.

We will introduce a ticketing system for minor customs offences. This helps to streamline the enforcement process by automatically requiring any offender who fails to pay the composition sum to be brought to court. The offences for which the ticketing system is applicable will be prescribed. Clause 14 of the Bill provides for this change.

We will also enable Singapore Customs to appoint persons such as a taxpayer's bank or employer as an agent for recovering unpaid duties. This is similar to the provision for the Income Tax Act and GST Act. Clause 13 of the Bill provides for these changes.

The next change pertains to the use of image systems for the retention of trade documents. Traders must retain trade documents, regardless of whether these are in hardcopy or electronic form, for no less than five years. Clauses 10 to 12 of the Bill allow traders to keep an electronic image of the records instead of the original documents, at any time after the goods in question have been removed from customs control.

We will also introduce a provision to facilitate the implementation of the Compressed Natural Gas (CNG) duty. In Budget 2009, we announced that CNG will be dutiable from 1 January 2012. The Customs Act will therefore be amended to define CNG as a dutiable fuel, and to extend the three-quarter tank rule to include CNG tanks. Clauses 2, 4, 18 and 20 of the Bill provide for this change.

To conclude, the Customs Act is being updated to enhance customs enforcement, to allow Singapore Customs to share information collected under the Customs Act with domestic public agencies where public interests are of concern, and to improve the administration of the Act. 33 Dr Lily Neo asked the Acting Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports (a) whether there is a national definition of poverty line and, if so, what is the current definition; (b) how many households fall below this line; and (c) whether more assistance can be rendered to such households.

Some establish a poverty line to identify those who need help. For example, in the United States, the 2010 threshold for a family of four works out to be about US$1,860 (S$2,390) per month. Based on this threshold, the US' poverty rate is about 15.1%. On the other hand, other countries like Canada and New Zealand do not have an official poverty line. Canada, for instance, adopts an income threshold, determined using family expenditure data, in guiding its assessment of the populace's needs.

There is no hard and fast rule on how to determine needs. In Singapore, we take an approach of tailoring our social safety net to different groups, depending on their needs and circumstances. This approach is more flexible and no less effective than countries that choose to adopt a poverty line. Our help schemes typically cover the bottom 20th percentile of households, with the flexibility to go beyond if the family's circumstances merit special consideration. This approach allows us to provide more targeted and customised forms of assistance with real outcomes for families and individuals.
