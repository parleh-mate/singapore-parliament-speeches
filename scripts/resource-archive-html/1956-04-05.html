    In assessing the position of Singapore today, hovering as it does on the brink of self-government, my thoughts go back to the India of ten years ago, because, by a curious coincidence, I lived in India for the last ten years of the great country's life as a dependent territory, just as I have spent a similar period in Singapore, and several points of comparison occur to me between the India of 1946 and the Singapore of today, poised, both of them, on the eve of achieving independence.

    The first is that, whereas Britain was about to cede to the Indian peoples a vast country which had been theirs for hundreds of years before the British appeared on the scene, in the case of Singapore Her Majesty's Government is about to cede a territory for which all the communities, including the British, have had, one might say, a more or less equal share in building up. Or to put it another way: whereas nobody could seriously question that India belonged and always had belonged to the Indians as the indigenous race, there could be some legitimate argument on that score regarding Singapore.

    Secondly, that whereas India was and is a vast sub-continent measuring thousands of square miles, Singapore is a small island of 220 square miles or thereabouts which one perhaps might compare with Bombay Island in point of size and population, though it is a place of far greater strategic importance to the free world than is apparent from its size.

    Thirdly, that whereas India had considerable experience of democratic rule and of elected or partially elected provincial and local governments, in some cases going back decades before 1946, Singapore has had little, indeed insufficient, experience of this sort.

    Fourthly, that whereas India had just emerged from the Second World War in which its army had fought victoriously and with the greatest distinction, and in which it had been geared industrially to contribute immensely to the
