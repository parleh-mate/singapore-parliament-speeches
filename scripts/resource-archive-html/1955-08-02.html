(INTERPRETATION)
(Message from the Governor)


    Mr Speaker: Hon. Members, I have a message from His Excellency the Governor. It takes the form of a letter addressed to me, and is dated the 2nd of August, 1955. Here is the message:

    "On the 25th July you delivered to me a certified copy of the Resolution passed by the Legislative Assembly on that date and, in a reply which I handed to you on the same afternoon, I explained that I would require time to study the implications of the Resolution before I could put you in a position to convey a reply to the Assembly.

    I communicated the terms of the Resolution to Her Majesty's Principal Secretary of State for the Colonies, and, during his recent visit to Singapore, he took the opportunity to discuss both with the Chief Minister and with myself, the various issues raised.

Column: 517



    As discussions are to be continued when the Secretary of State returns to Singapore on the 15th August, I am not yet in a position to send a full reply. I would, however, be grateful if you would convey the following observations to the Legislative Assembly.

    On the first point raised in the Resolution, I am very glad to be able to inform Honourable Members that the Secretary of State has asked me to assure them of the continuing sympathetic interest which Her Majesty's Government takes in Singapore's problems and of the sincerity of their policy to assist Singapore to achieve self-government within the British Commonwealth.

    On the second point raised, I gladly repeat my former affirmation of my sympathetic desire to give the Government all possible help in the execution of its policies. In so doing I do not propose to exercise my discretionary powers in a didactic or harsh manner, or in any way calculated to frustrate the Government in discharging the responsibilities which it has accepted.

    On the third and fourth points in the Resolution, valuable and friendly talks have taken place between the Secretary of State, the Chief Minister, and myself, and examination of these important points has been carried as far as it could be at present. As I have already indicated, it is proposed to resume these talks on the return of the Secretary of State to Singapore on the 15th of August and I hope to be able to send a substantive reply to the Assembly on the 18th August."

    Mr Speaker: Before somebody moves the adjournment, I have a communication to make.

    On The 25th of July in this Assembly, I was asked by the Hon. the Chief Minister to consider whether a report, which appeared in The Singapore Free Press of Friday, 22nd July, was a breach of the liberties and privileges of this House.

    The report was headlined "Royal message attacked" and "MARSHALL: I FELL FOR IT", and reads as follows:

    'The Chief Minister, Mr David Marshall, in the special Legislative Assembly meeting this morning said that his faith in the workability of the Rendel Constitution was strengthened by the messages of the Queen and the previous Governor, Sir John Nicoll.

    'Referring to the sentiments of the Queen about the constitutional progress of Singapore, Mr Marshall said:

    "It was full of promise. I fell for it - hook, line and sinker.

    "I believed every word of it."

    'Then after a reference to Sir John Nicoll's message, he added: "Now the claws of the beast show through the velvet glove."'

    Hon. Members will remember that a Bill the short title of which is "The Legislative Assembly (Powers and Privileges) Ordinance, 1955" went through all its stages in this Assembly and is now awaiting the signification of Her Majesty's pleasure in accordance with section 62 of the Singapore Colony Order-in-Council, 1955.

    Certain powers and privileges of this Assembly are defined in that Bill, and clause 24 (1) (c) makes it an offence for any person to publish "any writing containing a gross, wilful or scandalous misrepresentation of the speech of any member in the proceedings of the Assembly".

Column: 532



    As I see it, it was perhaps unfortunate that the writer of the article should have seized upon a reference to the Royal Message and made headlines of it without regard to the context of the whole speech.

    The Chief Minister appears to have assumed, as he was indeed entitled to assume, that the sentiments of Her Majesty the Queen were, in fact, the sentiments of Her Majesty's Government. To quote the words of Sir Thomas Erskine May:

    "The Queen cannot be supposed to have a private opinion apart from that of Her responsible advisers."

    The Chief Minister's "attack" (to adopt the word used in the report), as was apparent from his whole speech, seems to me, in fact, to have been an "attack" on Her Majesty's Government and the interpretation put on the Rendel Constitution by Her Majesty's Representative in Singapore.

    This regrettably does not end the matter.

    The writer, in the first paragraph of his article, has put words in the mouth of the Chief Minister, which I cannot find in the Official Report of the Chief Minister's speech made in this Chamber. In addition, the words in the final paragraph of the article were so taken out of the context of the speech that a reader might well consider that they referred to what is described in the article as "the sentiments of the Queen about the constitutional progress of Singapore".

    With these additions, the impression must have been created in the minds of some readers, as pointed out by the Chief Minister, that the Chief Minister was guilty of casting some reflection on Her Majesty. This particularly is extremely unfortunate. I was and am entirely satisfied that no disrespect was shown or intended, and the Chief Minister has himself stated that he personally resents very strongly the suggestion that he should be capable of discourtesy to Her Majesty.

    The inclusion of the words in the final paragraph of the article out of the context of the Chief Minister's speech resulted in misrepresentation, which is to be deplored.
