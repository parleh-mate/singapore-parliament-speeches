PRESIDENT'S ADDRESS



    The President (accompanied by two A.D.C.s) entered the Chamber, accompanied by Mr Speaker, who was preceded by the Serjeant at Arms (without the Mace), the Second Assistant Clerk, the First Assistant Clerk and the Clerk.

    On the Clerk announcing "The President", the President delivered his Opening Speech.

8.31 p.m.

    The President (Dr Benjamin Henry Sheares): Honourable Members, we have

gathered today at the start of a five-year term of this fourth Parliament since 1965.

    It will be idle to pretend that the security and economic prospects are better now than when we met in October 1972. Then the Americans were still in Vietnam and backing the Thieu regime. Now, they have departed even from Thailand. 1972 was before the oil crisis, and more rapid growth was in the offing. Now, there is uncertainty and anxiety amongst the major industrial nations of the West. It is an open question whether they can co-ordinate their economic policies.

    My government has again been returned to office. The PAP won in all constituencies. This government has the highest number of votes, and the highest percentage of votes (72.4%) cast in the six general elections held since representative government in 1955. This vote of confidence imposes a heavy responsibility on my Ministers to act fairly but firmly. My government will exercise its authority to govern with circumspection and moderation.

    It also places on all Honourable Members, especially those not holding office, the duty to ensure that all shades of opinion out in the constituencies are vigorously voiced in Parliamentary questions and debates. No major policy or legislation should be passed without the closest scrutiny, and passed only after the most thorough debate.

    The mandate on which my government was elected is clearcut. The issues were fought for months, indeed years, before the elections. Many of the opposition political parties, both those who took part in the elections and those who did not, repeated the demands of the Communist Party of Malaya (Malaya including Singapore) (CPM), regularly voiced over their clandestine radio. These demands include the unconditional release of all communist and pro-communist detainees, abolition of the Internal Security Act, and abolition of National Service.

    The people have rejected these and supported the government, But it would be naive to believe that the Communist Party of Malaya, in their armed struggle for a communist Malaya, will not try to abuse the opportunities of the democratic processes, and the mass media, to help camouflage their armed struggle to grab power. They now style themselves as the "new left". But their techniques of oft repeated lies, and their exploitation of innocent soft-hearted or soft-headed "liberals", are long-established techniques and tactics of the communists, learned by the CPM from other communist parties in Europe and Asia, repeated with minor variations on new audiences in different places. And there is always the younger, politically innocent, if not naive, generation.

    The vote is also a renewed mandate not to be beguiled away from a most necessary law, which the British themselves drafted and promulgated when faced with communist insurgency in 1948, namely, detention of communist and pro-communist elements without going through the processes of a public trial accorded to ordinary criminals. My government, in accord with humane sentiment, will release them if they will denounce the CPM's use of force to seize power, and themselves forswear the use of force to unseat a constitutionally elected government.

    Lest it be forgotten, it is a mandate also to reject the soft-headed, pernicious policies that all the good things in life, from bus rides to PUB charges, can be given for free, or otherwise to reduce the fares or charges when the costs are going up. Only education, medical treatment and housing will be subsidised, in order to help everyone to be educated and healthy to be able to look after himself. But nominal charges must be paid even for schools, to pay for the extra curricular activities, and hospitals, to prevent unnecessary pressure on medical facilities.

    Before us are some terrifying examples of what can happen to those who go in for soft options and subsidised living, a temptation in any one-man-one-vote election campaign. When my Prime Minister visited China last year, his realistic communist hosts described such sad situations as "teachers by negative example".

    My government holds firm to this simple truth: that to get from each his best, rewards and recognition must go to those who excel in consistently high performance. Between the soft solutions of some West European socialists of equal rewards, and the hard realities of Asian conditions, my government has no doubts that the people of Singapore unequivocally uphold Asian precepts - "From each his best; To each his worth". The communists in China have a variation of this in their aphorism: "Ge jin suo neng, an lao fen pei" () - from each according to his ability, to each according to his labour.

    However, we are different from China. We have access to modern science and technology. We have rapid economic and material transformation that comes from these modern management methods, capital inputs and marketing knowhow. But we must not abandon the cardinal precepts of our long-proven culture systems, precepts which enabled some Asian civilisations to survive, and without a break in continuity, floods. famines, pestilence, wars, disasters and foreign conquests.

    Whilst we admire and must learn and acquire their science and technology, their management skills and marketing knowhow, we do not seek to model our lives on Western social mores and their contemporary life-styles. We must learn the experience of the Japanese - how, in less than a hundred years since the Meiji Restoration of 1868, they have become a modern industrial state, without abandoning their own cultural traditions. Their experience is more relevant to us than those of the countries of Western Europe. The exceptions are perhaps West Germany and Switzerland.

    We have to formulate our way of life, taking what is best from the West and fitting it into the Singapore context. We must not allow our values and our philosophy of what is good government to be overwhelmed by the standards and norms of the contemporary West, regardless of their relevance to our social, economic and political conditions, simply because, for the time being the West have the material abundance and technological superiority. Let us select the relevant factors in their societies, factors which have made them strong and have been proven by the test of time. Then we can incorporate these factors into our system without damage to ourselves.

MINISTRY OF FINANCE


Economic Policy

    Over the next few years, our aim will still be to make Singapore grow fast again. Old and new curbs on trade by the richer countries in the world will limit such growth. But Singapore should grow by at least 6- 8% every year. We can then provide enough jobs for workers And we can enjoy an increasingly higher standard of living because we can pay for our needs in food, housing, education and health and for more and better goods and services.

Monetary and Fiscal Policy

    We shall maintain the Singapore dollar as a strong and stable currency. The money supply will be expanded moderately. The money, bond, capital and foreign exchange markets will be further developed in our financial centre. The intention is that there will always be finance at reasonably low interest rates to encourage sound projects.

    There will, however, be no extravagance in public sector expenditures. These will be more efficiently controlled by a new style budget by 1979 This budget will enable the work programmes of each Ministry of Government to be examined against its targets and its performance Funds can then be allocated on their review

    As Singapore grow, what we collect from our present taxes grows also. With good growth, we shall rarely have to put up tax rates or think of major new taxes to pay for our increased needs. But we may want, as one way of helping to encourage or discourage one kind of activity over another, to change a tax or add another to our tax system. We may wish to favour manufacturing, international finance and banking. reinsurance, warehousing of capital goods and equipment for sale to regional and international markets, and tourism and conventions. A Fiscal Policy Implementation Committee will suggest how best we can do this.

Column: 20



Industrial Development

    We want much more manufacturing in Singapore. We shall therefore continue, through the Economic Development Board, to offer special help, by lower taxes or otherwise, to investors who set up new industries or expand old ones.

    We have ready now, in our industrial estates, land and factory buildings served with power, water, telephone and other services. If necessary, we shall build more, especially smaller, factory buildings. These will be sold at low prices to manufacturers who support bigger ones by supplying small parts or do other services for them

    We need to keep down costs so that the goods we make will not be too dear to compete with those other countries produce. We shall watch carefully therefore against too high Government charges for industry such as for port services or rates for electricity and water. But we may have, from time to time, to meet higher prices such as for oil. We shall seek, through the National Wages Council, single annual wage increases which reward workers who produce more.

    We shall spend more on Training Centres to give better training to our workers so that they have the highest manual skills for delicate, precision work. And we shall also persuade those who own or manage businesses to learn how to make better use of their workers and their machinery and to sell their products also in new and wider markets overseas.

Trade Development

    We shall help in a number of ways to make trade flow more easily. We shall do more to improve regional markets by increasing ASEAN economic cooperation and making preferential trading arrangements. Finance for exports will be made more easily available. The Monetary Authority of Singapore will keep its rate for rediscounting export bills attractive. We shall arrange with the Development Bank of

Column: 21

Singapore for long term loans to be given on easy terms to sell ships built in Singapore yards. The Export Credit Insurance Scheme will be extended to cover business and political risks for capital goods also. Small industries will get increased aid from the Small Industries Finance Scheme.

Tourism

    We shall encourage more tourists to come to Singapore, especially from ASEAN countries. We shall promote more conventions for business and professional men. We shall try to increase the number of group tours by getting more chartered flights into Singapore.

MINISTRY OF DEFENCE


    The General Elections of December 1976 proved that the Republic's citizens accept national service as a necessary duty of the young. Only in this way is it possible for a small country to achieve the minimum security required for confidence and development.

    However, since our defence forces have to import nearly all their weapons and equipment, the rising prices of these products impose a severe financial burden. It is therefore necessary to exercise the utmost economy in defence expenditure. In November 1976, a committee of Finance and Defence Ministries' officials was appointed to maintain a continuing surveillance over costs and to prune down expenditure.

    It is also necessary to economise in the use of manpower. As a result of successful family planning in past years, the number of young men available for national service in future years will show a steady but relentless decline. In ten years' time, the number available will be nearly 30% less than what it is now. There are a number of implications which are being studied in the Ministry of Defence. It will certainly be necessary to trim down manpower establishments and reduce or even abolish vocations which do not

Column: 22

contribute much to the combat efficiency of army units. It will also be necessary to increase the number of regulars, especially in the NCO ranks. In the longer term, if force structure is to be maintained at current levels, it is more than likely that national service will have to be extended to women, for a start, possibly on a selective basis, to fill up non-combat posts.

GOH KENG SWEE
Minister of Defence

MINISTRY OF LABOUR


    With an open economy. Singapore will inevitably be affected by the uncertain international economic situation in the immediate years ahead. Without natural resources of its own, the Republic must move to minimise the adverse effects of international economic developments. In the labour field, the Ministry will continue to work with unions and employers to contribute to economic growth through the promotion of a stable and peaceful industrial climate, the fostering of a disciplined and productive workforce and the safeguarding of the welfare, safety and health of our workers.

I Industrial Relations

    Singapore's main resource is manpower, trained and productive. Our workers must work harder, upgrade their skills and increase their productivity. Union leaders at headquarters and branch levels as well as workers should accept that future wage increases have to be orderly and related to higher productivity and economic growth. Fringe benefits which have escalated in recent years must not be extended in range or quantum. Those which are bizarre or not conducive to economic progress will have to be discontinued. The National Wages Council will examine the question of the full offsetting of its wage guidelines against all forms of increases of remuneration. It will also consider whether employees who job-hop should be granted the full wage increase recommended. Rationalisation of fringe benefits will also be looked into by the

Column: 23

Council. At the plant level, employers will be encouraged to implement productivity incentive schemes to ensure greater efforts on the workers' part and reward for such additional performance.

    The Ministry will further improve its industrial relations services by adopting a more alert and positive approach to the prevention and settlement of industrial disputes. A departmental service will be introduced to build up a corps of experienced labour and industrial relations officers.

II Manpower Utilisation

    Measures will be taken by the Employment Service to secure employment for more persons on its register of unemployed. The present Disablement Resettlement Scheme for the training and subsequent employment of disabled persons will be extended to industrially disabled workers.

    Although about 4 per cent of its workforce are registered as technically unemployed at the Employment Service, Singapore still has to grant work permits to large numbers of foreign workers. Young Singaporeans have become choosy about their employment preferring not to accept "hard" jobs even though they are better paid. They prefer to remain unemployed until a "soft" job to their liking is found. The Ministry of Labour is examining a study group's recommendations for encouraging Singaporeans to work in the construction industry. Studies will be undertaken of other industries which are avoided by Singaporeans so that corrective action can be taken.

III Industrial Safety and Health

    Special attention will continue to be paid to reducing the accident rate of the construction industry and the shipbuilding and repairing industries. The Factory Inspectorate will ensure better compliance by contractors of the provision of life-line anchorages in view of the large number of construction workers injured or killed through falling from heights. It will conduct further studies on means of providing life-line anchorages for walls without openings.

    To reduce the number of workers being struck by falling objects, the lnspectorate will step up enforcement. Contractors will be required to provide safety measures such as safety helmets, overhead shelters and proper debris disposal facilities. They will also have to supervise their workers to ensure that they comply with safety rules. With more machines being used, training of operators in their proper handling will be intensified. Shipyard personnel and contract workers will continue to be trained in safety measures, ship-inspection and fire-fighting. Shipyards will be further encouraged to introduce self-regulating systems of safety and health as part of their management functions and assistance will be provided where necessary.

    The Factory Inspectorate will work with SISIR and professional bodies to produce codes of practice for the design and erection of scaffolds, formworks. machine guarding, lifting equipment and pressure vessels. Amendments to the Factories Act will be introduced to provide for enhanced penalties for offences which are likely to cause the death of or bodily injury to workers. The Ministry is studying a proposal to establish an Occupational Safety and Health Institute to provide training, consultancy and research in industrial safety and health. A report on the matter has been submitted by an ILO expert on occupational safety and health.

    The Industrial Health Department will take measures to protect workers from cancer-causing substances used in industry. Additional programmes to improve the competence of industrial health nurses will be introduced.

Training of supervisors and junior and middle management executives will be given more attention. Consultancy in small business management will be introduced while those in industrial engineering, management techniques and low cost automation will be continued. Electricity

    The growth in maximum demand of electricity of 11% per year in 1975 and 1976 is indicative of the general trend of economic recovery in the Republic after the low growth of 3% in 1974 following the oil crisis. Over the next 5 years, a 58% increase is expected and more power projects are underway to cater for the 1980's.

    Stage I of Senoko Power Station has been completed and work is currently in progress on Stage II comprising three 250 MW units. This stage which is estimated to cost $522 million and is scheduled for completion in 1980 will increase the total generating capacity to 2090 MW. Plans for Stage III of Senoko are presently under study. For demand beyond the 1980's, another power station will be necessary and its proposed location is under consideration. Concomitant with the generation of more electricity is the extension of transmission and distribution system which will cost $791.7 million over the next 5 years.

    69% of the Board's annual recurrent expenditure before depreciation and interest charges is on fuel oil for electricity generation. Continuous efforts are being made to increase operational efficiency of generators to conserve the consumption of fuel oil. As a result of increasing efficiency, the Board has, so far, been able to absorb the 10% increase (from $160 to $177 per ton) in the price of fuel oil since October 1975, without increasing the price of electricity to consumers. However, the latest increase in oil price will necessitate an upward revision of the tariffs. Economy in the usage of electricity will not only benefit consumers but will also save foreign exchange for the Republic.

    Construction of Western Catchments Scheme, which will commence this year, covers the four new reservoirs by the damming of four rivers - Murai, Poyan. A Philosophy of Government

    Some 40 years ago, in the depths of gloom of the last war, the British worked out their Beveridge plan, a welfare state which will care for people from cradle to grave, from womb to tomb.
