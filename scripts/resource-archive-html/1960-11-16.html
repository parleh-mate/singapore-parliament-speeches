    When the Criminal Law (Temporary Provisions) Amendment Bill was moved in August 1959, it was intended that power should be granted to the Minister to direct that a supervisee shall remain within doors and to prohibit the supervisee from visiting specified areas which have been the scene of his past criminal activities. When the law was passed, the power of the Minister to direct that a supervisee shall remain within such area as may be specified in a Supervision Order was interpreted to mean that a person under Supervision Order can be prohibited from visiting any area specified in the Order. In an appeal against the acquittal of a supervisee for breaking this condition, it was held by the High Court that the power to confine a person on a Supervision Order to a particular area does not entitle the Minister to impose the condition that

Column: 840

he shall not visit the area specified in the Order.

    Sir, in Government's campaign against secret societies, it has been found that such a provision in the Criminal Law has been effective in curbing secret society activities. The continued use of this condition is therefore very desirable in order to prevent persons placed under Supervision Orders from visiting areas which have been the scene of their past secret society activities. The Criminal Law (Temporary Provisions) Ordinance is therefore being amended to make this condition intra vires the law. This condition is used judiciously and is applied mainly to those secret society gangsters who, although they do not live or work in certain streets or areas, have been concentrating their activities or exerting their evil influence in those streets or areas.
